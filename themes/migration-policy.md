# Background

Migration is a structural feature of the 21st-century international system, not an episodic crisis. The combination of conflict-induced displacement, climate-driven mobility, demographic asymmetry between aging industrial economies and young developing ones, and wage differentials creates sustained cross-border movement that national policy frameworks are not designed to absorb. Migration policy has become simultaneously an instrument of statecraft (used to coerce neighbors, extract concessions, and manage labor markets) and a stress test of governance (revealing the gap between constitutional commitments and political capacity to honor them).

The concept file consolidates the "protection vs control" tension that runs through multiple source analyses: route-based approaches that promise better coordination but can legitimize externalized control; pragmatic engagement with repugnant regimes whose cooperation is needed for returns; and the weaponization of anti-migration sentiment by political entrepreneurs who substitute scapegoating for governance.

# Current Situation

## Route-Based Approaches: One Concept, Two Logics

Knapp and Koch ([SWP]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/swp.html)) map the rise of route-based approaches (RBA) — treating migration corridors as connected political and humanitarian spaces rather than isolated country contexts. The approach has gained traction across the IFRC (which pioneered the humanitarian version), UNHCR, IOM, the [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) "Whole-of-Route Approach," and national governments from the UK to Switzerland to Denmark [1].

The same concept serves opposite purposes. For humanitarian organizations, the RBA promises better protection, coherent service provision along corridors, and the bridging of the forced-migration/economic-migration dichotomy. For European governments, it structures cooperation with origin and transit states on returns, border management, and anti-smuggling operations — a vehicle for the externalization of migration control. The EU's Team-Europe Initiatives along the central Mediterranean and Atlantic routes, and its migration deals with [Tunisia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/tunisia.html) (2023) and Mauritania (2024), illustrate the control orientation: partner selection and financing follow the route, but protection standards are secondary [1].

The concept's analytical appeal rests on its ambiguity. Until a shared definition, common indicators, and verifiable protection standards exist, "route-based" will remain a container into which any government can pour its preferred priorities. Knapp and Koch recommend binding standards through the UN80 process: joint data collection, transparent work-sharing, and reviewable protection indicators [1].

## Externalization and Its Limits

The EU-Taliban engagement documented by Giustozzi ([RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html)) is an extreme case of externalization logic: a European policy of engagement with a regime it does not recognize, driven by the need to manage Afghan migration. The Taliban offered to cooperate on returns in exchange for transfer of Afghan consulates in Europe to their control. [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html), initially closer to opponents of normalization, began deporting Afghans to Kabul in August 2024 and has since handed the Bonn consulate and Berlin embassy to Taliban-appointed representatives — driving the broader EU shift [2].

The logic is structural: the Taliban hold the stronger negotiating position because of the migration issue. There is no viable alternative to consular transfer if returns are to be processed. Hundreds of thousands of Afghans in Europe without legal status, plus hundreds of thousands more in [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) seeking to enter Europe, create an unsustainable status quo that member states are already undermining through bilateral deals. Critics of the EU's approach offer no alternative other than maintaining that status quo. The case demonstrates that externalization extends to engagement with regimes whose values are diametrically opposed to European constitutional norms — and that migration pressure is sufficient to override those norms [2].

## Xenophobic Mobilization as Governance Substitute

Müller ([SWP]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/swp.html)) documents the South African case in which anti-migration sentiment has been organized into a sustained national campaign that functions as a substitute for governance. See [South Africa]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/south-africa.html). The pattern — a liberation movement whose transformation promise was unfulfilled, a large unemployed youth population, service-delivery failure, and a visible migrant minority — is not unique to South Africa. The instrumentalization of xenophobia by political entrepreneurs (Operation Dudula, March and March, and the MK party's backing) illustrates how anti-migration sentiment can be networked, amplified via social media, and used to destabilize an already-fragile coalition government [3].

# Key Dynamics

- **Protection and control are not separable in practice**: Route-based programs that combine service provision with return cooperation risk using protection narratives to legitimize externalized control. The same program can improve conditions along a corridor and deepen the surveillance capacity of authoritarian transit states.
- **Migration pressure overrides values**: When the volume of unwanted migration becomes politically unsustainable, governments engage with regimes they would otherwise shun. The EU-Taliban case is the leading edge; the pattern generalizes to any regime controlling a significant migration corridor.
- **Scapegoating scales faster than governance**: Networked xenophobic mobilization — social-media-amplified, "concerned citizen"-framed, linked to but plausibly deniable by political parties — can outpace institutional responses. The South African template is exportable.
- **Regional spillover is a first-order consequence**: Deportation and return policies affect neighboring states whose absorptive capacity is limited. South Africa's returns to Zimbabwe, Mozambique, and Malawi; Europe's returns to Afghanistan and North Africa — each creates downstream instability that feeds back into migration pressure.

# Analysis

## The Protection-Control Convergence

Knapp and Koch identify a structural convergence: protection and control are no longer negotiated separately but within a shared programmatic frame. This is politically efficient — it allows governments to claim humanitarian credentials while pursuing enforcement — but it risks concealing fundamental goal conflicts. When governments strategically use protection-oriented narratives to make externalized border control politically palatable, the protection content erodes over time. The route-based approach becomes a technocratic framing that depoliticizes normative conflicts over who has the right to move and who has the obligation to protect [1].

The recommendation is not to abandon route-based approaches but to bind them: verifiable protection indicators, independent monitoring, local-actor participation in planning and evaluation, and a conscious inclusion of South-South mobility patterns rather than treating all routes as European-bound. The Horn of Africa and the [Sahel]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/sahel-insurgency.html), where conflict-induced displacement intersects with climate mobility and labor migration, are the test cases [1].

## The Authoritarian Bargain

Giustozzi's analysis of the EU-Taliban engagement reveals the structure of the authoritarian bargain that externalization produces. The Taliban do not need to moderate their behavior on women's rights, education, or governance to extract concessions — they need only control the migration corridor. The more repressive the regime, the more leverage it holds, because repression produces the very outflows that Europe seeks to manage. European diplomats have held repeated informal talks with Taliban representatives in Doha and the UAE since 2021; the June 2026 Brussels visit was not a departure but a public step in a process long underway [2].

The case illustrates a general proposition: migration externalization empowers authoritarian regimes at the expense of both European values and the populations seeking protection. The only available counter — conditioning engagement on human-rights improvements — has failed because the migration pressure is sufficient to override the conditionality [2].

## Fair Recruitment and the Protection Gap

Knapp and Landmesser ([SWP]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/swp.html)) expose a structural vulnerability in [Germany's]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html) skilled-labour migration architecture that the route-based and externalization debates have largely overlooked: the exploitation of migrant workers begins not at the border or in the workplace, but at the recruitment stage in the country of origin. Germany's reform of the Skilled Immigration Act (FEG, 2020 and 2023) was designed to attract third-country workers to fill demographic and sectoral shortages. By March 2026, 3.31 million third-country nationals were employed in Germany — surpassing the 2.51 million EU citizens working there. But the very expansion of legal pathways that policymakers celebrate has enlarged the surface area for exploitation, because the recruitment intermediaries who connect workers to employers operate in a near-regulatory vacuum [4].

Private recruitment agencies function as gatekeepers between origin-country labour markets and German employers, particularly small and medium-sized enterprises that find international recruitment too complex to manage independently. These intermediaries charge fees that can reach €15,000 in sectors like meat processing, creating debt bondage before the worker even departs. Misleading information, document confiscation, and increasingly digital fraud via social media and AI-generated content compound the risk. The ILO estimates that abusive recruitment generates $32 billion annually — $27 billion in withheld wages and $5 billion in fees — and the Employer Pays Principle, which holds that workers should not pay for their own recruitment, remains rarely implemented in practice [4].

Specific migration corridors illustrate how regular and irregular pathways intersect. Indian students are lured by private universities and agencies with false promises of skilled employment, only to end up in precarity within the platform economy — delivering for Wolt, UberEats, and Lieferando. Vietnamese migrants arrive via vocational training visas or through multi-stage corridors transiting Poland, finding themselves in nail salons, hospitality, or sex work. The Bangladesh-Libya-Italy corridor demonstrates how ostensibly regular recruitment channels bleed into irregular movement, with the same actors exploiting both [4].

Germany's regulatory framework has striking blind spots. Unlike many origin and destination countries, Germany has no comprehensive licensing or registration system for private recruitment agencies — a business registration has been sufficient since 2002. The only fee cap is €2,000 under Social Code Book III (Sections 296–299), a fraction of what agencies actually charge. Germany has not ratified ILO Convention 181 on private employment agencies, and no specialized supervisory authority exists. The government's 2025 National Action Plan for Business and Human Rights (NAP A/Z) acknowledges recruitment risks, but its measures remain limited and voluntary. Crucially, social partners such as trade unions are not involved in bilateral labour agreements — the Kenya deal being a case in point — so the agreements lack the enforcement infrastructure that workplace-level monitoring would provide [4].

At the European level, the [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) framework is equally thin. The European Labour Authority (ELA) does not formally cover third-country nationals in its mandate, and no EU-wide instrument targets unfair recruitment practices. Cross-border cooperation with origin countries on fair recruitment is at an early stage; GIZ development-cooperation projects (THAMM plus, PAM, MEG) offer promising models but remain underutilized. The planned EU Talent Pool — a platform matching third-country workers with European employers — could become an opportunity for fair recruitment if Germany appoints a national contact point and embeds certification standards [4].

The policy prescription that Knapp and Landmesser advance has three pillars. First, a regulatory framework for private recruitment agencies combining licensing and accreditation with binding obligations, voluntary certification, and meaningful sanctions — a hybrid model that leverages market regulation without abandoning state enforcement. Second, corporate accountability through a tripartite approach engaging trade unions, diaspora organizations, and employer alliances such as the WE-Fair initiative. Third, transnational cooperation: extending ELA's mandate to third-country nationals, operationalizing the EU Talent Pool, and embedding fair-recruitment standards in bilateral agreements with key origin countries — India, Morocco, and Vietnam. The Philippines, with its 60-plus bilateral labour agreements, demonstrates that origin-country leverage can be used to protect workers abroad rather than merely to export them [4].
