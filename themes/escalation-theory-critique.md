# Background

Western strategic studies has built its analytical apparatus on a foundation of formal models imported from mathematical economics: game theory (Schelling, von Neumann), escalation theory (Herman Kahn's escalation ladder), and rational-choice frameworks that assume unitary state actors pursuing calculable utility functions. [Aurelien](/actors/aurelien.md) argues these models fail to explain contemporary conflicts — [Ukraine](/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.md), Israel-Hamas/Hezbollah, the [US-Iran War](/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.md) — because their foundational assumptions do not hold.

# Current Situation

The critique has three dimensions, each illustrated by a current conflict:

1. **Unreconcilable objectives**: In the US-Iran conflict, the US wants to destroy Iran as a functioning state; Israel wants to eliminate the only regional challenger; Iran wants an end to sanctions, regional dominance, and US eviction from the Gulf. These positions have zero overlap — there is nothing to negotiate. Game theory assumes a bargaining range exists; when it does not, the models produce nonsensical prescriptions [1][2].

2. **Non-unitary actors**: The rational-actor model treats states as unitary agents. In practice, the US-Iran war was [Trump](/actors/trump.md)'s personal decision, not a state-level rational calculation — as [O'Brien](/actors/phillips-obrien.md) demonstrates. Iran's decision-making involves the IRGC, the Artesh, the pragmatists, and the Supreme Leader's faction — not a single utility-maximizing agent [1][3].

3. **Asymmetric information is the norm, not the exception**: Game theory models assume information asymmetries that can be resolved through signaling. In practice, the parties to contemporary conflicts do not understand each other's objectives, constraints, or internal dynamics — and signaling is interpreted through ideological filters that systematically distort reception [1].

# Key Dynamics

- **Escalation ladder assumes linear, reversible steps**: Kahn's escalation ladder assumes that conflicts move through discrete, recognizable steps and that de-escalation is the mirror image of escalation. In practice, the US-Iran war involved simultaneous escalation in some dimensions (military strikes) and de-escalation in others (diplomatic channels), with no single "rung" on the ladder [1].
- **Deterrence models assume rationality**: Deterrence theory assumes that adversaries will respond rationally to threats. When the adversary's objective structure makes irrational behavior rational (from their perspective), deterrence fails — not because the threat was insufficient but because the adversary's utility function does not weight costs and benefits as the model assumes [1].
- **Western policy built on these models**: The failure of Western policy in Ukraine, the Middle East, and the Indo-Pacific is partly a failure of the analytical models that generated the policy. The models are not merely incomplete but actively misleading — they generate predictions that are systematically wrong because their assumptions are systematically violated [1].

# Analysis

[Aurelien](/actors/aurelien.md) does not propose an alternative formal model but argues for a historical-analogical approach: understanding conflicts through their specific historical contexts, the particular actors involved, and the irreducible complexity of their objective structures. This approach is less predictive but more accurate — it does not generate the false precision of formal models but it also does not generate their false predictions [1].

The critique aligns with [O'Brien](/actors/phillips-obrien.md)'s leader-centered framework: if wars are choices of individual leaders, not state-level rational calculations, then models that assume state-level rationality will systematically fail to predict when and how wars occur. The critique also aligns with [Tchakarova](/actors/velina-tchakarova.md)'s simultaneity framework: if multiple crises interact in ways that no single model can capture, then models that analyze each crisis in isolation will miss the systemic dynamics that drive the [global system rupture](/themes/global-system-rupture.md) [1][3][4].
