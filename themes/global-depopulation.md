# Background

Global fertility decline is occurring across countries at all income levels, from South Korea's catastrophic low to historically high-fertility societies now approaching replacement. 2023 was the first year global fertility fell below the replacement rate. The drivers are complex and mutually reinforcing: smartphones and social media, declining socialization and coupling, housing costs, educational intensity, modern anxiety, and the dissolution of the social structures that historically supported family formation [1].

The implications are structural. Every major economic and political system — welfare states, pay-as-you-go pensions, economic growth models, military recruitment, geopolitical power projection — assumes a growing or stable population. Depopulation invalidates these assumptions simultaneously, creating a slow-motion crisis that is invisible in daily news cycles but transforms every long-term calculation.

# Current Situation

- Global fertility fell below replacement rate in 2023 [1].
- South Korea's fertility rate is the lowest in the world, well below 1.0 [1].
- The decline is global, not regional: immigration can delay but not reverse the trend, as source countries are also experiencing fertility decline [1].
- AI and deep learning are identified alongside depopulation as the two most important trends shaping the next decades [1].

# Key Dynamics

- **Welfare state unsustainability**: Pay-as-you-go systems assume a growing base of contributors relative to beneficiaries. Depopulation inverts this ratio, creating fiscal stress that compounds over decades [1].
- **Economic growth reversal**: Standard economic models assume labor force growth as an input to GDP growth. A contracting labor force means that maintaining GDP requires extraordinary productivity gains — gains that may not be achievable as the [energy and resource](/themes/energy-and-resources.md) basis of the economy contracts [1].
- **Geopolitical power shift**: Population size has historically correlated with national power. A global population contraction redistributes the demographic foundations of power, with differential timing: some regions (Africa, parts of South Asia) will maintain younger populations longer than East Asia and Europe [1].
- **Housing and urban dynamics**: Population decline interacts with housing markets in complex ways — some areas depopulate while others remain expensive due to agglomeration effects, creating uneven geographic impacts [1].

# Analysis

[Derek Thompson](/actors/derek-thompson.md) frames depopulation as the structural trend that renders conventional policy debates obsolete. The left-right argument over welfare state design, immigration policy, and economic management assumes a population trajectory that no longer exists. The implication is that the political instability and institutional dysfunction visible across Western democracies may be downstream effects of demographic contraction — the systems were designed for a population pyramid that is inverting [1].

The interaction between depopulation and [energy and resources](/themes/energy-and-resources.md) is particularly significant: a contracting population requires less energy and materials in absolute terms, but the economic systems built around growth cannot adjust to contraction without crisis. The "simplification" described by [Nate Hagens](/actors/nate-hagens.md) may be partly demographic — a smaller population is a simpler system, but the transition from a growth-based to a contraction-based economy is itself destabilizing [1][2].

## Africa-Europe Demographic Complementarity

The demographic divergence between Africa and Europe creates a structural basis for labour and education migration. By 2050, Africa is expected to account for approximately 25% of the global working-age population, while the working-age population in many European countries is shrinking. Traditional source countries for migrant workers (Vietnam, India) are themselves ageing, making sub-Saharan Africa an increasingly important future source. However, the potential to attract workers from young African societies to ageing European labour markets has barely been addressed at the policy level. Regular migration can bring positive development impacts through remittances (which significantly exceed official development assistance in low- and middle-income countries), "brain gain" (education incentives), and "brain circulation" (returning migrants applying new skills). The widespread "brain drain" concern does not reflect labour market conditions across much of sub-Saharan Africa, though it applies in specific sectors like healthcare [3].
