# Background

AI has emerged as a central arena of [great power competition](/themes/great-power-competition.md). The [United States](/actors/united-states.md) and [China](/actors/china.md) are leveraging control over different layers of the global technology stack to gain strategic advantage. The US leads in high-end compute (semiconductors, data centers) and frontier models; China excels in AI adaptation, hardware manufacturing, and open-weight AI. Both nations are now actively exporting their technology to secure allies and establish global dependency — a competition that extends beyond the [AI military race](/themes/ai-military-race.md) into the diplomatic and economic domains.

# Current Situation

## US Strategy: Export the Entire Tech Stack

The White House's AI Action Plan (July 2025) has a core objective: "export the entire U.S. tech stack" to secure allies and establish global dependency on US technology ahead of competitors. Executive Order 14320 reinforces this, stating the need to ensure US AI technologies, standards, and governance models are adopted worldwide. The US defines an AI tech stack as encompassing AI-optimized hardware, data pipelines, AI models and systems, security measures, and AI applications. The Commerce Department has been instructed to establish an AI Exports Program.

The strategy is built on the premise that exporting a robust stack would expand the US global AI footprint, guide standards, and lock nations into dependency on US models before China can combat it. The approach was informed by the first Trump administration's experience witnessing Huawei's global telecommunications expansion.

## China's Strategy: AI+ Initiative and Open Source

China's State Council released its own AI action plan in August 2025: the "Artificial Intelligence +" Initiative. It sets specific targets — 70% AI adoption rate by 2027, 90% by 2030 — and targets comprehensive integration across six sectors: science and technology, industry, consumption, livelihoods, governance, and global cooperation. China aims to share AI technology through multilateral institutions (especially the UN), promote open-source AI development, and support Global South countries in enhancing their national AI capabilities. China positions itself as a reliable, inclusive partner — in contrast to the US framing of adversarial competition.

## Open-Source AI as Vector of Influence

Chinese open-weight AI models are now used by as many as 80% of American AI start-ups. Chinese models account for 17.1% of global downloads, ahead of the US at 15.8% — a dramatic shift from two years ago when American models dominated with 60%+ of downloads. But the licensing agreements embedded in these models carry political and legal risks:

- **DeepSeek V3** removes restrictions on AI use for "justice, law enforcement, immigration or asylum processes" — aligning with Chinese authoritarian governance practices.
- **Tencent** has inserted clauses prohibiting use that "violates or disrespects the social ethics and moral standards of other countries or regions" — language matching China's Deep Synthesis Regulation, which requires AI to adhere to "correct political direction, public opinion guidance, and value orientation."
- Disputes would be litigated in Chinese courts under Chinese law, creating exposure to political manipulation.

## Critical Mineral Interdependency

The tech stack competition intersects with [critical minerals](/themes/critical-minerals.md): the US has restricted advanced chip exports to China, while China has introduced a rare-earth licensing regime granting Beijing power to dictate which companies and countries receive resources essential for semiconductors and defense applications. This volatile interdependency risks global technological bifurcation.

# Key Dynamics

- **Dated unipolar assumptions**: The US export strategy relies on the premise that nations will import a full US tech stack, but as countries develop sovereign AI strategies, this assumption is untested. The framework often puts the US at odds with partners' national interests.
- **Sovereign AI as aspiration**: Many nations aim to free themselves from US and China dependence by developing advanced models and uplifting domestic workforces. But US and Chinese companies control the most critical sectors — compute, chips, foundational models — leaving most countries without truly independent options.
- **Middle-power hedging**: The structure has not produced a purely bifurcated competition. Middle powers have carved out significant room to maneuver by strategically balancing relations with both powers while developing sovereign components. Countries with limited AI capacity are becoming geopolitical battlegrounds for technological expansion.
- **Open-source as soft power**: China's open-source approach — offering capable, cheap models with embedded political licensing terms — is a form of technology diplomacy that operates below the threshold of formal export agreements but creates durable dependencies and legal exposures.

# Analysis

### Two Competing Models

The US approach is adversarial and export-competitive: "Counter Chinese Influence in International Governance Bodies." It focuses on exporting US standards, values, and models to limit market access to adversaries. China's approach is inclusive and multilateral: promoting shared governance through the UN and supporting Global South AI development. The framing difference creates a strategic opening for China to gain narrative wins by promoting seemingly more inclusive concepts of shared governance — even as its open-source models embed Chinese legal and political norms.

### Licensing as Regulatory Export

Chinese open-weight AI licenses function as a form of regulatory export. By adopting these models, firms bind themselves to contracts shaped by Chinese regulation — importing Chinese political content into their products. The "social ethics" clauses match Chinese regulations requiring "correct political direction," effectively extending Chinese content moderation standards into the global AI ecosystem. Unlike hardware dependencies (where rip-and-replace is expensive but straightforward), software-layer dependencies create legal entanglements and potential for political manipulation at the margins.

### The AI-Critical Minerals Nexus

The competition over AI technology is inseparable from the competition over [critical minerals](/themes/critical-minerals.md). The US restricts advanced chips; China restricts rare earths. Both are attempts to leverage control over critical inputs to shape the other's AI development trajectory. The result is a volatile interdependency where each side can inflict costs but neither can achieve autonomy — a structural condition that pushes middle powers toward hedging strategies and sovereign AI investments, even when true sovereignty is unattainable.
