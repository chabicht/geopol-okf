# Background

AI has emerged as a central arena of [great power competition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/great-power-competition.html). The [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) and [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) are leveraging control over different layers of the global technology stack to gain strategic advantage. The US leads in high-end compute (semiconductors, data centers) and frontier models; China excels in AI adaptation, hardware manufacturing, and open-weight AI. Both nations are now actively exporting their technology to secure allies and establish global dependency — a competition that extends beyond the [AI military race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html) into the diplomatic and economic domains.

# Current Situation

## US Strategy: Export the Entire Tech Stack

The White House's AI Action Plan (July 2025) has a core objective: "export the entire U.S. tech stack" to secure allies and establish global dependency on US technology ahead of competitors. Executive Order 14320 reinforces this, stating the need to ensure US AI technologies, standards, and governance models are adopted worldwide. The US defines an AI tech stack as encompassing AI-optimized hardware, data pipelines, AI models and systems, security measures, and AI applications. The Commerce Department has been instructed to establish an AI Exports Program.

The strategy is built on the premise that exporting a robust stack would expand the US global AI footprint, guide standards, and lock nations into dependency on US models before China can combat it. The approach was informed by the first Trump administration's experience witnessing Huawei's global telecommunications expansion.

## China's Strategy: AI+ Initiative and Open Source

China's State Council released its own AI action plan in August 2025: the "Artificial Intelligence +" Initiative. It sets specific targets — 70% AI adoption rate by 2027, 90% by 2030 — and targets comprehensive integration across six sectors: science and technology, industry, consumption, livelihoods, governance, and global cooperation. China aims to share AI technology through multilateral institutions (especially the UN), promote open-source AI development, and support Global South countries in enhancing their national AI capabilities. China positions itself as a reliable, inclusive partner — in contrast to the US framing of adversarial competition.

## Open-Source AI as Vector of Influence

Chinese open-weight AI models are now used by as many as 80% of American AI start-ups. Chinese models account for 17.1% of global downloads, ahead of the US at 15.8% — a dramatic shift from two years ago when American models dominated with 60%+ of downloads. But the licensing agreements embedded in these models carry political and legal risks:

- **DeepSeek V3** removes restrictions on AI use for "justice, law enforcement, immigration or asylum processes" — aligning with Chinese authoritarian governance practices.
- **Tencent** has inserted clauses prohibiting use that "violates or disrespects the social ethics and moral standards of other countries or regions" — language matching China's Deep Synthesis Regulation, which requires AI to adhere to "correct political direction, public opinion guidance, and value orientation."
- Disputes would be litigated in Chinese courts under Chinese law, creating exposure to political manipulation.

## Critical Mineral Interdependency

The tech stack competition intersects with [critical minerals]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/critical-minerals.html): the US has restricted advanced chip exports to China, while China has introduced a rare-earth licensing regime granting Beijing power to dictate which companies and countries receive resources essential for semiconductors and defense applications. This volatile interdependency risks global technological bifurcation.

# Key Dynamics

- **Dated unipolar assumptions**: The US export strategy relies on the premise that nations will import a full US tech stack, but as countries develop sovereign AI strategies, this assumption is untested. The framework often puts the US at odds with partners' national interests.
- **Sovereign AI as aspiration**: Many nations aim to free themselves from US and China dependence by developing advanced models and uplifting domestic workforces. But US and Chinese companies control the most critical sectors — compute, chips, foundational models — leaving most countries without truly independent options.
- **Middle-power hedging**: The structure has not produced a purely bifurcated competition. Middle powers have carved out significant room to maneuver by strategically balancing relations with both powers while developing sovereign components. Countries with limited AI capacity are becoming geopolitical battlegrounds for technological expansion.
- **Open-source as soft power**: China's open-source approach — offering capable, cheap models with embedded political licensing terms — is a form of technology diplomacy that operates below the threshold of formal export agreements but creates durable dependencies and legal exposures.

# Analysis

### Two Competing Models

The US approach is adversarial and export-competitive: "Counter Chinese Influence in International Governance Bodies." It focuses on exporting US standards, values, and models to limit market access to adversaries. China's approach is inclusive and multilateral: promoting shared governance through the UN and supporting Global South AI development. The framing difference creates a strategic opening for China to gain narrative wins by promoting seemingly more inclusive concepts of shared governance — even as its open-source models embed Chinese legal and political norms.

### Licensing as Regulatory Export

Chinese open-weight AI licenses function as a form of regulatory export. By adopting these models, firms bind themselves to contracts shaped by Chinese regulation — importing Chinese political content into their products. The "social ethics" clauses match Chinese regulations requiring "correct political direction," effectively extending Chinese content moderation standards into the global AI ecosystem. Unlike hardware dependencies (where rip-and-replace is expensive but straightforward), software-layer dependencies create legal entanglements and potential for political manipulation at the margins.

### The AI-Critical Minerals Nexus

The competition over AI technology is inseparable from the competition over [critical minerals]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/critical-minerals.html). The US restricts advanced chips; China restricts rare earths. Both are attempts to leverage control over critical inputs to shape the other's AI development trajectory. The result is a volatile interdependency where each side can inflict costs but neither can achieve autonomy — a structural condition that pushes middle powers toward hedging strategies and sovereign AI investments, even when true sovereignty is unattainable.

### Huawei in NATO Networks: The Turkey Case

[The Cipher Brief]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-cipher-brief.html) identifies [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) as the sharpest illustration of how Chinese telecom embedded in a NATO ally's critical infrastructure creates a strategic dependency that cuts against alliance interoperability — and now against the F-35 program. Turkey's leading systems integrator, Netaş, is roughly 48 percent owned by ZTE, and Huawei is deeply embedded in the networks operated by Turkcell, Türk Telekom, and Vodafone Turkey. Under [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html)'s 2017 National Intelligence Law, that ownership is not a passive investment — Beijing can compel any Chinese firm, anywhere it operates, to hand over data on demand, and corporate assurances of independence carry no legal weight against that obligation [4].

Washington has already treated this exact problem as disqualifying. In 2021, a $23 billion F-35 and drone package for the [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html) collapsed, in part because Huawei was building Abu Dhabi's 5G network and American intelligence had identified a suspected Chinese military-linked facility at Khalifa Port. In May 2026, the Trump administration urged NATO members to spend a portion of the 1.5% of GDP allocated to defense-related spending on removing Huawei components from their domestic networks, highlighting the vulnerabilities posed by Chinese equipment and hacking campaigns such as Salt Typhoon. Several NATO allies — Sweden and the [United Kingdom]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-kingdom.html) in particular — have been proactive in securing their systems; Ankara remains a laggard. The risk is not simply to the F-35 itself but to its entire operating environment: a fighter operating within an ecosystem saturated by Chinese-produced telecom equipment, reliant on deployment infrastructure whose roots tie back to Beijing, for decades to come [4]. See [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) for the full F-35 reinstatement analysis and [NATO Ankara Summit 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html) for the summit context.

### The Digital Protectorate

[RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) (Francisco Carneiro Júnior) defines the stakes of tech stack dependency in sovereignty terms: a state that outsources its AI capability to foreign companies with organic ties to a foreign government is not sovereign — it is a "digital protectorate." 21st-century sovereignty is measured not only in territory, foreign-exchange reserves, or multilateral seats, but in the capacity to process strategic information autonomously and operate critical security systems without dependence on suppliers answerable to another government [3].

Palantir Technologies illustrates the pattern. Founded in 2003 by Peter Thiel and Alex Karp with CIA seed funding via In-Q-Tel, its Gotham platform fuses satellite imagery, drone footage, financial records, and telecommunications metadata into a single operational picture. By 2026 the company was embedded in approximately a dozen countries, supplied the Maven Smart System to [NATO]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/nato.html) under a no-bid contract (2025), processed data for 90 million UN World Food Programme beneficiaries since 2017, and ran the IAEA's safeguards-monitoring platform MOSAIC since 2015. Each customer must now assess what it means to depend operationally on a company that has publicly defined its allegiance as an instrument of American national defence [3].

**Switzerland** supplies the one documented case of a government formally assessing Palantir against a sovereignty standard and rejecting it. An internal Swiss Army study concluded Palantir's systems could be structurally incompatible with Swiss data sovereignty — even with servers on Swiss soil, the company remains subject to US law that can compel American government access to data processed anywhere. Palantir's response was instructive: rather than contesting the reporting, it invoked a Swiss judicial "right of reply" mechanism against the independent magazine *Republik*, a manoeuvre the European Federation of Journalists classified as a potential Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation. A $300 billion company used a defamation-adjacent legal mechanism against a small outlet for accurately reporting that the company's own host government had assessed it as a sovereignty risk [3].

**[United Kingdom]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-kingdom.html)** supplies the opposite case: exposure without reckoning. The NHS Federated Data Platform — the central system through which English NHS trusts manage patient data — operates under a £330 million Palantir contract. Digital-rights organisations mounted a legal challenge on inadequate consultation and data-sharing grounds; the platform went live regardless. The manifesto's publication prompted no formal reopening of debate, even as healthcare data for tens of millions of citizens continued flowing through infrastructure operated by a company that had just described itself as an instrument of American national defence [3].

Political scientist Don Moynihan identifies the pattern at the level of theory: a fusion of state power and Silicon Valley technology pursued without commensurate accountability, in which dominant infrastructure providers shape defence and digital-sovereignty policy through contracts that by design resist legislative and judicial scrutiny. Almost no government that signed such a contract conducted the kind of structural risk assessment Switzerland's army did before the manifesto made the allegiance explicit. Most will now have to decide whether to conduct one after the fact — a strictly harder position from which to negotiate [3].

## The AI Stack, Vertical Integration, and the National Champions Trap

[Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html)'s interview with Lina Khan reframes tech-stack diplomacy from a competition-between-states question into a competition-within-the-stack question. Khan frames the AI supply chain as a stack in which each layer — chips, hyperscalers, cloud infrastructure, models, apps and services — has economic properties that can easily tip toward monopoly through network effects, as happened in Web 2.0 and digital markets. Allowing monopolization at one layer without rules such as common carriage or equal access on equal terms can result in other layers becoming monopolized or distorted, depriving the public of the full promise and innovation of the technologies. The FTC had been investigating bottlenecks at the chip layer (Nvidia as a core gatekeeper) and the cloud layer (hyperscalers getting privileged access across the AI supply chain) [5].

The conflict of interest is structural and has direct implications for the US-China tech-stack competition. Microsoft, Amazon, and Google — the firms the US export strategy relies on — have their own cloud computing businesses, and insofar as AI models heavily rely on cloud computing, these firms have incentives to favor models that are inefficient (consuming more compute) rather than efficient. The DeepSeek revelation — a Chinese model rivalling American sophistication with much greater efficiency — brought that conflict to bear. Allowing the American giants to control these markets risks innovating on trajectories the incumbents determine rather than letting competition drive efficiency. The "interlocking directorates" pattern — Microsoft and OpenAI, Google and Amazon — raises questions about whether existing monopolists are gaining outsized control over competitively strategic decisions through opaque financial relationships [5].

Khan's cautionary tale is directly relevant to the export-the-stack strategy: Boeing's late-1990s merger approval allowing it to buy up its last US rival, McDonnell Douglas, led down a path where Boeing's planes started falling apart and people died. A lack of competition probably played a role, compounded by a culture shift from engineering to McKinsey-type consulting. The US national-champions approach to AI — protecting incumbents who curry favor with the White House — risks the same trajectory. The fundamental AI breakthrough came from Google, which did nothing with it until people left and brought it to other places; the empirical record shows that breakthrough, paradigm-shifting innovations have come from outsiders and competitive markets, while monopolies are good only at incremental improvements on existing technologies. The Trump administration's risk of regulatory capture — rules favoring the biggest, most powerful incumbents at the expense of startups and newer firms that may have better ideas — directly undercuts the innovation advantage the US export strategy depends on [5]. ## Hyperscaler Psy-Ops and Circular AI Compute Demand

Ed Zitron (*Where's Your Ed At*) exposes a demand-creation dynamic that the tech-stack diplomacy framework must account for: hyperscalers are funding the appearance of AI demand through circular investments in their own customers. Microsoft, Nvidia, and SoftBank have all invested in OpenAI; Oracle, Nvidia, and SoftBank have all invested in xAI; Nvidia invested in xAI, OpenAI, Anthropic, Mistral, Cohere, Perplexity, and Databricks — and all of those companies buy Nvidia GPUs. The result is "a giant, unreliable financial ring-around-the-rosie" in which Nvidia's revenue growth is sustained by money it invested in its own customers, giving them capital to buy Nvidia GPUs. Microsoft has amended its contract with OpenAI multiple times to reduce the percentage of revenue it takes from OpenAI's API and ChatGPT subscriptions, effectively subsidizing OpenAI's revenue numbers. This is not a market signal — it is a manufactured signal [6].

The diplomatic consequence is that the "AI demand" used to justify the export-the-stack strategy, the hundreds of billions of hyperscaler capex, and the emergency permits for data center power generation may be substantially fictional. Zitron documents that hyperscalers have begun cutting data center projects and refusing to sign new leases, citing "multiple sources that say that OpenAI has been unable to pay" — OpenAI attempted to pay its 2025 compute bills with equity rather than cash, and Microsoft confirmed that OpenAI will not pay cash for the next five years. If the demand underpinning US AI diplomacy is circular and unsustainable, then the tech-stack strategy rests on a fiscal foundation that can collapse independent of any Chinese competitive action. The "Lehman Brothers of the AI bubble" scenario — OpenAI's collapse seizing up AI data center debt and pulling back compute demand — would invalidate the assumptions under which allied governments signed hyperscaler contracts [6].
