# Background

Endless Warfare is a framework for understanding how U.S. adversaries — [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), and [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) — pursue an enduring approach to erode and supplant U.S. power, influence, and global leadership over time. It is not simply another label for the [gray zone]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html) or [cognitive warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/cognitive-warfare.html). Both are important elements, but Endless Warfare describes the adversary's persistent, long-term strategy, not just the elements that enable it [1].

The United States is not at peace. It is already in a continuous, long-term conflict with determined adversaries who are waging warfare without crossing the threshold into open conflict. Endless Warfare incorporates gray zone activity, cognitive warfare, weaponized negotiations, proxies and surrogates, and other subversive networks.

# Key Dynamics

## A Different Strategic Mindset

Endless Warfare is conceptually different from conventional war — particularly given the ambiguous nature of gray-zone activity and cognitive warfare — and therefore demands a different approach. Yet because it is a continuous state of conflict, it requires the same level of national leadership and vision, organization, critical thinking, sense of urgency, and collaboration with allies that conventional conflict demands. The framework may require a senior White House official, such as a Deputy National Security Advisor for Strategic Competition, to integrate interagency action and coordinate a sustained national response [1].

## Strategic Deterrence in the Gray Zone

Adversaries have calculated that there are more gains than risks in the gray zone. Actions that seek to counter or defend against gray zone activity but that do not impose meaningful costs or create credible deterrence may simply reward gray zone activity. An effective strategy rests on deliberate preparation, clear communication, a national approach, and the resolve to change the risk calculus of adversaries. The message: "We will see what you're doing, we will publicly attribute it to you, and we will impose costs that exceed your gains" [1].

## Cognitive Advantage

Countering the impacts of cognitive warfare is essential to countering Endless Warfare. Cognitive Security — the protection of human cognition and decision autonomy — is also about offense: outthinking, outpacing, and outmaneuvering adversaries in the cognitive domain. America's national narrative is both sword and shield, projecting power and countering adversary narratives. In contrast to America's fragmented approach, China's United Front Work Department, Russia's Active Measures networks, and Iran's diverse network of surrogates reflect centralized national direction and a long-term horizon [1].

## Weaponized Negotiations

In the era of Endless Warfare, adversaries often use negotiations as a continuation of conflict by other means — not as instruments of resolution. The central challenge is distinguishing when talks aim to legitimately resolve a conflict versus when they are designed to shape its next phase. Key principles for countering weaponized negotiations [1]:

- No upfront concessions to get an adversary to the table
- Establish clear overarching objectives and non-negotiable redlines early
- Proactively counter narratives during negotiations
- Concessions must be conditions-based with automatic snap-back mechanisms
- Be willing to walk away

## Countering Endless Warfare Networks

The ultimate goal is systematically confronting the adversary networks that pose an enduring threat. These networks fall into two broad categories [1]:

**Institutional networks**: military organizations, intelligence services, party organizations, proxy structures, and state-directed organizations such as IRGC-QF, MSS, GRU, and FSB, as well as specialized units responsible for assassinations, sabotage, influence operations, and cyber-attacks.

**Ambiguous networks**: ghost fleets, shell and front companies, criminal organizations, illicit financial systems, logistics systems, influence networks, cyber hacktivists, proxy militias, smuggling organizations, and similar entities.

A sustained, network-centric strategy employs three mutually reinforcing lines of effort [1]:

1. **Identify and prioritize** the networks that pose the greatest long-term threat.
2. **Degrade and impose costs** on institutional networks — they are unlikely to be dismantled but can be constrained and made costly.
3. **Dismantle and prevent regeneration** of ambiguous networks by severing the financial, logistical, technological, and organizational systems that support them.

# Analysis

## Adversary Network Profiles

Each adversary illustrates different approaches to Endless Warfare through both institutional and ambiguous networks [1]:

- **Iran**'s network of proxies and surrogates, built over decades, allows Tehran to project power, coerce and intimidate its neighbors, and get inside the decision space of its adversaries at relatively low cost. The IRGC is an enabler of Iran's distributed networks with global reach.
- **Russia**'s networks conduct cognitive warfare against the U.S. and the West and conduct sabotage, assassinations, and political coercion across [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html). Russia's "ghost fleets" have allowed it to generate billions in revenue despite Western sanctions.
- **China**'s cyber networks probe and penetrate U.S. critical infrastructure to sustain access, collect information, and provide a disruption capability during a crisis. Its networks enable sophisticated influence operations inside the U.S. and the transfer of critical technologies that directly enhance its military capabilities.

Shared and overlapping networks in gray and ambiguous spaces — ghost fleets, front companies, terror networks, illicit financial organizations, and criminal enterprises — allow adversaries to bypass sanctions, sustain operations, and reduce international pressure. These networks are resilient, adaptive, and self-recovering, having been carefully developed over decades [1].

## Endless Warfare Will End When It Becomes Ineffective

Endless Warfare will not end because adversaries choose peace. It will end when it is no longer effective. Four takeaways frame the counterstrategy [1]:

1. Endless Warfare is a distinct adversary strategy already underway by China, Russia, and Iran and should be treated as a current strategic threat — not just a future one.
2. Endless Warfare is sustained by sophisticated and interconnected networks that must become a primary target for U.S. and allied efforts.
3. Countering Endless Warfare requires a proactive, persistent, and network-centric approach backed by national-level leadership and coordinated interagency action.
4. Strategic deterrence in the gray zone, cognitive advantage, disciplined negotiations, and network-focused disruption are core tools of that sustained national response.
