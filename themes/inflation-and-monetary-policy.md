# Background

Inflation is growing throughout the world, from Asia to the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html). The IMF forecasts global inflation will increase to 4.4% by end-2026 — nearly one percentage point higher than originally predicted. The [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) and the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz predictably shocked the energy market, resulting in higher prices for resources and transportation due to increased freight costs, insurance premiums, and extended delivery times. The [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html)'s exit from OPEC and strikes on production and infrastructure facilities compounded the disruption [1].

The timing is especially bad. In 2022-2023, before the war in Iran, inflation was already a problem — triggered by COVID-19 supply chain disruptions, demand shifts, and large-scale economic support measures. Then came the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) and associated sanctions on [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), triggering another rise in energy prices. Things improved in 2024-2025 with reduced inflationary pressures and monetary easing, but inflation remained higher than expected, even in the US, as did interest rates [1].

The through line is geopolitical risk, not market forces. The sharp rise in energy prices is driven more by trade policy uncertainty, the complexity and cost of bypass shipping routes, and the unpredictability of the Iran war. The IEA reported that oil and fuel shipments through the Strait of Hormuz fell by nearly 1.8 million barrels per day in Q1 2026, warning that the global oil market will likely remain significantly undersupplied until October even if the conflict ends soon [1].

# Current Situation

## Regional Breakdown

### The United States

The US entered the shock with April CPI at 3.8% year on year, with energy accounting for more than 40% of the monthly rise. The energy index was up almost 18% over 12 months. The Fed held the rate at 3.5-3.75%, explicitly citing higher global energy prices and Middle East uncertainty. The US is not as dependent on Middle East energy as other countries (~10% of imported oil), but it imports fertilizers from the region — disruptions caused nitrogen fertilizer prices (urea, ammonium nitrate) to rise sharply. The bigger concern is expansion from volatile goods to core indicators: annualized core CPI rose 2.8% year on year, above the Fed's 2% target [1].

### Europe

Eurozone consumer prices increased 3% year on year in April 2026, with energy prices accelerating 10.9%. The ECB's March projections built in a war-related energy shock with 2026 baseline inflation at 2.6% and growth at only 0.9%. Germany inflation accelerated to 2.9%; [France]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/france.html) to 2.5%; Italy to 2.8%; Poland to 3.4%. The [United Kingdom]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-kingdom.html)'s CPI was 3.3%, with the Bank of England holding at 3.75% and warning of further rises. The UK is especially sensitive because fuel and food feed quickly into household inflation psychology. The eurozone is the most exposed advanced economy bloc — the immediate shock arrived via imported energy, the medium-term growth shock via industry, freight, and household real incomes, creating the clearest stagflation policy trade-off. After eight consecutive rate cuts, the ECB is keeping the deposit rate at 2%, but expansion of core inflation may force a return to tighter policy [1].

### Asia

China, [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html), and [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) import 60-70% of their oil from the Middle East. India is the most vulnerable — importing nearly 85% of its fuel, the vast majority through Hormuz. April inflation rose to 3.48%, a 13-month high; wholesale inflation at a 3.5-year high. Food inflation reached 4.2%. Sustained high oil prices worsen the current account deficit, increase dollar demand, depress the rupee, and raise imported inflation further. China showed relative resilience — April inflation at 1% month on month and 1.2% year on year, transitioning from deflation to inflation. Producer prices rose 1.7% month on month. China's "appropriately accommodative" policy and $3.34 trillion in forex reserves give it more room to cushion the shock. Japan imports over 90% of its oil from the Middle East, with 80% through Hormuz. Core inflation accelerated to 1.8%; the BoJ kept its rate at 0.75% but is under pressure to raise rates, risking slower growth. Japan is more likely to experience stagflation than China [1].

### Central Asia

Central Asia is not directly affected by Middle East energy disruption. [Kazakhstan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/kazakhstan.html) inflation fell to 10.6% year on year; [Uzbekistan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uzbekistan.html) to a historic low of 7%. Russia continues as a key energy supplier. However, the region is characterized by elevated inflation and extremely tight monetary conditions [1].

### Russia

High oil prices bring revenue, but inflation and sanctions offset gains. Rosstat estimated annual inflation at 5.58% in April; the Bank of Russia lowered the key rate from 15% to 14.5%. Persistently high inflation and interest rates constrain growth. Weekly international reserves reached $771 billion in May — strong external liquidity, but domestic inflation risk persists if sanctions tighten. Russia has a war economy where the state is the dominant economic agent. Higher oil prices can improve near-term fiscal arithmetic even as they worsen business conditions for internationally exposed firms [1].

### Turkey

[Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) raised its year-end inflation target from 16% to 24% and suspended its forecast range. CPI rose 4.18% month on month in April, taking annual inflation to 32.37%. Food inflation is expected to reach 26.3% by end-2026. The central bank maintains one of the tightest monetary policies in the world — funding banks at a 40% overnight rate versus the 37% base rate. The challenge is the interaction of imported energy, high inflation, exchange-rate sensitivity, and the need to preserve reserves and disinflation credibility simultaneously [1].

### Latin America

[Mexico]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/latin-america.html)'s April inflation was 4.45%; Banxico cut to 6.5% but cited the Iran war as a reason for inflation's duration. Brazil's inflation accelerated to 4.39%, with food and beverage prices rising 1.34% monthly. Brazil maintains a 14.5% interest rate. Brazil is more attractive than Turkey and Mexico in a commodity-led oil shock because its external cushion is better and inflation credibility stronger [1].

## Three Scenarios

GPF identifies three scenarios, all anchored to the fact that the baseline entering mid-May 2026 already embeds an oil shock relative to prewar assumptions [1]:

**Scenario 1 (Mild)**: De-escalation within 2-3 months, no sustained Hormuz closure, Brent $80-$95 over six months. Governments slow rate cuts briefly. Business sentiment revives cautiously.

**Scenario 2 (Baseline — most likely)**: Periodic attacks, shipping friction, higher insurance costs, Brent $95-$110 over six months. Governments slow rate cuts and wait. Rising costs and declining purchasing activity. Investment and business activity slow, reflected in lower economic growth. This is the most likely result.

**Scenario 3 (Stagflation)**: Intermittent effective Hormuz closure for up to three months, broader sanctions, Brent $115-$140. Central banks tighten monetary policy. Currency depreciation to 2022 levels and beyond. Economic recession — the least desirable outcome for governments and businesses alike.

## Impact on Business

The shock transmission is gradual: fastest in retail fuel and traded goods, slower in utilities (3-6 months), food and wages (6-12 months), supply chain effects (3-24 months), and second-round effects (1-3 years). Inflation puts pressure on supply chain management — higher costs lead to production delays and material shortages. Tight monetary policy increases borrowing costs, limiting investment in capital projects, expansion, and innovation. Small and medium-sized enterprises are particularly vulnerable [1].

Supply chain strategy is shifting from "friend-shoring" to node-by-node vulnerability mapping. The highest-risk nodes are Gulf shipping lanes, petrochemical intermediates, fertilizers, energy-intensive components, and routes where insurance and war-risk premiums can spike faster than physical freight rates. Alternate suppliers are prequalified, supplier finance contingency lines are built, and minimum inventory buffers are considered only where substitution times are long. Contract architecture matters: fuel surcharge clauses, forex indexation bands, reopener clauses at defined Brent thresholds, sanctions compliance escape clauses, and rerouting/insurance pass-through language for maritime trade [1].

## Investor Risk

The effects of inflation are felt asymmetrically. The US remains the best large market for relative resilience. Brazil is more attractive than Turkey and Mexico in a commodity-led oil shock. Japan is the most exposed developed equity market if the oil shock coincides with a weakened yen. In Europe, exporters with pricing power and limited energy intensity can outperform, but broad industrial beta is exposed. India is structurally attractive long-term but among the most exposed major economies to a prolonged Gulf energy shock in the near to medium term. If inflation persists, markets will prioritize resilience, pricing power, and geopolitical insulation over rapid growth — accelerating capital rotation toward commodities, defense, energy, and inflation-resistant sectors [1].

# Key Dynamics

- **Geopolitical risk, not market forces, is the primary driver**: The inflation surge is driven by trade policy uncertainty, bypass shipping costs, and Iran war unpredictability — not cyclical economic dynamics.
- **Asymmetric impact**: Countries heavily dependent on imports with weak currencies, large fiscal deficits, and limited monetary credibility are disproportionately affected. Strong reserve currencies, diversified economies, and credible institutions absorb shocks better.
- **Core inflation transmission**: Sustained energy price pressure gradually contributes to core inflation through food, transportation, agriculture, and industry — driving up the overall price of less sensitive goods.
- **Central bank caution**: The Fed, ECB, and BoE have all signaled discomfort with the inflation side of the shock even while acknowledging weaker growth. The IMF has advised central banks to prepare to raise rates if the ceasefire breaks down.
- **Resilience over efficiency**: Prolonged inflation and geopolitical instability accelerate capital rotation toward commodities, defense, energy, and inflation-resistant sectors, fundamentally reshaping investment priorities.

# Analysis

## Inflation as a Cascade Vector

The inflationary shock is a secondary cascade of the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html), operating on its own timeline even as the primary energy shock is reversed. The [Tindale-Tchakarova Cascade]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) model — with 6-12 month lags for food and wage effects, and 1-3 year horizons for second-round effects — explains why inflation will persist well after the trigger event is addressed. The World Bank's projection of fertilizer prices up 31% and urea up 60% in 2026 confirms that the agricultural dimension is intensifying even as the energy dimension recedes [1].

## Connection to Global System Rupture

The inflationary spiral is a primary mechanism through which the [Global System Rupture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) propagates. Geopolitical risk drives inflation, which forces central banks to tighten or pause easing, which slows growth, which increases political risk — a vicious cycle. The asymmetry of impact across regions means that the rupture's intensity varies by theater even as its direction is consistent: persistent cost inflation and volatility everywhere, but stagflation risk concentrated in the most exposed economies [1].

## Hormuz Blockade: Fertilizer-to-Food Price Cascade

Colibasanu traces the inflationary transmission from the Hormuz blockade through fertilizer to food prices. Approximately one-third of globally traded urea and nearly half of seaborne sulfur transit the strait, with Middle East exports representing around 40% of global urea supply (22 million metric tons annually). At the New Orleans hub, fertilizer prices rose from $516 to $683 per metric ton in the conflict's first week. The cascade operates through reduced fertilizer application rates — farmers facing doubled input costs apply less — leading to lower agricultural output, which feeds into food inflation on the 6-12 month lag documented in the cascade model above [2].

The fiscal dimension compounds the inflationary mechanism. Governments in import-dependent economies face a choice between absorbing higher fertilizer subsidy costs to protect farmers' margins or allowing the cost to pass through to food prices. Colibasanu emphasizes that the effects are most severe in economies with limited fiscal capacity, where the government cannot subsidize the gap and farmers cannot absorb it — meaning the fertilizer-to-food cascade hits hardest where resilience is already weakest. This reinforces the asymmetric impact pattern identified in the regional breakdown: countries with weak currencies, large fiscal deficits, and limited monetary credibility bear disproportionate costs [2].

## S6 Domain: ECB/Fed Repricing Amid Oil Re-Spike

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html)'s Day 144 diagnostic places the Financial System Stress domain (S6) at CRITICAL, with central banks facing a compound repricing challenge. The ECB's interest rate decisions must be made into an oil re-spike and a storage refill shortfall simultaneously — the energy price surge pushes inflation upward while the growth shock from energy costs and corridor closures pushes toward easing, creating a classic stagflation policy trap. The Fed's hiking repricing holds, meaning markets are pricing in a higher-for-longer trajectory despite weakening growth. Critically, there is no safe-haven Treasury bid — the absence of the traditional flight-to-quality dynamic during a systemic crisis suggests that the US fiscal position itself is being questioned by markets, removing the last monetary buffer from the global financial system [3].

The absence of a safe-haven Treasury bid is structurally significant for the inflation framework: it means that the Federal Reserve cannot rely on automatic market stabilization through Treasury demand during the oil re-spike, and must instead choose between allowing inflation to run hot or tightening into a growth shock — the same dilemma the ECB faces, but without the institutional cushion that a functioning safe-haven dynamic would provide. The combination of ECB policy collision and Fed repricing without a Treasury bid creates a synchronized central-bank stress pattern that amplifies, rather than absorbs, the energy-driven inflationary shock [3].
