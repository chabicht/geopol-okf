# Background

Modern defense capability is increasingly shaped by the resilience of energy flows and digital systems rather than purely kinetic strength. The [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) exposed how quickly energy supply chains can be disrupted — the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, mining, and attacks on nearby vessels demonstrated that the fuel logistics underpinning military operations are a core vulnerability. Meanwhile, NATO's Locked Shields 2026 exercise simulated large-scale attacks on power grids, telecom networks, and government systems, revealing the increased vulnerability of digital and critical infrastructure to cyber disruption [1].

The defense industry is undergoing structural realignment: from platform-centric capability to resilience-centric capability. Future battlespaces will be defined not by platforms alone but by the integration of energy, cyberspace, and infrastructure into systems capable of operating under sustained disruption.

# Current Situation

## Energy and Cyber Resilience

The binding constraint on modern military operations is no longer kinetic reach but the energy and digital substrate that sustains it. In US Army deployments, more than 70 percent of logistics tonnage is fuel; some 55 percent is consumed by the logistics systems themselves. Communications, intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance, air defense, and data processing all rely on uninterrupted electricity, typically generated on-site through fuel-based systems. Fuel must be delivered through contested logistics routes, while digital systems require constant, reliable power. This dual dependency creates a structural vulnerability that adversaries can exploit by targeting supply lines rather than front-line forces [1].

Militaries are responding with transportable microreactors, microgrids, and storage systems to reduce reliance on fuel convoys, alongside autonomous resupply systems that lower exposure by adopting distributed logistics models. The shift represents a fundamental doctrinal change: from concentrated, fuel-dependent logistics chains to distributed, self-sustaining energy systems that can operate in contested environments without continuous resupply [1]. The doctrinal shift mirrors the broader [drone warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) transition from expensive centralized platforms to cheap distributed systems.

The cyber domain is no longer a support function but a primary battlespace. NATO's Locked Shields 2026 exercise convinced defense officials that commercial cybersecurity capabilities must be integrated into military operations, blurring the line between defense and private sector infrastructure. NATO and EU plans highlighted critical digital gaps, as well as [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html)'s untenable reliance on US cloud infrastructure. The exercise revealed that private sector capabilities often exceed those of military organizations [1].

These systems share a specific constraint: power. Hyperscale data centers, secure cloud infrastructure, and AI-driven defense systems all depend on stable and scalable energy. Yet global investment remains imbalanced; far more capital is directed toward energy generation than storage and distribution. This creates a bottleneck, particularly in contested environments where energy cannot be reliably transmitted. The defense industry's ability to field AI-enabled systems is constrained not by computing power but by the energy infrastructure required to run them [1]. The [AI military race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html) is therefore not only about models and deployment speed but about the energy infrastructure that powers AI systems — the energy-cyber bottleneck is the physical substrate of the AI competition, and it is currently underinvested relative to the attention devoted to model development and chip fabrication [1].

# Key Dynamics

- **Energy resilience as warfighting capability**: The ability to sustain operations without continuous fuel resupply is becoming a primary measure of military effectiveness. Militaries that cannot operate in energy-contested environments face a structural disadvantage regardless of their kinetic capabilities [1].
- **Cyber-physical convergence**: The boundary between military cyber operations and commercial cybersecurity is dissolving. Defense doctrines now require integration of commercial capabilities into military operations — a shift that blurs public-private boundaries and creates new dependency relationships [1].
- **Distributed logistics**: Autonomous resupply and distributed energy systems are replacing concentrated logistics hubs, reducing the vulnerability of supply chains to targeted attacks [1].
- **Investment imbalance**: Capital flows disproportionately toward energy generation rather than storage and distribution, creating a bottleneck in contested environments where transmission is the binding constraint [1].

# Analysis

Davidson argues that the defense industry can expect structural realignments driven by the convergence of energy vulnerability and cyber-physical integration. International businesses and investors can anticipate more risk at choke points and vulnerable infrastructure. Key opportunities are emerging in energy resilience (microgrids and storage), cybersecurity, and secure infrastructure. Capital will likely flow to firms that enable operational continuity and integrated energy-cyber solutions aligned with defense priorities [1].

## The Coming Defense Boom

Davidson and Colibasanu (GPF) argue that the global defense sector is entering a structural growth cycle rather than a temporary wartime surge. Countries are no longer only importing defense systems but increasingly seeking to become exporters or co-producers of specialized technologies. The wars in [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) and the [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) have exposed the weakness of lean inventories, slow production lines, and defense industrial systems designed for peacetime rather than sustained conflict [3].

### Defense Industrial Mobilization

In 2021, the last full year before Russia invaded Ukraine, world military expenditures stood at roughly $2.1 trillion. By 2025, it had reached nearly $2.9 trillion — too large an increase for temporary wartime procurement. The wars in Ukraine and the Middle East exposed how quickly allied stockpiles can be exhausted, especially as the US and [NATO]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/nato.html) transferred munitions and equipment to Kyiv. Even short conflicts — Nagorno-Karabakh, India-Pakistan tensions — force states to reassess military readiness and procurement timelines. Interceptors, ammunition, and precision munitions can be consumed faster than peacetime industry can replace them [3].

### NATO 5% Spending Target

NATO's move from a 2 percent spending benchmark toward 5 percent gives industry a longer demand signal than annual procurement alone. The commitment includes 3.5% of GDP for core defense spending and another 1.5% for broader defense- and security-related investment by 2035. SIPRI estimates that if all NATO allies met the full 5 percent target in 2035, annual alliance spending would reach roughly $4.2 trillion [3].

In Asia and Oceania, SIPRI estimates military spending rose 8.1% in 2025 to $681 billion — the region's fastest annual increase since 2009. [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html), and [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html) all increased spending, while the Philippines and South Korea raised their budgets to modernize their forces [3].

### Opportunity Matrix

The defense boom will not benefit all firms equally. Value moves toward firms that control scarce upstream inputs: semiconductors, critical minerals, energy systems, secure communications, specialty metals, nuclear-grade components, and certified production capacity. The most strategically valuable firms may not be those producing final weapons platforms but those controlling the technologies, materials, energy systems, and manufacturing capacity that enable military production. The strongest position belongs to firms that control capacity governments cannot quickly recreate [3].

Key opportunity areas include: prime defense contractors, munitions and missile inputs, air defense and counter-drone systems, defense electronics and semiconductors, cyber/AI/battlefield software, drones and autonomous systems, shipbuilding and naval sustainment, critical minerals and rare earths, specialty metals and advanced materials, energy and grid infrastructure, nuclear and SMR supply chains, logistics and sustainment networks, allied co-production partners, and defense-adjacent industrial suppliers [3].

### Three Scenarios

1. **Steady defense expansion** — Geopolitical pressure sustains demand; winners are established defense contractors and suppliers controlling critical inputs.
2. **Accelerated rearmament** — Major conflict or alliance shift triggers surge; severe shortages in labor, machine tools, energetics, critical minerals, semiconductors, and advanced electronics.
3. **Fragmented defense industrial blocs** — Geopolitical fragmentation outpaces spending growth; defense market becomes competing regional ecosystems with increased costs and reduced economies of scale.

Across all scenarios, the common theme is that industrial capacity is a strategic asset. The ability to produce, sustain, and deliver defense capabilities defines national power and competitive advantage [3].

## AI and Cybersecurity Convergence

Colibasanu (GPF) identifies a structural transformation at the intersection of AI and cybersecurity that directly affects defense industrial resilience: cyberspace is evolving from a human-versus-human into a machine-versus-machine environment, with AI enabling automated reconnaissance, vulnerability identification, and attack execution at unprecedented speed, and with the weakest link shifting from humans to AI agents themselves [2]. The full cyber-threat landscape analysis — AI-generated phishing, "living code" runtime enforcement, AI supply-chain reviews, and the three-category threat taxonomy — is tracked in [Cognitive Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/cognitive-warfare.html), which owns the cognitive/cyber dimension. Here the defense-industrial dimension: the next phase of the AI race may depend on access to the most advanced AI capabilities for cybersecurity, and states and corporations with superior defensive AI systems will gain major strategic advantages in sustaining military operations under sustained cyber contest [2].

## Geoeconomics: The Economic-Military Nexus

[George Friedman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) draws on Adam Smith to frame the inextricable relationship between economic and military power. Smith understood that the essential role of government for economics was a standing and powerful military force, without which the economy could not survive. To have military power is to have enough economic success to build a military; to have economic power is to have a strong enough military to fend off those that covet the nation's wealth. A nation that is wealthy but weak is the most vulnerable to predatory nations; a nation that is strong but poor is vulnerable to internal strife [4].

The defense industry is unique in that the level of government control is enormous — a primary customer of a defense company is normally its own country. Competition between defense industries in different countries depends to a great extent on the government's policy in that country. Geoeconomics uses geopolitics as a lens to observe the effects of geopolitical confrontation on national economies — distinct from geopolitics, which looks at the entirety of a nation's power and interest [4].

## NATO Infrastructure and Mobility

Antonia Colibasanu (GPF) reports that the Ankara summit addressed the gap between political commitments and operational capability on NATO's eastern flank. Allies announced a planned 27 billion euro ($31 billion) investment to modernize NATO's fuel storage and distribution infrastructure and develop new pipelines extending eastward — adapting Cold War pipeline architecture to present strategic geography, where the main conventional threat lies much farther east [5].

Military mobility should be understood as a network rather than a series of transport projects. Its effectiveness is determined by its weakest point. A port may receive a large shipment, but the surrounding railway may lack suitable wagons. The Bucharest-Sofia corridor remains one of the most vulnerable sections of the wider corridor — dependent on national roads, ageing railway infrastructure, and a limited number of Danube crossings. NATO now maintains nine multinational Forward Land Forces battlegroups in Bulgaria, Estonia, Finland, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Romania, and Slovakia. The central question is no longer whether allies are willing to defend the eastern flank but whether they can build the interconnected industrial, logistical, and regional architecture required to do so under wartime conditions [5].

On the Black Sea sidelines, Romania, Bulgaria, and [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) expanded the Mine Countermeasures Black Sea Task Group mandate to include protection of critical undersea infrastructure — operationally significant for Romania's Neptun Deep offshore energy project. The NATO Engine aims to connect unused civilian and defense manufacturing capacity. Under the Drone Edge initiative, allies plan to invest more than $40 billion over five years in counter-drone capabilities [5].

## Russian Force Generation (Case)

Greg Whisler and Michael Kofman (War on the Rocks) document [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html)'s wartime force generation as a case study in defense industrial mobilization under sustained combat — a deployment that grew from a small peacetime army to over 700,000 personnel, achieving nearly 200% growth in tank battalions and 400% growth in infantry battalions while sustaining three years of casualty-intensive offensive operations [6]. The operational detail — recruitment pipeline, equipment refurbishment, the 2025 halt in force growth as monthly recruitment was offset by losses, and the fiscal untenability of rising regional bonuses — is tracked in the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) conflict file. The industrial-mobilization lesson for this theme is the paradox: Russia demonstrated a strong ability to generate and sustain forces — including high artillery and drone-fire rates throughout the war — but could not translate mass into breakthroughs. The problem was not logistical but an inability to concentrate forces at the decisive point. This case illustrates that defense industrial resilience is necessary but insufficient: the ability to produce and sustain forces must be paired with doctrinal adaptation and the capacity to employ combat power effectively at scale. [NATO]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/nato.html) planners must account for a post-war Russian military that is much larger, with expanded drone and precision-strike capabilities on their borders — even if its ability to employ that mass effectively remains in question [6].

## Inverting the Cost Curve

The cost-exchange framework — cheap mass (drones, mines, swarming boats) defeating expensive platforms, with defending often costing a hundred times more than attacking — is tracked canonically in [Drone Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html), which owns the Iran and Ukraine cost-exchange data and the invert-the-curve agenda (layered counter-drone systems, low-cost offensive strike, mine countermeasures, civilian resilience). The defense-procurement-policy implication that belongs here: the [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) and [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) wars demonstrate that two decades of procurement choices — exquisite platforms bought in small numbers, acquisition concentrated in a few large and often slow-moving prime contractors, and chronic neglect of the civilian systems and infrastructure that adversaries now treat as the primary battlespace — have produced a structural cost asymmetry that no spending uplift alone can repair. NATO has earmarked 1.5% of GDP for civilian resilience and innovation; the binding constraint is reforming procurement and decision-making so capabilities reach the force before the current window closes [7].

## Spending is Not Capacity

Ball, Crompton, and Detter ([RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html)) argue that Britain's defense debate is dominated by inputs (spending totals as % of GDP) rather than outputs (capacity and readiness). A larger defense budget does not automatically produce more defense capability. A ship that cannot be repaired quickly enough is not full capacity. A weapons system without sufficient munitions, spares or trained personnel is not full capacity. A base with poor housing, weak maintenance and inadequate resilience is not merely an estate management problem — it is a contributor to poor overall readiness [8].

They propose three reforms: (1) a Defence Capacity Statement with every major spending uplift, showing not just the amount spent but the capacity created (munitions output, drone production, ship repair, deployable units, training capacity, stockpile resilience, housing quality, maintenance reduction); (2) Capacity Schedules for each service and Top Level Budget holder, providing visibility of assets, condition, maintenance backlog, liabilities, bottlenecks, and capital projects; (3) a Defence Capacity Map classifying the assets, bottlenecks and liabilities that shape readiness. Weak accounting is evidence of weak management visibility — if provisions for liabilities are understated, today's budget is flattered at the expense of tomorrow's force. Deferred maintenance is not free; it is a hidden claim on future defense capacity [8].

## Critical Infrastructure as the Military Mobility Substrate

Retired Gen. Mike Minihan and Rear Adm. Mark Montgomery ([The Cipher Brief]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-cipher-brief.html)) argue that the binding constraint on American military power is not kinetic reach but the ability to maneuver people, equipment, fuel, information, and combat power through commercial infrastructure the Department of Defense does not own. Every deployment begins on commercial railroads, moves through civilian ports and airports, depends on privately owned communications networks, and increasingly relies on digital logistics systems linking government and commercial partners. [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) does not need to destroy American combat power to gain an advantage — it simply needs to delay it: every day equipment sits in a rail yard, every hour a port is offline, every aircraft waylaid by corrupted logistics data creates the friction an adversary seeks in the opening days of a conflict. For more than two years, US intelligence officials have warned that Chinese cyber operators are compromising hundreds of American transportation, communications, energy, and logistics systems, gaining persistent access so that Beijing can disrupt and destroy systems at the time of its choosing. Ports rely on automated cranes and digital cargo management; railroads depend on computerized dispatching and signaling; airports operate through integrated flight planning and logistics software; fuel distribution, maintenance scheduling, cargo visibility, and command and control all depend on data moving securely across interconnected networks [9].

The policy prescriptions follow the resilience logic already developed in the energy-cyber domain above: harmonize cybersecurity standards across transportation sectors so operators spend more time improving security than satisfying overlapping regulations; provide cybersecurity grant programs for under-resourced transportation infrastructure operators (smaller ports, airports, and rail operators cannot reasonably defend themselves against nation-state adversaries without federal partnership); and begin exercising the way the US expects to fight — national, regional, and local exercises should assume degraded communications, cyberattacks against transportation systems, corrupted logistics data, and contested movement inside the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html). Military and commercial networks need to work together to dynamically reroute forces around cyberattacks, infrastructure failures, or contested logistics. Decision advantage will increasingly come not from owning more platforms, but from orchestrating movement better than adversaries can disrupt it. The needed public-private collaboration has not occurred; many of these communities will come together for the first time during a national emergency [9]. See [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) for the mobility doctrine framing and [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) for the pre-positioning threat.

## Acquisition Reform and the Steering Gap

Pete Newell ([The Cipher Brief]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-cipher-brief.html)) — retired Army colonel, former director of the Army's Rapid Equipping Force, and co-creator of Hacking for Defense — argues that the Department of War's ambitious acquisition reform (scrapping JCIDS, replacing program offices with portfolio executives, building a Warfighting Acquisition System designed for speed) has improved the middle phases of innovation while leaving three of six phases with no organizational owner. Successful innovation runs in six phases: detect, define, develop, deploy, assess, distribute. The reforms invested almost entirely in develop and deploy. Nobody persistently monitors how the threat evolves at the tactical edge; nobody scopes each unit's problem with enough precision to drive useful solutions; nobody measures whether fielded systems actually work against an adversary who adapts after every engagement; and nobody moves what one unit learns to every other unit facing the same threat at operational speed. The department built a faster engine; nobody built the steering — the mechanism that decides which problems the engine should be pointed at, whether the solutions worked, and who else needs to know [10].

The counter-drone fight is the clearest test case. Washington treats it as an engineering puzzle — build a better jammer, field a cheaper interceptor — but the problem is a cycle-time gap, not a technology gap. Soldiers engage FPV [drones]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) that cost a few hundred dollars with $400,000 Stinger missiles, because the cheap interceptors proven in [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) still have no fast path into US formations. A new drone variant appears on the battlefield every week, built from commercial parts and open-source software; a firmware update that defeats a jammer costs nothing and takes hours; the US counter, even through the reformed system, takes months. Newell draws the structural parallel to the counter-IED campaign in [Afghanistan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/afghanistan.html), where $75 billion was spent and the fight was lost anyway: "Drones are IEDs that fly." The adversary does not need to out-technology the US; he only needs to out-cycle it [10].

The industry obligation follows from the gap. Companies that want to matter in this market should do three things differently: invest in problem discovery (put engineers and business developers forward with operational units to understand problems before proposing solutions — "55 minutes on the problem, five on the solution"); build for adaptation, not for the requirement (if a product cannot change in weeks — modular hardware, software-defined behavior, upgrades at firmware speed — it is obsolete on delivery, because the adversary's development cycle runs in days); and plug into the new portfolio structure as a sensor, not just a supplier (the fusion cells that Portfolio Acquisition Executives need cannot function without industry feeding data in and absorbing assessment data out). The workforce is where the reform agenda is thinnest: the authorities changed faster than the people who must wield them, and the Defense Acquisition University's conversion to a Warfighting Acquisition University needs to scale experiential, problem-first education of the kind Hacking for Defense has pioneered over a decade [10]. See [Drone Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) for the counter-drone cycle-time analysis and [Revolution in Military Affairs]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/revolution-in-military-affairs.html) for the broader doctrinal frame.

## Right to Repair and the Forward Sustainment of Unmanned Systems

Dan Justice ([War on the Rocks]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/war-on-the-rocks.html)) argues that the Navy's coming unmanned fleet cannot be sustained overseas without confronting the right-to-repair problem and the original-equipment-manufacturer (OEM) dependency that has come under scrutiny as the Pentagon grapples with the strategic consequences of proprietary restrictions. Two organizational models compete. The first is an organic, expeditionary approach — newly established robotics warfare and dual-track surface warfare officers deploying alongside their platforms from semi-permanent forward bases, complementing "right to repair" efforts and the reactivation of shore intermediate maintenance activities. It demands a cascade of reforms: standing up parent squadrons, creating and filling billets, building training pipelines, and folding new commands into existing global force management plans. The knowledge gap is acute: because the final composition of the unmanned fleet remains undecided, developing a specialized training pipeline for platforms whose engineering requirements and operational numbers remain a moving target is structurally difficult [11].

The second model outsources repair and sustainment to private contractors — a pay-for-service framework that saves money by offloading training, logistics, stockpile management, and personnel administration. This model has come under scrutiny as the Pentagon grapples with the strategic consequences of OEM dependency: the drive for right-to-repair protections and the legislative pushes to reclaim intellectual property rights reflect a growing concern that proprietary restrictions keep sailors from performing even basic troubleshooting. A viable contractor model for the hybrid fleet could route work through a separate third-party entity to sidestep manufacturer-specific IP barriers, paired with efforts at allied and partner bases alongside their own forces in support of a regional sustainment framework. This "lead sustainment integrator" model would not fully return repair ownership to sailors but offers efficiency, professionalized expertise, and geographic reach with enough distance from the original manufacturers to avoid the trap of vendor lock [11].

The optimal path may be a blended approach: private contractors secure small logistics sites near likely ports, manage forward storage, navigate host-nation customs, and maintain large inventories, building the local relationships and administrative familiarity that simplify theater entry; service members then arrive as transient maintenance teams to perform the technical repairs and depart once the vessel returns to sea. This division of labor lets the commercial sector absorb the persistent administrative and logistics friction of a forward footprint while reserving the technical work for military personnel with the legal and operational mandate to operate in non-permissive environments. The full geographic and legal analysis — distributed austere ports, archipelagic passage rights for uncrewed vessels — is tracked in [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html) and [Drone Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) [11].

## Ukraine's Military-Industrial Complex and EU Integration

Ryvkin (GPF) reports that Ukraine's defense production capacity grew from roughly 1 billion euros in 2022 to approximately 55 billion euros by 2026, with 82% of suppliers to the Ukrainian army homegrown by 2025. This transformation extends beyond domestic capacity: starting in 2024, Ukraine established joint ventures with Rheinmetall (Germany), BAE Systems (UK), and KNDS (Franco-German), embedding Western prime contractors directly into Ukrainian production. The [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) shifted from a financing-and-purchasing posture to integrating Ukraine's defense innovation ecosystem into its own industrial base via BraveTech EU — a joint initiative backed by 50 million euros each from the EU and Ukraine, linking Ukraine's defense technology platform to the European Defense Fund, European Defense Agency, and EU Defense Innovation Scheme [12].

The comparative-advantage division is structurally clear: Ukraine supplies battlefield-tested designs and combat data from tens of thousands of combat flights used to train AI models, while the EU provides capital, procurement access, large-scale manufacturing, and industrial facilities less exposed to Russian missile attack. Russia's declining arms exports are creating a market niche that Ukraine is occupying — centered on drones, AI, electronic warfare, and battlefield management software. US market entry is also accelerating: Swarmer (AI-based drone software) debuted on NASDAQ; General Cherry (a Zaporizhzhia-based FPV drone producer) signed deals for US production sites; and Sine Engineering (drone jamming protection) was selected as the first project for the U.S.-Ukraine Reconstruction Investment Fund under the "minerals deal." Japan and Taiwan are also reportedly seeking Ukrainian defense technology [12]. See [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html).

## The $54B DAWG and the Budget-Driven Acquisition Problem

Todd Harrison (AEI), speaking at a Brookings Institution panel on the FY2027 defense budget, identifies the proposed $1.5 trillion package as fundamentally budget-driven rather than strategy-driven. The administration set an arbitrary target — 5% of last year's GDP — and instructed the Department of Defense to fill it, rather than deriving spending from threat priorities or force-design logic. The telltale sign is the absence of hard tradeoffs: the budget simultaneously funds new F-15s, continued F-35 production, and sixth-generation fighter development, as though the department faces no resource constraints. When everything is funded, nothing is prioritized [13].

Harrison singles out the $54 billion Defense Autonomous Warfare Group (DAWG) line item as the most egregious example. The FY2027 request represents a 243-fold increase over the prior year's DAWG funding — exceeding the entire Marine Corps budget request of $52.8 billion and consuming nearly 15% of the $350 billion reconciliation package. Yet the budget justification provides almost no programmatic detail: no quantities, no specific drone types, no operational concept. Harrison's demand is basic transparency — "tell us quantities, tell us specific types of drones, tell us what you're going to be using it for" — and the absence suggests the department itself has not yet defined the program. The [drone warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) investment trajectory is thus shaped not by operational requirements but by budget arithmetic [13].

Harrison advances a plausible alternative reading: the $1.5 trillion figure may function as a five-year pre-funded budget rather than a single-year authorization. The department cannot realistically absorb spending at that annual rate, and the reconciliation mechanism could allow slower expenditure across multiple years. If so, the headline number is a fiscal positioning device, not an annual spending plan — and Congress will need to distinguish serious programs from filler when it sorts through the request [13].

The battleship program illustrates the same strategy-budget disconnect. The proposed timeline — starting a clean-sheet design in FY2027, procuring the lead ship in FY2028, and reaching full-rate production at one per year by FY2031 — does not, in Harrison's judgment, pass the laugh test. No naval architect could credibly compress design, prototyping, and production ramp into four years. The program appears to have been inserted because the budget's sheer size eliminated the pressure to make difficult choices. Harrison expects billions to be spent chasing the concept before it is eventually cancelled [13].

Mara Karlin (SAIS/Johns Hopkins) sharpens the strategic paradox. The Trump national defense strategy emphasizes the Western Hemisphere and a circumscribed China focus limited to the first island chain, while downgrading European involvement — a posture that, if taken at face value, would justify a much smaller budget. Yet the request reaches what Karlin calls "World War levels" of spending. The number is not merited by the strategy as written, leaving analysts with what she describes as perplexity: either the strategy document understates the actual threat assessment, or the budget is disconnected from it [13].

Harrison concludes that the congressional budget process itself requires structural reform — changing the fiscal year start date, reorganizing committee jurisdictions, and rethinking the authorization-appropriation split. He draws an explicit parallel to the early 1970s, when Congress last recognized that its budgeting machinery was broken and undertook fundamental reform. Without such reform, the gap between strategy and spending will persist regardless of which party controls the executive [13].

## Agentic AI and the Make-or-Buy Shift in Defense Software

Jacob Green (War on the Rocks) argues that the rapid maturation of agentic AI has fundamentally moved the make-or-buy line in defense software acquisition. The secretary of defense's commercial-first policy — buy commercial solutions first, modify if necessary, develop only as a last resort — was sound when the alternative was years-long government development. But agentic AI tools have collapsed the cost and time of in-house development to a degree that inverts the calculus: the department should now buy infrastructure, agentic tooling, and model access commercially, and build applications in-house. Green's own pilot experience is illustrative — a 60,000-page regulatory data corpus wrapped in a Model Context Protocol (MCP) server, built in under 20 hours of focused attention for approximately $1,700 in model inference costs, against a COCOMO-estimated $840,000–$1.2 million through a conventional small-business prototype award [14].

The procurement architecture Green proposes has three commercial layers. First, departmental procurement of cloud infrastructure and on-site hardware to establish common, accredited development environments — consolidating what bespoke program offices currently duplicate. Second, model-agnostic agentic harnesses capable of serving both casual chat users and enterprise developers, procured through AI gateways that provide a single endpoint to multiple models with consolidated monitoring and flat-rate pricing. The gateway approach hedges against the volatile cadence of AI capability growth: rather than betting on a single model, the department can switch to the best performer at any given moment. Third, open source tools — Google's Gemma models, Hermes Agent, Open Code, and others — offer capability with no licensing costs and, critically, full source-code transparency for security review. Green contends that the security argument against open source applies equally to all software and is addressed through the same scanning and red-teaming already standard practice [14].

The most disruptive implication is for the department's large data platforms. The FY2027 budget earmarks approximately $2.3 billion for the Maven Smart System and Joint Fires Network alone, with additional enterprise data platforms adding significant annual costs. These platforms lock the department into proprietary ecosystems with escalating integration labor and mounting switching costs. Green argues that the Model Context Protocol — an open-source standard for agentic context retrieval, now adopted industry-wide including by major platform vendors — can replace costly system-to-system integrations with real-time agent-to-agent data retrieval. Each authoritative data source would expose an MCP server; any compliant agentic application could query data on demand without pre-built integrations or duplicated data stores. This realizes the Data Mesh vision the department has pursued: data lives in its functional domain system and is accessible as a product through agent interactions, rather than aggregated in a centralized platform. The department would retain choice and buying power, stop paying recurring licensing for capabilities it can stand up internally, and finally achieve the plug-and-play open architecture ecosystem its own guidelines prescribe [14].

To enable this shift, Green calls for pre-accredited development and production environments with streamlined onboarding for internal developers. Current accreditation timelines of 6–18 months before a team can begin building and deploying software would be eliminated for pre-approved environments, compressing delivery timelines more than any vendor-side reform. Internal competition would be introduced through reimbursable models and a central work-request system where the department's existing development organizations — Army Software Factory, Science and Technology Centers, program offices — bid on projects across service lines. This market mechanism incentivizes efficiency without mandating personnel reductions [14].

Green is explicit that agentic development is not unsupervised vibe-coding. He envisions a gated pipeline with human review at critical phases — requirements decomposition, development, testing, cyber compliance, documentation, deployment, and monitoring. Agents are grounded in living documentation written in plain English markdown, forced to check code against acceptance criteria before advancing. This discipline makes sustainment tractable: because architecture, software bills of materials, and runbooks stay current as built-in workflow artifacts, development can transfer between teams — or to agents that were never part of the original build — without the knowledge loss that plagues government software today. The broader significance for the [AI military race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html) is that the decisive advantage may lie not in owning the best models but in building the organizational infrastructure to convert commercial AI capability into mission software at speed — a capability that currently favors whichever actor restructures its acquisition and development processes around agentic workflows first [14]. See also [Revolution in Military Affairs]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/revolution-in-military-affairs.html).

## The Attrition Industrial Gap

[Aurelien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/aurelien.html) argues the West can never fight an attrition war against [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) successfully because the necessary industrial and engineering infrastructure does not exist and cannot be recreated — it is doubtful it ever existed on the necessary scale in the first place. This applies irrespective of the technical capability of Western equipment and the limited potential for increasing force size. The political consequences are enormous and have not been considered at all [15].

The military market is so internationalized that Western countries typically import at least 50% of their components and sub-assemblies, not to mention raw materials. There are probably half a dozen countries in the world that could bring F-35 production and maintenance to a screaming halt. By sending its weapons to [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html), the West is making the longer-term problem worse — depleting stockpiles that cannot be rebuilt in any relevant timeframe. Drones will not help, since the Russians will always have a manufacturing advantage. The Cold War expectation that conflicts would be short, and therefore large missile stockpiles were unnecessary, created the structural deficit now exposed in both Ukraine and [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) [15].

Talk of "rearmament" should not be taken seriously when the industrial base to sustain it does not exist. The real problems the West faces are primarily conceptual and psychological — an over-dependence on abstract thinking and adherence to norms derived from the past. The West no longer knows what it wants military forces for, or how it would employ them [15]. See [Revolution in Military Affairs]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/revolution-in-military-affairs.html).

## Military Culture as a Component of Defense Resilience

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) identifies a cultural dimension of defense resilience that is typically overlooked in industrial-capacity analyses: the institutional values a military celebrates directly shape its procurement, training, and operational priorities. The US military under Secretary Hegseth is prioritizing testosterone screening — the "High-T Department of War" — and romantic sacrifice rhetoric over the education, technological sophistication, and adaptability that actually correlate with battlefield success. The historical record is instructive: the bravest soldiers of World War II (the Japanese) lost; Italian soldiers were brave but underequipped; US and British soldiers prevailed through superior logistics and technology, not superior valor. An institution that equates warfighting with testosterone levels — a metric medically linked to infertility — is not merely pursuing eccentric cultural politics; it is misallocating the human-capital investment that underpins defense industrial resilience. The binding constraint on modern warfare is not courage but the energy, digital, and intellectual substrate that sustains operations. A military culture that celebrates sacrifice over adaptability will under-invest in exactly the capabilities — education, technical training, systems thinking — that the [Revolution in Military Affairs]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/revolution-in-military-affairs.html) demands [16]. See [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html).

## THAAD and Patriot Munitions Depletion

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) provides specific stockpile data that quantifies the structural deficit exposed by the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html). Pre-war THAAD inventory stood at approximately 360 interceptors; 190–290 were expended (53–80%) by the April ceasefire, leaving approximately 70–170 remaining. No new THAAD deliveries are scheduled from August 2023 through April 2027; production is expanding from 96 to 400 per year but the gap is years long. Pre-war Patriot PAC-3/MSE inventory stood at approximately 2,330; 1,060–1,430 were expended (45–61%), leaving approximately 900–1,270 remaining, against a production rate of approximately 600 per year. Combined, the two systems yield roughly 1,500 remaining interceptors — with a two-per-target doctrine, perhaps 1,000 successful intercepts against an adversary arsenal of 3,500 missiles. An anonymous US official stated: "We do not have enough to safely sustain operations, and I don't think the White House is aware of that" [17]. See [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html), [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), and [Taiwan Standoff]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html).

## Munitions Depletion, Interceptor Production, and the Tomahawk Delay

Ueki provides specific data quantifying the defense industrial crisis exposed by the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) and its direct impact on [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html)'s defense modernization. Approximately half of the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html)' key munitions were consumed in 39 days of the air and missile campaign against Iran, with more than half of that expenditure going to interceptor missiles (THAAD, Patriot, SM-3). Between 1,900 and 3,000 interceptors were used in less than two weeks of combat — against a US production capacity of 600 Patriot PAC-3 interceptors per year (50 per month). At this exchange ratio, peacetime production cannot sustain even a brief high-intensity conflict, let alone replenish depleted stockpiles for a potential [Taiwan contingency]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) [18].

The Tomahawk delay exemplifies how allied readiness is collateral damage of US munitions consumption. [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) purchased 400 Tomahawk missiles as the main component of its counterstrike capability — initially planned for Block V delivery starting in 2026, then restructured to 200 Block IV and 200 Block V for earlier delivery from 2025. Reports indicate delays of up to four years due to US munitions use in the Iran war, impeding Japan's plans to rapidly strengthen deterrence. Defense Minister Koizumi emphasized the need to increase domestic production to avoid dependency risks. The underlying problem is the offense-defense cost asymmetry: a $4 million Patriot interceptor against cheap drones that cost a fraction of that amount. China dominates drone production — DJI alone holds over 70 percent of the world civilian market share — and drones are much cheaper and easier to manufacture than interceptors. The gap in the offense-defense balance is expected to widen in attrition warfare, where sustained cheap-drone attacks exhaust expensive interceptor stockpiles [18].

In response, the US and Japan have launched Operation Supercharge — agreed by Secretary Hegseth and Minister Koizumi in Singapore on May 30, 2026 — to accelerate codevelopment and coproduction of missiles such as SM-3 Block IIA and AMRAAM. The US government has also launched a plan to seek drone dominance, addressing China's rapid production and allied dependence on Chinese drone supply chains. These initiatives represent a structural shift from procurement-based readiness to industrial-base cooperation, but the production timelines mean that near-term gaps will persist [18]. See [Drone Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) and [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html).
