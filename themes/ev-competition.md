# Background

The automotive industry is one of the central pillars of a country's industrial power. It requires vast manufacturing ecosystems, promotes domestic supply chains of specific parts, sustains millions of jobs, and is a symbol of modern economic strength. The transition to electric vehicles is not merely a technological shift but a contest over industrial power — the foundations of the industry are changing, and the state that controls the EV supply chain controls the future of automotive manufacturing [1].

[China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) treated EVs as a strategic industry long before many traditional automakers did, building advantages across batteries, mineral processing, production scale, charging infrastructure, and low-cost assembly. The [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) and [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html) are responding with tariffs, industrial policy, domestic content rules, and critical mineral strategies, but protection alone cannot create a full EV supply chain [1].

# Current Situation

## China's Dominance

EVs were designated a priority area in Beijing's 10th Five-Year Plan (2001-2005). Through subsidies, tax incentives, procurement policies, industrial planning, infrastructure investment, and production mandates, Beijing sought to create both supply and demand for EVs. The state-level focus on the entire ecosystem led to improved quality and lower prices. Many Chinese EV firms no longer depend on subsidies but still benefit from the industrial ecosystem Beijing helped create [1].

Key data points illustrating China's dominance:

- Global electric car sales exceeded 20 million in 2025, with China alone selling more than 13 million. EVs represented almost 55% of new car sales in the Chinese market [1].
- China accounted for nearly three-quarters of global electric car production in 2025, while Chinese EV exports doubled to more than 2.5 million units [1].
- China overtook [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) as the global auto sales leader in 2025 [1].
- China was responsible for more than 80% of global battery cell production through 2025 [1].
- Battery prices in China were roughly $84/kWh in 2025; North American and European prices were as much as 55% higher [1].
- China was the key source of growth in global supply for refined cobalt, lithium, graphite, and rare earths [1].

## Battery Technology as Core Advantage

The most important advantage for Chinese companies lies in battery technology. In the combustion-engine era, engine design was a core strength of US, European, and Japanese automakers. In the EV era, the battery plays that role — it is the most expensive component, the main determinant of range and performance, and the foundation of cost competitiveness. China's dominance over batteries owes to its position in supply chains of raw and processed [critical minerals]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/critical-minerals.html). The IEA's critical mineral outlook for 2025 showed growing demand for lithium, cobalt, graphite, nickel, and rare earths, driven heavily by EVs and battery storage [1].

## United States Response

With the exception of Tesla, US companies have been relatively reluctant to ramp up EV production rapidly, instead focusing on profitable trucks and SUVs. Their response to supply chain issues is more focused on security risks than market-driven policies. Policy volatility compounds the problem, as battery plants, mines, and refineries require long investment horizons. Tax credits for EVs incentivized procurement, but consumer-side support has been inconsistent [1].

Washington has stronger bipartisan agreement to reduce Chinese imports than to curb the adoption of EV production itself. Lawmakers intend to pull production into the US or allied economies, but lack the scale and innovation to effectively compete with China's whole-process ecosystem. Mining companies, refiners, battery recyclers, cathode and anode producers, industrial construction firms, grid operators, charging companies, and logistics providers stand to benefit from the policy push. [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html), Canada, [South Korea]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html), Japan, and selected European markets may benefit, provided they can fit into US rules on origin, ownership, and processing [1].

The US has effectively excluded the leading edge of China's export drive with 100% tariffs on Chinese EVs, making [China Shock 2.0]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) largely a Europe problem [2].

## European Union Response

[Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html) is more exposed because its automotive sector is deeply embedded in its industrial base. [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html), [France]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/france.html), Italy, Spain, Czechia, Slovakia, and Hungary all have major economic and employment interests tied to car manufacturing. A disorderly EV transition would affect carmakers but also steel, chemicals, machinery, electronics, logistics, repair networks, and regional labor markets. The sector supports more than 13 million jobs and generated 273 billion euros in gross value added in 2023 [1].

Europe's response is built around strategic autonomy, regulation, and selective industrial support. The Critical Raw Materials Act is central — it means to expand extraction, processing, and recycling and to reduce dependency on single suppliers. The 2030 benchmarks include 10% of annual consumption from extraction, 40% from processing, 25% from recycling, and no more than 65% dependence on a single third country. Europe's main constraint is cost: energy prices, labor costs, permitting delays, and fragmented national priorities make it difficult to match China's scale and speed [1].

## Missing Layers and Opportunities

The main opportunity for Western competitors lies in the missing layers of the EV supply chain: mining, refining, battery materials, recycling, charging infrastructure, logistics, software, and grid integration. Tariffs cannot create an EV supply chain. Facilities need to be developed that can produce battery-grade lithium, refine graphite, build cathode plants, expand charging networks, and reduce the cost of industrial energy. This is already driving policy support for mining, refining, battery materials, recycling, and charging infrastructure [1].

# Key Dynamics

- **Whole-process ecosystem vs fragmented competitors**: China's integrated approach — from mineral refining to vehicle assembly to charging infrastructure — gives it structural advantages that Western fragmentation cannot match [1].
- **Battery as the new engine**: Control of battery technology and its mineral supply chain is the foundation of EV industrial power. China's 80%+ share of battery cell production is the decisive metric [1].
- **Tariffs as insufficient response**: Both the US and EU have used tariffs to slow Chinese vehicles entering their markets, but tariffs cannot create an EV supply chain. The gap between protection and capability is the central challenge [1].
- **Critical minerals as bottleneck**: The EV transition is inseparable from the [critical minerals]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/critical-minerals.html) competition — lithium, cobalt, graphite, nickel, and rare earths. China's dominance in mineral processing creates a structural dependency for Western economies [1].
- **Overcapacity and consolidation in China**: China's intense domestic competition has lowered prices and improved quality but also created overcapacity and margin pressure. Some Chinese EV firms will consolidate or fail; the survivors are likely to be stronger, more export-oriented, and harder to dislodge [1].

# Analysis

## China Shock 2.0 and the EV Frontline

[Adam Tooze]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/adam-tooze.html) frames the current wave of Chinese export competition as "China Shock 2.0" — distinct from the early-2000s "China Shock 1.0" which was a low-tech, low-wage cost story felt primarily in the US. China Shock 2.0 is above all about Europe and about China's progress into advanced industrial sectors, above all vehicles. The EU-China trade deficit has roughly doubled since COVID. The surge is concentrated in green energy goods and chemicals. Contrary to the price-dumping thesis, unit values of Chinese exports are rising — the surge is a deliberate industrialization push backed by policy, not exchange-rate manipulation [2].

CSIS estimates that Chinese EV industry subsidies totaled $231 billion from 2009 to 2023. German automakers paid out €31 billion in dividends in 2023 alone rather than reinvesting — complacency that has compounded European vulnerability. The Chinese export surge reflects structural industrial policy, not weak home demand [2].

## Germany at the Epicenter

[Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html) is at the epicenter of China Shock 2.0. Germany's trade balance with China swung by €27 billion between 2021 and 2025, with 60% of that swing accounted for by vehicles. Volkswagen announced plans to cut up to 100,000 jobs. Chancellor Merz has taken up the accusation of an undervalued Chinese currency and called for a new Plaza Accord. Tooze frames this as "mercantilist-on-mercantilist violence": Germany is itself a chronic trade surplus country, so China Shock 2.0 is competitive surplus against surplus [2].
