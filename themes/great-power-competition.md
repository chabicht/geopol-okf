# Background

US hegemony established after the Cold War is now contested by [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html). Two frameworks capture the competitive structure: Tchakarova's "DragonBear axis" — the coordination between China as economic engine and Russia as military disruptor — and Kiesewetter's "CRINK alliance" (China, Russia, Iran, North Korea) — the broader coalition cooperating militarily and technologically against NATO across every theater.

Great power competition operates across multiple theaters simultaneously: military, economic, technological, and ideological. The defining feature is not competition in any single domain but the convergence of competition across all domains — creating a systemic contest in which advantage in one theater can offset disadvantage in another.

# Current Situation

The competitive landscape is defined by asymmetries in energy, alliance architecture, and strategic bandwidth:

- **Energy asymmetry**: The [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) consumes 16% of world energy with 4% of the population; China uses almost twice as much energy as the US. The energy balance underwrites the competitive balance — China's manufacturing capacity is a direct function of its energy throughput.
- **India's forced alignment**: [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html) has no choice but to balance China and will stick with America despite Trump — not because of ideological affinity but because the absence of alternatives. Partnership with the US is not a choice but the lack of one.
- **German strategic awakening**: [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html) is publishing a military strategy; NATO has set a 5% spending target for 2035. The European component of the Western coalition is belatedly recognizing the competitive reality.
- **Latin America as theater**: The rightward wave in [Latin America]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/latin-america.html) is converting ideological alignment into a contest for hemispheric spheres of influence.
- **Africa as arena**: The [Sahel insurgency]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/sahel-insurgency.html) and broader African instability create an arena for Turkish, Russian, and Western competition — with drones, minerals, and security partnerships as the currency of influence.
- **Bangladesh as new contested space**: [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html)'s $9.2 billion investment push into [Bangladesh]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/bangladesh.html) extends great power competition into the Bay of Bengal. Bangladesh pursues strategic autonomy among China, [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html), and the US, but economic influence does not automatically translate into political alignment [10].
- **South American sovereignty**: South America is becoming a secondary theater of U.S.-China competition, with Brazil building domestic defense capacity and Argentina rebuilding relevance through U.S.-linked security cooperation. The contest plays out through ports, infrastructure, and defense supply chains rather than formal alliances [11].
- **Germany-China trade tensions**: Chancellor Merz's call for a new "Plaza Accord" to address China's undervalued currency has alarmed Beijing. China views the proposal as economic coercion and has responded with rare-earth export controls, while Brussels remains hesitant to provoke a trade conflict that could hamper EU rearmament [12].
- **EU security partnership diversification**: The EU has concluded 12 Security and Defence Partnerships since 2024, spanning three circles — EEA/accession candidates, NATO partners beyond the EU (Canada, UK), and global partners (Japan, South Korea, Australia, India, Ghana). The SAFE instrument's €150 billion in defence loans with a "Buy European" clause makes SDPs a prerequisite for preferential access to the European armaments market. This represents the EU's effort to become a center of gravity for security cooperation in its own right, diversifying beyond US dependence [13].
- **Arctic competition**: The [Arctic]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/arctic.html) is emerging as a theater where digital sovereignty, submarine cable infrastructure, and great power competition converge. The EU, [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), and the US all pursue competing interests. Russia suspended cooperation after the Ukraine invasion; Trump claimed Greenland; China seeks influence through research and investment. The EU is investing €347 million in submarine cable projects, designating the Arctic as one of 13 Cable Projects of European Interest [14].
- **Southeast Asia as contested theater**: Southeast Asia has independent strategic weight beyond being a secondary arena of US-China rivalry. ASEAN states (projected fourth-largest economy by 2030, 684 million people) actively diversify partnerships — seeking OECD membership, exploring BRICS engagement — while resisting pressure to choose sides. [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) is the most influential external actor, followed by the US; Japan and Australia are long-standing partners. [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html) and the [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) are deepening engagement but remain limited in visibility and investment [15].
- **India-led Asian containment coalition**: Friedman (GPF) argues that Modi's July 2026 Asia tour signals the emergence of an Asian coalition (India, Japan, Australia, Indonesia, New Zealand, Vietnam) that is more significant than the Quad because it includes an economic dimension and Indonesia. The network would threaten China on land and sea, potentially limiting China's maritime access to Europe and the Middle East and making China dependent on a trans-Pacific route. This evolution relieves the US from being the primary force containing China — consistent with broader American disengagement from the Eastern Hemisphere [16].

# Key Dynamics

- **Bandwidth as key vulnerability**: The Western coalition is being tested on multiple fronts simultaneously. The DragonBear axis and CRINK alliance are engineered to exploit bandwidth limitations — the structural constraint that Western democracies, with their legislative processes, electoral cycles, and bureaucratic fragmentation, cannot respond to simultaneous pressure across all theaters at full capacity.
- **Axis engineered to exploit bandwidth**: The coordination between China, Russia, Iran, and North Korea is designed to generate concurrent crises that stretch Western attention and resources. Each crisis individually is manageable; the simultaneity is the weapon.
- **Personal leadership matters more than abstract national interest**: Wars are choices of individual leaders, not state structures. Putin's decision to invade Ukraine and Trump's decision to strike Iran were personal choices that state-level analysis could not predict — exposing the limitation of structural realist frameworks that treat states as unitary rational actors.

# Analysis

## Power Asymmetry

[The Honest Sorcerer]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-honest-sorcerer.html) observes that the US maintaining global dominance with half the economy of China generates structural friction — the material and energy basis of US power is increasingly mismatched to the scale of its global commitments. The US consumes 16% of world energy with 4% of the population; China uses nearly twice as much. The implication is that the competitive balance is shifting not because of policy failure but because of biophysical fundamentals [1].

Rajagopalan and Rajagopalan argue that India has no option but to balance China — partnership with the US is the absence of choice, not the affirmation of alliance. India's alignment is structurally determined by the China threat, meaning that it will persist regardless of US political leadership. Trump's unpredictability does not change India's strategic calculus because the calculus has no alternative endpoint [2].

## Alliance Dynamics

Kiesewetter's CRINK alliance framework identifies the cooperative structure: China, Russia, Iran, and North Korea cooperate militarily and technologically against NATO in every theater. The alliance is not a formal treaty structure but an operational coordination — technology transfer, intelligence sharing, mutual logistics — that generates simultaneous pressure across multiple domains [3].

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html)'s DragonBear axis framework captures the bilateral core of this coordination: China as the economic engine providing resources, technology, and diplomatic cover; Russia as the military disruptor creating kinetic pressure that diverts Western attention and resources. The axis uses coordinated pressure to exploit Western bandwidth — the structural inability of democracies to sustain high-intensity engagement across all theaters simultaneously [4].

## Leadership vs Structure

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) argues that wars are choices of individual leaders, not state structures. Putin's decision to invade Ukraine and [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html)'s decision to strike Iran were personal choices that no structural realist framework predicted — exposing the limitation of theories that treat states as unitary rational actors responding to systemic incentives. The implication is that great power competition cannot be analyzed without attention to the specific individuals making decisions at specific moments [5].

This is not a rejection of structural analysis but a correction of its overextension. Structural factors — energy balance, alliance architecture, demographic trajectories — set the parameters of competition. But within those parameters, the timing, intensity, and direction of competitive action are determined by leaders whose decision-making cannot be reduced to state interest. The [Cold War 2]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html) is structural in its dimensions but personal in its inflection points [5].

## Spheres of Influence Asymmetry

[Michael Beckley]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/michael-beckley.html) argues that the US possesses the only true sphere of influence — asymmetric hegemony in the Western Hemisphere rooted in the Monroe Doctrine and sustained by geographic isolation and overwhelming military dominance. China and Russia cannot consolidate spheres in their own neighborhoods because they are surrounded by states that resist their dominance. The asymmetry is dangerous but creates an opening: as allies rearm and the US leans into its hemispheric advantage, a tougher, more resilient free world is possible. The implication is that declinist narratives extrapolating from specific failures (e.g., the Iran war) miss the structural resilience of the US position [6].

## Re-anchoring: The US-China Accommodation Thesis

[Geopolitical Futures]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) argues that US-China relations are the most important geopolitical issue in 2026. Both nations need an economic understanding — China's economy has been weakened by tariffs, and the US needs to bring down consumer goods prices. A military accommodation could follow, based on transparency measures like mutual notification of exercises. The most contentious issue is [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html), but a potential compromise (formal Chinese sovereignty with extensive autonomy, capped US military commitments) seems plausible. If achieved, this would establish a "bipolar world with two powers not engaged in either economic or military confrontation" — re-anchoring the global system and dramatically reducing conflict risk [7].

The consequence for [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) would be a further decline: the Sino-Russian relationship is transactional, not eternal, and an entente between the US and China would leave Moscow facing a historical competitor along its long border. Russia should be seen as a regional, not global, power — that honor belongs to the US and China alone. For [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html), the reduction of the Russian threat and the absence of US-China confrontation would force the EU to confront its own fragmentation: the Franco-German engine is constrained, sovereigntist movements are rising, and the EU may devolve into a multiregional system rather than a unified bloc [7].

## Mercantilist-on-Mercantilist Economic Competition

[Adam Tooze]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/adam-tooze.html) provides an economic dimension to great power competition that complements the military and diplomatic frameworks. He identifies four disrupters of the 2026 world economy — Trump's trade rampage, US fiscal incontinence, the AI boom, and China's economic gear-shift — operating simultaneously but partially disconnected. The US AI boom is sucking in imports from [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html) and Mexico but not from China; the US-China bilateral trade truce is holding and the deficit has narrowed. Meanwhile, China Shock 2.0 is devastating European industry, particularly German automotive, through a mercantilist competition in which both sides are surplus economies. The economic competition is thus not a simple US-versus-China binary but a more complex geometry in which Europe bears the brunt of Chinese export displacement while the US maintains a separate, AI-driven expansion dynamic [8].

The financial dimension reinforces the asymmetry: China's capital controls wall in $50 trillion in wealth, preventing Chinese elites from participating in the US AI asset boom. The yuan is undervalued not by market forces but by financial repression — calling for revaluation without addressing the capital control system is, in Tooze's framing, a euphemism. The "gilded cage" dynamic — in which non-US investors benefit from $13 trillion in US asset profits over the last decade while China's bourgeoisie is excluded — drives structural tension within China's political economy even as it sustains the export machine [8].

## Energy Resilience as Competitive Vector

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) frames the post-Iran-war energy market as a competitive arena between the Western-led IEA architecture and the DragonBear axis. The oil price collapse — Brent from above $180 to around $72 — was not a supply recovery but a demand management success: coordinated reserve releases of 300M+ barrels, Chinese demand destruction (40% import decline), and global demand contraction of 5.5M bpd in Q2 2026. This demonstrated that global demand management and reserve coordination can now absorb shocks that would have been unmanageable a decade ago — a quiet win for the Western-led IEA architecture in its competition with the DragonBear axis over who controls energy resilience tools [9].

China's demand pullback also demonstrates Beijing's growing willingness to manage its own exposure unilaterally rather than rely on price signals alone — a form of strategic autonomy that middle powers will study closely. The implication is that energy resilience is no longer merely an economic function but a competitive capability: the side that can better absorb and manage supply disruptions gains systemic advantage. [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s retained leverage over [Hormuz]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/hormuz-toll-system.html) means the underlying rupture has not closed, only been priced as if it has — a single incident could reprice the entire system within days [9].

## The Middle Power Mirage

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) challenges the proposition that a collective middle powers strategy can function as a third force between Pax Americana 2.0 and the DragonBear. Elbridge Colby, speaking from the US Department of War, dismissed the concept as a distraction — a handful of allies mistaking a threat to American primacy for a genuine one. His argument: middle powers share no coherent basis for alignment, and no alternative country or coalition can match the American defense industrial base. But as Tchakarova notes, this is not the language of a superpower indifferent to the project — it is the language of one that has noticed [17].

The debate exposed five fault lines. Phil Gordon captured the contradiction: it is extraordinary to treat allies like vassals, threaten their territorial integrity, impose unilateral tariffs, and then express surprise when those allies seek autonomy. Gerard Araud supplied the missing basis for alignment: middle powers cooperate not out of virtue but out of shared interest in not being crushed between the Chinese rock and the American hard place. Shashank Joshi made the cleanest analytical point: it can be simultaneously true that middle powers want more American engagement and that they hedge against the genuine risk of abandonment — engagement and hedging are concurrent, not mutually exclusive. Alexander Clarkson noted the awkward fact that American shipbuilding is in such crisis that the US Navy orders from Korean and Italian yards. Ben Woodfinden read the thread as both a swipe at Carney and the most useful thing Washington has said about the middle power project [17].

Tchakarova's geopolitical judgment: the single country that genuinely possesses strategic autonomy as a middle power right now is [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html). Everything else is aspiration mislabeled as capability. To expect that European powers like [France]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/france.html) or [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html), or Canada, will develop a functioning middle power strategy on any near horizon is wishful projection. It will take European and other middle powers decades, not years, to reach anything resembling strategic autonomy — and only through what Tchakarova calls "civilizational effort": significant sacrifice of the social welfare system, hard-core reindustrialization, the brutal filling of defense gaps, and genuine skin in the game [17].

### The Narrated World

The binding constraint is mindset, not resources and not geography. Tchakarova identifies three compounding deficits: mindset, capabilities, and political cohesion — the last two downstream of the first. The specific pathology is what she terms the "narrated world": European elites increasingly inhabit a reality manufactured by institutions, consultants, and public relations machinery, in which narration replaces analysis and virtue replaces vision. Crisis-management summits, emergency packages, and beautifully produced Davos speeches are the native output. This is why Europe debates whether the new Cold War even exists while Washington debates how to win it. These are post-heroic societies, demographically aging, psychologically fatigued, and ideologically divided by hubris, moral complacency, and strategic passivity [17].

### If You Cannot Be a Side, Join a Side

The verdict: a middle power that can neither anchor its own bloc nor commit to an existing one has not found a third way — it has only found a slower path to becoming someone else's terrain. The strategy exists: an Anglo-Pacific bloc of networks organized around raw materials, chips, rare earths, critical infrastructure, advanced industry, AI, and countless minilateral defense arrangements, set against the DragonBear. What is missing is not the design — what is missing is the mindset to pay for it in the only currency systemic players accept [17].

## Russia's Last Window: Sino-American Conflict and European Deterrence

[RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) argues that a crisis or major war between the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) and [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) would represent [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html)'s "last window" to fundamentally revise the European order in its favour. However, Russia's best options in a Sino-American war might not be in Europe but rather in ensuring a Chinese victory in the Pacific — after which matters in Europe could be resolved to its satisfaction. The Russian Pacific Fleet has received roughly half of Russia's newest Yasen-class submarines; submarines allocated to the Northern Fleet can be reallocated east via the northern sea route. Russia does not have the firepower of a US or China but has enough key capabilities — submarines and missile launchers — to potentially swing an even balance between them [18].

Within this context, the function of European forces shifts: instead of fighting into the Baltic, the key determinant of events in the Pacific would be whether Russian capabilities most relevant to the Pacific balance could be pinned in European Russia. This requires the capacity to pressure the Russian Northern Fleet in its bastions and deep precision strike capabilities to hit targets deep within Russia, limiting Russia's freedom to reallocate air defence and strike capabilities. Currently, [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html) has relatively little on either count. The global context complicates Russia's assessment: Russian officials treat allied military capabilities as part of an aggregate Western military balance, not independent of the US. [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html)'s acquisition of Tomahawk cruise missiles is discussed in Moscow less as a Japanese capability than as an extension of American power [18].

## NATO-IP4 and the Divergence with US China Policy

[RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) identifies a growing tension between NATO's Asian partnerships (IP4) and the second Trump administration's China policy. China's backing of [Putin]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/putin.html)'s 2022 escalation catalyzed NATO-IP4 mutual attraction, validating security "indivisibility" between the Euro-Atlantic and Indo-Pacific theatres. The Ukraine war created real material links: South Korean defence manufacturers restocking European armouries, North Korean troops fighting in Ukraine with Russian tech transfer in return, lessons flowing to Japan and Taiwan. IP4 is perceived as a network linking the two security environments through industrial cooperation, interoperability, technological innovation and strategic coordination [19].

However, the latest US National Defence Strategy treats Euro-Atlantic/Indo-Pacific separation as desirable. The Trump administration's "constructive strategic stability" détente with China implies not causing offense to Beijing by "ganging up" with Europeans and Asians together. This shift suggests US interests may be diverging from those of NATO's Asian and European partners. Even if Washington loses interest, the IP4 values access to NATO as a platform for accessing lessons from Ukraine. [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html)'s PM Takaichi is seeking to leverage the G7 and NATO to manage supply chain pressure from China, while the US has no interest in the use of these structures to complicate US diplomacy with Beijing. The "Europeanisation" of NATO may further complicate Asian engagement: [France]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/france.html) is wary of constraints on China diplomacy, and Spain is pulling toward Beijing [19].

## Private Capital as Instrument of National Power

Hamlet Yousef ([The Cipher Brief]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-cipher-brief.html)) argues that Cold War 2.0 will be won as much in boardrooms, factories, ports, shipyards, bankruptcy courts, and investment committees as on traditional battlefields. Much of the modern battlefield now sits in the private sector — capital markets, technology, [critical minerals]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/critical-minerals.html), supply chains, ports, telecommunications, manufacturing, energy, intellectual property, and information. [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) understood this years ago: the CCP does not separate commercial activity from national strategy the way the West often does, treating finance, industry, technology, diplomacy, intelligence, media, and military power as parts of the same system. The Belt and Road Initiative combines state-backed financing, ports, telecommunications, mining rights, construction, energy, logistics, commodity access, and political/media influence — capital creating strategic leverage without firing a shot [20].

The Western answer should not be a watered-down version of Chinese state capitalism but the mobilization of what authoritarian systems struggle to replicate: deep, innovative, risk-tolerant capital markets. The next phase is not just building new companies but acquiring, recapitalizing, modernizing, and scaling strategic assets that already exist — mines, processing facilities, ports, defense suppliers, energy infrastructure, semiconductor capacity, logistics networks, telecommunications, and advanced manufacturing, including autonomous shipbuilding capabilities. Private equity firms, distressed investors, activist funds, and turnaround specialists already know how to identify weak assets, acquire companies during periods of dislocation, restructure liabilities, replace poor management, consolidate industries, and create value where others see a mess — the mechanics of corporate raiding and economic warfare are already part of the private-equity world. The model only works if the investments are commercially sound; profit and patriotism do not have to be competing ideas [20].

The historical precedent is the Letter of Marque: Congress once authorized privately financed vessels to capture enemy shipping under a legal framework established by government — the private sector supplied the ships, crews, and capital and accepted the risk; government defined the adversary, set the rules, and provided legal authority. The modern equivalent would be financial, legal, and commercial: tax-advantaged national-security funds, political-risk insurance, export-credit support, allied co-investment vehicles, faster regulatory review for strategic acquisitions, purchasing commitments, and carefully managed threat briefings for vetted investors. Government should set the objective, not run the deal: identify strategic priorities, provide clear demand signals, enforce laws, share appropriate threat information, and create incentives where markets are not properly pricing national-security risk. What it should not do is try to become a venture capitalist, private-equity manager, or operating executive. The fiscal argument reinforces the strategic one: every strategic capability financed primarily by private investors is one less burden placed entirely on the federal balance sheet — deficit reduction through growth, investment, and a larger tax base, not through austerity [20]. See [Cold War 2]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html) for the conflict-level framing and [Latin America]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/latin-america.html) for the proposed first test of the approach.

## National Champions vs Competition: The China AI Parallel

[Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html)'s interview with Lina Khan identifies a structural tension in the US approach to great-power AI competition: the national-champions strategy risks replicating the Boeing failure pattern while ceding the innovation advantage to [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html). Khan's February 2025 *Times* op-ed "Stop Worshiping the American Tech Giants" framed the choice starkly: paradigm-breaking innovations historically come from competitive markets, not from markets where gatekeepers decide who enters. The catalyst was DeepSeek — a Chinese AI firm whose model rivalled American sophistication with much greater efficiency, shocking Silicon Valley and Wall Street. The conflict of interest is structural: the major American cloud providers have their own cloud computing businesses, so they have incentives to favor AI models that are inefficient (consuming more compute) rather than efficient. Allowing the American giants to control these markets risks innovating on trajectories the incumbents determine rather than letting competition drive efficiency [21].

The cautionary tale is directly relevant to the competition with China. The late-1990s merger approval allowing Boeing to buy up its last US rival, McDonnell Douglas, led down a path where Boeing's planes started falling apart and people died — a lack of competition that probably played a role, compounded by a culture shift from engineering to McKinsey-type consulting. The Trump administration's AI policy embodies the same national-champions risk: the CEOs of the major technology companies have made currying favor with the White House a top business priority, and there is a very serious risk of regulatory capture — rules favoring the biggest, most powerful incumbents at the expense of startups and newer firms that may have better ideas. The empirical record on the Schumpeter-Arrow debate shows that monopolies can be good at incremental improvements on existing technologies, but the breakthrough, paradigm-shifting innovations have come from outsiders and competitive markets. The fundamental AI breakthrough came from Google — which did nothing with it until people left and brought it to other places. "Openness and competition have been such a key driver of American innovation and market growth" [21]. The structural risk for the US in the great-power competition is that protecting national champions undermines the very competitive ecosystem that produced the AI lead the strategy depends on. See [Tech Stack Diplomacy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/tech-stack-diplomacy.html) and [AI Military Race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html).

## The $1.5 Trillion Budget Paradox: Strategy-Budget Disconnect

The Trump administration's FY 2027 defense request — roughly $1.5 trillion when combining the base budget, reconciliation package, and supplemental — exemplifies a strategy-budget paradox at the heart of US great power competition posture. Mara Karlin identifies the core contradiction: the Trump national defense strategy prioritizes the Western Hemisphere and narco-terrorists as the primary threat, mentions [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) only in a circumscribed way limited to the first island chain, and signals a desire to downgrade involvement in European affairs. The strategy's language "reads a lot softer than almost any recent defense strategy" [22]. Yet the budget request is at World War spending levels — a mismatch Karlin calls out directly: the number "isn't really merited by the strategy." If one were genuinely prioritizing only the Western Hemisphere and a narrowly scoped China focus while disengaging from most other regions, a quite small defense budget would suffice. The paradox reveals an incoherent great power competition posture: massive resources without strategic direction [22].

Todd Harrison of AEI adds a procedural dimension: the budget was not strategy-driven but budget-driven — an arbitrary 5%-of-GDP target imposed late in the process, with the Defense Department told to fill in programs to reach the number. The result avoids hard tradeoffs, funding fourth-, fifth-, and sixth-generation fighters simultaneously, and includes a $54 billion autonomous warfare line item with almost no programmatic detail. The implication for [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) great power competition is that the spending escalation may reflect a multi-year pre-funding strategy rather than genuine annual readiness — but without a coherent strategic framework, the resources risk being consumed by filler rather than capability that serves competitive priorities [22].

## China's National Total War: Preparing for Great Power Conflict

Wang and Beauchamp-Mustafaga (RAND) detail how [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) conceptualizes great power conflict not as a military-vs-military exchange but as whole-of-system confrontation. China's "national total war" strategy — embedded in Xi Jinping's Strong Military Thought and the military strategic guideline — prepares the PLA for "future great power wars" in which "confrontation unfolds across all domains and dimensions through national total war," according to Academy of Military Science officials [23]. The concept evolved from decades of systems-confrontation thinking, expanded after the COVID pandemic's "people's war" mobilization into a requirement that non-military institutions — media, financial systems, civilian infrastructure — coordinate with the military to deliver wartime effects [23].

The US features centrally as "the strong enemy" in Chinese operational concepts. Researchers in the PLA's *China Military Science* journal describe victory against the United States as requiring "accumulating the advantages of total war" to "fracture the hegemonic system, thereby alleviating pressure on our primary theater of operations" [23]. Civilian systems are weapons: cognitive effects target US social cohesion through "information munitions" embedded in social media platforms, aiming to manipulate perceptions, erode cohesion, support Chinese narratives, and induce surrender. China drew lessons from Ukraine, where it assessed that the US enhanced Ukrainian lethality through non-military tools — sanctions, intelligence declassification, Starlink — and is building analogous capabilities through the 15th Five-Year Plan's priority on strategic capabilities [23].

The operational expression is visible in the "all-domain megacity incapacitation warfare" concept published in *Military Art* for [Taiwan Standoff]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html): integrating blockade, precision strike, and cognitive attack to break Taipei's will to fight without requiring total occupation. But the authors stress the concept is not single-use — it describes specifically how China might execute national total war against the United States, blending kinetic and cognitive effects across civilian and military domains. This is how Beijing plans for great power conflict: not a force-on-force contest but a systems-level assault in which fracturing the adversary's social and institutional cohesion is the primary objective [23].

## The Illusion of Western Primacy

[Aurelien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/aurelien.html) argues the West massively overestimates its capacity to influence how crises evolve. It assumes its norms are superior, its doctrine is the best, and claims responsibility for outcomes it had little influence over. In Washington, the sheer size and complexity of the system — and the relentless personal struggles for control of every issue — mean the rest of the world has only a walk-on part. It is career suicide to suggest that anyone else has agency, especially the locals. This consensual hallucination persists until it stubs its toe on reality [24].

The West has now largely lost the ability to determine how crises will unfold — both at the day-to-day level of controlling the evolution of conflict (the Boyd Cycle) and in the longer term. This does not mean other states have become all-powerful; it means permanent Western primacy can no longer be assumed. Other states — [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) — now have the initiative. There is no sign that the West has realized this, or that its decision-making systems are able to genuinely understand and account for the objectives and capabilities of others. The fixation remains on what Western political systems can accept, as though that were all that mattered [24].

The most likely outcome across both the [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) and [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) crises is a long period of low-level tension, which would suit [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) and Iran respectively but would strain, and probably irreparably fracture, Western cohesion. It will not be the West that decides when it stops [24]. See [Endless Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/endless-warfare.html).

## The Iran War Has Broken US Military Readiness for China

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) assesses that the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) has degraded US military readiness to the point that he now doubts the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) could win even the opening battles of a [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) war. Combined Patriot and THAAD interceptor inventories stand at approximately 1,500; with a two-per-target doctrine, that yields perhaps 1,000 intercepts against a Chinese missile arsenal of roughly 3,500. US bases in Guam and the Marianas could be devastated at the start of a Pacific conflict. The "short war scenario" — in which the US runs out of systems and suffers such high losses it cannot continue — has become plausible. The Iran war, a conflict of choice against a secondary adversary, has consumed the munitions that would be needed for the primary great-power competition, inverting the strategic priority [25]. See [Taiwan Standoff]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) and [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html).

## The Nuclear Dimension of the Escalation Trap

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) identifies a strategic pattern with broad implications for great power competition: [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s underground hardening of its nuclear program at Pickaxe Mountain near Natanz demonstrates how determined regimes defeat airpower not by contesting the skies but by burrowing beneath them. [Israeli]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) intelligence reports thousands of centrifuges moved 300–450 feet beneath solid rock — beyond the reach of conventional bunker-busting munitions. The US and Israeli bombing campaign achieved tactical successes but Iran responded by moving enrichment deeper underground. The strategic problem was not solved; it was transformed [26].

The pattern carries implications beyond [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html). In great power competition, air superiority — the foundational assumption of US power projection — faces an asymmetric counter: deeply hardened underground facilities that no conventional munition can reliably reach. As adversaries observe Iran's successful adaptation, the model is likely to proliferate. [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) have extensive underground military infrastructure; the Pickaxe Mountain precedent demonstrates that the strategic logic of deep hardening applies to any determined regime facing a conventionally superior adversary. The result is a diminishing-returns dynamic: as targets become harder to destroy, the strategic return on airpower declines while pressure for more dangerous escalation options — including ground power or nuclear weapons — grows. This is the Escalation Trap applied to the nuclear dimension: tactical military superiority that deepens strategic entrapment rather than resolving it [26].

Pape's fear-driven escalation framework adds a further dimension. Leaders escalate based on what they believe adversaries are capable of, not what later proves true — the 2003 Iraq WMD precedent. Uncertainty favors escalation. In the great power context, this means that intelligence assessments of adversary underground facilities — whether accurate or not — generate their own coercive momentum toward military action. The structural implication for the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) is that its conventional superiority, however overwhelming, may be progressively neutralized by adversary adaptation, narrowing the gap between tactical dominance and strategic impotence [26]. See [Escalation Theory Critique]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/escalation-theory-critique.html) and [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html).

## S3/S7: DragonBear and Maritime Corridor Legitimacy Competition

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html)'s Day 144 diagnostic identifies a newly normative cross-domain pairing between S3 (US-China and DragonBear) and S7 (Political Order Fragmentation). The S3 domain indicators include Russian military training for [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) — the Kremlin-approved programme transferring Ukraine-war combat lessons to the PLA — and the US-China freedom-of-navigation exchange over the Strait of Hormuz. With both the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) and [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) monetizing the strait (the US through its declared routing regime and toll proposals, Iran through its control of transit), freedom of navigation becomes available for rhetorical capture by the party least committed to enforcing it [27].

The S3→S7 normative pairing is structurally consequential for great power competition. Unlike the S1→S5 generative pairing (which produces new physical disruption vectors) or the S2→S4 fusion (which compresses the food system from both ends), the S3→S7 pairing operates in the normative domain: it is a competition over the legitimacy of maritime order, not over the physical control of corridors. [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) can invoke freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz — positioning itself as the defender of open maritime corridors — while simultaneously conducting CCG patrols east of [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) that challenge freedom of navigation in the Indo-Pacific. The rhetorical capture is available to whichever party is least committed to enforcing the principle universally, because enforcement requires bearing the operational costs that China can avoid while collecting the diplomatic benefits of invocation. This normative competition is a new feature of the great power contest: it is not a dispute over facts on the water but over who controls the language that describes them [27].

## Russia-China-NK Axis Limiting Japan Reengagement

[RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) (James Brown) identifies a structural constraint on [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html)'s ability to serve as a wedge between [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) and [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html): the deepening Russia-China-North Korea military axis. Russia's participation in joint aviation and naval patrols with China — intended as a show of force against Japan — and its sharing of military technology with both China and North Korea make rapprochement with Tokyo strategically unattractive for Moscow even as Japanese advocates of reengagement pursue diplomatic openings. Russia is also contributing to Beijing's campaign to portray Tokyo's security reforms as a dangerous return to militarism. The irony is that Russia's own diplomats have undermined the reengagement track: when Japan proposed resuming foreign-minister-level contacts, Moscow responded with preconditions (lifting sanctions, ending support for Ukraine) that effectively foreclose the opening. A wiser Russian strategy would exploit Japanese divisions to widen gaps between Japan and its European partners; instead, the Russia-China-NK axis is self-reinforcing, narrowing Moscow's diplomatic options while strengthening the Indo-Pacific alignment against it [28]. See [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html).

## China-Russia Military Coordination and the CRINK Architecture

China-Russia military coordination has emerged as an operational challenge below the threshold of war, creating a gap in the alliance planning of the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) and [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html). Aizawa identifies the December 2025 joint strategic bomber flight as a case study in coercive coordination: timed to China's political interests (pressuring Japan over Takaichi's Taiwan statement), with a route extending toward Tokyo, and involving nuclear-capable platforms. The sustained pattern of short-of-war pressure — intensifying horizontally, vertically, and qualitatively — blurs the boundary between deterrence and compellence. The US-Japan alliance is structured to deter overt aggression, but China can now apply military pressure with Russian support without crossing the armed conflict threshold. Aizawa argues that the alliance must develop compellence options and Flexible Deterrent Options that address the full spectrum of coercive pressure, not just the binary of peace vs. war [29].

Ueki challenges the CRINK framework as a basis for alliance strategy. CRINK does not exist as a strategic coalition; China does not treat it as a group, and cooperation with each member remains bilateral. No CRINK military exercise has occurred. China opposed a trilateral exercise with Russia and North Korea proposed by Shoigu. Treating CRINK as a unified coalition risks driving the four countries toward closer coordination — the opposite of the intended effect. Instead, the US and Japan should develop joint strategies for each country individually, based on each country's specific interests and circumstances. Ueki also argues for revisiting the "liberal deterrence" strategy — combining military deterrence with economic engagement — since China still benefits greatly from economic relations with advanced economies. China's competing strategic objectives (CRINK partnerships vs. Global South leadership vs. global power recognition) create opportunities for engagement that a purely militarized deterrence framework would miss. China's exports to the US far outweigh exports to Russia, and China has been actively strengthening relations with non-CRINK countries including ASEAN, Japan, France, the UK, Germany, and Canada — demonstrating that its most consequential relationships remain with the advanced economies it would alienate by fully embracing an "axis of upheaval" [30]. See [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), and [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html).
