*Question: The Russians have been fighting with one hand tied behind their backs for years, because they haven’t been able to attack the enemy supply sources in Western countries. It’s reminiscent of the sinking of the “Lusitania” during World War I. If you want to get into a war, you keep on goading the country you want to attack, until it attacks you. Come to think of it, it’s reminiscent of Pearl Harbor as well.*

Response: I’d argue they don’t have one hand tied behind their back; they are missing a hand. They could choose to attack the supplies coming from Europe, but they don’t. Given Russia’s performance in Ukraine, the military’s ability to mount another attack on a European nation is extremely limited. Why would Russia think it could do better against second country? Why would it divide its already inadequate forces? Poland is a fundamental problem for Russia. The country has developed a substantial, well-armed and well-trained military. An attack in the Baltics, let alone an attack on Poland, would almost certainly trigger a Polish response. Moreover, the U.S. has some 10,000 troops in Poland. This is a problem for Russia. But President Donald Trump’s one great virtue is that no one can predict what he’lldo next. If he chose to send 10,000 troops into the battle, he would also certainly reinforce those troops. I don’t know if he would go through with this, but neither does Putin.

The reason Putin has not expanded the war is that the Russian army has performed poorly, that any attack on any western-oriented country will likely evolve into a Polish response, and it might also include a U.S. response. For over four years, on the other hand, Putin has behaved predictably, failing to defeat Ukraine and trying to convince his nation and the world that he is still a dangerous man leading a powerful country.

*Question: Use of tactical nuclear weapons by Russia was an option threatened by a frustrated Putin early in the Ukraine “Special Operation.” Regarding Russia’s nuclear threat, it’s likely your analysis is the correct conclusion. However, peace in Ukraine for now could be an on-ramp for a greater Russian plan to dismantle NATO in little bites. Divide and conquer. Death by a thousand cuts. Five years from now Russia could be back to a stronger and wiser army with a greatly weakened NATO. Ukraine will still be there for either domination or conquest. Isn’t this a possible outcome?*

Response: I do not think that five years is enough time to reconstitute a failed military. The fundamental failure of the Russian military can be traced to the senior commanders and the strategic and tactical way in which they fought this war. You cannot reconstitute and nurture the senior command and revise the basic doctrine and strategy of an army in so short a time, nor can you develop new doctrine and build a field command trained in the old doctrine with new strategy and tactics. It takes time to retrain an army, and it takes money to buy or develop the hardware that the new doctrine and strategy will require. Russia may well be a great power again someday, but that will take a new senior and field grade officers – and possibly a new political leadership as well.

Importantly, European nations would also have five years under your scenario to unite, build up their military and resist any future Russian threat. European nations have the money and technology to do so if they can agree to unite and work together.

*Question: I am one of those who eagerly devour anything under your byline, and for several reasons, not the least of which are your obvious patriotism and sense of humor and respect for your readers. However, I seem to observe your recent articles to be more analytical of micro and strategic issues than the macro geopolitical matters that are your forte. While I am as sincerely eager to consider your well-informed views of the micro matters, I am curious as to any reasons you care to share about your recent seeming relative shift of interest to them. Are you simply responding to reader questions? Or am I missing your perceptions of how seeming micro matters are influencing macro movements?*

Response: My passion is to understand the broad structure of the geopolitical system. But I have an obligation to my readers to also see the details of that system so that I can determine whether I was right. To do that I have to, at times, pay a great deal of attention to the details and how they are unfolding to confirm my general view, and sometimes to discover that my view was wrong. The principle of my method is that it is possible to anticipate the evolution of the global geopolitical system, and while that necessarily entails a larger macro view, the details certainly matter.

That said, I plan next week to start laying out what I think is the next phase in the broader picture.

*Question: You write “The Declaration of Independence failed in one profound way. It did not include Africans in the family of humans. They could be enslaved and deprived of life, liberty and most certainly the pursuit of happiness.” Isn’t this failure in the Constitution rather than the Declaration? The exclusion of Africans was a political act in its day wrong as many are in hindsight. Political acts compound across generations and haunt us each and every day. If it was an original sin it was but just the beginning. It says “all men” which doesn’t exclude anyone in the whole world.*

Response: The problem at that time was that the world didn’t consider a slave to be a man or a citizen. This was not solely an American issue, so I do not consider it an American sin or “original” to America. It is something that had existed for centuries before America was discovered. The Civil War had to be fought so that the full meaning of the Declaration of Independence could be realized.

The concept of original sin does not mean a sin without precedent. Original sin is a Christian doctrine based on the Book of Genesis, in which Adam and Eve were told by God not to eat from the tree of life. A serpent, some say the devil in disguise, persuaded Eve that she should eat from the tree God had forbade them to eat from, and she persuaded Adam to do so. This was the original sin. It is Christian doctrine that, as a result of this original sin, all who descended from them – that is, everyone – would be born sinful. The sins that humans commit are different, but they all commit sins. When I spoke of original sin of slavery I was not saying it was a new sin, but that it was the sin common to all humans, and this was the original sin of this nation when it was born.

Now I should say that I am a Jew, and Jews do not read Genesis the same way Christians do. I strongly suspect that my father is turning over in his grave at me using this Christian concept. At the same time, my Christian wife is smiling with pleasure in my being caught using a Christian doctrine to make a point. My own sin is trying to be clever. Sorry Dad. And get that expression off your face, dear.
