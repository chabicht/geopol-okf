On the tenth anniversary, Beijing said what it says every year. The award is “illegal and invalid,” “null and void and has no binding force,” and China “neither accepts nor recognises it.”

The line worth reading twice came next. China, the Foreign Ministry said, “does not accept any means of third-party dispute settlement or any solution imposed on China.”

That is not a claim about the 2016 tribunal. It is a claim about a category of institution, and it is the same claim Moscow makes about the International Criminal Court. Whether that shared position amounts to coordination or merely to convergence is a question I will return to, and I want to be careful about it rather than assume the answer.

What the award said, and what it did not do

The 12 July 2016 award, issued by an Annex VII tribunal seated at the Permanent Court of Arbitration, found unanimously against China. No legal basis for historic rights within the nine-dash line. No Spratly feature generates an exclusive economic zone. China violated Philippine sovereign rights and caused severe damage to the reef environment.

The award is final and binding. Ten years on, Mischief Reef is a functioning airbase, Subi and Fiery Cross are hardened installations, and the pressure has migrated to Second Thomas Shoal, Scarborough, Sandy Cay and Reed Bank.

But “the award changed nothing” is a lazy conclusion, and I want to reject it before building on it. The award is the legal predicate for Manila’s assertive transparency campaign, which is the single strategy of the past decade that has demonstrably imposed cost on Chinese behaviour. Publicising water-cannon attacks works precisely because there is an authoritative ruling establishing that the Philippines is being coerced inside its own EEZ rather than merely losing an argument about contested waters. The award is not inert. It is doing work. It is simply not doing the work of enforcement, which is a different thing, and the two get conflated constantly.

The list, and what it does and does not tell us

Fourteen states signed a joint statement on 12 July: the United States, United Kingdom, Japan, Australia, New Zealand, Canada, the Philippines, Germany, Italy, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Romania, Slovenia. The European Union issued a separate text through the High Representative, affirming the award as final and legally binding and demanding full implementation. Japan issued a third in Motegi’s name.

My first instinct on seeing that list was to read the absences as strategic positioning. France, India, South Korea, Vietnam, Indonesia, Malaysia, Poland, the Netherlands, and nineteen other EU member states, all declining to sign. That reading is available, it is the one most commentary will reach for by mid-week, and it is partly wrong. I want to walk through why, because the corrected version is more interesting than the first draft.

First caveat: absences are not always decisions. Joint statements of this kind are drafted in one capital and circulated on a clock. States drop off for reasons that have nothing to do with strategy: clearance not obtained in time, caretaker governments, a foreign ministry that simply did not prioritise it. Ireland’s absence and Poland’s absence do not carry the same information, and treating every gap in the list as a signal is a way of manufacturing findings. Only absences corroborated by an independent pattern of behaviour should bear analytical weight.

Second caveat, and it is fatal to the naive reading. On the same weekend, in Manila, a forum titled “A Decade of the Arbitral Award: Credible Deterrence in Defense of the West Philippine Sea” convened envoys from Australia, Canada, the European Union, France, Germany, India, Japan, South Korea, New Zealand and the United Kingdom, who collectively signalled intent to expand defence and technical cooperation with the Philippines. The French Ambassador, Marie Fontanel, put it about as bluntly as a diplomat can: legal principles alone are not enough, and when rules are challenged, words must be backed by practical cooperation.

So France, India and South Korea were not absent from the anniversary. They were in Manila, on the record, talking about deterrence. They declined one document and turned up to another.

That is the finding. It is not that the coalition is thin. It is that the coalition is real and is refusing a particular vehicle.

The vehicle, not the position

Once you see it this way, the pattern reorganises itself.

The fourteen signatories are, with almost no exception, United States treaty allies or states actively seeking deeper American security commitment. The document is drafted in Washington, released by the State Department, and reads as an alignment instrument, because that is what it is.

The states that declined it are not declining the law. India named the 2016 award explicitly in a joint statement with the Philippines in 2023, a hardening of a position that had been studiously vague in 2016. France sails freedom of navigation operations, holds the second-largest exclusive economic zone in the world on the strength of its Indo-Pacific territories, and sent its ambassador to argue for credible deterrence. South Korea showed up alongside them. The European Union, which contains all of the nineteen EU states that did not sign, issued its own text calling for full implementation.

What these states are declining is co-signature in an American column.

For France the reason is doctrinal and long-standing: strategic autonomy requires that French Indo-Pacific presence be legible as French rather than as an auxiliary function of United States containment policy. Paris will act, and will act with the Philippines, and will not do so under a Washington letterhead. The EU statement is the instrument that lets it say the same thing in a European voice.

I should be honest that a plainer explanation competes with mine. Most EU states route Indo-Pacific legal positions through Brussels as a matter of routine, and on that reading the seven EU members who also signed the American text are the anomaly requiring explanation, not the nineteen who did not. I think the French case survives this, because France is the EU state with the most independent capacity to act and the most articulated doctrine about not being subsumed. But it is an interpretation, not a fact, and readers should hold it loosely.

For India and South Korea the logic is different again, and closer to hedging in the ordinary sense: both have substantial exposure to China, both are deepening security ties with Manila, and neither will accept the reputational cost of formal enrolment in an American-led legal bloc when the same substantive position can be advanced bilaterally at lower price.

Where the argument does hold: ASEAN

The Southeast Asian column of the joint statement reads: the Philippines, and nobody else.

Not one of the other ten ASEAN members signed, in the year the Philippines holds the chair. Not Vietnam, which has the second-strongest claim in the Spratlys and the longest record of Chinese coercion. Not Indonesia, whose Natuna EEZ is transected by the nine-dash line. Not Malaysia, which has a standing China Coast Guard presence at Luconia Shoals. Not Brunei, also a claimant.

Here the absence is corroborated by a decade of consistent behaviour, which is what makes it evidence rather than noise. ASEAN has never officially recognised the 2016 award. At the ministerial in Vientiane two weeks after the ruling, every proposed reference was vetoed without explanation or counter-proposal. Consensus rules mean that Phnom Penh and Vientiane hold what is, functionally, a Chinese veto over the bloc’s language.

And Vietnam is the sharpest case, because Vietnam is not silent. Hanoi files notes verbales, protests bilaterally, and has invoked the award’s reasoning without naming it. What Hanoi will not do is appear in a collective, American-branded alignment column, because the four noes forbid it and because Vietnam has to live with the consequences on a shared land border and a shared sea.

The distinction matters and I got it wrong in my first pass. The claim “middle powers will not defend the law” is false. The claim “middle powers will not defend the law inside an American-led coalition instrument” is true, and it is the more consequential finding.

The France-Vietnam symmetry

This is the piece I most want to keep, and the corrected evidence strengthens rather than weakens it.

France and Vietnam both declined the joint statement. Both are middle powers with real capability and real stakes. Both refuse to function as instruments of a larger power’s contest.

France declines from surplus. It has the territory, the platforms and the doctrine to act on its own account, it wants the credit for doing so in its own name, and its ambassador spent the anniversary weekend in Manila arguing for deterrence. Declining the American text costs France nothing and buys it the thing it values most, which is the appearance of independent agency.

Vietnam declines from deficit. It cannot act alone, it cannot absorb retaliation, and its calculation is about the morning after. Declining the American text costs Vietnam the chance to consolidate a legal position it badly needs, and buys it the only thing it can afford, which is deniability.

Identical behaviour. Opposite causal structure. The autonomous hedge and the coerced hedge, visible in the same document on the same day.

This distinction predicts different futures. The autonomous hedger will act unilaterally whenever its own interests are engaged and will never be reliable as a coalition member, in either direction. The coerced hedger will align only when the coercion is lifted, which means its alignment is a lagging indicator of the local balance of power rather than an expression of preference. Most of the hedging literature treats these as one phenomenon. They generate forecasts that are wrong in opposite directions.

What this says about polarisation, and who is doing the polarising

The mechanism is that once a legal position acquires a bloc identity, states that need neutrality between the poles are forced into neutrality toward the norm — or, as here, into expressing the norm through channels that do not read as enrolment.

I have been arguing for some time that this is how Cold War 2.0 corrodes international law: not by breaking the rules but by polarising them. I still think that is right. But I want to attribute the polarisation honestly, because the tidy version of this argument blames Beijing alone and the tidy version is one-sided.

A fourteen-nation statement led by Washington and populated overwhelmingly by treaty allies is a bloc instrument. States reading it as such are reading it correctly. The polarisation is co-produced. Beijing works to make defending the award look like taking a side; Washington’s drafting practice does a good deal of that work for it. That France, India, South Korea and Vietnam all found the same workaround — endorse the substance, refuse the vehicle — is a comment on the vehicle as much as on Chinese pressure.

The DragonBear caveat

I should be disciplined here rather than reflexive.

There is no visible bear in this episode. Russia is not a party, is not named in any of the texts, took no observable action tied to the anniversary, and has no maritime stake in the Spratlys. My framework argues that Chinese and Russian rejection of adjudicatory institutions is a coordinated project rather than a coincidence, and I continue to think the evidence for that is strong in the aggregate — the joint naval exercises in these waters, the parallel positions on the ICC and on sanctions, the shared insistence that great-power core interests are non-justiciable.

But the aggregate case is not this case. Reading DragonBear into an episode where one of the two is entirely absent is the exact move that critics of the framework accuse it of, and they would be right to accuse it here. What the anniversary demonstrates is the Chinese half of a pattern I believe is joint. That is worth saying, and it is not the same as demonstrating the pattern.

On the water, and on the calendar

None of this is unfolding against a backdrop of Chinese restraint.

In March 2026 the China Coast Guard declared “clearing operations” at Scarborough Reef, broadcasting coordinates by radio for a timed exclusion zone and deploying at least six coast guard vessels, fifteen maritime militia vessels and a PLAN warship to drive off Philippine fishing craft. Scarborough sits roughly 120 nautical miles from Luzon, inside the Philippine EEZ, as the award confirmed explicitly.

In May, after Defence Secretary Gilberto Teodoro named China as ASEAN’s principal challenge at the Shangri-La Dialogue, Beijing sanctioned him personally with an entry ban. A sitting defence minister of an ASEAN state, barred for something said at a conference in Singapore.

Fourteen states signed a statement. None of them will escort a resupply mission to Second Thomas Shoal. That gap, between the declaratory coalition and the operational one, is where Beijing works, and the Manila forum’s language about “credible deterrence” is an attempt to close it that has not yet been tested.

A correction to the calendar, and to my own earlier framing. I had treated the ASEAN Ministerial Meeting on 21 July as the decisive test. It is a test, but it is not the one that matters most. Marcos is pinning the Code of Conduct to the ASEAN Summit in Manila in November. That is the real deadline, and the COC is the real prize.

What to watch, and what would prove me wrong

The November summit, not July. Watch specifically for any COC provision requiring joint development with China to the exclusion of external parties. That would render the award moot by contract rather than by defiance. No rejection would be needed, only a signature.

Whether the Manila forum produces anything. France, India and South Korea talked about practical cooperation and technical assistance. If that materialises as capability transfer to the Philippine Coast Guard, my “declaratory coalition” framing weakens considerably and the vehicle-refusal reading is vindicated. If it evaporates, the refusal was cost avoidance dressed as doctrine.

Whether the Teodoro sanction is repeated. One instance is an incident. A second, against an official of another ASEAN state, establishes individual coercion of Southeast Asian decision-makers as a standing tool.

And the falsifier for my ASEAN claim. I have argued that ASEAN cannot produce award language under any chairship. If the 21 July communiqué contains indirect language — “full respect for legal and diplomatic processes undertaken in accordance with UNCLOS,” or similar — then a Philippine chairship extracted something a decade of chairships could not, and my characterisation of ASEAN as structurally non-agentic on this file takes real damage. I will say so if it happens.

Ten years

The award’s power is not the power to compel. It never was. Its power is the power to force states to declare themselves, and in a bifurcating system, that is a power most states would rather not be subject to.

What the tenth anniversary reveals is not that the law has no defenders. It has more than I initially credited, including in Manila that weekend. What it reveals is that defending the law and joining the bloc have become difficult to distinguish, and that a growing number of capable states — France from strength, Vietnam from weakness, India and Seoul from calculation — are now spending real diplomatic effort on the problem of how to do the first without being seen to do the second.

That problem did not exist in 2016. Its existence is the story.
