Over the past few days, U.S. forces said they struck more than 300 Iranian targets, including missile and drone systems, coastal surveillance, naval targets and supporting infrastructure. The reinstated U.S. blockade in the Strait of Hormuz, meanwhile, seeks to restrict traffic entering or leaving Iranian ports while formally permitting neutral passage through the strait. Iran, for its part, has struck U.S. positions and selected commercial vessels but has not inflicted damage on a comparable scale, nor has it expanded its campaign against the Gulf’s principal energy infrastructure.

In other words, the renewed fighting has become an unequal but ultimately inconclusive contest. Destroying Iranian capabilities is not the same as compelling Tehran to accept U.S. terms. Washington can weaken Tehran’s ability to threaten shipping and prevent it from exercising control over the Strait of Hormuz. It cannot, however, cheaply guarantee normal commercial passage or force Iran to surrender its remaining leverage without accepting a prolonged campaign. If Iran can’t withstand the campaign indefinitely, it can preserve enough uncertainty to deny the U.S. a clean political victory.

Washington has larger goals in mind than simply defeating Iranian naval forces. It wants to deprive Iran of its ability to exercise de facto authority over an international waterway. This means keeping allied and neutral commerce moving, preserving its role as the Gulf’s principal security guarantor and denying the tolls imposed by Iran.

To be sure, the military balance strongly favors the United States. American forces can identify and destroy Iranian maritime and coastal systems faster than Tehran can replace or employ them. They can also escort selected vessels and strike targets far beyond the immediate coastline. Yet Iran’s remaining systems are dispersed across a large territory, and commercial confidence depends not on how many Iranian assets are destroyed but on whether another vessel might still be hit.

Achieving all of Washington’s objectives would require a sustained campaign to suppress Iranian threats, protect commercial traffic and defend regional bases. U.S. officials have said the strikes are meant to shape operations that could prepare the battlefield for more complex missions if ordered. Washington can sustain such a campaign, but its costs would rise with time.

Iran’s immediate objective is narrower than Washington’s – and therefore easier. It does not need to defeat the U.S. Navy or close the Strait of Hormuz completely. It needs only to preserve enough capacity to strike occasional vessels, raise insurance and operating costs, and demonstrate that U.S. protection isn’t fully guaranteed. Washington must provide security continuously; Iran needs only intermittent success. A single attack can restore uncertainty after numerous safe transits and keep commercial traffic below normal levels.

But Iran’s margin for victory is shrinking. U.S. strikes are degrading the military systems needed to sustain disruption, while the blockade is constraining seaborne oil exports that provide Iran with critical foreign currency and state revenue. Overland routes through the North-South corridor can only partially offset these losses.

As the margin narrows, limited attacks become less effective because they impose lower costs on Washington than Tehran is absorbing in return. Iran’s clearest means of changing the calculation would be to attack Gulf energy infrastructure or expand maritime disruption to the Bab el-Mandeb strait. Either option could increase oil, shipping and insurance costs while forcing the U.S. to commit more interceptors, surveillance assets and naval forces to protecting the wider region.

Escalation would also carry much greater risks for Iran. Attacks on Saudi, Emirati or Qatari energy facilities could end the Gulf states’ role as mediators, deepen their military cooperation with Washington, bring Israel back into the campaign and justify broader strikes on Iranian territory. Iran’s strongest remaining escalation option, in other words, is also the one most likely to unite its adversaries. Iran must choose between accepting a pause while it still has leverage or escalating in a way that could turn a limited maritime contest into a broader threat to the regime.

Put simply, Iran is on the clock. Reduced exports, military attrition and damage to transportation networks are steadily eroding its ability to sustain the conflict. Missile and drone inventories are finite, while strikes on launchers, command networks and production facilities limit Tehran’s ability to employ and replace them. Iran must preserve enough of its remaining arsenal to deter a broader war or escalate if limited disruption no longer produces leverage. (The U.S. strike on the Aq Taqeh Khan railway bridge in Aqqala County, located on a corridor linking Iran to Central Asia and onward to China and Russia, demonstrated that pressure would not remain confined to the waterways and coastlines.) These losses are also accumulating amid uncertainty over Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, whose prolonged absence from public view has weakened the regime’s ability to project clear authority.

Yet time isn’t exactly on Washington’s side either. The U.S. can absorb the costs of the campaign far longer than Iran, but a prolonged operation would place growing demands on forces and munitions already committed across several regions. Further Iranian escalation would require the U.S. to defend not only its personnel and bases but also ports, shipping routes and energy infrastructure throughout the Gulf region. That would consume air and missile defense interceptors, surveillance capacity and naval assets, and it would cost a lot of money. If commercial traffic remains disrupted, the economic costs would compound the military burden. Because the campaign also depends on continued Gulf access and cooperation, prolonged resource expenditure without restored passage or Iranian concessions would become increasingly difficult to justify. The approaching U.S. midterm elections would sharpen this pressure, but the larger constraint is the cost of sustaining regional protection without a clear political result.

The two sides are therefore pursuing different forms of exhaustion. Iran is trying to outlast Washington politically even as military losses, economic pressure and leadership uncertainty weaken its own position. The U.S. is trying to force Tehran to pause before the costs of regional defense exceed the political value of the campaign.

If neither side decides to escalate, Iran is more likely to seek a ceasefire before the U.S. abandons its core objectives (even if Tehran briefly intensifies its attacks to improve its bargaining position). Iran’s release of a U.S. citizen amid the renewed exchanges suggests Tehran already has de-escalation in mind.

Washington has greater room to adjust the practical terms of the confrontation. It could ease blockade enforcement, allow limited Iranian exports or modify transit and escort procedures without abandoning its core demand that Iran stop threatening commercial passage.

A pause would likely involve Iran scaling back attacks enough for commercial traffic to recover while preserving its formal claims over Hormuz and presenting the outcome as proof that resistance forced negotiations. Washington would likewise portray any reduction in attacks as evidence that military pressure worked.

The result would be a truce rather than a settlement. Neither side would have resolved the underlying dispute, and both would retain incentives to test the balance sometime in the future. Washington can keep winning the strikes, and Iran can keep denying Washington a clean victory, but neither can sustain that contradiction indefinitely.
