Elbridge Colby, speaking from the Department of War, has done the middle power conversation a favor by taking it seriously enough to attack it. His argument is blunt. A collective middle powers strategy, in his telling, is not a genuine threat to American primacy but a distraction that a handful of allies and partners may mistake for one, squandering time, money, and political capital in the process. He frames himself and his colleagues as flexible realists who read the international scene through interest, geography, economics, and military power, and he insists that middle powers share no coherent basis for alignment.

The rest of the thread is a demand-signal argument dressed as reassurance. Colby claims an upsurge, not a reduction, in the desire for engagement with the United States. Under Trump, he says, countries can no longer take American engagement for granted, and that scarcity has sharpened rather than dulled their appetite. He anchors the claim in the defense industrial base. No alternative country or coalition can match the American DIB in quantity or quality, access to it is a privilege rather than a right, and there is no credible free world alternative to American technology. His closing move is the telling one. Allies should spend more and invest in their own defense industrial bases, but collaboratively with America rather than in a vain attempt to replicate or supplant it.

Read carefully, this is not the language of a superpower indifferent to the project. It is the language of one that has noticed.

The Reactions: Five Fault Lines

The responses that followed sorted themselves into recognizable camps, and each exposes a different weakness in either Colby’s position or the middle power thesis itself.

The first camp went straight for the contradiction between conduct and complaint. Phil Gordon captured it most sharply, observing that it is extraordinary to treat allies like vassals, threaten their territorial integrity, impose unilateral tariffs, question security commitments, and divert contracted arms deliveries, and then express surprise when those allies seek autonomy or alternative partners. Gideon Rachman pressed the same point as a question directed back at Colby. Yes, a middle power strategy is difficult and sub-optimal, but when Washington threatens the territory and sovereignty of its own allies, what exactly does it expect them to do. Gerard Araud supplied the missing basis for alignment that Colby denied existed. Middle powers cooperate not out of virtue but out of interest, specifically their shared interest in not being crushed between the Chinese rock and the American hard place, and their common stake in a rules based order that keeps them from that fate.

The second camp read the thread as evidence rather than argument. Wolfgang Ischinger turned Colby’s own words against him. The very need to say all this publicly signals a dawning recognition in Washington that many allies have lost trust in American reliability, and he asked what would remain of the United States as a superpower without its global web of partners and allies. Sreemoy Talukdar reinforced the point with the Pentagon’s own reported anxiety that Trump’s erratic conduct may have permanently damaged transatlantic relations, citing polling in which only one in ten Europeans still regard the United States as an ally, and invoking the Zakaria observation that a Europe spending more on defense becomes less dependent, less deferential, and a smaller customer for American arms.

The third camp refused the binary altogether. Shashank Joshi made the cleanest analytical point in the exchange. It can be simultaneously true that middle power allies want more American engagement and that they want to hedge against the genuine risk of abandonment, and the signs of that limited hedging are visible in many places at once. This is the observation Colby’s framing cannot absorb, because his thread treats engagement and hedging as mutually exclusive when they are in fact concurrent.

The fourth camp attacked the hard power premise directly. Alexander Clarkson noted the awkward fact that American shipbuilding is in such crisis that the US Navy is ordering from Korean and Italian yards while American logistics depend on bases hosted by allies, and he characterized Colby as typical of an elite so marinated in the mythology of its own power that it neglected to sustain the structures that power actually requires. Ragip Soylu added the concrete tell, that the administration appears troubled by the Canada-led defense bank, which is precisely the kind of institution-building that would matter.

The fifth camp built the affirmative case for the project. Ben Woodfinden, writing for a Canadian audience, read the thread as both a swipe at Carney and the most useful thing Washington has said about the middle power project Carney now de facto leads. His honesty is worth preserving. Colby is telling hard truths that middle power advocates should keep top of mind rather than wave away because they dislike the source. The hard power gap is real and enormous, and convening is easy while execution is the hard part, meaning shipyards that actually launch submarines, air defense batteries that exist outside a procurement announcement, and magazines refilled after half their contents went to Ukraine. That is years of unglamorous work with no ribbon-cutting, and it is the only thing that converts a coalition of the anxious into something a great power must reckon with. Surya Kanegaonkar built the most systematic counter-case, arguing that constrained middle powers will seek trusted partners of similar size and low threat perception, and that a consortium sharing an interest in avoiding weaponizable dependencies, building sanctions-proof trade and payment networks, deepening technology and defense ties, and playing to individual strengths can extract value from a ruptured system. He placed India at the center of an emerging tripolar order, argued that tripolarity is more stable than bipolarity because it widens optionality, and read Colby’s dismissiveness through the Sun Tzu axiom of appearing weak when strong and strong when weak.

My Geopolitical Judgement

Let me offer my two geopolitical cents against this background of opposed assessments.

The single country that genuinely possesses strategic autonomy as a middle power right now is India. Everything else in this debate is aspiration mislabeled as capability. To expect that European powers like France or Germany, or Canada, will develop a functioning middle power strategy on any near horizon is not analysis but wishful projection, and it creates dangerous expectations in citizens who deserve to be told the truth. It will take European and other middle powers decades, not years, to reach anything resembling strategic autonomy.

Is it feasible. Yes, but only through what I call civilizational effort, meaning a significant sacrifice of the social welfare system, hard-core reindustrialization, the brutal filling of defense gaps, and genuine skin in the game from citizens, industries, companies and governments together. Is it realistic? No, not with the current mindset. And mindset, not resources and not geography, is the binding constraint. For readers encountering my work for the first time, this is worth pausing on, because it is the analytical core of everything I argue about Europe and the middle powers.

When I say mindset, I do not mean attitude or morale in the casual sense. I mean strategic culture, the way a society perceives itself and its position in the world before it makes a single policy choice. My argument, developed across my writing on European grand strategy, is that Europe and most aspiring middle powers suffer from three compounding deficits. The first is mindset. The second is capabilities. The third is political cohesion. The last two are downstream of the first. A society that cannot see the contest clearly will never fund the capabilities or forge the cohesion the contest demands.

The specific pathology is what I term the narrated world. European elites increasingly inhabit a reality manufactured by institutions, consultants, and public relations machinery, in which narration replaces analysis and virtue replaces vision. Crisis-management summits, emergency packages, and beautifully produced Davos speeches are the native output of this world. They feel like action while changing nothing structural. This is why Europe debates whether the new Cold War even exists while Washington debates how to win it, and it is why the European Union has mastered the language of risk mitigation while forgetting the grammar of hard power. These are post-heroic societies, demographically aging, psychologically fatigued, and ideologically divided by hubris, moral complacency, and strategic passivity. That is the mindset problem in one sentence. It is a self-perception problem before it is a spending problem.

This is also why I insist our problems are self-inflicted. They were not imposed by China, by Russia, or even by Trump. They were constructed from within, out of pure ideology and a vague moral compass that mistook the absence of visible threat for the absence of real threat. Decades of outsourcing existential functions, energy to Russia, security to the United States, manufacturing and increasingly technology to Asia, produced a structural habituation to dependency that no procurement announcement can quickly undo. But because these problems were constructed by choice, they are in principle reversible. That is the one genuinely hopeful thing I can say, and it is the whole reason the argument matters. Reversal is available. It simply begins with mindset and with getting back in touch with reality, not with another summit.

Here is the strategic reality the convening class prefers to avoid. We are in a new Cold War between two systems, Pax Americana 2.0 and the DragonBear, the structural alignment of Chinese and Russian power across every systemically relevant domain. In that contest, middle powers without sufficient strategic leverage or autonomy will not be neutral conveners. They will be the first victims, hollowed out or cannibalized for the sake of global supremacy, or they will become the battlefield itself. There is no comfortable third space for the underprepared. And the uncomfortable truth is that many actors, friends and foes alike, are so upset by the very prospect of Pax Americana 2.0 that they would tacitly prefer a totalitarian global system governed by the Marxism-Leninism of Pax Sinica. The hubris runs in every direction, Carney’s included.

The Only Question That Matters

So let me end not with a policy prescription but with the question that cuts through all of it. Is Carney, or any other prospective leader of a future middle power camp, actually ready to take the place of the United States or China as a systemic player in the interconnected proxy wars that define this era? Not to convene a conversation about them. To stand inside them. Ukraine. Iran and the Strait of Hormuz. The Indo-Pacific flashpoints. These are the theaters where systemic players are tested, and they are tested in blood and industrial capacity, not in communiqués.

Because ultimately this was never about a graceful speech in Davos or a beautifully rendered PowerPoint on middle power convergence. It is about real skin in the game. That means industries retooled and defense sectors rearmed, yes, but it also means human lives placed at stake, soldiers and populations committed to outcomes a systemic player is willing to enforce. That is the price of the seat. The convening class wants the status of a great power without the exposure of one, and that combination does not exist. It never has.

The verdict, then, is clear and it is brutal in its simplicity. If you cannot be a side, join a side. A middle power that can neither anchor its own bloc nor commit to an existing one has not found a third way. It has only found a slower path to becoming someone else’s terrain. Colby is right that shared nervousness is not a strategy. He is wrong that no strategy exists. The strategy exists, an Anglo-Pacific bloc of networks organized around raw materials, chips, rare earths, critical infrastructure, advanced industry, AI, and countless minilateral defense arrangements, set against the DragonBear. What is missing is not the design. What is missing is the mindset to pay for it in the only currency systemic players accept.
