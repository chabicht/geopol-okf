Each NATO summit has its hallmarks. Beyond the friendly white Ankara cats roaming the Beştepe Presidential Complex, the NATO summit in Ankara will be remembered as the summit where a different, more European-led NATO took shape, with Ukraine firmly anchored at its side.

To be sure, there were echoes of the infamous 2018 NATO summit in Brussels at which President Trump chided underperforming allies and threatened to withdraw the United States from NATO if they didn’t step up. This time, the gripes were directed at Spain for its low defence spending, Denmark for its refusal to cede Greenland to the United States, and other major allies for their refusal to join the US military campaign in Iran.

Yet ultimately, the Ankara summit delivered. The [Summit Declaration](https://www.nato.int/en/about-us/official-texts-and-resources/official-texts/2026/07/08/the-ankara-summit-declaration), a pithy six paragraphs, provided a strong statement of solidarity on both “the long-term threat Russia poses to Euro-Atlantic security and stability” and the “need for Iran to fully respect freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz”. European NATO allies and Canada demonstrated progress in reducing dependence on the United States.

Announcements included more than $90 billion in [additional defence expenditure](https://www.nato.int/en/what-we-do/introduction-to-nato/defence-expenditures-and-natos-5-commitment) in 2025, new pledges to largely fill ‌the [gaps left by the United States](https://www.defensenews.com/global/europe/2026/07/03/europeans-to-fill-almost-all-gaps-left-by-us-in-nato-defense-plans-source-says/) in NATO’s defence plans, and the announcement of [over $50 billion in procurements](https://defence24.com/defence-policy/nato-summit-in-ankara-defence-deals-worth-50-billion) in critical areas such as integrated air and missile defence, deep precision strike, and high-altitude reconnaissance drones.

There was also good news on Ukraine. Allies pledged €70 billion in military assistance, equipment, and training for Ukraine for 2026, and – in a surprising yet welcome twist – President Trump promised to grant Ukraine a licence to produce the Patriot air defence interceptor missiles, which have been invaluable in defending Ukraine from Russian ballistic missile attacks.

Given this progress and demonstrable shift toward a more European NATO, how can allies sustain this momentum, and where do they go from here? Three lessons are instructive and should inform allies’ future behaviours.

First, the summit underscored the importance of focusing on delivering results as opposed to ‘fighting the scenario’. The old NATO, in which the United States led and provided the bulk of its military capabilities, is not returning. European allies and Canada have the means to do more, and the United States is itself increasingly overstretched militarily and economically.

A more balanced alliance will be a more resilient one, in which Europeans have greater agency and capability and are thus a better and more valuable partner to the US. While some allies will continue to use Trump’s belligerence as an excuse for their own inaction, the smarter ones will take their cue from NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte and model allies such as the Nordics, Baltics, and Germany, by adopting a pragmatic, results-focused approach to delivering a more European NATO.

Second, the summit confirmed Ukraine’s centrality to a credible European defence. The Summit Declaration’s statement that “Ukraine contributes to transatlantic security” elevates its relationship with the alliance and formally recognises Ukraine’s role as not just a partner but as a security contributor to NATO.

This acknowledgement is validated by several promising defence cooperation initiatives announced at the summit, including [Project Freya](https://kyivindependent.com/anti-ballistic-coalition-to-meet-in-france-to-support-ukraines-homegrown-freya-air-defense-project/), which will develop an anti-ballistic missile system drawing on Ukrainian rockets coupled with German Hensoldt radars and Norwegian command and control systems; contracts for drone coproduction with European and Gulf nations; and integration of Ukraine’s FP5 Flamingo missile system with European industry.

Going forward, Ukraine should influence everything from how NATO fights and manoeuvres on the battlefield to how it integrates emerging technologies with conventional legacy platforms. Through creative coproduction and co-development arrangements, Ukrainian industry can help Europe produce and innovate at scale – and at a lower cost.

Third, the Ankara summit recognised the role of the private sector in the delivery of a more capable, more European NATO. The dedicated NATO summit Defence Industry Forum (NSDIF26), the accompanying [Strategy for NATO-Industry Cooperation](https://www.nato.int/en/about-us/official-texts-and-resources/official-texts/2026/07/08/strategy-for-industry-nato-cooperation), and the decision to share NATO’s aggregate defence shortfalls and capability targets directly with defence industry all signal that industry cannot be an afterthought – it needs a seat at the table.

In the near term, this gives industry the long-term certainty and visibility it needs to scale up production and invest. Over time, trust will build among these public-private partners, allowing them to address jointly many of the unnecessary frictions in the Defence Acquisition Lifecycle, such as overly restrictive export controls, intellectual property barriers, and environmental regulations.

Financing mechanisms such as the [Multilateral Defence Mechanism](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/joint-statement-from-member-countries-of-the-multilateral-defence-mechanism) announced by Finland, Poland, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom and the [Defence Security and Resilience Bank spearheaded](https://www.reuters.com/world/americas/nine-countries-commit-to-global-defence-bank-says-canada-2026-07-07/) by Canada and Luxembourg provide additional assurance to investors that European defence investment will not be subject only to the whims of changing national governments or competing budgetary priorities.

Far from a disaster, the Ankara summit showed promising signs that European allies and Canada have finally internalised both the threat to transatlantic security and the need for more defence spending and a more European-led NATO.

A delivery-focused approach that leverages the combined strengths of Ukraine and the private sector will help them get there.

[*Rachel Ellehuus*](https://www.rusi.org/people/ellehuus) *is Director-General of the Royal United Services Institute (RUSI), London.*
