*China–Russia military cooperation is not a series of episodes. It is a coordination architecture or what I was defining as a modus operandi of strategic coordination in all systemically relevant domains since 2015. July 2026 shows us both its reach and its one hard boundary.*

There is a persistent analytical error in Western coverage of the China–Russia relationship. Each joint exercise, each arms transfer, each summit communiqué is treated as a discrete event, catalogued and then filed away. The question that follows is almost always the wrong one: are they allies yet? Is there a treaty? Would Beijing fight for Moscow, or Moscow for Beijing?

This framing misreads what is actually being built. What I have long termed the DragonBear does not describe an alliance. It describes a strategic coordination across all systemically relevant domains: maritime, informational, technological, strategic, and increasingly experiential. The partnership does not need the formal architecture of alliance to reshape the balance of power. July 2026 offers a concentrated illustration of why.

**The maritime layer: interoperability under construction**

Joint Sea-2026 is underway in the Yellow Sea off Qingdao, running from 6 to 13 July. Russia deployed the guided-missile cruiser Varyag, the corvette Rezkiy, the diesel-electric submarine Ufa and the rescue ship Igor Belousov from its Pacific Fleet. China fielded two destroyers (Anshan and Kaifeng), the frigate Wuhu, a Yuan-class submarine, a supply ship and a rescue vessel.

The composition matters less than the content. The two navies established a joint command and conducted anti-submarine warfare, submarine rescue, air and missile defence, and surface-strike exercises. This is not symbolic choreography for domestic audiences. It is interoperability under construction: the slow, deliberate accretion of a capacity to operate together under a shared command structure. Notably, Beijing is using the drills to draw on Russian submarine-rescue expertise, an operational competence China is still building as undersea competition intensifies.

The exercise closes with a joint patrol somewhere in the Pacific. These patrols are not new. They run back to 2021, when Chinese and Russian warships completed their first joint circumnavigation of Japan, and they have recurred often enough to constitute a pattern rather than a provocation. That is precisely the strategic function. Repetition normalises presence, and presence reconfigures the operational map. Each patrol lowers the threshold of what counts as routine, and every routine expands the space in which the two navies can act without triggering alarm.

**The experiential layer: the transfer of combat knowledge**

If the naval drills are the visible surface, the deeper development is quieter and more consequential. Reuters has reported a Kremlin-approved programme transferring Russia’s Ukraine-war combat lessons to the Chinese military. The underpinning agreement was reportedly signed on 2 July by a Russian major general and a Chinese senior colonel. The subject matter is precisely what one would expect from more than four years of high-intensity war: drone warfare, anti-ship missile exposure, the tactical realities of a contested modern battlefield.

Read the asymmetry with care, because it is the analytical heart of the matter. Russia holds combat experience, accumulated at enormous cost across more than four years in Ukraine. China holds industrial scale, technological weight, advanced equipment, simulators and instructor expertise, but has not fought a war in decades. Internal Russian assessments reviewed by Reuters reportedly praised the caliber of Chinese training infrastructure while noting Beijing’s lack of actual combat experience. Each partner supplies exactly what the other lacks.

This is the DragonBear logic in its purest form. Not an alliance, but a division of labour. And the direction of the knowledge transfer carries a strategic sting. A Russia degraded by war, increasingly dependent on Beijing economically and industrially, becomes the weaker partner in the relationship. Yet in becoming weaker it transfers its hard-won expertise upward, to a rising China that can absorb and industrialise those lessons at scale. Some regional analysts read this directly against a possible Taiwan contingency, where drone and missile threats would feature prominently. The West may therefore emerge from Russia’s war facing a more capable and more assertive PLA than it faced before.

**The strategic and technological layers**

The May Putin–Xi summit in Beijing added the political scaffolding. The two leaders announced roughly 40 new agreements, including a declaration on the formation of a multipolar world, and jointly denounced US plans for the Golden Dome missile defence system as a threat to strategic stability. Xi hailed an “unyielding” partnership; Putin described relations as having reached an “unprecedentedly high level.”

Beneath the rhetoric, the substantive cooperation continues in the domains that rarely make headlines: space, where the International Lunar Research Station framework and BeiDou–GLONASS navigation compatibility efforts advance a non-US-led institutional route, and military-technical cooperation more broadly. When these layers are placed alongside the maritime and experiential dimensions, the coordination becomes legible across every systemically relevant domain simultaneously. That simultaneity is the signature of the DragonBear. It is not any single act but the synchronisation across acts that produces strategic effect.

A necessary caution, because precision is the whole discipline. There is no mutual-defence treaty. Much of the summit-level language functions as deterrent signalling, information and psychological warfare aimed at Washington rather than binding commitment. Analysts are right to distinguish a genuine alliance from the picture-perfect imagery of authoritarian partnership. But the correct conclusion is not that the cooperation is shallow. It is that even minimal coordination against the US and its allies is now a variable that NATO and allied planners can no longer treat as background noise.

**The boundary: where the red line actually runs**

And here we arrive at the boundary that defines the partnership, and defines it more sharply than any communiqué. As Russian nuclear saber-rattling accelerated through 2026, with prominent Russian voices openly urging the Kremlin to reach for nuclear weapons after Ukrainian strikes, Beijing reportedly delivered Moscow an ultimatum-form warning: that there can be no thought whatsoever of using nuclear weapons in Ukraine. The message was relayed through European channels ahead of the Ankara NATO summit, and by several accounts represented a firmer Chinese stance than at any previous point in the war.

This is the crucial datum, and it inverts the usual assumption about where escalation control sits. The DragonBear coordinates across every domain except the one where China’s global standing and strategic interest diverge absolutely from Russia’s escalation logic. Beijing enables the war machine and simultaneously polices its nuclear threshold, because a Russian nuclear strike would collapse the carefully managed posture that lets China be enabler and responsible power at once. The red line runs through Beijing, not Moscow. Coordination and constraint are exercised by the same hand.

**The question worth asking**

The strategic question is no longer whether Beijing and Moscow are strategic allies. It is whether the DragonBear needs to be one to reshape the global balance of power. My answer, consistently, has been clear on the matter:

The DragonBear does not need to be an alliance to reshape the balance of power amid new Cold War against America. This is what makes this bilateral relationship increasingly dangerous and unpredictable.
