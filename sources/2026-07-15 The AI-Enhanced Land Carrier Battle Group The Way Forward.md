The historical evolution of naval aviation offers useful ideas for overcoming the attritional nature of current drone and AI-dominated land warfare

The war in Ukraine is the first AI and drone-centric conflict and is characterised by attrition. Stuck in the ‘muddy, empty battlefield’, a term first used to describe the effects of modern firepower during the Russo-Japanese War of 1905, the war in Europe is gradually escalating into a new iteration of 20th century total war, including the routine targeting of industry and population centres.

Many have questioned the sustainability of armoured platforms in a drone-saturated battlefield. As Russia’s destroyed armour in Ukraine [reaches staggering numbers](https://www.oryxspioenkop.com/), the conceptual challenge should be: how can we regain mechanised manoeuvrability while simultaneously avoiding unsustainable losses?

This issue is not confined to Ukraine. The Israeli Defense Forces (IDF), despite the potent air capabilities they demonstrated during the June 2025 operation in Iran, were nevertheless thrust into a protracted attrition warfare scenario along the Lebanese border from October 2023 to 2024. With their primary focus on Gaza, 5-7 IDF brigades were deployed to defend the northern front. This mechanised force found itself immobilised in a ‘zero targets’ battlefield stalemate, pinned down by Lebanese Hezbollah anti-tank missiles and drone threats. Even when the IDF went on the offensive in October 2024, following devastating airstrikes to decapitate the leadership of the organisation, its offensive remained confined to an area from which the enemy had long withdrawn and was unwilling to defend.

Thus, the question persists and remains valid beyond the conditions of the Ukrainian battlefield: how can modern militaries regain manoeuvrability and initiative while avoiding prohibitive attrition in the new drone-dominated environment? Lessons from the past may suggest an answer.

## Air to Surface Integration

Naval warfare in the 1930s included a novel blend between three major classes of naval platforms – cannon-based warships (destroyers, cruisers and battleships), submarines and aircraft carriers. Each represented a distinct approach to maritime warfare, and these approaches were not contradictory. As the war progressed, it became evident that all these classes of vessel operating together were necessary for sustained and effective naval campaigns. However, navies that more fully embraced the naval-aviation revolution were better prepared for war.

On land, combat and early campaigns in the Second World War were characterised by the tank. Mobile, armoured and lethal, the tank brought key elements of naval warfare to land combat – men moving and fighting with new capabilities thanks to a tactical mechanised platform. Much has been written about the great tank battles of the Second World War, but it is often overlooked that these battles, like naval warfare of the period, were generally dominated by airpower. For instance, once the Allies established air superiority over Normandy, German armour could only operate after dark and was no match for Allied forces on the operational level, even if German tank crews remained formidable tactical opponents.

At sea, effective air support could only be delivered through floating airfields – aircraft carriers. Escort-carrier-based airpower played a significant role in defeating the German sea-denial campaign. Karl Doenitz, commander of the German U-boat force and later the entire Kriegsmarine (Nazi war navy), admitted in his memoirs that the arrival of escort carriers in significant numbers during the spring of 1943 determined the outcome of the Battle of the Atlantic against his U-boat force.

In the Pacific, command of the sea was not merely supported by, but dominated by aircraft carriers. Wherever American naval airpower prevailed, Japanese fleets suffered prohibitive losses, and their forces were ultimately unable to successfully defend occupied Pacific islands from landings by US forces.

The aircraft carrier concept, in all its variations, was designed to make air power fully available and integral to the tactics of naval task forces. The air components were adapted to the demands of the navy rather than being constrained by the range, payload and performance requirements for contemporary land-based aircraft.

As carriers became the centre of gravity in naval warfare, they required protection from enemy carriers, battleships, cruisers, destroyers, submarines and land-based air threats. Naval doctrine shifted focus to heterogeneous task forces that ensured naval airpower remained protected and effective. Their highest priority mission was to locate and destroy enemy carriers.

## A Land-Carrier Battle Group for Modern Warfare

In our current context, the massed use of UAS in Ukraine creates an area-denial effect on the frontlines and immediate surrounding areas. While beneficial for Ukrainian defence, it does not assist in regaining the operational initiative on the battlefield. Most inexpensive UASs, primarily multi-copter type first-person view drones (FPVs), have limited range and rely on human-based command and control. They can effectively target attacking enemies but depend on meticulous planned efforts to reliably identify and neutralise high-value targets such as artillery, rival drone units and air-defence assets in greater tactical depth.

However, this is where the historical naval analogy holds potentially useful ideas for the land domain. Just as aircraft became a manoeuvring asset for naval operations, drones could support land manoeuvre operations by seeking, suppressing, and eliminating area-denial threats. Mechanised formations with integrating drone units that can be launched, tasked and coordinated while on the move, in coordination with electronic and other intelligence assets, could deny adversaries safe rear areas for deploying their stationary short-range drone teams.

By mounting abundant UAS storage, launch and command and control systems onto more traditional land platforms, we can ensure a mobile forward air cover and strike force, providing continuous tactical UAS support. To protect such assets, they must be paired with effective mobile counter-UAS platforms for short range air defence (SHORAD), which should also be capable of neutralising anti-tank missiles and loitering munitions targeting the formation. The primary objective of this formation would be to suppress and eliminate area-denial assets, including artillery, drone operators and ground-based air defence batteries. Specialised reconnaissance UAVs will support this mission by detecting targets within a broader perimeter. Some targets may be neutralised by platform-based drones, while others may require rapid precision fires to be delivered by howitzers or rocket artillery dedicated to supporting the formation in its advance. Thus, the third component of this formation would be a tactical missile carrier.

Regaining initiative and advancing will inevitably bring this hypothetical Land-Carrier Battle Group (LCBG) into close contact with the enemy's defensive lines, where direct fire and infantry support will be essential, alongside the intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance (ISR) and lethal effects from the drone carriers. This is where traditional tanks and armoured personnel carriers will once again prove their value.

This combined arms formation, centred around new air and anti-air components, has the potential to provide conventional infantry, tanks, and combat engineers with the essential ISR, fire support and suppression effects against the enemy needed to regain manoeuvrability on the battlefield.

## AI – Holding It Together

To navigate the technical complexities of operating aircraft from a ship, naval airpower has traditionally compensated with relative tactical simplicity. The sea lacks specific landscapes and terrain features, making target location a matter of range and persistence rather than overcoming concealment and camouflage. When issues and ambiguities arise, the nature of maritime warfare in the 20th century has generally allowed for information and decisions to be communicated via radio and datalinks.

None of this holds true on today’s rapidly changing land battlefield, where cover and concealment are paramount, and infiltration tactics executed by small teams of 2-3 soldiers have replaced mechanised formations in many contexts. If the Land Carrier Battle Group (LCBG) is to succeed in a context where frontlines are dispersed and fluid, it will require excellent communications links, accurate real-time geolocation capabilities on platforms and munitions. Above all, the concept requires rapid decision-making capabilities to be localised both on the vehicles and the UAS they carry.

Simply squeezing human FPV operators into land carrier vehicles will not suffice. Platform size is not the primary limitation, but rather the intensity and tempo required. Competing LCBGs will vie for the advantage of first identification and first strike, but big-data AI-enhanced situational awareness may enable a better-informed team to anticipate enemy locations and movements. Just as the radio-enabled mission-command system defined the supremacy of German Panzer divisions over their British and French opponents during the May 1940 Battle of France, AI-enhanced integration could elevate today’s FPV-dominated systems to the next level.

As tactical environments become increasingly dynamic and challenging, greater pressure will be placed on integrating platforms, sensors, munitions, decision-makers, electronic warfare (EW) assets, and more. Current approaches to AI tools for battlefield applications, which focus primarily on support to higher-echelon HQs and improvements to smart-munition warhead seekers, require transformation if the LCBG concept is to work.

## Conclusion

Attritional warfare poses a nightmare for open democratic societies. Fear of attritional conflict may intensify a tendency among democratic leaders to resort to appeasement policies in a futile attempt to avoid conflict.

It is the responsibility of military thinkers, defence-tech communities, and leaders to ensure that we do not become mired in this modern iteration of the First World War western front, but rather pursue the necessary conceptual, technological, and organisational breakthroughs to restore the viability of manoeuvre warfare.

AI and drones should propel us forward, not backward. Rethinking our mechanised platform-centric combined arms concepts, as well as our AI battlefield applications, and primarily integrating these two, may represent a promising path forward.
