This week, a drone threat was declared for the first time ever in the Russian regions of Novosibirsk and Omsk, according to the Russian Emergency Situations Ministry. The reason for the threat declaration: Ukrainian drones, which ventured 2,500 kilometers (1,550 miles) beyond the border, deep into Siberia, to attack the Omsk oil refinery, the largest refinery in Russia. It was one of Ukraine’s longest-range strikes of the war, and a reminder that one of Russia’s greatest strengths – its immense size – is also a weakness.

Russia is not lacking for high-quality air defense systems, from S-500 surface-to-air missile system to the A-235 Nudol, capable of intercepting ballistic missiles and satellites, and down to the Pantsir-SMD, Russia’s smallest system, designed specifically for drone defense. Even if Russia’s military exaggerates its 98 percent interception rate, the effectiveness of its defenses is not really in doubt. Ukraine has certainly put them to the test. From the evening of July 6 to early July 7, Russia said it destroyed 452 drones (430 of which were headed toward Moscow) over more than a dozen different regions – marking Ukraine’s largest drone attack in two years. Despite multiple attempts, Ukrainian drones have managed to hit the Moscow Oil Refinery just once, on June 18.

The challenge is the sheer number of air defenses needed to protect Russia’s vast territory, combined with the concentration of defenses in the area of Russia west of the Urals, where approximately 80 percent of the population lives. Drones are difficult to detect beyond even tens of kilometers, so defenses must be spread out. However, just to cover the line of contract with Ukraine, Russia would need as many as 500 Pantsir units, which it simply doesn’t have. In a slowing economy short on workers and funds, ensuring the rapid mass production and deployment of high-quality air defenses, even just for strategic facilities, is not feasible. With each attack, Ukraine is able to identify gaps in Russia’s radar and missile coverage – information that it can then use to plan the next attack.

In fact, undefended and uninhabited parts of Russia could even serve as launch sites for Ukrainian strikes. This is precisely what happened in June 2025, when trucks traveling through Russia unleashed drones that struck targets in the Murmansk, Irkutsk, Ivanovo, Ryazan, and Amur regions. There are abandoned Soviet airstrips in remote villages throughout the country that could used as launch platforms. Given the vast distance between Ukraine and the recently attacked Omsk oil refinery, and the fact that drones don’t have the luxury of flying in straight lines but instead conceal themselves by winding paths through favorable terrain, some Russian analysts have speculated that the Omsk refinery attack might have originated in Kazakh territory. Ukraine does possess long-range drones capable of such an attack from its own territory, but they would require so much fuel that they would have to sacrifice firepower.

To counter the drone threat, Russia is finally – almost four and a half years into the Ukraine war – [developing](https://ria.ru/20260629/belousov-2101672144.html) a unified information environment to increase situational awareness, according to remarks by Defense Minister Andrei Belousov in late June. This includes software enabling units at the tactical level and above to identify targets, assess the battlefield, manage combat operations and coordinate more effectively. Authorities are also stepping up recruitment efforts, particularly on social media, to create more mobile fire teams armed with first-person view (FPV) interceptors. One such unit is the [BARS-Moscow volunteer detachment](https://www.kp.ru/daily/277790/5264875/), which will eventually consist of 2,500 personnel signed to 12-month contracts and split into 500 mobile teams to protect civilian areas of Moscow, Tula, Ryazan and Kaluga.

Ironically, the bureaucracy that Russia built up to exercise control over its sprawling territory is a liability when it comes to reacting swiftly and flexibly to external threats. Centralized control may be useful for connecting remote territories to the decision-making center during peacetime, but it has proved unreliable in war. For example, Ukrainian drones have been targeting Russian oil refineries for months, but not until late May 2026 did Russia’s Defense Ministry [authorize](https://geopoliticalfutures.com/russias-size-and-centralization-become-liabilities/%E2%80%A2%09https:/www.rbc.ru/business/28/05/2026/6a1850ad9a79477349237757) private companies to acquire anti-aircraft artillery systems, radar systems, special vehicles, radars and electronic warfare stations for their own defense. The building of protective structures is complicated by regulations set by the Ministry of Emergency Situations and Rostekhnadzor, the state technology and ecology watchdog, with violators subject to fines.

During the weeks or months that Russia’s centralized system is amending legislation and training volunteers, Ukraine’s drone strikes will continue inflicting damage on Russia’s economy. Besides much-discussed fuel shortages, the attacks also affect businesses dependent on uninterrupted internet service as well as airports and airlines, whose operations are vital in such a large territory. In less than five months in 2026, the number of canceled flights in Russia was up 4.3-fold compared with the same period a year ago. The number of long delays has doubled on average, and since May, airport closures occur almost every day. The main reason is the near-daily imposition of temporary flight restrictions due to drone threats. The cancellation of a Boeing 737-800 flight – the most common aircraft in Russia – can cost an airline between 15 million and 23 million rubles ($200,000 to $300,000). In other words, disruptions to air travel are costing airlines and related industries up to billions of rubles per day. The government may eventually be forced to come to their rescue, putting a further dent in the state budget.

When defending against an invading army, Russia’s immense territory can be a blessing. However, faced with waves of hard-to-detect drones capable of launching from almost any remote area – and even within Russia itself – that same size becomes a curse. The centralized system that Moscow established to maintain control continues to hamper its response, and it will be difficult to catch up with the pace of innovation in drone warfare.
