[**As Iran Declines, Turkey Advances**](https://geopoliticalfutures.com/as-iran-declines-turkey-advances/)  
By Kamran Bokhari  
July 15, 2026

*Question: In the analysis, Israel is not mentioned, but it still is the dominant economic and military power in the region. Also, how dominant can Turkey ultimately be with a faltering economy, where its currency continues to deteriorate, its inflation remains stuck around 30 percent and its unemployment rate remains high? Can a weak economic power become a regional and perhaps world power?*

Response: Israel is now the fourth largest economy in the Middle East, with a GDP of $600 billion. Turkey and Saudi Arabia are roughly tied for first with a $1.3 trillion GDP, with the UAE the third largest and a bit higher than Israel’s. Certainly, Turkey has economic problems, particularly inflation, but that is a problem many nations have, particularly given the oil crisis. On the other hand, Turkey’s economy is much larger than that of Israel, which has never been a major economic power in the region.

Israel has had significant military power along its own borders, in dealing with Egypt, Syria and Lebanon. Israel’s operation against Iran is not yet over, with the outcome of that engagement being far from clear and heavily predicated on American action there. If by “region” you mean only the countries bordering Israel, then Israel has had a great impact on them. But if you mean the Middle East as a region, which is what I consider the region to be, its military impact has been very limited. At this point, Israeli military action is primarily focused on nonstate actors, such as Hamas and Hezbollah. Turkey, on the other hand, has a much larger military than Israel, having the second largest force in NATO, second only to the United States, and with a growing defense industry. It is true that Israel has had much more combat experience than the Turkish military, but that cuts two ways. Israeli troops are far more experienced than Turkish troops, but Israel’s much smaller military has been in nearly constant combat for more than two years, which means that smaller and much used forces are likely tired, which limits Israeli military capacity as well.

Israel’s economy has certainly evolved, but its impact on the region economically has been minimal, while its military actions have been limited to borderlands; on the whole, the Israeli military has not been used widely in the region because, among other reasons, it does not have the reach. Turkey’s economic and military power is of much greater significance than Israel’s, and while its economy is strained, like those of many other nations at this time, its intrinsic economic and military power is significantly greater than Israel’s with the important exception that Israel has nuclear weapons, while Turkey does not.

[**A Major Move by India**](https://geopoliticalfutures.com/a-major-move-by-india/)  
July 13, 2026

*Question: The U.S. is not ceding ground – it is transforming its architecture of power. The defense agreements you mention are not signs of offshore balancing; they are logistical anchors. Recent Master Ship Repair Agreements with Indian shipyards, the integration of MQ-9B drones into global maritime monitoring and the unprecedented transfer of GE jet engine technology are turning India into the U.S. Navy’s forward logistical base. In a potential conflict with Beijing, hosting U.S. warships makes Indian ports immediate military targets, permanently stripping New Delhi of the luxury of neutrality.*

*Narendra Modi is not building an independent Asia. He is pragmatically swapping a declining Russian safety net for deep structural integration with the Pentagon, in exchange for elevation to the commanding heights of the global economy. Does this truly signal American withdrawal, or rather the most sophisticated tethering of New Delhi in modern history?*

Response: The United States is not “ceding ground” but is very much trying to reduce its military exposure in the world. This is seen in both President Trump’s statements and the policy papers published under him (e.g., the National Security Strategy), but with the Iran action in conflict with his stated policy. It is not policy that is the question, however. It is the American withdrawal from the role it played in the world during the Cold War.

I agree that the president’s primary purpose is to limit America’s military exposure to the world. It is not ceding ground, save that the necessary step for implementing that strategy is to withdraw forces from the world. In doing that, the U.S. no longer is responsible for the safety of a region but turns that responsibility to the defense of nations in that region. He can do that only by reducing American presence in that region and thus turning the area that had been an American responsibility – and hence under American military control – over to regional powers. Hence his strategy of reducing American presence in Europe and turning responsibility for European defense over to Europeans. In reducing responsibility for the region, he is also choosing to reduce both American presence and therefore power in the region. He is not being forced to cede power, but is choosing to cede power. But the U.S. remains very engaged in the world economically – and in terms of defense building and spending – and India is a part of this in economic terms as well.
