For much of my career there has been a debate about whether modern technology has ushered in a Revolution in Military Affairs (RMA). The impetus in this debate has been the growth in accuracy and range in modern weaponry, which, some argued, threatened to fundamentally change the nature of war. Notably the idea of there being a coming revolution in how wars were conducted was first a [Soviet framing which emerged in the mid-1970s](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/233011653_Through_the_Looking_Glass_The_Soviet_Military-Technical_Revolution_and_the_American_Revolution_in_Military_Affairs). It was then taken by [Andy Marshall at the Office of Net Assessment in the Pentagon and specifically reframed as an RMA](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232846892_The_Impact_of_the_Office_of_Net_Assessment_on_the_American_Military_in_the_Matter_of_the_Revolution_in_Military_Affairs).

The great event which first popularized the RMA debate was the First Gulf War. It started a discussion with partisans on both sides. Here is one of the most important articles from that discussion at the time, [Andrew F. Krepinevich](https://csbaonline.org/people/andrew-f-krepinevich/) [’s](https://csbaonline.org/resource/the-military-technical-revolution-a-preliminary-assessment/) *[The Military-Technical Revolution: A Preliminary Assessment](https://csbaonline.org/resource/the-military-technical-revolution-a-preliminary-assessment/).* It is worth a read if you want to delve into it.

Now the whole idea of there being an RMA was debatable and it caused quite a stir. If you want an overview from a skeptic about the concept, [there is this piece by Stephen Biddle which is also worth a look](https://www.comw.org/rma/fulltext/victory.html).

Now many arguments about the RMA not existing were not as good as Biddle’s. They were given by militaries that were not keen on changing, were too comfortable in their old ways and did not want to imagine that their old ideas were heading to obsolescence. And those with this outlook were then arguably saved by the outbreak of the War on Terror in 2001. For two decades from that point, the US was involved in mostly counter-insurgency operations very different from large state-to-state war, and the stress returned to boots on the ground. Indeed, it became quite fashionable for people at this point to pooh-pooh the idea of an RMA as flighty and frivolous.

*I am running through this subject quickly here, but would be happy to go back in the future if people want more—it is just that this piece has a different purpose.*

My personal view was that people were not understanding what an RMA might be and how it would operate. The need to think that any future war would be a modern version of combined arms (tanks, aircraft, mobile artillery, infantry, etc.) fighting was one of the reasons, I would argue, that the Russian military was so drastically overrated. Analysts did not want to admit that cheaper, accurate, ranged fires was making that kind of warfare increasingly less feasible.

It is worth noting that one of the leading analysts who overrated Russian power drastically is also one who dismisses the idea that we are witnessing an RMA, such as [in this](https://www.wsj.com/world/war-tech-change-c22ec6d6?mod=author_content_page_1_pos_3) *[Wall Street Journal](https://www.wsj.com/world/war-tech-change-c22ec6d6?mod=author_content_page_1_pos_3)* [piece](https://www.wsj.com/world/war-tech-change-c22ec6d6?mod=author_content_page_1_pos_3):

What this analyst is doing is using a framing device that has also been used by legacy arms manufacturers to pretend that the changes we are seeing are not so great. They focus on the drone discussion, and try to isolate the change to that variable. You see, it cannot be a real RMA if it is a question of drones causing a revolution or not.

Probably the best example of this was [in this](https://www.theatlantic.com/national-security/2026/03/who-needs-tanks-age-drones/686540/?gift=BQHDq1p24LRO8cUUEyLQ6ywmWvCDw5v91yW3l6fWEMw&utm_source=copy-link&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=share) *[Atlantic](https://www.theatlantic.com/national-security/2026/03/who-needs-tanks-age-drones/686540/?gift=BQHDq1p24LRO8cUUEyLQ6ywmWvCDw5v91yW3l6fWEMw&utm_source=copy-link&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=share)* [article](https://www.theatlantic.com/national-security/2026/03/who-needs-tanks-age-drones/686540/?gift=BQHDq1p24LRO8cUUEyLQ6ywmWvCDw5v91yW3l6fWEMw&utm_source=copy-link&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=share) which contained an interview with the CEO of Rheinmetall, one of Europe’s largest legacy arms manufacturers.

*When I brought up the drones that Ukraine has used so effectively against Russian tanks, the company’s chairman and CEO, Armin Papperger, was withering in his dismissal. “This is how to play with Legos,” he told me. He did not expect them to disrupt his industry. “What is the innovation of Ukraine?” Papperger asked. “They don’t have some technological breakthrough. They make innovations with their small drones, and they say, ‘Wow!’ And that’s great. Whatever. But this is not the technology of Lockheed Martin, General Dynamics, or Rheinmetall.”*

Later the article went on to have this exchange.

*Ukraine now makes more drones than any democracy in the world, and wealthy nations in Europe, Asia, and the Middle East are lining up to buy them. But when I asked the CEO of Rheinmetall what that could mean for his business model, he bristled. “Who is the biggest drone producer in Ukraine?” Papperger demanded. I listed the ones that I had visited in Kyiv two weeks earlier, Fire Point and Skyfall, which make hundreds of thousands of drones a month for the Ukrainian armed forces. “It’s Ukrainian housewives,” Papperger said of their factories. “They have 3-D printers in the kitchen, and they produce parts for drones,” he said. “This is not innovation.”*

Both of these arguments are based on a cute intellectual trick. They are saying: “We cannot be having an RMA as drones are a relatively simple technology. Toys do not make revolutions.”

Of course, what this argument is hiding is that any RMA covers far, far more than drones. It is basically a technological leap forward in a wide range of areas (hardware and software) that is leading to a revolutionary change that is already changing the fundamentals of many aspects of war: from how and when wars are started, to how they are waged, to their ethics and, finally, to how they are decided.

Not to spoil a good mystery, but the RMA is not the creation of one specific weapons system, it is emerging from a wide range of technologies that are leading in one direction that I will say is unstoppable. That is the **“de-humanizing” or “de-populating”** of the battlefield.

Having a human on this battlefield is ridiculous—and it will change everything.

*I prefer “de-humanizing” as that has both material and ethical meaning. It is the kind of phrase that will make people think deeply about what is happening.*

What does “de-humanizing” war mean? At its basic level, it means drastically reducing the number of human beings that will go into direct combat. Rapid changes in both software [(the move towards autonomous systems on both the micro and macro level as laid out in this piece)](https://open.substack.com/pub/phillipspobrien/p/we-are-on-a-twin-track-road-to-an?r=1tgexa&utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web) combined with technological changes now happening in the manufacture of all types of platforms (naval vessels, ground vehicles, and yes, drones) are leading this change right now. More and more systems do not need to have a human directly in control of them and this means already that the battlefield is increasingly less reliant upon, and far more dangerous for, human beings.

We are seeing just the basic glimpses of where we are going in Ukraine. The growing automation of weapons is already leading to fewer humans in the loop. Either one person operating a long way away from the weapon, or indeed the weapon operating by itself, is where we are heading. If a weapon can be controlled at a distance or autonomously, designing it for a human being simply makes it more expensive and less effective.

And here is where hardware changes are kicking in with the software changes to lead to an RMA. Certain components, for instance, are getting better and better (and more crucial) that will push the human away from the fighting. Here are two that might seem innocuous but will be crucial in this change; small cameras/sensors and powerful batteries.

Right now all those camera shots you see from FPV drones are part of a sensor technology revolution that is growing in leaps and bounds. Just a few years ago, sensors weighed close to 100 lbs and needed to be carried in large, powerful, slow and vulnerable drones. [Now they are the size of your palm, weigh a few pounds, and can fit on small drones](https://insideunmannedsystems.com/isr-sensors-smaller-size-greater-capability/). They can have infrared technology and see much further than a human can dream of.

*No “power” can hope to be a power going forward unless it can manufacture in mass its own supply of small sensor/cameras. For both the USA and Europe this is a major question, as the Chinese dominate the market. I know in Europe there is a keen awareness that there must be a localized supply and investments are being considered.*

Just as sensors are getting smaller, lighter and more effective, battery improvements are transforming how long weapons can operate without needing a recharge. For instance, the move [to solid-state batteries is allowing for safer operation, faster recharging and longer operational time](https://www.ayaatech.com/news/solid-state-battery-on-drone/).

I could go on and on—but basically the message is all the same. As weapons are becoming increasingly autonomous or capable of being human controlled at a distance, they are becoming more effective, more able to find targets, destroy targets, and more able to operate for longer periods before needing any maintenance.

Do the math.

As I said before, this de-humanizing of war, which is unstoppable, will lead to transformations in all aspects. I mentioned how wars start, how they are fought, how they are settled and finally their ethics above. I cannot possibly discuss all of those here, but let’s take a quick look at the first question—how they start.

In the past, leaders, for political reasons, have had to calculate how the human cost of launching a war could change domestic politics. In some cases it has restrained war a great deal (think of the USA). In the de-humanized war, this will be less of a consideration, and instead the calculation will be more strictly military/economic. “Can my de-humanized systems secure the political goals that I want?” If a leader calculates that they can, they would be far less restrained in the use of military force.

It is not a rosy future I am imagining, but it is coming and will be here sooner than you think.

It will be the greatest revolution in military affairs since the development of metal weapons.
