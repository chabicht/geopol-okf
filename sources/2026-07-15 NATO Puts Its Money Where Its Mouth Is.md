The most recent NATO summit, held in Ankara on July 7, marked a new chapter for the alliance. Unlike most other summits meant to redefine strategy, this one was used to emphasize, in Secretary-General Mark Rutte’s words, that the alliance’s focus had shifted “decisively from setting targets to delivering results” – including accelerating production, removing barriers to defense cooperation, strengthening resilience and ensuring that higher expenditure produces forces and capabilities that can actually be deployed and sustained. These results were made possible by commitments made by alliance members at The Hague in 2025 to allocate 5 percent of gross domestic product to defense- and security-related requirements by 2035.

Developments at the summit can be sorted into five broad categories. The first is political. Allied leaders reaffirmed their commitment to collective defense under Article 5 and to NATO’s 360-degree approach to deterrence and defense. This was politically important; the U.S. is pushing for Europe to take responsibility for European defense, and anti-American factions in Europe have scored political points by criticizing the U.S. for its unwillingness or inability to maintain its commitments.

The second is financial. The summit highlighted the accelerated redistribution of responsibility within the alliance – in terms of not only defense expenditure but also the financing of the industrial and infrastructural capacity needed to support rearmament. In 2025, European allies and Canada increased investment in core defense requirements by more than $139 billion, an annual rise of nearly 20 percent. Leaders also announced more than $50 billion in new procurement agreements and committed to expanding manufacturing capacity, improving trans-Atlantic industrial cooperation and reducing barriers to defense trade among allies.

Central to this financial architecture is the proposed Defense, Security and Resilience Bank, a Canadian-led initiative intended to provide participating governments and companies with long-term, lower-cost financing for defense, security and resilience projects. The institution would seek to address financing gaps in defense supply chains, particularly those affecting smaller and medium-sized companies. It could also support governments undertaking large, multi-year investments in production facilities, strategic infrastructure, critical supply chains and national resilience. Importantly, the DSRB is not formally a NATO institution, but its promotion at the Ankara summit demonstrated how the alliance’s defense investment agenda is beginning to generate complementary initiatives outside NATO’s existing common funding mechanisms.

The third category is technological. The summit endorsed the Strategy for Industry-NATO Cooperation and the NATO Innovation Scale-Up Package, and it launched initiatives such as the NATO Front Door for Industry and the NATO Engine. The Front Door is intended to provide companies, particularly smaller and non-traditional suppliers, with a clearer point of access to NATO procurement and innovation opportunities. The NATO Engine aims to connect unused or adaptable civilian and defense manufacturing capacity in allied countries. Under the new Drone Edge initiative, allies plan to invest more than $40 billion over five years in counter-drone capabilities. The central challenge, it seems, is not whether governments allocate additional funds toward defense but whether NATO members can build a broader financial and industrial ecosystem capable of absorbing those resources and translating them into equipment, ammunition, production lines, maintenance capacity, resilient infrastructure and operational readiness.

The fourth category is the eastern front. Allies pledged $70 billion in military equipment, assistance and training for Ukraine in 2026 and affirmed sovereign commitments to sustain at least an equivalent level of support in 2027. Indeed, NATO’s European members and Canada now provide a majority of military assistance to Ukraine, reinforcing the broader redistribution of responsibility across the Atlantic and in keeping with the U.S. demands. Through NATO Security Assistance and Training for Ukraine, headquartered in Wiesbaden and supported by three logistics hubs on the esstern flank, in Poland and Romania, the alliance is also attempting to make the delivery, maintenance and coordination of this assistance more systematic and sustainable.

The Black Sea falls roughly into this fourth category. On the sidelines of the summit, Romania, Bulgaria and Turkey signed an amendment expanding the mandate of the Mine Countermeasures Black Sea Task Group from its original focus on mine clearance and freedom of navigation to include the protection of critical undersea infrastructure. Coming online in July 2024, the task group brings together the three NATO allies bordering the western Black Sea under a rotating six-month command. Its existing missions include maritime surveillance, the identification and neutralization of hazards to shipping, mine countermeasures operations, and related search-and-rescue activities. The new mandate adds surveillance and reconnaissance intended to deter, identify and facilitate a rapid response to deliberate attacks against undersea infrastructure. This extension is operationally significant because drifting mines and deliberate attacks on infrastructure represent different but increasingly interconnected forms of maritime risk.

The new mandate does not publicly assign MCM Black Sea to the protection of specific commercial installations. Yet, its practical relevance is closely connected to the rapid development of offshore energy and communications infrastructure in the western Black Sea. The most immediate example is Romania’s Neptun Deep project, whose offshore architecture includes production wells, subsea systems, a platform and an approximately 160-kilometer (99-miles) pipeline connecting the field to the Romanian coast at Tuzla. Pipeline installation began in May 2026, and first production is expected in 2027.

For Black Sea infrastructure projects, the practical benefit lies mainly in continual presence, improved maritime-domain awareness and a faster capacity to investigate or respond to suspicious activity. Regular patrols and reconnaissance can help identify mines, unregistered vessels, unusual movements, interference around exclusion zones or other indications of preparations for an attack. A visible naval presence can also raise the costs of covert action by increasing the likelihood that hostile activity will be detected and attributed. The task group cannot eliminate every threat to widely dispersed undersea infrastructure, but it can reduce the surveillance gaps in which sabotage, mine laying or other forms of grey-zone interference are most likely to occur.

The fifth category – and potentially the most consequential for NATO’s eastern territories – is deterrence. Specifically, the elevation of energy, infrastructure and logistics resilience from supporting considerations to central components of deterrence. Allies announced a planned 27 billion euro ($31 billion) investment to modernize NATO’s fuel storage and distribution infrastructure and develop new facilities, including pipelines extending toward the east. Although the technical details are unclear, the decision recognizes the operational reality that militaries cannot deter, reinforce or fight without secure and sufficiently distributed supplies of fuel.

The summit also extended the meaning of defense preparedness beyond weapons platforms. NATO launched projects involving defense and critical raw materials, multinational space capabilities, scalable ammunition production and a secure, cloud-enabled digital architecture. The Ankara Declaration committed the alliance to an interoperable trans-Atlantic warfighting cloud, while a new contract for NATO’s Protected Business Network is intended to provide classified, cloud-enabled operations. Together, these initiatives point toward a more integrated conception of military power in which industrial supply chains, data, communications, raw materials and civilian manufacturing capacity become part of the alliance’s deterrence posture.

This emphasis is particularly important for NATO’s eastern flank. Since 2014, the alliance has constructed a forward defense posture stretching from the Arctic and Baltic regions to the Black Sea. NATO now maintains nine multinational Forward Land Forces battlegroups in Bulgaria, Estonia, Finland, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Romania and Slovakia. These formations are permanently present, combat capable and designed to expand toward brigade size when required.

Yet the development of this force posture has advanced faster than the infrastructure needed to sustain it. This is the central military-mobility problem confronting the eastern flank. Forward-deployed forces are intended primarily to deter aggression, provide situational awareness and ensure that any attack immediately engages several allied countries. In a high-intensity conflict, however, the defense of the region would depend on NATO’s capacity to move much larger forces from North America and Western Europe toward threatened territory. That requires ports able to receive heavy materiel, airfields capable of supporting dispersed operations, railways and rolling stock suitable for armored vehicles, bridges with adequate load capacity, secure road corridors, logistics hubs, maintenance facilities, ammunition storage and reliable fuel distribution. Europe has fallen wildly short in this regard.

This means that NATO’s reinforcement challenge begins well before forces reach the front. Military convoys must navigate different national procedures for customs, dangerous goods, oversized cargo, railway access and border crossings. Civilian infrastructure operators must be incorporated into defense planning, while sufficient rail wagons, heavy equipment transporters, port personnel, fuel suppliers and repair capacity must be available during a crisis. Digitalization can accelerate permissions and prioritization, but fragmented national systems and concerns over cybersecurity and NATO compatibility continue to obstruct the establishment of a common framework. The risk is that a deployment that appears rapid in planning documents becomes delayed by accumulated administrative and infrastructural bottlenecks once it crosses several national jurisdictions.

Geography complicates things further. Reinforcing Poland and the Baltic states depends heavily on west-east connections and on access through the narrow corridor linking Poland and Lithuania. Reinforcing Romania, Bulgaria and the wider Black Sea region requires reliable transport routes through Central and Southeastern Europe, with additional capacity at ports in the Adriatic, Aegean and Black seas as well as the Danube. Yet some of the most serious weaknesses in this network have been identified for years without being adequately resolved. For example, the road and rail connection between Bucharest and Sofia remains one of the most vulnerable sections of the wider corridor, still dependent largely on national roads, ageing railway infrastructure and a limited number of Danube crossings. Few credible alternatives have been developed, leaving military and commercial traffic exposed to congestion, disruption and single point failures. This is particularly problematic because parts of the railway and road network are among the oldest in the region and are poorly adapted to the rapid movement of heavy military equipment. Such routes cannot be treated as separate national projects: a modernized railway in one country has limited operational value if the connecting line across the border remains slow, if a bridge cannot carry heavy armor, if border terminals lack capacity, or if the destination does not possess sufficient unloading, storage and maintenance infrastructure.

For this reason, military mobility should be understood as a network rather than a series of transport projects. Its effectiveness is determined by its weakest point. NATO may possess sufficient aircraft to transport personnel but not enough capacity to move heavy armor. A port may be able to receive a large shipment, but the surrounding railway may lack sidings or suitable wagons. The relevant measure is therefore not how many miles of infrastructure have been modernized but how rapidly NATO can move a formation from its point of entry to its operational area and sustain it there under conditions of disruption, cyberattack and long-range strikes.

The decision to invest 27 billion euros in NATO fuel storage and distribution infrastructure must be assessed against this background. NATO’s established pipeline architecture was constructed principally during the Cold War to sustain forces concentrated along the alliance’s former containment line in Western and Central Europe. Extending storage and pipeline facilities eastward amounts to adapting that system to NATO’s present strategic geography, where the main conventional threat and the greatest reinforcement requirements lie much farther east.

Ankara therefore represented more than another increase in defense spending. It reflected one of the first NATO attempts to close the gap between political commitments and operational capability. This is especially significant because the summit effectively acknowledged that NATO’s eastern flank has evolved into an active strategic front. As important, it marked a shift from measuring adaptation through declarations and spending targets toward judging it by production rates, movement times, infrastructure capacity and sustainment. The central question is no longer whether allies are willing to defend the eastern flank but whether they can build the interconnected industrial, logistical and regional architecture required to do so under wartime conditions.
