Security in the Red Sea basin is often boiled down to one thing: the Yemeni Houthi rebels’ ability to disrupt commercial shipping, particularly as the war in Iran raises concerns over maritime shipping outside the Strait of Hormuz. But the basin’s strategic landscape is increasingly shaped by broader geopolitical developments, including Israel’s recognition of Somaliland, Turkey’s expanding military partnership with Somalia, the destabilizing effects of Sudan’s civil war, Ethiopia’s drive to secure maritime access, and Egypt’s efforts to reassert its regional role. The region’s future stability will depend not only on protecting shipping lanes but also on the intersecting rivalries unfolding among the states that border and influence the waterway.

Recent developments illustrate how quickly the strategic importance of the Red Sea is expanding. According to a July 7 report from Reuters, Saudi Arabia is weighing a multibillion-dollar expansion of its East-West oil pipeline to the Red Sea – a project that could add up to 2 million barrels per day in capacity – as it seeks to reduce reliance on the Strait of Hormuz for energy exports. A day earlier, container shipping giants Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd announced they would reroute a key Asia-Mediterranean service back through the Red Sea and Suez Canal rather than around Africa’s Cape of Good Hope.

Elsewhere, Ethiopia’s military leadership continued to signal its determination to secure maritime access, with Navy Commander Kindu Gezu calling it “inevitable” that Ethiopia would regain what he described as its “unfairly lost” access to the Red Sea, while Army Chief Birhanu Jula told senior officers that “elaborate preparations” were needed to achieve that objective. Egypt’s July 3 inauguration of the Octagon, a sprawling military headquarters designed to centralize command and control, improve crisis management and integrate intelligence capabilities, further reflected Cairo’s ambitions to enhance its regional military posture.

Taken together, these developments show that the Red Sea basin’s security challenges extend well beyond the long-standing threat posed by the Houthis at the Bab el-Mandeb. The Houthis themselves avoided direct involvement in the Iran war and remain focused primarily on consolidating control over northwestern Yemen – including the capital, Sanaa, and much of the country’s Red Sea coastline – while contending with the Saudi-backed internationally recognized government based in Aden and the UAE-backed Southern Transitional Council.

The more consequential changes are taking place around Yemen, where states are pursuing competing military, economic and political objectives. As a result, the Red Sea is evolving from a theater dominated by non-state maritime threats into one defined by overlapping interstate rivalries with potentially broader implications for regional stability.

Israel’s recognition of Somaliland in 2025 is an important aspect of this emerging competition. It reflects Israel’s effort to extend its security perimeter into the southern Red Sea, near the Bab el-Mandeb. A relationship with Somaliland could give Israel the means to monitor and counter Houthi activity while helping to safeguard maritime routes critical to Israeli trade and energy flows. The move also aligns with Israel’s broader effort to shape its strategic environment by limiting the influence of regional competitors, particularly Turkey, which is also expanding its military and diplomatic presence in the Horn of Africa and the wider Middle East. Importantly, Israel’s recognition could also stoke existing tensions in Somalia and the broader Horn of Africa by sharpening regional rivalries and introducing another layer of competition into an already fragile security environment.

Ethiopia’s renewed campaign for Red Sea access is driven by the strategic consequences of Eritrea’s independence in 1993, which left Addis Ababa landlocked and dependent primarily on Djibouti for maritime trade. Under Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed, Ethiopia’s access to the sea has increasingly been framed as a national security priority, so the government has sought arrangements with coastal actors, including Somaliland and Puntland, to diversify its maritime options. The fallout from the Tigray War (2020-2022) has complicated the matter; relations with Eritrea remain tense, and Addis Ababa wants to prevent Asmara from curbing its regional ambitions while managing internal political and ethnic challenges. Ethiopia’s efforts to reach the ocean, therefore, reflect a broader push to restore strategic autonomy, expand regional influence and reduce vulnerability to neighboring powers.

Sudan’s civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces is another major source of instability, particularly because Sudan possesses a strategically significant coastline along the Red Sea. The conflict has become an arena for regional competition as outside actors back rival factions in pursuit of influence over Sudan’s political future, security architecture and access to maritime infrastructure. A prolonged war – or the consolidation of power by one faction – could create opportunities for external powers to establish military, economic or logistical footholds along Sudan’s coast.

Egypt’s role in the Red Sea basin, meanwhile, is particularly significant given its control over the Suez Canal. After decades of relative geopolitical retrenchment, Cairo is trying to reassert itself as a regional power, which in light of the conflict in Gaza has raised concerns in Israel. Cairo’s approach includes efforts to influence the trajectory of Sudan’s civil war, continued pressure on Ethiopia over the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam dispute and closer alignment with Saudi Arabia, including the 2017 transfer of sovereignty over the Red Sea islands of Tiran and Sanafir to Riyadh. Collectively, these initiatives reflect Egypt’s effort to reclaim its traditional role as a central Arab and Red Sea power while shaping the emerging order around the waterway.

The evolving relationship between Egypt and Turkey adds another dimension to changes underway in the region – one that is especially concerning for Israel. Ankara has expanded its influence in northeastern Africa through its presence in Libya, where it maintains ties with rival authorities in Tripoli and Benghazi, its support for the Sudanese Armed Forces and its strategic partnership with Somalia. As an Eastern Mediterranean power, Turkey sees greater engagement across the Red Sea and toward the Indian Ocean as essential to securing its regional position. President Recep Tayyip Erdogan’s visit to Ethiopia in February was the latest expression of a broader Turkish strategy to build political, economic and security partnerships in the Horn of Africa and increase Ankara’s influence in a region of growing geopolitical importance.

The cumulative effect of these developments is that the Red Sea basin is becoming a far more contested strategic arena than it has been. Though maritime insecurity is still an immediate concern, the deeper transformation is being driven by competing visions of regional governments and other actors as they seek influence over access routes, alliances and strategic chokepoints. The basin is thus becoming a space where local conflicts, interstate rivalries and broader geopolitical competition converge, creating risks that extend beyond disruptions to commercial shipping.
