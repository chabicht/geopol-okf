# Background

The United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) governs a $2 trillion intra-regional market with all three economies deeply interdependent. The agreement succeeded NAFTA and is subject to review and renewal. The Trump administration's preference for bilateral deals over trilateral frameworks, combined with concerns over trade deficits, rules of origin, and possible tariff circumvention, has put the agreement under pressure [1].

# Current Situation

The July 1 USMCA negotiations ended before they truly began, with Washington making clear that it does not want to renew the agreement on current terms and prefers bilateral deals with Canada and [Mexico]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/mexico.html). This reflects US concerns over trade deficits, rules of origin, and possible tariff circumvention, as well as the [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) administration's push for better terms in key sectors such as the automotive industry [1].

Although USMCA remains in force, North America is entering a period of tough bilateral bargaining that will increase uncertainty for cross-border companies. Mexico and the US are set to negotiate on July 20, with Mexico signaling openness to Washington's demands. With a $2 trillion intra-regional market at stake and all three economies deeply interdependent, some agreement is likely, but its terms could significantly reshape North American trade [1].

# Analysis

The USMCA renegotiation reflects the broader trend of security decisions increasingly shaping the global economy. Tariffs, sanctions, defense spending, and critical mineral dependencies are no longer separate policy areas. The US preference for bilateral deals over trilateral frameworks creates both risk and opportunity for Mexico to negotiate terms favorable to its strategic interests, but also exposes the structural asymmetry of a relationship where Mexico's manufacturing sector is deeply integrated into US supply chains [1].

The automotive sector is a particular focus, connecting this negotiation to the broader [EV industrial competition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ev-competition.html) between the US, China, and Europe. Rules of origin requirements and tariff structures will shape whether North American automotive supply chains can compete with China's integrated EV ecosystem [1].

### Section 338 Tariffs: The USMCA Override

[Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html) reports that the [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) administration invoked Section 338 of the Smoot-Hawley Trade Act — an obscure, never-before-used provision — to impose 50% tariffs on Canadian goods, explicitly overriding USMCA origin rules ("regardless of whether a good originates under the USMCA"). The White House framed this as retaliation for provincial alcohol bans, but those bans were themselves responses to Trump's earlier tariffs and annexation threats (his demands that Canada become the 51st state). The tariffs are almost surely illegal and definitively violate USMCA, which Trump himself negotiated. Krugman argues this confirms that a deal with Trump lasts only until he feels like breaking it — a conclusion already reached by the European Commission and Iran's IRGC. The immediate economic impact is limited (the auto industry is exempt), but the action puts all North American commerce on notice that cross-border flows may be cut off at any moment. Trump also floated Canadian wildfire smoke as a tariff pretext, which Krugman dismisses as absurd given the scale and inaccessibility of Canada's boreal forest. The invocation of Smoot-Hawley's Section 338 adds a new dimension to what Krugman already considers the worst US trade policy since that 1930 disaster. [2]

### Section 338 Tariffs: The Coercion Escalation

Luck documents the human and economic costs of imposing Section 338 tariffs on USMCA-compliant goods. For every dollar in tariff revenue (~$9.9 billion total), US border workers absorbed 5–10 cents in lost wages. Canadian visitor decline cost 10,000–30,000 border community leisure and hospitality jobs and $0.5–1 billion per year in lost wages. The US Travel Association estimated $5.7 billion in lost travel spending (2025). Kurmann et al. found 14,000–42,000 jobs lost at small retail and leisure establishments, concentrated in fewer than 3% of US ZIP codes, with lower-income and Democratic-leaning areas hardest hit. Brown-Forman's Canadian sales dropped 62%; Kentucky and Tennessee spirits exports to Canada fell roughly 50%. The consumer boycott persisted — land crossings remained approximately 20% below 2024 levels in March–April 2026 even after tariff adjustments. Luck's central argument is that consumer boycotts are not state action: state-to-state economic statecraft cannot reliably dictate citizen behavior, and "the coercer pays too" — through both game-theoretic dynamics and independent citizen choices. Credibility and consumer trust do not recover on the schedule of trade negotiations [3].

Gundermann, Grant-Chapman, and Marroquín Bitar provide the legal and structural analysis. Section 338 of the Tariff Act of 1930 allows the president to impose up to 50% tariffs or ban imports from countries discriminating against US products; this is the first presidential use of the statute, and no court has interpreted it. Three separate proclamations targeted alcoholic beverages, motor vehicles, and dairy. The tariffs apply regardless of USMCA compliance status and are not imposed on goods already subject to Section 232 duties. The $20.2 billion in targeted goods represents 4.9% of total US imports from Canada (2024). Legal questions abound: the ITC's monitoring role is unclear, the statute may have been implicitly repealed by later legislation, and the tariffs likely violate WTO commitments. The dairy proclamation is flawed — US dairy exports to Canada remain below quota thresholds — and the auto proclamation targets non-auto goods except motorcycles. Additional motivations include punishing Canada as the only retaliator besides China, demonstrating trade tools post-IEEPA striking, and replacing Section 122 tariffs expiring July 24. US-Mexico negotiations advanced speedily while Canada was reluctant to negotiate, making Section 338 a leverage tool to force Canada to the USMCA table. Mexico's conciliatory posture kept negotiations moving; sustained threats had the opposite effect on Canada, with Prime Minister Carney calling for resolution while coercion shrank political room for engagement. The action raises fundamental uncertainty about US willingness to abide by USMCA terms.
