# Background

During the [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html), the closure of the Strait of Hormuz disrupted global energy supply. President [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) announced "Project Freedom" — a plan to provide military escorts for oil tankers transiting the strait. The plan required regional partners to provide basing and airspace access for US military operations [1].

# What Happened

- **May 2026**: Trump launched Project Freedom to escort tankers through the Strait of Hormuz.
- [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html) refused to allow use of its bases or airspace — even after direct personal intervention by Trump.
- Saudi Arabia cited lack of confidence in the coherence of U.S. strategy.
- The plan was shelved within days of launch [1].

The collapse was a direct consequence of the credibility gap created by the administration's maximalist rhetoric. Officials had spoken of "elimination," "obliteration," and "unconditional surrender" — language that exceeded what the campaign could deliver and cost Washington the confidence of its partners. A list of things to break is not a strategy, and Washington never provided one [1].

# Analysis

## Iran's Wedge Strategy

[Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s kinetic targeting of Gulf Cooperation Council countries served a strategic purpose: sending an unambiguous message to Washington's regional partners that supporting this campaign has a price. The cruise missile and drone strikes on Saudi territory made that price real. When Iran struck the [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html)'s oil terminal, [Kuwait]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) and Saudi Arabia closed their airspace to U.S. military aircraft, fearing more of the same on their own soil [1].

Project Freedom's collapse demonstrated that superpowers still need partners — but when Washington called for solidarity, those partners hesitated. Iran was imposing costs, and Washington offered no reason for regional states to pay the price. The [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html), bearing the brunt of Iranian attacks while watching its Gulf neighbors offer little solidarity, subsequently quit OPEC and threatened to leave the Arab League [1].

The episode marked a structural fracture in Gulf cohesion and the US-led security architecture in the [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html). See also the [Iran Ceasefire MoU]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html) for the diplomatic resolution that followed [1].
