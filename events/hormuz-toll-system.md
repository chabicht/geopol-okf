# Background

Throughout the [US-Iran War of 2026](/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.md), [Iran](/actors/iran.md) demonstrated that the Strait of Hormuz remains open not because the [United States](/actors/united-states.md) enforces freedom of navigation but because Iran permits it. The war crystallized Iran's chokepoint control into a concrete proposal: a permanent toll system for Hormuz transit, negotiated through [Oman](/actors/oman.md), that would formalize Iran's role as gatekeeper of 20% of global oil flows.

# What Happened

- During the war's later phases, Iran proposed charging approximately $2 million per tanker transit, payable in rials — a structure that would both generate revenue and internationalize the rial [1].
- By May 2026, Iran and Oman were in formal talks over a permanent toll system. The Iranian ambassador to France described the system as transparent [2].
- [Trump](/actors/trump.md) rejected the plan, insisting the strait should be "open, free, with no tolls" [2].
- The toll system proposal survived the June 17 [ceasefire MoU](/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.md), remaining on the table as a structural feature of post-war Hormuz governance.

# Analysis

[The Honest Sorcerer](/actors/the-honest-sorcerer.md) frames the toll system as the logical endpoint of Iran's geographic leverage: if control of the chokepoint is the real source of Iranian power, then monetizing that control through a toll system is the rational strategy. The toll system transforms Iran's coercive leverage from a binary weapon (open or closed) into a continuous revenue stream that institutionalizes Iran's role in global energy governance [1].

[Robert Pape](/actors/robert-pape.md) argues that Iran's leverage over Hormuz is structural, not tactical — it persists regardless of the balance of conventional forces. The toll system proposal is the diplomatic manifestation of this structural leverage: Iran is not asking permission but describing the terms under which it will continue to permit passage [3].
