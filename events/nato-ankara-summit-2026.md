# Background

The 2026 NATO Summit was held in Ankara on July 7-8, hosted by Turkish President Erdoğan at his neo-Ottoman palace. The summit followed the previous year's Hague summit, where NATO leaders agreed to invest 5% of GDP on defense — a pledge that helped placate [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) and sent a deterrence message to [Putin]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/putin.html). The Ankara summit was positioned as a "delivery" summit, focused on converting spending pledges into capabilities, faster defense production, and sustainable support for [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) [1].

# Current Situation

## NATO 3.0

Secretary General Mark Rutte introduced the concept of "NATO 3.0" — a stronger [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html) in a stronger [NATO]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/nato.html). The framework has three pillars: record European defense investment, backfilling gaps left by US force reductions, and a pre-agreed summit declaration. The "Trump Trillion" — $1.2 trillion in extra defense expenditure since Trump's first term, including a 20% increase in 2025 — was presented to Trump in the Oval Office as a framing device to maintain his engagement with the alliance [1].

## Force Model Gaps

The Pentagon informed allies in May 2026 that it would shrink the pool of capabilities assigned to NATO defense plans. Reported cuts include a third of F-15 fighter jets, half of armed drones, aircraft carriers, and strategic bombers. Supreme Allied Commander General Alexus Grynkewich stated that European allies have "largely filled the gaps" left by US reductions, with the main remaining gap being strategic bombers — which Europeans do not possess and cannot replace like-for-like. Deep precision strike capabilities are limited in number and range [1].

## Ukraine Aid Pledge

NATO allies pledged €70 billion in military assistance to Ukraine for 2026 and "at least equivalent levels" of support in 2027. The figure includes €30 billion from the €90 billion EU loan to Ukraine plus €40 billion in bilateral support — roughly equivalent to current European assistance levels, but without any direct US funding [1].

## Summit Declaration

All 32 allies pre-approved a short declaration reaffirming "ironclad commitment to collective defense under Article 5," describing [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) as a "long-term threat to Euro-Atlantic security," and stating that [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) must never have a nuclear weapon and must respect freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz. The declaration's early consensus was designed to reduce the risk of Trump disavowing it, as he did with the 2018 G7 declaration [1].

## Fragile Unity

Despite the constructive setup, Trump lashed out on Truth Social, repeating claims that NATO allies "were not there for us" and that the relationship is "not reciprocal." German Chancellor Merz rebuked Trump's spending criticism, noting Germany is doubling its defense budget within four years. The summit's success depends heavily on Rutte's diplomatic skills and Trump's relationship with Erdoğan, whom Trump calls "a hell of a leader." The next summit, planned for Albania in 2027, was not mentioned in the declaration — reflecting reluctance to repeat yearly drama [1].

## Defense Industrial Mobilization

The summit's central questions — defense investment, support for Ukraine, air defense, industrial production, and burden-sharing — are increasingly economically consequential because rearmament is no longer solely a military issue but an industrial and fiscal one. Any commitments made at the summit could accelerate demand for defense production, logistics, energy infrastructure, electronics, shipyards, skilled labor, and dual-use transport networks. The 5% GDP target (3.5% core defense + 1.5% security-related investment by 2035) gives industry a longer demand signal than annual procurement alone. SIPRI estimates that if all allies met the full target in 2035, annual alliance spending would reach roughly $4.2 trillion [2].
