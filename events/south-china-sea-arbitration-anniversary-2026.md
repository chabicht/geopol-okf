# Background

On July 12, 2016, an Annex VII tribunal seated at the Permanent Court of Arbitration issued a unanimous award against [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) in a case brought by the Philippines under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). The award found no legal basis for historic rights within the nine-dash line, no Spratly feature generates an exclusive economic zone, and China violated Philippine sovereign rights and caused severe environmental damage to reefs. The award is final and binding. Ten years on, Mischief Reef is a functioning airbase, Subi and Fiery Cross are hardened installations, and pressure has migrated to Second Thomas Shoal, Scarborough, Sandy Cay, and Reed Bank [1].

# Current Situation

The tenth anniversary produced a complex diplomatic landscape: a joint statement signed by 14 states, a parallel deterrence forum in Manila, and a sharp analytical finding about the difference between defending the law and joining a bloc.

## The Joint Statement

Fourteen states signed a joint statement on July 12: the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html), [United Kingdom]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-kingdom.html), [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html), [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html), New Zealand, Canada, the Philippines, [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html), [Italy]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/italy.html), [Estonia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/estonia.html), [Latvia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/latvia.html), [Lithuania]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/lithuania.html), [Romania]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/romania.html), and Slovenia. The [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) issued a separate text through the High Representative affirming the award as final and legally binding [1].

## The Manila Forum

On the same weekend, a forum titled "A Decade of the Arbitral Award: Credible Deterrence in Defense of the West Philippine Sea" convened envoys from Australia, Canada, the EU, [France]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/france.html), Germany, [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html), Japan, South Korea, New Zealand, and the United Kingdom. The French Ambassador, Marie Fontanel, stated that legal principles alone are not enough and words must be backed by practical cooperation [1].

## On-the-Water Escalation

In March 2026, the China Coast Guard declared "clearing operations" at Scarborough Reef, broadcasting coordinates for a timed exclusion zone and deploying at least six coast guard vessels, fifteen maritime militia vessels, and a PLAN warship to drive off Philippine fishing craft. Scarborough sits roughly 120 nautical miles from Luzon, inside the Philippine EEZ. In May, after Defence Secretary Gilberto Teodoro named China as [ASEAN]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/asean.html)'s principal challenge at the Shangri-La Dialogue, Beijing sanctioned him personally with an entry ban — a sitting defence minister of an ASEAN state barred for something said at a conference [1].

## The Manila-Hanoi Enhanced Strategic Partnership

Shortly before the anniversary, the [Philippines]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/philippines.html) and [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html) elevated their ties to an enhanced strategic partnership, agreeing to establish maritime hotlines, expand coast guard coordination, and reaffirm their commitment to international law in the South China Sea. [Victoria Herczegh]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) frames the timing as particularly significant: pairing the two ASEAN states with the strongest South China Sea claims and institutionalizing practical mechanisms to manage Chinese maritime pressure. Although Vietnam continues to balance cooperation with strategic autonomy (its "four noes" forbid formal military alliances), the partnership reflects a shared view that maritime stability is better preserved through closer coordination than through isolated bilateral engagement with Beijing [2].

# Analysis

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) provides the definitive analysis of the anniversary's strategic significance [1].

## The Vehicle, Not the Position

The key finding: states are not declining the law — they are declining co-signature in an American column. The 14 signatories are almost exclusively US treaty allies or states seeking deeper American security commitment. The states that declined it are not refusing the substance. India named the 2016 award explicitly in a 2023 joint statement with the Philippines. France sails freedom of navigation operations and sent its ambassador to argue for credible deterrence. South Korea showed up alongside them. The EU issued its own text calling for full implementation [1].

For France, the reason is doctrinal: strategic autonomy requires that French Indo-Pacific presence be legible as French rather than as an auxiliary function of US containment policy. For India and South Korea, the logic is hedging: both have substantial exposure to China, both are deepening security ties with Manila, and neither will accept the reputational cost of formal enrolment in an American-led legal bloc when the same substantive position can be advanced bilaterally at lower price [1].

## The France-Vietnam Symmetry

France and [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html) both declined the joint statement. Both are middle powers with real capability and real stakes. Both refuse to function as instruments of a larger power's contest — but from opposite causal structures:

- **France declines from surplus**: it has the territory, platforms, and doctrine to act independently, wants the credit for doing so in its own name, and its ambassador spent the anniversary in Manila arguing for deterrence. Declining the American text costs France nothing and buys the appearance of independent agency.
- **Vietnam declines from deficit**: it cannot act alone, cannot absorb retaliation, and its calculation is about the morning after. Declining costs Vietnam the chance to consolidate a legal position it badly needs, and buys it the only thing it can afford: deniability. The "four noes" forbid formal alignment, and Vietnam has to live with the consequences on a shared land border and a shared sea [1].

Identical behaviour, opposite causal structure. The autonomous hedge and the coerced hedge, visible in the same document on the same day. This distinction predicts different futures: the autonomous hedger will act unilaterally whenever its interests are engaged and will never be reliable as a coalition member. The coerced hedger will align only when the coercion is lifted — its alignment is a lagging indicator of the local balance of power [1].

## ASEAN Structural Non-Agency

Not one of the other ten ASEAN members signed, in the year the Philippines holds the chair. Not [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html), which has the second-strongest claim. Not [Indonesia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/indonesia.html), whose Natuna EEZ is transected by the nine-dash line. Not [Malaysia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/malaysia.html), which has a standing China Coast Guard presence at Luconia Shoals. ASEAN has never officially recognised the 2016 award. Consensus rules mean that Phnom Penh and Vientiane hold what is functionally a Chinese veto over the bloc's language [1].

The November ASEAN Summit in Manila is the real deadline. Marcos is pinning the Code of Conduct to that summit. The COC is the real prize — watch specifically for any provision requiring joint development with China to the exclusion of external parties, which would render the award moot by contract rather than by defiance [1].

## Polarisation Is Co-Produced

The mechanism: once a legal position acquires a bloc identity, states that need neutrality between the poles are forced into neutrality toward the norm. A 14-nation statement led by Washington and populated overwhelmingly by treaty allies is a bloc instrument. Beijing works to make defending the award look like taking a side; Washington's drafting practice does much of that work for it. That France, India, South Korea, and Vietnam all found the same workaround — endorse the substance, refuse the vehicle — is a comment on the vehicle as much as on Chinese pressure [1].

## The DragonBear Caveat

Russia is not a party to the South China Sea dispute, is not named in any of the texts, took no observable action tied to the anniversary, and has no maritime stake in the Spratlys. The aggregate case for a coordinated Chinese-Russian rejection of adjudicatory institutions remains strong — joint naval exercises, parallel positions on the ICC and sanctions, shared insistence that great-power core interests are non-justiciable. But reading DragonBear into an episode where one of the two is entirely absent is the exact move critics of the framework accuse it of. What the anniversary demonstrates is the Chinese half of a pattern believed to be joint [1].
