# Background

The Camp David Accords were negotiated at the Carter White House in September 1978 between [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/egypt.html)'s Anwar Sadat and [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html)'s Menachem Begin, mediated by U.S. President Jimmy Carter. They emerged from a multi-front regional war: Sinai (Egypt), the Golan Heights (Syria), the West Bank (Jordan), and the Gaza Strip (the Palestinian territories), with Gulf states funding Egypt and Syria and using oil as a political weapon, and the Cold War superpower competition overlaying the regional conflict. The [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) provided Israel with military and financial support; the Soviet Union backed the Arab states.

The Egyptian front was the only one truly capable of inflicting significant damage on Israel in 1973. After addressing its prior military weaknesses, rebuilding its armed forces, and crossing the Suez Canal to destroy the Bar-Lev Line, Egypt coordinated with Syria to divide Israeli efforts across two fronts and imposed costs that made the Egyptian front impossible to leave open indefinitely. Sadat's conditions for peace were substantial: the return of occupied Egyptian territories under full Egyptian sovereignty, guarantees for Palestinian rights in the West Bank and Gaza, the inclusion of Palestine in the negotiations, and an Israeli withdrawal from Syria [1].

# What Happened

- September 17, 1978: The Camp David Accords were signed, producing a framework for peace between Egypt and Israel and a parallel framework for Palestinian autonomy.
- Egypt recovered Sinai under a defined timetable with full sovereignty restored; Israel completed its withdrawal in 1982.
- Sadat had insisted on linkage between the first phase of Sinai withdrawal and Palestinian autonomy — at least in Gaza. Carter acknowledged the importance Sadat attached to that linkage.
- The Palestine Liberation Organization rejected the framework; Egypt could not speak on behalf of a people against their will.
- The Arab League suspended Egypt's membership in response to the separate peace.
- The Multinational Force and Observers was created by the agreement to verify the treaty's reciprocal security provisions.
- The Syrian and Palestinian fronts were left outside the bilateral settlement.

# Analysis

## The Front Separation Pattern

[Ahmed Saber Abbas]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/war-on-the-rocks.html) (War on the Rocks) identifies the structural pattern that makes Camp David analytically useful for the present: closing — or attempting to close — the front that imposes the greatest immediate cost while detaching the unresolved fronts from the wider political and bargaining context that had linked them. Such an agreement may end a war on one front while redistributing the conditions of conflict across the others [1].

Egypt did not enter the peace process as an instrument of Israeli design. Recovering Sinai, ending the burdens of war, and rebuilding the relationship with Washington were Egyptian objectives in their own right. But Egypt also entered the negotiations with a form of leverage no other Arab front possessed: its army had crossed the canal, established a position on recovered Egyptian territory, and demonstrated that the military balance could not be managed without addressing Sinai itself. The thing Egypt could threaten and the thing it sought to recover were closely connected, making its leverage directly convertible into a sovereignty-restoring agreement [1].

The separation of fronts did not emerge because Egypt simply abandoned the other fronts. It emerged from overlapping constraints: [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) refused to deal with the Palestine Liberation Organization, Jordan declined to act as a Palestinian surrogate, and the PLO rejected the Camp David framework. A prior U.S.-Israeli memorandum on Sinai II had already stated that Egypt's commitments stood on their own and were not conditional on developments between Israel and the other Arab states — formally ensuring that the Egyptian track could advance even if the other fronts did not [1].

## Partial Peace Redistributes War

The most prominent result of separating the fronts during Camp David was not that it made future conflict inevitable but that it made comprehensive peace harder to achieve and deepened political divisions among Arab states at the very moment when a wider settlement would have required greater coordination. Egypt recovered Sinai while the remaining fronts were left without the Arab state most capable of linking their grievances, military weight, and bargaining position [1].

This was the opportunity Iran's ideology was later able to exploit. Iran did not create the Palestinian cause, Syrian claims, or Lebanese resistance, but the separation of the fronts gave Tehran an opening it could not have created alone: unresolved national causes that were no longer tied together by an Arab state capable of fighting for them and bargaining over them as one regional issue. Iran found its clearest opening in [Lebanon]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/lebanon.html) after the 1982 Israeli invasion, building Hizballah as a resistance force linked to its revolutionary project; through its alliance with Syria it expanded its reach to the Palestinian front. What had once been centered on Arab states capable of fighting and bargaining over national territory increasingly became a network of armed actors operating across fronts where sovereignty remained unresolved [1].

## The Present Parallel

Abbas argues that the same pattern is reasserting itself in the negotiations surrounding the [Iran Ceasefire MoU]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html) — sharper now that the memorandum has begun to unravel. Israel rejected the notion that the MoU should unify the fronts within a single agreement and include Lebanon in both the ceasefire and any future settlement. When the Trump administration opened separate negotiations on Lebanon, the result preserved the distinction Israel insisted on: the Iranian track could move forward while Lebanon was dealt with on separate terms [1].

The difference from Camp David is that Iran's leverage — unlike Egypt's — cannot be converted into a sovereign Lebanese claim. That makes Lebanon easier to separate, and more dangerous once separated: treated not as a state whose sovereignty must be restored but as a security problem to be managed, with Israeli withdrawal conditional on Hizballah's disarmament rather than governed by a defined timetable. The Camp David analogy is not mechanical — Lebanon is not Sinai — but the principle is clear: peace agreements endure when they close conflict without diminishing sovereignty, and fail when they defer it.
