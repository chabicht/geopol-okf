# Background

On December 26, 2025, Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu announced Israel's official recognition of Somaliland — the breakaway region of Somalia that declared independence in 1991 but has remained internationally unrecognized for nearly 35 years. The move was the first of its kind by any nation and signals a potential inflection point in the geopolitics of the [Horn of Africa]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/africa.html) and the Red Sea corridor.

Somaliland was colonized by the British (British Somaliland) while the rest of Somalia was Italian Somaliland. After decolonization in 1960, Somaliland declared independence before voluntarily unifying with Somalia to form the Somali Republic. The union was deeply unequal, with political power concentrated in Mogadishu and northern provinces marginalized. Following the 1969 Siad Barre coup, repression intensified, culminating in the aerial bombardment of Hargeisa in the late 1980s — among the most violent episodes in Somalia's history, with as many as 100,000 killed. When the Somali state collapsed in 1991, Somaliland reclaimed its colonial-era borders and declared independence again.

Despite meeting many functional criteria of statehood — competitive elections, peaceful transfers of power, relative internal stability — Somaliland remained excluded from formal diplomatic recognition, limiting its access to international finance, binding security agreements, and global institutions.

# Current Situation

## Strategic Motivation

Israel's recognition is not an endorsement of Somaliland's democratic credentials but a strategic investment in a rapidly militarizing region. The Red Sea has become increasingly contested due to the conflicts in Gaza and Yemen, where Houthi operations have directly threatened Israeli and Western interests. Recognition creates the option of future access to territory near the Bab el-Mandeb Strait — a chokepoint central to global trade and operations against the Houthis. Israeli intelligence channels had been in contact with Somaliland counterparts for more than a year prior to recognition.

## Emerging Blocs

Israel's move has solidified rival alignments in the Horn of Africa:

- **Israel-UAE-Ethiopia alignment**: A loose bloc centered on Somaliland and Red Sea access. The UAE's DP World manages the port of Berbera (51% ownership); Ethiopia has explored Berbera as an alternative to Djibouti. Addis Ababa signed a January 2024 MoU to secure sea access through Berbera, signaling it would consider recognizing Somaliland.
- **Saudi Arabia-Egypt-Eritrea alignment**: Positioned as defenders of Somali sovereignty and Red Sea security. Saudi Arabia's Red Sea Gateway Terminal is expected to sign a 30-year concession for Djibouti's port of Tadjourah ($180M initial investment). Egypt and Eritrea openly condemned the Ethiopia-Somaliland MoU, fearing Ethiopian naval projection into the Red Sea.

## Turkey's Role

[Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) has emerged as Somalia's most important external patron, providing military training, arms, infrastructure investment, and diplomatic support — largely at the expense of the UAE, whose relationship with Somalia deteriorated over port concessions and a 2018 cash-seizure incident. Turkey built a large military training base in Mogadishu and signed major port and infrastructure deals in 2024, becoming Somalia's primary external security and economic partner. This reinforces the Israel-Turkey rivalry in the region.

## Great-Power Implications

Somaliland maintains relations with [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html), offering a rare diplomatic foothold in Africa that could complicate [China's]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) strategic environment near the Red Sea. In 2025, Hargeisa approached Washington offering military basing and critical minerals in exchange for recognition. While the US already has a base in Djibouti and sees little benefit in upsetting the status quo, the Taiwan connection could change the calculus — linking Middle Eastern security competition with [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html) strategic rivalry.

# Analysis

### Red Sea as Extension of Middle East Rivalry

The Horn of Africa is no longer a peripheral theater. The Israel-Turkey rivalry that defines the new [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) security architecture now extends to the Red Sea corridor. Israel's recognition of Somaliland and Turkey's military buildup in Somalia represent competing projections of influence through proxy states and port access. The UAE plays both sides — working with Israel on Somaliland while trying to maintain relations with Somalia — reflecting the layered, transactional nature of contemporary security partnerships. See [Layered Security]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/layered-security.html).

### Port Infrastructure as Geopolitical Currency

Control of port infrastructure is the primary vector of competition: DP World at Berbera, Saudi Arabia's Red Sea Gateway Terminal at Tadjourah, Turkey's port deals in Mogadishu, and China Merchants Port at Doraleh (Djibouti). Each port concession represents a strategic foothold that translates commercial investment into military and diplomatic leverage. The pattern mirrors the broader [great power competition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/great-power-competition.html) in which infrastructure investment is the currency of influence.

### Somaliland as Link Between Theaters

Somaliland's Taiwan connection makes it a node linking Middle Eastern security competition with Indo-Pacific strategic rivalry. Beijing maintains a military base in Djibouti; Somaliland's openness to alternative partnerships introduces the possibility of Western or allied arrangements that could constrain Chinese freedom of action near the Red Sea. The international community may find Somaliland increasingly difficult to ignore as it becomes implicated in multiple overlapping rivalries.
