# Background

The [PRC]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) has conducted increasingly frequent blockade exercises around [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html) since Taiwanese President William Lai Ching-te's inauguration in May 2024. Justice Mission 2025, held December 29-30, 2025, was the second such exercise of that year and the most operationally detailed to date. See [Taiwan Standoff]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) and [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html).

# What Happened

- The PLA deployed 18 PLAN vessels from the Eastern Theater Command, 14 of which positioned north, south, and east of Taiwan, with four operating 160-170 nautical miles east of Taiwan's southernmost point [1].
- 201 air sorties were conducted, 125 of which crossed the median line between the PRC and Taiwan [1].
- 14 China Coast Guard vessels operated around Taiwan, Matsu, and Wuqiu — the first time CCG ships patrolled around Pratas Island during a blockade exercise [1].
- Eight exercise zones surrounded Taiwan's main island, corresponding to major port cities: Keelung (north), Kaohsiung (south), and Hualien (east) [1].
- The exercise simulated interdiction of Taiwanese energy imports and blockade of major port cities [1].
- H-6 bombers deployed for strikes east of the first island chain; the Type 075 LHD *Hainan* transited the Bashi Channel for counter-intervention operations — the first exercise to include Type 075 vessels [1].
- PHL-16 multiple launch rocket systems fired live rounds north and south of Taiwan, capable of reaching all of Taiwan from the PRC's east coast [1].
- No PLAN aircraft carriers or Type 055 destroyers participated, suggesting the exercise practiced only the inner layer of a larger multi-domain blockade [1].
- The exercise was announced without warning, adding a deception and surprise dimension [1].

# Analysis

## Blockade Doctrine in Practice

The ISW assesses that Justice Mission 2025 rehearsed operational elements of a campaign to isolate Taiwan while using the rehearsal to enhance PRC political and psychological pressure. The exercise emphasized naval and coast guard assets to enforce a blockade and rehearsed counter-intervention operations against U.S. forces. The absence of aircraft carriers indicates the exercise practiced only part of a larger operation — carrier groups would form the outer layer in a real contingency, blocking intervention from the Western Pacific [1].

## Coast Guard as Blockade Enforcement

The 14 CCG vessels operated near PLAN ships, suggesting coordination. The PRC likely intends to use the CCG to intercept civilian vessels under "law enforcement" pretexts while the PLAN enforces anti-access/area denial against foreign militaries. CCG ships came within 1.6 nautical miles of Wuqiu and 1.3 nautical miles of Matsu — within prohibited waters — demonstrating willingness to breach Taiwan's defensive perimeters [1].

## Coercive Psychological Warfare

The exercise served coercive purposes: intimidating Taiwan's DPP government, deterring [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) from supporting Taiwan, and demonstrating the PRC's capacity to sever energy imports. PRC propaganda emphasized the blockade aspects, but the exercise may also have rehearsed combining blockade with decapitation strikes or amphibious assault against Taiwan's east coast [1].

## Implications for PLA Adaptation

The exercise occurred amid debates about the [PLA]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html)'s ability to learn from contemporary warfare. ISW analysis suggests the PLA's pre-existing investments in expensive, sophisticated platforms are skewing its lessons from [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) and Middle East conflicts, where cheap attritable systems dominate. Xi's purges and institutional rigidity discourage adaptation, potentially creating an asymmetric advantage for Taiwan [2].
