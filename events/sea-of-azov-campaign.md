# Background

The Sea of Azov is a small, shallow body of water connected to the Black Sea only through the narrow Strait of Kerch (spanned by the Kerch Bridge between [Crimea]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/crimea-isolation-campaign.html) and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html)). Since the full-scale invasion in 2022, Russia has controlled every inch of the Azov coastline, making it a secure logistical corridor — a "Russian lake." With road and rail communications to Crimea degraded and the Russians wary of overusing the Kerch Bridge for fuel, sea transport became the primary alternative for resupplying the peninsula [1].

The Sea of Azov also serves as a conduit for stolen [Ukrainian]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) grain and Russian grain exports — up to a quarter of Russia-controlled grain shipments pass through Azov. Oil shipments also transit the sea, and with global sanctions failing to reduce Russian oil exports, Ukraine has employed "kinetic sanctions" — drone strikes on vessels — to impose costs directly [1].

# What Happened

Over several weeks in early-mid July 2026, [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) dramatically accelerated a campaign to sink or damage Russian-controlled shipping in the Sea of Azov using both air and sea drones:

- **28 vessels attacked** by July 11, including 21 oil tankers, four tugboats, two cargo vessels, and one specialized dredging vessel [1].
- UAV (air drone) attacks target the bridges of vessels — crippling but not always destroying them, rendering them uncontrollable while preserving the option of full destruction via sea drones [1].
- The campaign is part of the Crimea "switch off" operation to isolate the peninsula [1].
- On July 10, Russia forbade all new traffic through the Kerch Strait between the Sea of Azov and the Black Sea. There is no indication when the restriction will be relaxed [1].
- If attacks remain at current levels and Russia cannot develop countermeasures, the Sea of Azov will remain functionally closed to shipping [1].

# Analysis

## Strategic Significance

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) argues the Sea of Azov campaign deserves far more coverage than it is receiving. The campaign is strategically impactful because it simultaneously:

- Strangles Russian supply lines to Crimea (the only remaining alternative to the degraded Kerch Bridge and road/rail routes)
- Disrupts Russian grain and fuel exports at a time when the Russian economy is under severe strain
- Demonstrates that Ukraine can deny Russia use of a body of water Moscow has controlled since 2022
- Extends the [Crimea isolation campaign]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/crimea-isolation-campaign.html) from the Black Sea into Azov

O'Brien notes that the Russians do not appear to have a working counter to this campaign. The open question is whether Russia can change the dynamic — if not, the campaign will further strangle supplies to Crimea and damage Russian grain and fuel shipments [1].

## A Ukrainian Lake

O'Brien frames the strategic inversion: "Using air and sea drones, the Ukrainians have turned the Sea of Azov into a Ukrainian lake." The body of water that was supposed to be a Russian lake — with every inch of coastline under Russian control — is now functionally closed to Russian shipping by Ukrainian drone power [1].

## Connection to the Revolution in Military Affairs

O'Brien identifies the Sea of Azov campaign as an example of the broader [Revolution in Military Affairs]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/revolution-in-military-affairs.html) — the de-humanizing of the battlefield through autonomous and remotely-controlled systems. The campaign demonstrates how cheap drones can deny a major power control of a strategic waterway without requiring a conventional naval force [1].
