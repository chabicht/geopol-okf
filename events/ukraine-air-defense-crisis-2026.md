# Background

Russia's ballistic missile campaign against [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) has escalated dramatically in 2026, exploiting Ukraine's depletion of Patriot interceptor missiles. The [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) under [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) ended all US military aid (as opposed to sales) to Ukraine upon taking office in 2025, and has slow-walked or blocked resupply of Patriot interceptors. At the [NATO Ankara Summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html), Trump made a vague pledge to give Ukraine a "license" to manufacture Patriots — a process that will take years, if it materializes at all [1][2].

# What Happened

## Scale of the Escalation

Russia's monthly ballistic missile launches have roughly tripled year-over-year: 28 missiles in a comparable month a year ago versus approximately 70+ now. January 2026 set a record of 91 ballistic missiles in a single month, and Russia has sustained a high tempo since. Attacks are expected to increase significantly next winter when they can do the most damage [1].

## Interception Rates Falling Behind

Ukraine intercepted about 89% of all aerial threats (drones, cruise missiles) in June 2026 overall, but only 40% of ballistic missiles. Ballistic missile interception requires Patriot interceptors — and on July 6, Ukraine's air force admitted they had none or almost none left [1].

## Notable Recent Strikes

- **June 2**: 33 Iskander-M ballistic missiles fired alongside cruise missiles and 650+ drones; only 11 of 41 ballistic missiles (27%) were shot down.
- **June 15**: 34 Iskander-M ballistic missiles in a combined strike; roughly half intercepted. Killed 5, injured 35 in Kyiv.
- **July 6**: 29 ballistic missiles fired at Kyiv; all 29 struck their targets — part of a barrage that killed at least 19-27 people.
- **July 8**: Third ballistic missile strike on Kyiv in six days, killing 4.
- Recent weekend strikes used Iskander-M/S-400 ballistic missiles from the Bryansk area alongside cruise missiles and drones [1].

## Cumulative Toll

At least 60 deaths in Kyiv and its region since the start of July alone [1].

# Analysis

## Trump's Strategic Gift to Putin

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) argues that Trump has provided Putin with a massive strategic gift: at least one more whole winter, and possibly two or three, of unrestricted ballistic missile bombardment. The mathematics are damning — Japan, after many years of licensing, only produces 30 Patriot PAC-3 interceptors (the ones Ukraine needs) annually. A Ukrainian license to manufacture Patriots, even if granted, would take years to yield meaningful production [1].

O'Brien's assessment: Trump does not actually want to help Ukraine. He wants to be *seen* to be supportive because Ukraine is doing better and Putin is struggling. The vague Patriot license pledge at the [NATO summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html) was a performative gesture that, in substance, means no existing Patriot interceptors from US stocks — gifting Putin years to hammer Ukraine with ballistic missiles [1][2].

## The Patriot License Illusion

O'Brien identifies critical unanswered questions about Trump's Patriot license pledge:

1. **Will the license actually be granted?** No update since Trump's statement.
2. **What will be covered?** Trump spoke only of "Patriots" — which could mean PAC-2 interceptors (which Germany licenses) rather than the PAC-3s Ukraine urgently needs.
3. **Manufacturing timeline**: Even with a license, it will take years to reach reasonable production rates. The process is extremely complex, requiring many sub-contractors of advanced components with existing long order books.
4. **No consultation with manufacturers**: Trump admitted he had never discussed this with US arms manufacturers who would have to be involved — suggesting a spur-of-the-moment decision for plaudits, not a worked-out pledge [2].

## Russian Awareness

The fact that Russia is upping its strike rate — three attacks on Kyiv in six days — precisely as Ukraine is running out of Patriot interceptors shows Russian awareness of the help Trump has provided and will continue to provide. For at least a year, if not longer, Russia can do extensive damage with ballistic missiles knowing Ukraine has few options to shoot them down [1].
