# Background

In August 2025, [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) and [Putin]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/putin.html) held a summit in Anchorage, Alaska. The meeting was positioned as an opportunity to reset US-Russia relations and potentially advance peace negotiations in the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html). The summit concluded without any meaningful diplomatic resolution and nothing was committed to paper [1].

# Current Situation

## The Term That Exists Only in Moscow's Vocabulary

Russian media branded the post-summit atmosphere the "spirit of Anchorage" (*dukh Ankoradzha*) — a phrase with no American counterpart. In February 2026 Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov described it as a "set of mutual understandings" between the US and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), yet pointed to no written document, and the American side offered no parallel account. The Russian vocabulary itself is elastic: Peskov's "set of understandings" (*nabor ponimanii*) and the "preliminary results" (*predvaritelnie rezultati*) of the face-to-face meetings are formulations that, absent any agreed text, each side is free to interpret as it wishes [1].

With no agreement to hold Russia to, the "spirit" functions in Russian discourse as a flexible propaganda instrument. It is invoked either to credit the Americans for supposedly listening to the Russian position, or to accuse the Trump administration of "betraying" Anchorage by continuing to support [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) — a commitment that was never actually made [1].

## Collapse

The construct eroded quickly. By October 2025 Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov was already arguing that the US had shifted its rhetoric on the Ukraine war, attributing this to [European]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html) pressure. By June 2026 Lavrov was suggesting, in a notably regretful register, that the Alaska summit may have been a US device to buy time for Kyiv's rearmament. The [Iran war]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) briefly benefited Moscow via higher oil prices and diverted American attention, but the same distraction also consumed Trump's bandwidth for the Putin relationship — a source of frustration in Moscow [1].

## Peskov's Linguistic Shift

On 6 July 2026, the eve of the [NATO Ankara Summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html), Peskov told state outlet Vesti that the "special operation" in Ukraine had become a war, attributing the change to Western intervention. [Lukashenka]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/lukashenka.html) repeated the framing the next day — a break with his prior caution — interpreted as a concession to Moscow at a moment when [Zelensky]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/zelensky.html) was pressing him to disable relay systems supporting Russia's drone operations [1].

# Analysis

[RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) treats Anchorage as a case study in US-Russian diplomatic misalignment. The institute argues that Russian statecraft layers an abstract, quasi-religious vocabulary of "mission," "fate," and "destiny" over concrete doctrine and economic data — a register that is hard for counterparts to engage with on ordinary diplomatic terms. The "spirit of Anchorage" illustrates the resulting trap: Moscow mints an ambiguous verbal framework, loads it with whatever content the current propaganda line requires, and then charges the other party with betraying a commitment that was never recorded. The construct is thus less a diplomatic outcome than a narrative instrument designed to keep the counterparty perpetually on the defensive [1].
