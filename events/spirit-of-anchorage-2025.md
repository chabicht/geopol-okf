# Background

In August 2025, [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) and [Putin]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/putin.html) held a summit in Anchorage, Alaska. The meeting was positioned as an opportunity to reset US-Russia relations and potentially advance peace negotiations in the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html). The summit concluded without any meaningful diplomatic resolution and nothing was committed to paper [1].

# Current Situation

## The Term That Exists Only in Moscow's Vocabulary

Following the Anchorage summit, Russian media alighted on the term "the spirit of Anchorage" (*dukh Ankoradzha*) to describe the general atmosphere of agreement and collegiality they felt it contained. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov referred to it in February 2026 as a vague "set of mutual understandings" between the US and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), but there is nothing tangible to hang this understanding on — no sense from the American side that they have similar understandings, or the same interpretation of what this concretely refers to. The same vague allusions to "a set of understandings" (*nabor ponimanii*), or "preliminary results" (*predvaritelnie rezultati*) of the face-to-face meetings further obfuscate the picture, as there was no final written document after Anchorage to base this on. Each side came away with their own interpretations of the outcome [1].

Instead, the so-called spirit of Anchorage became in Russia a catch-all term to refer to the general ambiguity around its relationship with the US. As there is no formal agreement to hold Russia to, it can mean whatever Russian media outlets wish to weaponise it for — variously the Americans' apparently pleasing willingness to listen to the Russian point of view, or reproach the Trump administration for its support of [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) and betrayal of the essence of Anchorage [1].

## Collapse

It did not take long for the floridity around Anchorage to dissipate. By October 2025, Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov was noting that the US had changed its rhetoric on the Ukraine war — allegedly under pressure from [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html). More recently in June 2026, Lavrov maintained with an oddly regretful tone that he did not "even want to think" that the Alaska summit had been expressly designed by the Americans to buy time for Kyiv to rebuild its military potential. While the [Iran war]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) had been hailed as a boon for the Russians due to the temporary spike in oil prices and shifted American attention, that distraction also left less time for Trump to dedicate to his relationship with Putin — a cause of disappointment for Moscow [1].

## Peskov's Linguistic Shift

On 6 July 2026, on the eve of the [NATO Ankara Summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html), Peskov gave a significant interview to government-controlled Vesti in which he directly stated the "special operation" in Ukraine had now morphed into a war, expressly due to the intervention of western countries. [Lukashenka]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/lukashenka.html) echoed Peskov's statement the following day — something he had historically avoided doing, but designed to appease Moscow given pressure from [Zelensky]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/zelensky.html) to disable relay systems used to support Russia's drone activity [1].

# Analysis

[RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) frames the Anchorage episode as a useful example of where the Russians and Americans continually talk at cross purposes. Part of the problem in dealing with Russia is that it oscillates between hard doctrines, specific strategies and hard economic data, while at the same time overlaying this with a much more abstract and occasionally religiously-themed framework about its "mission," its "fate," or "destiny" in the world, which are much harder to grapple with in international affairs. The "spirit of Anchorage" — a diplomatic construct with no formal basis — exemplifies this pattern: Russia creates an ambiguous verbal framework, assigns it whatever meaning suits the current propaganda need, and then blames the other party for "betraying" a commitment that was never made [1].
