# Background

Latin America's two largest oil corporations — Petroleos Mexicanos (Pemex) and Petrobras — have historically operated independently within their respective national markets. The growing pressure of the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html)'s consequences on energy transitions and supplier diversification, combined with Washington's renewed assertiveness in the Western Hemisphere, has created conditions for deeper energy cooperation among Global South states [1].

# Current Situation

[Mexico]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/mexico.html) and [Brazil]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/brazil.html) signed a memorandum of understanding establishing strategic and technical cooperation in the hydrocarbons sector. The MoU covers:

- Technology sharing in exploration and production, industrial processes, and regulatory frameworks
- Refining, petrochemicals, fertilizers, gas processing, energy efficiency, carbon capture, and environmental protection
- A project to explore creating biofuels from maguey
- A study on fertilizer production

## Strategic Rationale

For Pemex, the agreement aligns with its strategic plan for 2025-2035, which sets a production target of 1.8 million barrels of oil per day from the current 1.6-1.7 million bpd. Petrobras' deep- and ultra-deep-water technology is expected to help Pemex expand opportunities for exploring and exploiting new oil deposits in the Gulf of Mexico and revitalizing existing fields. ExxonMobil's evaluation of a potential acquisition of Woodside Energy Group, Pemex's partner on its only deep-water extraction project, makes this agreement even more important for Mexico's strategic autonomy [1].

For Petrobras, opening to exploration of fields beyond Brazil's waters offers additional expertise in production from mature oil fields and in industrial processes like refining, petrochemicals, and fertilizers. Brazil is the world's fourth-largest fertilizer importer, making the fertilizer production study particularly beneficial [1].

## Structural Significance

The MoU is emblematic of a larger trend: the diversification of strategic resource exploration, exploitation, and refinement, and the pursuit of Latin American cooperation. Mexico and Brazil (and to some extent Uruguay) are the only countries in the region pursuing strategic autonomy outside the political sphere promoted by Washington. While Mexico's pursuit of strategic autonomy aims to prevent domination by northern powers, Brazil seeks greater strategic depth and a dynamic, robust economy [1].

# Analysis

The memorandum presents a positive outlook but faces structural risks. It is valid for two years (with a possible extension) but does not constitute a binding investment commitment or a joint venture. Pemex and Petrobras are state-owned institutions, so the agreement's future depends on domestic political considerations and the state of the global economy. The MoU reflects the rapid diversification of strategic resource exploration and exploitation aimed at reducing vulnerabilities in an unstable international environment, alongside a stronger push for cooperation among countries of the Global South [1].
