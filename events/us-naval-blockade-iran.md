# Background

After the initial kinetic phase of the [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html), the United States imposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports — a counter-blockade designed to pressure Iran by cutting off its oil exports. This created a dual-blockade architecture: Iran's closure of Hormuz blocked inbound traffic, while the US outer-ring blockade blocked Iranian exports. The strategy drew historical parallels to Napoleonic blockade warfare, where blockades generate adaptive responses that ultimately undermine their effectiveness [1].

# What Happened

- The US imposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports, creating an outer-ring interception capability alongside the existing Hormuz-focused operations [2].
- US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent implemented secondary sanctions on Chinese banks facilitating Iranian oil trade, extending the blockade into the financial system [2].
- Iran threatened to block the Red Sea in response, expanding the conflict's geographic scope [2].
- The US struck the South Pars gas field — Iran's largest — in an escalation that also affected [Qatar]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/qatar.html), which shares the field [2].
- The USS George H.W. Bush carrier group was deployed to enforce the blockade [2].
- The Pentagon sought help from automakers to produce military vehicles, signaling industrial capacity constraints [2].

# Analysis

[The Honest Sorcerer]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-honest-sorcerer.html) argues the counter-blockade revealed the war's true objective: controlling oil flow to [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), not uranium enrichment. By blockading Iranian ports while imposing secondary sanctions on Chinese banks, the US was attempting to sever the energy pipeline sustaining the DragonBear axis [2].

[Endtropy]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/endtropy.html) draws the historical parallel to Napoleon's Continental System: a blockade designed to deny the adversary resources instead generates adaptive trade networks, smuggling, and financial workarounds that ultimately weaken the blockading power. The US counter-blockade accelerated the dedollarization of Chinese-Iranian trade, as the secondary sanctions on Chinese banks pushed trade into non-dollar channels [1].

[Andrew Davidson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) documents the blockade's cumulative effect during the renewed fighting: the reinstated blockade is constraining seaborne oil exports that provide [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) with critical foreign currency and state revenue. Overland routes through the North-South corridor can only partially offset these losses. As the margin narrows, limited attacks become less effective because they impose lower costs on Washington than Tehran is absorbing in return — making the blockade a central component of the "both sides on the clock" exhaustion dynamic. Iran's clearest means of changing the calculation would be to attack Gulf energy infrastructure or expand maritime disruption to the Bab el-Mandeb strait, but either option risks uniting its adversaries [3].

## The July 15 Blockade Reversal

[Heather Cox Richardson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/heather-cox-richardson.html) documents that on July 15, 2026, Trump reversed the reimposed blockade — declaring that the U.S. would become the "GUARDIAN OF THE HORMUZ STRAIT" and then, within 24 hours, claiming that Gulf allies told him they would rather invest directly in the U.S. than pay tolls. The reversal underscores the erratic character of the blockade instrument: announced as a strategic commitment, then abandoned when the cost crystallized. The same day, Trump convened a Situation Room meeting to discuss "new plans for devastating strikes" against Iran — shifting the instrument of pressure from economic blockade back to kinetic escalation [4].

## The July 14 Reinstatement

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documents that the blockade was reinstated on July 14, 2026 — the third consecutive night of coastal strikes — as Trump turned back to the economic instrument alongside the kinetic campaign, threatening to hit infrastructure (bridges, power plants) next. An empty tanker ignoring the blockade was disabled with Hellfire missiles on July 15. The reinstatement-reversal sequence within 48 hours illustrates the blockade's status as an instrument of impulse rather than strategy in Trump's hands [5].
