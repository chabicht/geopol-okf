# Background

Frontier AI models are approaching capability thresholds that pose national security risks across cyber, biological, chemical, radiological, and nuclear domains. As of 2025, frontier models were already "outperforming 94% of expert virologists" according to benchmark studies — setting a trajectory for continued rapid expansion of capabilities in the biological sciences. The day when frontier models can exceed critical capability thresholds by providing substantial uplift to the chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear development capabilities of moderately resourced state programs is approaching faster than institutional adaptation can match [1].

# What Happened

- **June 12, 2026**: The U.S. government forced Anthropic to take down its Mythos 5 model and its safeguarded form, Fable 5. The government action was triggered by the model's cyber capabilities, which were deemed hazardous enough to warrant intervention.
- Anthropic disputed the action, calling the trigger a "narrow, nonuniversal jailbreak" rather than evidence of systemic danger.
- Prior to the takedown, Anthropic had created the Project Glasswing collaboration to give cyber defenders controlled access to the model.
- **June 30, 2026**: Following agreement with the government, Anthropic restored access to Mythos and Fable [1].

The episode exposed a "pickup game" dynamic: absent a shared factual basis, standard processes, and structured public-private collaboration, government and industry invented the rules in the moment. Public-private trust in frontier AI is running low following the seemingly punitive and arbitrary way the government administered control [1].

# Analysis

## The AI Threat Fusion Center Proposal

Rebecca Hersman argues that the answer lies in more bridges — not higher walls — between government and the nation's frontier AI companies. She proposes an AI Threat Fusion Center: a standing body charged with intelligence coordination and public-private information fusion, established in partnership with frontier AI companies, built on mutual benefit, strong civil liberty protections, and sound governance [1].

The center would fill a specific gap: structured sharing of classified, threat-sensitive information on AI-related security risks that requires the communication, computational systems, facilities, and authorities of the intelligence community. It would conduct day-to-day sharing of company-sensitive and classified threat data, develop threat-informed risk assessments prioritizing high-risk sectors such as CBRN, and conduct classified evaluations of frontier models [1].

## Beyond Cyber to Biological Threats

The Mythos event was cyber-focused, but the next threshold-buster may be biological. Cyber attacks are common and fairly predictable, and organizational structures for sharing information at the unclassified level are more developed. Demonstrable use cases for biological risks are rare, and pre-established mechanisms for government-industry information sharing do not exist. Dual-use complexities and dependencies on highly sensitive threat information further complicate the terrain. As companies approach critical risk thresholds in the biological arena, the U.S. should expect to sit uncomfortably in a complex window of ambiguity — anxieties high, shared context low [1].

The proposal calls for starting with biological risks given the relative consensus that AI-enabled CBRN threats are on the horizon, building the partnership and infrastructure now rather than after the next crisis arrives [1].
