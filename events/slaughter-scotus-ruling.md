# Background

The *Slaughter* case was brought on behalf of an FTC commissioner arbitrarily fired by [Donald Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html). The Roberts Supreme Court used the case to overturn 90 years of precedent, ruling that the president has effectively unlimited power to direct, restructure, or gut regulatory agencies created by Congress with specific mandates. The Court stripped regulatory agencies of their independence from presidential whim, allowing Trump to fire civil servants who do not comply with his directives or to entirely defang agencies so they cannot serve their statutory function. [1]

# Current Situation

## Dictatorial Power Over Regulatory Machinery

The ruling grants [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) what [Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html) calls "dictatorial power" over the regulatory machinery of the US government. For any agency created by Congress — the FTC, EPA, FDA, NLRB, and others — Trump is now free to redirect its mission, fire non-compliant civil servants, or hollow it out entirely. The decision eliminates government by professional civil servants in favor of "government by henchmen and lackeys." [1]

## Fed Carve-Out

The Court preserved independence for the Federal Reserve alone, a carve-out Krugman reads as deeply hypocritical. There is no constitutional argument under which monetary policy independence is more sacred than independence of antitrust, environmental, food and drug, or air safety regulation. The carve-out is explained by the Court's fear of market reaction — the Wall Street Journal editorial board acknowledged that the Chief Justice and Justice Kavanaugh "simply don't trust Mr. Trump to run monetary policy." The implication: only those with large stock portfolios are protected from Trump's institutional destruction. [1]

## Partisan Application

Krugman emphasizes that the power granted is to *Trump specifically*, not to the presidency in general. The Court is "utterly partisan" — the moment a Democrat takes office, it will instantly decide that the president has almost no discretionary power. [1]

## Corruption Enablement

The ruling is fundamentally about enabling corruption. Trump's firing decisions have been motivated by personal retribution, prejudice, and greed — not policy disagreement. The decision frees Trump to grant regulatory favors to those who enrich him, his family, and his cronies: directing the FTC to approve mergers for friendly corporations, directing the EPA to waive pollution rules for allies, and gutting the NLRB to destroy workers' rights. [1]

# Analysis

Krugman frames *Slaughter* as the judicial capstone of oligarchic capture. The ruling arrives amid soaring corporate profits and rising monopoly power — profits as a share of national income keep climbing, reflecting "increased monopoly profits in an age of enshittification and the destruction of workers' rights." The major beneficiaries of this trend — America's oligarch class — are precisely those sharing wealth with Trump in return for favors. The Court has now removed the legal restraints on the favor-granting machinery. [1]

The event connects to the broader [ideology and democracy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ideology-and-democracy.html) crisis: a partisan Court enabling dictatorship while the public is distracted by manufactured debates over "socialism." The real radical movement, Krugman argues, is the right-wing effort to dismantle both social democracy and democracy itself. [1][2]
