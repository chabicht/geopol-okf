# Background

The [June 17 Versailles Memorandum]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html) ended the kinetic phase of the [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html), producing a fragile ceasefire. The arrangement was never a settlement — it paused the exchange while leaving [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s coercive infrastructure intact. Post-MoU escalation events (IRGC drone attacks on commercial vessels, US reprisal strikes, attacks on Bahrain facilities) confirmed the fragility from the outset.

# Current Situation

On July 7, three commercial vessels — including a Qatari LNG tanker and a Saudi crude carrier — were attacked in and near the Strait of Hormuz, with a tanker set ablaze off [Oman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/oman.html). Within hours, the US Treasury revoked the sixty-day oil waiver (General License X1 bars any new Iranian oil transactions from July 7, with wind-down only through July 17). In the early hours of July 8, US forces struck monitoring and surveillance centres and military bases across southern [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), despite a signaled pause for the funeral of slain Supreme Leader Khamenei, whose procession drew more than two million mourners in Najaf. Iran claimed retaliatory strikes against "85 US military installations"; air-raid sirens sounded in [Bahrain]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/bahrain.html) and [Kuwait]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/kuwait.html). Tehran invoked Article 51, declared the American strikes a violation of the Memorandum's first paragraph, and warned Gulf neighbours that hosting US operations constitutes complicity [5][6].

At the [NATO summit in Ankara]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html), [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) pronounced the Memorandum "over" and dealing with Tehran a "waste of time." He escalated both rhetoric and threat architecture: calling Iranian authorities "sick" and "scum," threatening to "take over" [Kharg Island]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), Iran's principal crude export terminal, and naming desalination plants and power facilities as potential targets. He floated re-imposing a naval blockade applying "only" to Iran, backed by NATO allies dispatching minesweepers to the strait [5][6].

Around July 13, 2026, the escalation deepened further. Trump reinstated the blockade; Iran resumed drone and missile strikes on shipping. Iran, emboldened by its success in defying America, demanded sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz. Trump declared that *he* owns the Strait and will collect 20 percent tolls on all shipping traffic — a proposal that would be wildly illegal and impossible to enforce. He subsequently shifted to demanding that countries invest in the United States as a condition for passage, which is equally illegal and unenforceable [1][2].

## The Refining Capacity Constraint

[Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html) argues that the ceasefire collapse will do less economic damage than expected — but not because Hormuz is stable. Rather, a structural disconnect has emerged between events at the Strait and the energy prices that matter: the world faces a global shortage of refining capacity, meaning that even if the Strait fully reopened, the additional crude could not be turned into usable products fast enough to matter.

Roughly 10% of world refining capacity is now out of operation. The primary cause is the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html): [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html)'s drone campaign against Russian oil refineries has been astonishingly effective, forcing Russia to ban gasoline and jet fuel exports and potentially begin importing refined products. This refining shortage means the "effective price" of oil to consumers (weighted gasoline + diesel prices) has remained far above pre-war levels even as crude prices nearly returned to pre-war levels. The consumer price rose approximately $75/barrel versus a $45/barrel rise in crude — demand destruction from high consumer prices kept crude demand depressed even before the latest collapse [1].

# Analysis

## Market Repricing and the Fertilizer Cascade

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) documents the sharpest market repricing since the Hormuz war began. Brent surged nearly 8 percent to over $80 per barrel — its steepest rise since the ceasefire began — while WTI approached $76, the Dow shed more than 800 points, and 10-year Treasury yields spiked toward 4.6 percent. Daily transits through the strait collapsed from a pre-war average of 125 vessels to just 16, with the US Navy-led Joint Maritime Information Center raising transit risk to its highest tier, "severe." Gulf tanker charter rates jumped from under $200,000 to roughly $300,000 per day [5].

The fertilizer front is where the rupture becomes systemic. The European Parliament approved up to €1.5 billion in aid to offset surging fertilizer prices driven explicitly by Hormuz disruptions: farmers become eligible for reimbursement of up to 80 percent of additional costs, member states may raise advance direct payments from 70 to 75 percent with immediate disbursement, the Commission mobilizes €500 million, and member states contribute up to €1 billion. Washington is intervening in parallel: Trump issued an emergency proclamation suspending anti-dumping duties on Moroccan phosphate imports, while the USDA launched the $500 million FIELDS program. The FTC has opened an industry-wide probe into precipitous price increases. Agriculture has joined semiconductors and energy as a securitized sector, with Washington and Brussels abandoning market orthodoxy for state-directed supply resilience [5].

## Hormuz as Cold War 2.0 Pressure Gauge

Tchakarova frames Hormuz as the pressure gauge of [Cold War 2.0]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html): every escalation transfers wealth toward the [DragonBear]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html)'s discounted-crude arbitrage — [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) absorbing sanctioned Iranian barrels — while inflating the West's rates, food costs, and fiscal burdens. The emergency fertilizer interventions mark a threshold: the allocation crisis has migrated from the Gulf into Western budgets. [Morocco]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/morocco.html)'s sudden phosphate leverage previews the coming scramble for resource-holding middle powers. For the Fourth Industrial Revolution, the lesson is infrastructural: AI-driven ship tracking, risk-tier algorithms, and insurance repricing now move markets faster than missiles, while the sulfur-and-ammonia chains feeding battery metals and chip-grade chemicals run through the same burning strait [5].

The re-escalation confirms that the fourth systemic crisis has entered its allocation phase: the contest is no longer over price but over who physically controls energy, water, and chokepoint infrastructure. Every US strike deepens Tehran's dependence on the DragonBear — China as the sink for sanctioned Iranian crude, Russia as the supplier of air defense regeneration — accelerating the consolidation of an armed Eurasian bloc that Washington's blockade logic inadvertently strengthens. Threats against desalination and power plants weaponize the water-energy nexus itself, setting precedents [Beijing]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) will study for [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) contingencies [5].

## Trump's Erratic Posture

Krugman notes that US policy has been wildly erratic — first 20% tolls, then demands for foreign investment, neither of which will happen. The overarching moral is the immense folly of a war that has left America and the world in a much worse place than if Trump had preserved the 2015 Iran deal [1].

## No Victory Path

Krugman argues the ceasefire collapse signals that Trump has given up on extracting anything resembling victory. A few days earlier, it appeared Trump would de facto pull out, take advantage of falling oil prices, and spin the story as an American victory. That was stupid and doomed, but at least a serious attempt to end the conflict would have required facing reality. Trump emotionally could not bring himself to admit failure — so the strategy shifted from "pretend we won" to "bomb Iran with no clear strategy." See [Trump Election Fraud Pretext]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/trump-election-fraud-pretext-2026.html) [2].

## The Escalation Trap Enters a New Phase

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) argues that the ceasefire collapse is not simply another round of strikes but evidence that the [Escalation Trap]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) has entered a new phase. Trump's declaration of the United States as "Guardian of the Strait of Hormuz," his 20% toll announcement, and his decision to reimpose the blockade constitute a political commitment that makes backing down significantly harder. The objective has shifted from punishment to reversing the strategic defeat [3][4].

The conflict is now driven by **two separate engines of escalation**: (1) strategic — Iran's insistence on controlling Hormuz vs. US insistence on freedom of navigation, and (2) revenge — the Supreme Leader's call to action creating an independent political objective. These engines are mutually reinforcing: even if one slows, the other can drive escalation. "This is no longer a simple cycle of tit-for-tat retaliation. It is an escalation spiral" [3][4].

Pape identifies three indicators to watch: (1) amplification of Iran's revenge campaign through state media, (2) expansion of US bombing beyond coastal defenses into deep infrastructure, and (3) preparations for littoral operations — amphibious shipping, Marine units, mine-clearing assets, special forces reconnaissance. The military is already creating the operational conditions for Stage 3 ground operations [3][4].
