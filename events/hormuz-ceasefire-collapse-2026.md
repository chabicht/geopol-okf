# Background

The [June 17 Versailles Memorandum]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html) ended the kinetic phase of the [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html), producing a fragile ceasefire. The arrangement was never a settlement — it paused the exchange while leaving [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s coercive infrastructure intact. Post-MoU escalation events (IRGC drone attacks on commercial vessels, US reprisal strikes, attacks on Bahrain facilities) confirmed the fragility from the outset.

# Current Situation

On July 7, three commercial vessels — including a Qatari LNG tanker and a Saudi crude carrier — were attacked in and near the Strait of Hormuz, with a tanker set ablaze off [Oman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/oman.html). Within hours, the US Treasury revoked the sixty-day oil waiver (General License X1 bars any new Iranian oil transactions from July 7, with wind-down only through July 17). In the early hours of July 8, US forces struck monitoring and surveillance centres and military bases across southern [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), despite a signaled pause for the funeral of slain Supreme Leader Khamenei, whose procession drew more than two million mourners in Najaf. Iran claimed retaliatory strikes against "85 US military installations"; air-raid sirens sounded in [Bahrain]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/bahrain.html) and [Kuwait]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/kuwait.html). Tehran invoked Article 51, declared the American strikes a violation of the Memorandum's first paragraph, and warned Gulf neighbours that hosting US operations constitutes complicity [5][6].

At the [NATO summit in Ankara]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html), [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) pronounced the Memorandum "over" and dealing with Tehran a "waste of time." He escalated both rhetoric and threat architecture: calling Iranian authorities "sick" and "scum," threatening to "take over" [Kharg Island]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), Iran's principal crude export terminal, and naming desalination plants and power facilities as potential targets. He floated re-imposing a naval blockade applying "only" to Iran, backed by NATO allies dispatching minesweepers to the strait [5][6].

Around July 13, 2026, the escalation deepened further. Trump reinstated the blockade; Iran resumed drone and missile strikes on shipping. Iran, emboldened by its success in defying America, demanded sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz. Trump declared that *he* owns the Strait and will collect 20 percent tolls on all shipping traffic — a proposal that would be wildly illegal and impossible to enforce. He subsequently shifted to demanding that countries invest in the United States as a condition for passage, which is equally illegal and unenforceable [1][2].

## The Refining Capacity Constraint

[Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html) argues that the ceasefire collapse will do less economic damage than expected — but not because Hormuz is stable. Rather, a structural disconnect has emerged between events at the Strait and the energy prices that matter: the world faces a global shortage of refining capacity, meaning that even if the Strait fully reopened, the additional crude could not be turned into usable products fast enough to matter.

Roughly 10% of world refining capacity is now out of operation. The primary cause is the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html): [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html)'s drone campaign against Russian oil refineries has been astonishingly effective, forcing Russia to ban gasoline and jet fuel exports and potentially begin importing refined products. This refining shortage means the "effective price" of oil to consumers (weighted gasoline + diesel prices) has remained far above pre-war levels even as crude prices nearly returned to pre-war levels. The consumer price rose approximately $75/barrel versus a $45/barrel rise in crude — demand destruction from high consumer prices kept crude demand depressed even before the latest collapse [1].

# Analysis

## Market Repricing and the Fertilizer Cascade

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) documents the sharpest market repricing since the Hormuz war began. Brent surged nearly 8 percent to over $80 per barrel — its steepest rise since the ceasefire began — while WTI approached $76, the Dow shed more than 800 points, and 10-year Treasury yields spiked toward 4.6 percent. Daily transits through the strait collapsed from a pre-war average of 125 vessels to just 16, with the US Navy-led Joint Maritime Information Center raising transit risk to its highest tier, "severe." Gulf tanker charter rates jumped from under $200,000 to roughly $300,000 per day [5].

The fertilizer front is where the rupture becomes systemic. The European Parliament approved up to €1.5 billion in aid to offset surging fertilizer prices driven explicitly by Hormuz disruptions: farmers become eligible for reimbursement of up to 80 percent of additional costs, member states may raise advance direct payments from 70 to 75 percent with immediate disbursement, the Commission mobilizes €500 million, and member states contribute up to €1 billion. Washington is intervening in parallel: Trump issued an emergency proclamation suspending anti-dumping duties on Moroccan phosphate imports, while the USDA launched the $500 million FIELDS program. The FTC has opened an industry-wide probe into precipitous price increases. Agriculture has joined semiconductors and energy as a securitized sector, with Washington and Brussels abandoning market orthodoxy for state-directed supply resilience [5].

## Hormuz as Cold War 2.0 Pressure Gauge

Tchakarova frames Hormuz as the pressure gauge of [Cold War 2.0]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html): every escalation transfers wealth toward the [DragonBear]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html)'s discounted-crude arbitrage — [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) absorbing sanctioned Iranian barrels — while inflating the West's rates, food costs, and fiscal burdens. The emergency fertilizer interventions mark a threshold: the allocation crisis has migrated from the Gulf into Western budgets. [Morocco]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/morocco.html)'s sudden phosphate leverage previews the coming scramble for resource-holding middle powers. For the Fourth Industrial Revolution, the lesson is infrastructural: AI-driven ship tracking, risk-tier algorithms, and insurance repricing now move markets faster than missiles, while the sulfur-and-ammonia chains feeding battery metals and chip-grade chemicals run through the same burning strait [5].

The re-escalation confirms that the fourth systemic crisis has entered its allocation phase: the contest is no longer over price but over who physically controls energy, water, and chokepoint infrastructure. Every US strike deepens Tehran's dependence on the DragonBear — China as the sink for sanctioned Iranian crude, Russia as the supplier of air defense regeneration — accelerating the consolidation of an armed Eurasian bloc that Washington's blockade logic inadvertently strengthens. Threats against desalination and power plants weaponize the water-energy nexus itself, setting precedents [Beijing]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) will study for [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) contingencies [5].

## Trump's Erratic Posture

Krugman notes that US policy has been wildly erratic — first 20% tolls, then demands for foreign investment, neither of which will happen. The overarching moral is the immense folly of a war that has left America and the world in a much worse place than if Trump had preserved the 2015 Iran deal [1].

## No Victory Path

Krugman argues the ceasefire collapse signals that Trump has given up on extracting anything resembling victory. A few days earlier, it appeared Trump would de facto pull out, take advantage of falling oil prices, and spin the story as an American victory. That was stupid and doomed, but at least a serious attempt to end the conflict would have required facing reality. Trump emotionally could not bring himself to admit failure — so the strategy shifted from "pretend we won" to "bomb Iran with no clear strategy." See [Trump Election Fraud Pretext]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/trump-election-fraud-pretext-2026.html) [2].

## The Escalation Trap Enters a New Phase

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) argues that the ceasefire collapse is not simply another round of strikes but evidence that the [Escalation Trap]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) has entered a new phase. Trump's declaration of the United States as "Guardian of the Strait of Hormuz," his 20% toll announcement, and his decision to reimpose the blockade constitute a political commitment that makes backing down significantly harder. The objective has shifted from punishment to reversing the strategic defeat [3][4].

The conflict is now driven by **two separate engines of escalation**: (1) strategic — Iran's insistence on controlling Hormuz vs. US insistence on freedom of navigation, and (2) revenge — the Supreme Leader's call to action creating an independent political objective. These engines are mutually reinforcing: even if one slows, the other can drive escalation. "This is no longer a simple cycle of tit-for-tat retaliation. It is an escalation spiral" [3][4].

Pape identifies three indicators to watch: (1) amplification of Iran's revenge campaign through state media, (2) expansion of US bombing beyond coastal defenses into deep infrastructure, and (3) preparations for littoral operations — amphibious shipping, Marine units, mine-clearing assets, special forces reconnaissance. The military is already creating the operational conditions for Stage 3 ground operations [3][4].

## A Truce, Not a Settlement

[Andrew Davidson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) frames the renewed fighting as a contest that neither side can convert into a clean political victory. The military balance strongly favors the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html), but destroying Iranian capabilities is not the same as compelling Tehran to accept US terms. Washington can weaken [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s ability to threaten shipping and prevent it from exercising control over the Strait of Hormuz; it cannot cheaply guarantee normal commercial passage or force Iran to surrender its remaining leverage without accepting a prolonged campaign. Iran needs only intermittent success — a single attack can restore uncertainty after numerous safe transits — while Washington must provide security continuously [7].

The likely outcome is a truce rather than a settlement. If neither side escalates, Iran is more likely to seek a ceasefire before the US abandons its core objectives, even if Tehran briefly intensifies attacks to improve its bargaining position. Iran's release of a US citizen amid the renewed exchanges suggests Tehran already has de-escalation in mind. Washington has greater room to adjust the practical terms — easing blockade enforcement, allowing limited Iranian exports, or modifying transit and escort procedures — without abandoning its core demand that Iran stop threatening commercial passage. A pause would likely involve Iran scaling back attacks enough for commercial traffic to recover while preserving its formal claims over Hormuz and presenting the outcome as proof that resistance forced negotiations. Washington would likewise portray any reduction in attacks as evidence that military pressure worked. Neither side would have resolved the underlying dispute, and both would retain incentives to test the balance sometime in the future [7].

## The Infrastructure-Strike Escalation (July 15-16)

[Heather Cox Richardson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/heather-cox-richardson.html) documents the next escalation phase. On July 15, Trump reversed the reimposed blockade within 24 hours — claiming Gulf allies told him they would rather invest directly in the U.S. than pay tolls — only to convene a Situation Room meeting with his top national security team to discuss "new plans for devastating strikes." Before the meeting, Trump told Fox News that after the "hard" strikes that week, "next week comes the power plants. Next week comes the bridges. We're gonna knock out all their power plants. We're gonna knock out all their bridges unless they get to the table and negotiate." Attacks on civilian infrastructure are usually illegal under international law. A former member of Trump's negotiating team told Laura Rozen of *Diplomatic* that the escalation was probably a ploy to kick-start negotiations — "a very risky and low probability gamble, but nothing else makes sense" [8].

The next day, Reuters reported that Iranian leaders had asked the Houthis they back in Yemen to close the Bab el-Mandeb strait — the second of the two main routes for Middle East oil exports, alongside Hormuz — in response to the threatened strikes on Iran's power network. Closure of the second strait would exacerbate energy shortages even as the U.S. [SPR]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/spr-depletion-crisis.html) dropped to its lowest level since 1983. Iran activated its [Red Sea]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html) proxy to widen the pressure without requiring itself to close Hormuz [9].

## The Casualty Threshold (July 15)

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) identifies a discrete escalation threshold crossed on July 15, 2026: Iran's use of heavy anti-ship missiles with up to 1,000-pound warheads against tankers in the Strait of Hormuz. The weapons are capable of catastrophic, ship-sinking destruction — far eclipsing minor drone strikes. Numerous US Navy ships had been dangerously repositioned close to Iran's coast, well within Iran's anti-ship cruise missile range. Pape's assessment is that even 30–40 US casualties would instantly shatter diplomatic options, forcing an uncontrollable, public-fueled demand for payback that could lock both sides into a costly spiral and make Stage Three politically difficult to avoid. The political logic of the war changes once those casualties occur: the imperative becomes responding to American deaths, not just protecting freedom of navigation. Neither Washington nor Tehran fully controls what happens once those casualties occur. The shift is from controlled brinkmanship to high-risk, uncontrolled escalation [10].

## The Second Front Opens (July 16)

Within 48 hours of July 16, Yemen's Iran-backed Houthis launched a coordinated barrage of ballistic missiles and drones targeting [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html)'s Abha International Airport in direct retaliation for airstrikes on Sanaa. Pape frames the assault as classic *horizontal escalation* that widens the war by opening a second maritime front at the [Bab el-Mandeb]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html) gateway. Until then, the war had centered on the Strait of Hormuz; opening a second front transforms the crisis from a regional confrontation into a broader contest over the security of global energy flows. The move expands the political stakes for every major actor while shrinking their room to compromise: leaving two strategic chokepoints under threat would appear politically unacceptable. [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) gains additional leverage without directly committing its own forces — operationalizing its Red Sea proxy to impose costs Tehran itself need not bear. As energy markets tighten and oil inventories continue falling, every new disruption raises both the economic costs of restraint and the political incentives for further escalation [11].

## Shaping Operations (July 17)

On the evening of July 17, US Central Command conducted multiple waves of airstrikes against [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), targeting key bridges, railway junctures, and power lines connecting key cities, particularly in Hormozgan province and around the major port city of Bandar Abbas. Pape assesses these as classic "shaping" operations in preparation for a ground assault, consistent with the air strikes over all six days in the previous week. The latest strikes disrupt the logistics and mobility of Iranian military forces in the south, while the prior strikes degrade coastal missile storage and launch sites, air defense systems, and surveillance facilities from Chabahar to Bushehr — along hundreds of miles of coastline that complicates the defender's problem in stopping any landing or seizure of specific islands. Once the United States begins shaping the battlefield for possible ground operations, stopping becomes politically harder than continuing. Iran's trap deepens in parallel: standing down after these strikes would risk signaling weakness both domestically and across the region. Pape expects multi-faceted escalation in the near future [12].

## Water Infrastructure Targeted (July 18)

On July 18, Iran crossed another escalation threshold: instead of concentrating on military bases, Iranian missile and drone strikes hit a vital power and water desalination plant in [Kuwait]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/kuwait.html) — part of the system that produces roughly 90% of the country's drinking water. Unlike past fragments from Iranian strikes that had inadvertently hit desalination plants, this plant was directly targeted. Pape identifies the strike as a categorical escalation: civilian survival infrastructure is becoming a direct battlefield target. The strategic logic runs counter to the conventional expectation that targeting civilians breaks their will — Pape's *Bombing to Win* research finds that when civilians suddenly lose access to life's basic necessities, survival rapidly overwhelms every other concern, "anger comes after survival." But the Escalation Trap operates in the opposite direction from civilian morale: leaders confronted with collapsing civilian conditions often seek to shift public anger outward, while neighboring states face growing pressure to respond to humanitarian and security spillovers. The result could be a wider regional war whose consequences become increasingly difficult for any side to control [13].

## The Five-Day Strike Wave (July 11–16)

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documents the full operational tempo of the reignition that produced the conditions Pape analyzed day by day. Trump declared the ceasefire formally over on July 8 at the [Ankara Summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html); the military meaning became clear over five consecutive days. July 11: CENTCOM struck approximately 140 targets and set terms (open the strait, halt shipping attacks, surrender enriched uranium); the IRGC struck the *GFS Galaxy* and declared Hormuz closed. July 12: US strikes on Qeshm Island, Bandar Abbas, Hajiabad; first use of one-way attack drones this campaign; Iran fired its widest missile/drone volley yet at UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman, and Jordan. July 13: Five-hour US strike wave on Bushehr, Chah Bahar, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa, Bandar Abbas; IRGC missiles struck two UAE-flagged tankers in Omani waters — 1 Indian crew killed; oil price spiked almost 10%. July 14: Third consecutive night of coastal strikes; Trump reinstated the [naval blockade]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/us-naval-blockade-iran.html) and threatened infrastructure (bridges, power plants) next. July 15: Seven-hour fourth-night strike wave plus a separate round on Greater Tunb Island; an empty tanker disabled with Hellfire missiles for ignoring the blockade; Kuwait intercepted Iranian missiles. July 16: Fifth consecutive day of strikes; Trump reportedly weighing ground operations and bombing an underground site linked to Iran's nuclear program. See [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) for the consolidated account [14].
