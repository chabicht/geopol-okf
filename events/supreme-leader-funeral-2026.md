# Background

The US-Israeli air campaign that opened the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) on February 28, 2026 killed Iran's Supreme Leader, his wife, three members of his immediate family, and approximately 50 of the country's highest political, military, intelligence, and religious leaders. Up to 3,700 Iranians were killed during the bombing campaign, including an alleged airstrike on the Shajarat al-Tayyiba Primary School in Minab that killed 168 civilians, including schoolgirls as young as seven [1].

# Current Situation

The funeral ceremonies produced perhaps the largest public mobilization in the history of the Islamic Republic, with millions filling the streets across the country and additional days of mourning still to come. Crowds chanted "Kill Trump," "Revenge, Revenge," "Kill Bibi," and "We won't let you go!" — evidence of a broad nationalist response that emboldens the regime to adopt more aggressive positions [1].

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) argues that the funeral's strategic significance is widely misread. Most commentary treats it as a political spectacle; the real story is how it changes [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s bargaining position. Massive demonstrations of nationalist resolve do not change military power overnight — they change the political incentives that determine how governments negotiate, escalate, and absorb economic pain. Leaders confronting millions of citizens demanding fierce payback are less likely to moderate their objectives [1].

# Analysis

## The 9/11 Analogy

Pape draws a direct parallel to the American experience after 9/11: the attacks did not produce a frightened public demanding strategic restraint, but an overwhelming surge of national unity and determination. National trauma fundamentally altered political incentives — the dominant instinct was not "never again" but "they hit us once; we must hit back harder." The same political logic is now visible in Iran. The funeral is not simply a display of mourning; it is evidence that the war has generated a broad nationalist response that emboldens the regime [1].

## New War Aim: Ending America's Gulf Presence

Pape identifies a strategic shift: the funeral does not simply make Iran more stubborn — it changes what Iranian leaders believe they can realistically achieve. The emerging objective is to use the political momentum to weaken and ultimately end the permanent American military presence on Iran's southern frontier. Iran is appealing directly to Gulf monarchies: the future security of Bahrain, Kuwait, [Qatar]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/qatar.html), the [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html), and [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html) depends not on larger American military deployments, but on reducing them. Iran's July 4 announcement of "special treatment" for friendly states under a new maritime fee structure extends this logic: states distancing themselves from Washington receive lower transit costs [1].

## August Shock Risk

Pape forecasts that August could become the decisive month of the war. Every week the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) draws down the Strategic Petroleum Reserve to cushion the global economy from Hormuz disruptions. As of June 19, the SPR had fallen to 326 million barrels, approaching its congressionally mandated operational floor of ~243 million barrels by mid-to-late August — precisely as hurricane season begins threatening Gulf Coast production. By August, Iran is likely to possess three forms of leverage simultaneously: growing nationalist confidence at home, continuing influence over maritime traffic through Hormuz, and an opponent with steadily diminishing economic room for more disruption [1].

## Three Signals to Watch

Pape identifies three observable indicators for whether August is breaking: (1) whether Iran begins explicitly linking maritime access to reducing the American military presence; (2) whether shipping through the southern Strait of Hormuz remains subnormal with rising insurance costs; (3) whether pressure on Hezbollah intensifies or fighting expands in Lebanon, signaling Washington and Jerusalem attempting to weaken Iran's regional position before Tehran reaches maximum leverage [1].
