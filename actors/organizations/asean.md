# Background

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) comprises 10 member states spanning Myanmar, Laos, [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html), Thailand, Cambodia, the Philippines, Brunei, Malaysia, Singapore, and Indonesia. The bloc is projected to become the fourth-largest economy by 2030 with 684 million people. ASEAN states are actively diversifying partnerships — seeking OECD membership (Indonesia, Thailand), exploring BRICS engagement, and resisting pressure to choose sides between Washington and Beijing [1][2].

# Current Situation

## Indonesia's ASEAN Energy Reserve Proposal

Indonesia has proposed an ASEAN-wide oil storage hub, a tacit recognition that the region remains structurally vulnerable to external energy shocks — particularly those linked to Middle East supply disruptions and maritime chokepoints. Jakarta has framed the initiative as a strategic reserve mechanism and a platform for deeper regional coordination, even as it simultaneously pursues its own national reserve expansion regardless of whether ASEAN adopts the broader framework. The renewed focus on collective storage signals that regional governments see energy as a geoeconomic security issue tied directly to trade resilience, inflation management, and industrial continuity, especially in light of the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) [2].

## Strategic Rationale

The proposal signals ASEAN's attempt to reduce strategic dependence on external powers while strengthening intra-regional bargaining power. If implemented, shared reserves would provide member states with greater protection against price spikes and supply interruptions and create leverage in negotiations with suppliers, refiners, and logistics operators. Indonesia is positioning itself as a de facto energy anchor to elevate its position in the bloc [2].

## ASEAN Power Grid and Integrated Energy Systems

ASEAN's broader strategy combines reserve building with power grid integration, renewable expansion, and cross-border electricity trade. Laos' growing role as a regional electricity exporter illustrates how energy interdependence is becoming more institutionalized. The ASEAN Power Grid initiative suggests oil reserves are one element in a larger push toward integrated energy systems [2].

## ASEAN 2045 Framework

The proposal coincides with broader ASEAN discussions around resilience, connectivity, and regional coordination under the ASEAN 2045 framework. Energy security is being integrated into the bloc's long-term economic architecture alongside digital infrastructure, manufacturing diversification, and supply chain resilience — particularly relevant as ASEAN seeks to position itself as an alternative production base to China [2].

# Key Dynamics

- **Energy as strategic resilience**: Energy security is no longer solely an economic concern but a geoeconomic security issue tied to trade resilience and industrial continuity.
- **Reducing external dependence**: Shared reserves and integrated energy systems reduce dependence on Middle East energy imports and external powers, while strengthening intra-regional bargaining power.
- **Indonesia's leadership bid**: Jakarta's proposal positions Indonesia as the de facto energy anchor, elevating its influence within the bloc.
- **Infrastructure investment opportunities**: New storage infrastructure would require billions in financing, engineering, shipping, insurance, and digital monitoring systems. Ports in western Indonesia could emerge as strategic energy nodes linking the Indian Ocean and Pacific supply chains [2].
