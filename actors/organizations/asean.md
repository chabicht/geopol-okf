# Background

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) comprises 10 member states spanning Myanmar, Laos, [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html), Thailand, Cambodia, the Philippines, Brunei, Malaysia, Singapore, and [Indonesia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/indonesia.html). The bloc is projected to become the fourth-largest economy by 2030 with 684 million people. ASEAN states are actively diversifying partnerships — seeking OECD membership (Indonesia, Thailand), exploring BRICS engagement, and resisting pressure to choose sides between Washington and Beijing [1][2]. The bloc's long-term vision is articulated through the ASEAN 2045 framework, which integrates energy security, digital infrastructure, manufacturing diversification, and supply chain resilience into a single economic architecture — particularly relevant as ASEAN seeks to position itself as an alternative production base to [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) [2].

# Current Situation

## Energy-Security Posture

ASEAN's energy-security posture has crystallized around a recognition that the region remains structurally vulnerable to external energy shocks — particularly those linked to Middle East supply disruptions and maritime chokepoints. Indonesia has proposed an ASEAN-wide oil storage hub, framing the initiative as both a strategic reserve mechanism and a platform for deeper regional coordination, even as Jakarta simultaneously pursues its own national reserve expansion regardless of whether ASEAN adopts the broader framework [2].

The strategic rationale is explicit: shared reserves would reduce dependence on Middle East energy imports and external powers while strengthening intra-regional bargaining power, providing member states with greater protection against price spikes and supply interruptions and creating leverage in negotiations with suppliers, refiners, and logistics operators. Indonesia is positioning itself as a de facto energy anchor to elevate its position within the bloc [2]. This leadership bid carries significant infrastructure implications: new storage infrastructure would require billions in financing, engineering, shipping, insurance, and digital monitoring systems, with ports in western Indonesia potentially emerging as strategic energy nodes linking the Indian Ocean and Pacific supply chains [2].

The storage initiative is one element in a larger push toward integrated energy systems. ASEAN's broader strategy combines reserve building with power grid integration, renewable expansion, and cross-border electricity trade. Laos' growing role as a regional electricity exporter illustrates how energy interdependence is becoming more institutionalized through the ASEAN Power Grid initiative [2]. The proposal coincides with the ASEAN 2045 framework's integration of energy security into long-term economic architecture, signaling that regional governments now treat energy as a geoeconomic security issue tied directly to trade resilience, inflation management, and industrial continuity — especially in light of the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) [2].

## Member Dynamics: Consensus Constraint and the South China Sea

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) identifies ASEAN's structural inability to act on the South China Sea arbitration. Not one ASEAN member besides the Philippines signed the 10th anniversary joint statement in July 2026 — not [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html), not Indonesia, not [Malaysia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/malaysia.html), not Brunei — in the year the Philippines holds the chair. ASEAN has never officially recognised the 2016 award. Consensus rules mean that Phnom Penh and Vientiane hold what is functionally a Chinese veto over the bloc's language. The November ASEAN Summit in Manila is the real deadline: Marcos is pinning the Code of Conduct to that summit. Watch specifically for any COC provision requiring joint development with China to the exclusion of external parties, which would render the award moot by contract rather than by defiance [3]. The contrast between ASEAN's ambition in energy coordination and its paralysis on the South China Sea reveals the bloc's defining structural constraint: consensus rules enable cooperation on non-contentious issues but deliver veto power to the most externally aligned member on anything that touches great-power competition.
