# Background

The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) is the primary regional intergovernmental organisation in West Africa, founded in 1975 to promote economic integration and political cooperation. Its security arm, the ECOWAS Standby Force (ESF), has been deployed multiple times with relative success — including in The Gambia since 2017 — and is considered a model for other regional standby forces. However, the ESF is activated by coalitions of willing member states, meaning deployments often reflect political agendas rather than collective institutional purpose, leading to incoherent action [1].

# Current Situation

## Institutional Crisis

ECOWAS is navigating its deepest institutional crisis since inception. The 2025 exit of Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger — and their formation of the Alliance of Sahel States (AES) — has deeply fractured the organisation. This places the [Sahel](/conflicts/sahel-insurgency.md) beyond the reach of ECOWAS peace and security initiatives, while security crises in the AES countries continue to spill over into coastal states. In the absence of decisive ECOWAS action, coastal states have self-organised into subregional formats such as the Accra Initiative, albeit with limited success [1].

## Counter-Terrorism Unit

Following an announcement by the ECOWAS Chiefs of Defence Staff in February 2026, a counter-terrorism unit of 1,650 troops within the existing ESF is expected to take shape. The number was revised down from an earlier proposal of 5,000 troops (Nigeria's Defence Minister) and a sweeping 260,000-strong force (ECOWAS CDS assessment), reflecting low member-state commitment. The unit is expected to be primarily military in nature, though the ESF comprises military, police, and civilian components. Key questions — mobilisation circumstances, mission location and scope mechanisms — remain vague or classified [1].

A maximalist version with advanced technologies (UAVs, AI-enabled ISR) would cost USD 2.5 billion annually. The current 1,650-strong unit is projected at USD 410 million per year — roughly equivalent to ECOWAS' entire annual budget. Member states aspire to self-finance rather than mobilise international funding, partly as a gesture to the AES states that have accused ECOWAS of being steered by external powers. Given strained state budgets across the region, this ambition casts doubt on near-term activation [1].

## Operational Constraints

The ESF faces deep-rooted challenges:

- **Political will**: Deployment reflects coalitions of willing states; the ESF countered the December 2025 coup attempt in [Nigeria](/actors/countries/nigeria.md) under Nigerian leadership, but similar will could not be mobilised for the Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger coups.
- **Training gaps**: Member states' approaches to deployment and training are shaped by domestic political priorities. Units eventually deployed are not always trained for their mandate — particularly acute for counter-terrorism.
- **Institutional bottlenecks**: Joint doctrines and policy frameworks take years to agree. The last Command Post Exercise was conducted in 2018; plans for a renewed edition fell victim to Secretariat bureaucratic processes.
- **Financial shortfalls**: Some member states pay contributions only in part, or not at all.
- **Structural inertia**: ECOWAS' bureaucratic processes stand in sharp contrast to the speed and flexibility required to confront violent extremist groups [1].

## AES Rapprochement

The counter-terrorism unit can be read as an olive branch to Bamako, Niamey, and Ouagadougou. The ECOWAS CDS underlined the need for close coordination with the AES, and Sierra Leone's CDS referred to a possible AES association, though this has not been elaborated publicly. Bilateral efforts have brought some advances — the reopening of the Niger–Benin border is within reach — but long-standing accusations and mistrust persist. ECOWAS heavyweights Côte d'Ivoire and Nigeria, whose financial and troop contributions will be decisive, remain highly sceptical of the Sahelian juntas. The AES states have not publicly responded to ECOWAS' overture and have established their own Unified Force, though its operational viability is equally uncertain [1].

# Analysis

The ESF counter-terrorism unit will likely remain a symbolic force for the foreseeable future. The envisioned mandate represents a significant departure from the ESF's experience with traditional peacekeeping. If cooperation with the AES materialises, it could take the form of small-scale joint military operations led by willing ECOWAS member states. The current ECOWAS Commission mandate under Omar Alieu Touray ends in July 2026, making decisive steps before the new Commission settles unlikely [1].
