# Background

The European Union is the political-economic union of European states, serving as the civilian complement to [NATO]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/nato.html)'s military architecture. Together the two institutions constitute the Western alliance system in [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html). The EU's geopolitical role has expanded beyond trade and regulation into sanctions policy, energy security, enlargement, and strategic autonomy — driven by the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) and the broader [global system rupture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html).

# Current Situation

## Strategic Posture and Alliance Role

The EU's strategic posture is defined by a contradiction at its core: it possesses the regulatory and economic weight to project power but lacks the institutional integration and military capability to act independently of the US security guarantee. The [global system rupture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) is not merely external pressure — it is testing the internal coherence of the EU itself, as member states diverge in their threat perceptions, energy strategies, and willingness to bear the costs of strategic autonomy. Along with [NATO]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/nato.html), the EU is part of the Western alliance architecture under strain from the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) shifting priorities.

Since 2024, the EU has sought to diversify its security relationships through 12 bilateral Security and Defence Partnerships (SDPs) with countries across Europe, North America, Asia, the Indo-Pacific, and Africa. This marks a genuine break with the EU's previous approach — for much of the post-Cold War period, external security relationships were organised around stable categories (EU members, NATO allies, the political West). The SDPs are driven by Russia's war in [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html), uncertainty about long-term US engagement in Europe, and the intertwining of economic security, technology, and defence [5].

The 12 partners fall into three circles. The inner ring comprises closely aligned countries (EEA/accession candidates): Norway, Iceland (focused on the High North), Albania, North Macedonia, and Moldova. The second ring consists of like-minded NATO partners beyond the EU: Canada and the [United Kingdom]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-kingdom.html), with the UK SDP marking a turning point in the post-Brexit relationship and Canada's reflecting its pursuit of middle-power partnerships amid US uncertainty. The outer ring reaches global partners: [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html), South Korea, [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html), [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html), and Ghana — with the Indo-Pacific focus clearly visible and India standing out as a traditional non-aligned country maintaining a Russian defence-industrial relationship. Notably absent from the portfolio are the United States, [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), Turkey (blocked by Cyprus conflict), and most of South and Central America and South Africa [5].

The SDPs are not mutual defence alliances — none mention NATO Article 5 or the EU's Article 42.7. They offer a menu of cooperation: institutionalised dialogue (annual/semi-annual consultations), thematic cooperation (cyber security, hybrid threats, counterterrorism, maritime security, space, sanctions), CSDP participation (missions, exercises, PESCO), and linkage to EU armaments instruments. The "Security Action for Europe" (SAFE) instrument provides €150 billion in loans to EU states for defence projects, with a "Buy European" clause requiring at least 65% of procurement to go to EU, EEA, Ukraine, or SDP partner firms. The SDPs thus become a political prerequisite for preferential access to the European armaments market — an instrument that simultaneously diversifies security ties and binds partners into the European defence-industrial ecosystem [5].

## Economic-Security Posture

The EU's economic security faces compounding pressures across energy, sanctions, and trade. EU gas storage ending June 2026 stands at only approximately 46% — a dangerously low level heading into the next winter cycle. Tchakarova flags this as a critical stress point in the Global System June 2026 update, linking it to the cascade of energy chokepoints that define the current rupture. The low storage level reflects the compounding effects of the Hormuz crisis, Russian supply disruption, and the structural transition away from piped Russian gas [1].

The sanctions architecture compounds the constraint. EU sanctions on [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) create secondary sanctions risk for Central Asia investors. Western countries are interested in [Central Asia]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/central-asia.html) but remain selective due to risk — the sanctions regime makes deep engagement with Russian-adjacent economies legally hazardous for European firms. This creates a paradox: the EU seeks to counter Russian influence in Central Asia but its own sanctions regime limits the economic instruments available [2].

On the trade front, the EU's deficit with [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) has roughly doubled since COVID, driven by China's progress up the industrial value chain. [Adam Tooze]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/adam-tooze.html) frames this as "China Shock 2.0" — focused above all on Europe and concentrated in vehicles, green energy goods, and chemicals. Ironically, even as the deficit with China widens, the EU's manufacturing trade surplus with the rest of the world is at new highs. The shock is thus "mercantilist-on-mercantilist violence" — competitive surplus against surplus. Setser and Tordoir recommend that the EU resort to a variety of trade defenses as a means of suasion. Tooze argues that if the EU is going to use government policy to shield industry from the consequences of Chinese policy and its own technological short-sightedness — at the expense of European consumers — there should be requirements for minimum investment levels: "European industry needs saving from itself" [4].

## Enlargement and Membership Dynamics

Enlargement is actively discussed in the context of [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html), Moldova, and the Western Balkans. Tchakarova treats EU enlargement as a recurring theme in the broader global system dynamic — the tension between expansion as geopolitical strategy and the internal capacity to absorb new members under conditions of economic strain and energy insecurity. The EU's enlargement ambitions thus run against the same structural constraints that shape its economic-security posture: an energy shortfall that limits fiscal headroom, sanctions that complicate engagement with neighboring economies, and the need to simultaneously manage internal coherence while projecting stability outward [3].

## Regional Engagements

The EU's regional engagement spans the Arctic, Southeast Asia, the Mediterranean, and Latin America — a portfolio that reveals both ambition and recurrent limitations in visibility and execution.

In the [Arctic]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/arctic.html), the EU is updating its 2021 strategy in 2026, with digital sovereignty as a cross-cutting theme. The Arctic is identified as a strategic node for Europe's digital infrastructure — 97% of global intercontinental data traffic runs over submarine cables, and the Arctic offers alternative Europe-Asia data routes away from the vulnerable Bab al-Mandab strait. The EU's Connecting Europe Facility is investing €347 million in strategic submarine cable projects, with the Arctic designated as one of 13 Cable Projects of European Interest. The EU opened a bureau in Nuuk, Greenland in 2024 and opposed [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html)'s territorial claims on the island. The EU has been a de facto observer in the Arctic Council since 2013 but lacks official status; a strategic partnership with Canada is under discussion. The EU's strength in the Arctic lies in combining innovation and sustainability — the Nordic countries serve as a digital hub with Europe's fastest supercomputer (LUMI in Kajaani, Finland) and significant cost advantages for data infrastructure [6].

In Southeast Asia, the EU is expanding free trade agreements, completing negotiations with [Indonesia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/indonesia.html) in 2025 (after nearly a decade) with similar agreements with Thailand, Malaysia, and the Philippines expected to follow. This is part of a broader de-risking effort to reduce dependence on China. The EU's security partnerships with Japan, South Korea, Australia, and India signal that the EU is now treating the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html) as integral to its strategic interests — though the depth of engagement remains limited compared to other actors in the region [7].

In the Mediterranean, the EU faces a structural visibility problem in [Tunisia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/tunisia.html). Despite being Tunisia's most important economic partner (85% of FDI, budget support, grants), European engagement is nearly invisible to the Tunisian public. The emblematic case: the "Chinese Bridge" in Bizerte is financed by the European Investment Bank but universally known as a Chinese project — built by a Chinese company, presented by the Chinese ambassador. France's donation of 165 buses went unnoticed while 461 Chinese buses received extensive media coverage. The visibility deficit stems from authoritarian constraints under President Saied, fear of meddling accusations, the 2023 migration MoU backlash (Saied returned €60 million), and the Gaza war's strain on relations. European diplomats struggle to inaugurate projects they finance. The EU retreats to diffuse initiatives (ELMED, Pact for the Mediterranean) where visibility is less politically sensitive. Meanwhile, [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) fills the visibility gap without European hesitation — European-funded infrastructure bankrolls major contracts awarded to Chinese companies in Europe's neighbourhood [8].

In Latin America, the EU is [Colombia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/colombia.html)'s third-largest trading partner and second-largest source of FDI after the US. For 2021–2027, the EU is providing at least €106 million through the Global Gateway strategy, covering renewable energy, green hydrogen, and regional electricity integration. In 2025, the EU and Colombia agreed to launch negotiations on a Partnership and Cooperation Agreement. Germany pledged up to €200 million under a 2023 bilateral climate partnership. However, EU cooperation falls short of its strategic potential: the EU is perceived as fragmented, hesitant, and strategically invisible. Financing materializes only once projects are well advanced; the EU has little presence during high-risk early phases. The Global Gateway procedures are difficult to navigate. From the European side, lack of coordination between EU institutions and member states hinders strategic alignment. Whether the EU can transform piecemeal cooperation into a coherent partnership is being closely watched by other Global South fossil fuel exporters as a test of European climate diplomacy credibility [9].

## Healthcare Cybersecurity and Critical Infrastructure Resilience

Bayerlein, Bendiek, Hemmelskamp, and von der Forst ([SWP]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/swp.html)) document the European response to a systemic threat: cyberattacks on the healthcare sector. The EuRepoC database recorded 351 significant cyberattacks on health facilities between 2022 and 2025. The EU's policy architecture has expanded rapidly: the Critical Entities Resilience Directive (CER) and the NIS-2 Directive (with corresponding national implementations such as Germany's KRITIS-Dachgesetz) pursue an all-hazards approach, with CER covering physical and digital threats and NIS-2 focusing on cybersecurity and extending reporting obligations. The EU Preparedness Union Strategy adds holistic crisis preparedness, and the EU Action Plan for Cybersecurity in Healthcare envisages an EU-wide support center for hospitals and "reaction playbooks." The structural challenge identified is that the exclusive focus on national security and EU-internal structures neglects the global synergies essential for effective health crisis management: cyber threats do not respect national borders, and attacks along supply chains (the Stryker and Octapharma cases) cross jurisdictions [10].

## The Whole-of-Route Approach to Migration

Knapp and Koch ([SWP]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/swp.html)) map the EU's "Whole-of-Route Approach" as the control-oriented application of route-based migration policy. Since 2022/23, the European Commission has developed action plans for the Western Balkans, the central Mediterranean, the western Mediterranean and Atlantic, and the eastern Mediterranean — each following the same logic: along the main migration routes toward the EU, measures on border management, return cooperation, anti-smuggling, and (limited) regular migration are pushed forward. The Team-Europe Initiatives along the central Mediterranean and Atlantic/western Mediterranean routes bundle EU instruments, member-state contributions, and development programs along selected corridors. The EU's migration deals with [Tunisia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/tunisia.html) (2023) and Mauritania (2024) emerged from rising migration along specific routes — partner selection and financing decisions are route-driven. The framework's inherent tension: protection and control are no longer negotiated separately but within a shared programmatic frame, risking the use of protection narratives to legitimize externalized control [11]. See [Migration Policy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/migration-policy.html).

## The Pragmatic Turn Toward the Taliban

Giustozzi ([RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html)) analyses the EU's engagement with the Taliban as a case of migration-driven externalization overriding values-based conditionality. The EU Delegation re-opened in Kabul in January 2022; informal talks in Doha and the UAE have continued since 2021. The June 2026 Taliban delegation visit to Brussels and [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html)'s handover of the Bonn consulate and Berlin embassy to Taliban-appointed representatives drove the broader EU shift. The Taliban offered to cooperate on returns in exchange for consular transfer — short of formal recognition but a major normalization move. The structural argument: the Taliban hold the stronger negotiating position because the migration issue is unsustainable. Hundreds of thousands of Afghans live in Europe without legal status; hundreds of thousands more are in [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) seeking to enter Europe; the closure of Afghan consulates has produced a surge in fake identity documents. Critics of engagement offer no alternative other than maintaining an unsustainable status quo that member states are already undermining through bilateral arrangements [12]. See [Afghanistan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/afghanistan.html) and [Migration Policy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/migration-policy.html).

## Critical Minerals Partnerships with Africa

Kaupenjohann (SWP) identifies the EU Global Gateway as the institutional framework for linking European critical minerals demand with [Africa]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/africa.html)n industrial development. The EU's Critical Raw Materials Act provides the regulatory foundation, but the strategic logic extends beyond legislation: energy transition, AI compute expansion, and defense industrial growth are simultaneously driving mineral demand to unprecedented levels, while [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html)'s 2025 heavy rare earth export restrictions have demonstrated how quickly concentrated supply chains can be weaponized. The EU's partnership architecture — long-term offtake agreements, joint ventures, and project financing — represents an attempt to build alternative value chains that are capital-intensive and slow to develop but structurally necessary [15].

The strategic alignment between European diversification and African industrialization is the partnership's core logic. African governments are imposing export restrictions on unprocessed minerals and local-content requirements to capture more domestic value added — creating short-term supply friction but reflecting a shared interest in reducing concentration risk. European companies can contribute the engineering expertise, equipment, and technical know-how needed to build African downstream processing industries, transforming the relationship from raw commodity extraction toward industrial partnership [15].

The competitive challenge is formidable. Building competitive processing capacity takes longer than a legislative cycle and faces entrenched Chinese competition. In copper smelting, Chinese capacity has expanded faster than global concentrate supply, driving treatment and refining charges into negative territory in 2025 — smelters paying miners for concentrate to maintain throughput. New African smelters must compete with operations that have achieved economies of scale and operate at negative margins. Africa's infrastructure deficit in energy and transport further extends the timeline, making the EU's partnership model realistic only on a decade-plus horizon despite the urgency of the strategic logic [15].

## Fair Recruitment and the EU Talent Pool

Knapp and Landmesser ([SWP]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/swp.html)) highlight a significant gap in the EU's migration governance: while the EU has developed extensive frameworks for border management and return cooperation, it lacks any EU-wide instrument against unfair recruitment of third-country workers. The European Labour Authority (ELA), established to facilitate access to the single labour market and combat undeclared work, does not formally cover third-country nationals in its mandate — leaving the fastest-growing segment of Europe's labour inflow outside its supervisory scope. The EU's regulatory competence in labour migration remains limited, with member states retaining primary authority over admission conditions and recruitment oversight [16].

The planned EU Talent Pool represents a concrete opportunity to close part of this gap. Designed as a platform matching third-country workers with European employers, it could embed fair-recruitment standards and certification requirements if member states appoint national contact points with enforcement capacity. Knapp and Landmesser also argue that fair-recruitment standards should be embedded in the EU's broader migration partnerships with transit and origin countries — bilateral agreements with India, Morocco, and Vietnam could replicate elements of the Philippines' model, which uses 60-plus bilateral labour agreements to protect workers abroad. Extending ELA's mandate to third-country nationals and operationalizing the Talent Pool would require political will that has so far been lacking [16]. See [Migration Policy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/migration-policy.html) and [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html).

# Analysis

The EU's geopolitical position is defined by contradiction: it has the regulatory and economic weight to project power but lacks the institutional integration and military capability to act independently of the US security guarantee. The [global system rupture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) accelerates the EU's need to become a strategic actor while simultaneously degrading the conditions under which it could do so. The energy storage shortfall is an acute vulnerability; the sanctions secondary risk limits strategic reach; and enlargement ambitions strain against absorptive capacity. China Shock 2.0 compounds the challenge: the EU must simultaneously manage energy transition, Russian hybrid threats, and an industrial displacement crisis driven by Chinese exports — while its overall trade surplus with the rest of the world remains at record highs, revealing the paradoxical nature of the mercantilist competition [4]. The SDP portfolio represents the most concrete institutional response to these pressures — diversifying security ties, binding partners into the European defence-industrial ecosystem, and building the architecture of strategic autonomy without yet possessing the capability to exercise it fully [5]. The recurring visibility and execution gaps in Tunisia and Colombia reveal the distance between the EU's ambitions and its operational reach — a pattern that other Global South actors are watching as a test of European strategic credibility [8][9]. The migration policy domain reveals the same pattern at its sharpest: the EU possesses the regulatory instruments (NIS-2, CER, the Whole-of-Route Approach, the Preparedness Union Strategy) but the protection content erodes under the pressure of enforcement priorities, and externalization extends to engagement with regimes whose values are diametrically opposed to European constitutional norms [10][11][12].

## BraveTech EU and Defense-Industrial Integration with Ukraine

Ryvkin (GPF) reports that the EU has shifted its approach to Ukraine from financing and purchasing to integrating Ukraine's continuously innovating defense sector into its own defense-industrial base. The centerpiece is BraveTech EU, a joint initiative that fast-tracks battlefield-proven technology by linking Ukraine's defense technology platform with the European Defense Fund, the European Defense Agency, and the EU Defense Innovation Scheme. The initiative is backed by 50 million euros from the EU matched by 50 million euros from Ukraine, with separate EU schemes designed to unlock hundreds of millions in private investment. The division of labor reflects comparative advantage: Ukraine supplies battlefield-tested designs and expertise — including combat data from tens of thousands of combat flights used to train AI models — while the EU provides capital, procurement access, large-scale manufacturing, and industrial facilities less exposed to Russian missile attack [13]. See [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) and [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html).

## S6 Domain: Storage Stress and ECB Policy Collision

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html)'s Day 144 diagnostic places the Financial System Stress domain (S6) at CRITICAL, upgraded from elevated. EU gas storage stands at 54% — dangerously low heading into the winter cycle, and a sharp deterioration from the approximately 46% level recorded at the end of June. The German peak is projected near 70%, reflecting the concentration of industrial demand in [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html) and the particular vulnerability of the German manufacturing base to energy shortfalls. The ECB faces a policy collision: its interest rate decisions must be made into an oil re-spike and a refill shortfall simultaneously — the energy price surge pushes inflation upward while the growth shock from energy costs pushes toward easing. The Fed's hiking repricing holds and there is no safe-haven Treasury bid, meaning the EU cannot rely on US fiscal-monetary coordination as a buffer [14].

The S5→S6 cross-domain pairing identified in the diagnostic runs through European storage, where corridor closure converts directly into a physical inventory shortfall with a winter trigger. This is a time-determinate linkage: the winter heating season creates a fixed deadline by which storage must be replenished, and the corridor closures (Hormuz dual blockade, Houthi blockade of [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html), Ukraine's Black Sea route lost) are preventing that replenishment. The mechanism is not financial contagion but physical conversion — maritime disruption becomes an inventory shortfall becomes a fiscal-monetary crisis, all on a seasonal clock that no policy intervention can reset [14].
