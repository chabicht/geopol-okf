# Background

Ethiopia is the most populous landlocked country in the world, and its lack of sea access is a defining strategic constraint. The pursuit of port access — particularly to Assab on the Eritrean coast — drives Ethiopian foreign policy and military posture. Ethiopia's alignment with the [UAE](/actors/uae.md) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) in Sudan's civil war places it on one side of a regional proxy conflict that intersects with the Tigray dispute.

# Current Situation

## Tigray Powder Keg

Ethiopia and [Eritrea](/actors/eritrea.md) are again on the verge of conflict over Tigray. The Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF) remains a political force, and the federal government's pursuit of sea access creates pressure on Eritrea, which controls the coastline. The risk of renewed conflict is compounded by Ethiopia's alignment with the UAE and RSF against Eritrea in the broader regional proxy competition [1].

## Red Sea Corridor

The militarization of the Red Sea corridor has elevated Ethiopia's strategic importance. Multiple external powers — the UAE, Egypt, [Turkey](/actors/turkey.md), [Israel](/actors/israel.md), and [Saudi Arabia](/actors/saudi-arabia.md) — are engaged in proxy alignments across the Horn of Africa. Ethiopia's pursuit of port access at Assab intersects with these great-power competitions, making a local conflict a potential trigger for regional escalation [1].
