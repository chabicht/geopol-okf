# Background

Geopolitical Futures is a strategic analysis publication founded by George Friedman that provides structured assessments of global geopolitical dynamics. GPF's approach emphasizes structural drivers — geography, demographics, military capacity, and economic fundamentals — over personality-driven analysis. The publication produces detailed country and regional assessments, often accompanied by maps that illustrate the geographic constraints driving political outcomes. GPF also publishes regular analytical articles by contributors including George Friedman, Antonia Colibasanu, Kamran Bokhari, and Ekaterina Zolotova, covering global geopolitical developments, Middle East security dynamics, and Russia-Ukraine war analysis [18][19][20][21][22][23][24].

# Key Frameworks

## Structural Drivers Over Personality

GPF's analytical approach prioritizes structural factors — geographic constraints, demographic trajectories, institutional capacity — over the individual leader decisions that [O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) emphasizes. This creates a complementary tension: GPF explains why structural pressures exist, while O'Brien explains why the timing and direction of action depend on the specific leader in power.

## GeoEconomicLens as Systemic Product

GPF's monthly GeoEconomicLens (GEL) report views the global economy through the lens of geopolitics — tracing how wars, sanctions, and chokepoint disruptions cascade through energy, food, defense, insurance, and financial markets. The methodology is systemic by design: rather than treating each crisis as a discrete event, each issue maps the propagation of geopolitical shocks across regions and sectors, with regional breakdowns, scenario sets, and a month-ahead outlook. The May 2026 issue examined the fallout from the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) across all major regions and sectors, with contributions from Friedman, Colibasanu, Davidson, Herczegh, Wordsworth, and Zolotova [15]. The June 2026 issue focused on inflationary pressures reshaping the global economy, covering the AI crisis, African resource nationalism, EU defense financing, Venezuela's reopening, ASEAN energy coordination, Russian logistics risks, a global inflation feature with three scenarios, and Australia-Vietnam hedging case studies [16]. The July 2026 issue focused on the coming defense boom and geoeconomic transformations — the US heavy rare earths program, Russia's fuel crisis, the yuan undervaluation debate, the structural defense boom feature, EV industrial competition, the geoeconomics of water, and de-dollarization trends, with new contributor Juan Francisco Munoz joining the GEL team [17].

## Friedman's Geographic Determinism

George Friedman's recurring posture is that geography, demographics, and institutional capacity set the envelope within which leader choices and events play out. His 2026 forecast framed US-[China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) accommodation as the most important geopolitical issue, with [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) as a regional power and the [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) devolving into a multiregional system [7]. He argues the Iran war's most important long-term effect is on US-China bilateral relations — China's abstention driven by economic dependence on the US market and Hormuz-transited energy, with potential for a post-war economic accommodation analogous to post-WWII US-Germany/Japan [15]. He frames India's July 2026 Asia tour (Indonesia, Australia, New Zealand) as an emerging coalition more significant than the Quad, with economic and naval dimensions that could fundamentally shift the global system [18]. His Adam Smith framing treats economic and military power as inextricable, with geoeconomics as the methodology for understanding geopolitical effects on national economies [19]. He argues Russia cannot expand the war to another European nation because of poor military performance, [Poland]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/poland.html) as a fundamental problem, and US troops in Poland [21]. His philosophical analysis of AI identifies a dual danger — failure to realize potential, and success that displaces workers en masse — with the singularity as modern myth (Golem/Talos parallels) and the Luddite lesson that job destruction without replacement creates social and economic chaos [16].

## The GPF Analyst Bench

GPF's analytical output is produced by a bench of analysts with distinct thematic lanes. **Antonia Colibasanu** covers European risk redistribution (her "BIF" framework tracks risk shifting from the PIIGS periphery to the Britain-Italy-France core, with energy shock transmitting through sovereign financing via German yields [15]), EU SAFE defense financing debates [16], the yuan undervaluation debate [17], the structural defense boom feature with Davidson [17], the geoeconomics of water [17], AI transforming cybersecurity [16], and the NATO Ankara summit delivery analysis [23]. **Ekaterina Zolotova** covers Russian economic strain (second consecutive GDP decline, capital flight to Dubai/Hong Kong/Tel Aviv, Communist Party warning of revolution risk [15]), Russian logistics risks (seaport cargo increases alongside structural vulnerabilities — Russian Railways crisis, Northern Sea Route decline, foreign carrier dependence [16]), Russia's fuel crisis (Ukrainian drone attacks reducing gasoline production 25% YoY [17]), the global inflation feature [16], and the structural liabilities of Russia's size and centralization against Ukrainian drone strikes reaching Siberia [22]. **Victoria Herczegh** covers China-Taiwan dual-track strategy (Beijing combining coercion with selective engagement; Taiwanese investment in China dropping from 80% to 4% [15]), ASEAN energy coordination (Indonesia's oil storage hub proposal, ASEAN Power Grid [16]), and Australia-Vietnam hedging case studies with Wordsworth [16]. **Ronan Wordsworth** covers AI talent reverse brain drain (Chinese engineers returning home, declining US PhD applications, visa uncertainty as push factor [15]), resource nationalism in Africa (Zimbabwe lithium ban, Ghana 30% state participation, Mali Barrick dispute [16]), EV industrial competition [17], and the Australia-Vietnam hedging case studies with Herczegh [16]. **Andrew Davidson** covers defense industry structural shifts (energy and digital infrastructure as core defense vulnerabilities, 70% of US Army logistics tonnage is fuel, microreactors and microgrids for contested logistics [15]) and the structural defense boom feature with Colibasanu [17]. **Kamran Bokhari** covers Turkey capitalizing on Iran's decline (Turkey's expansion across Syria, Lebanon, Iraq, and Egypt; Iraq as emerging Turkish-Iranian competition arena [20]) and the Red Sea as strategic basin (evolving from Houthi non-state threat theater to interstate rivalry arena [24]). **Juan Francisco Munoz** (new contributor as of July 2026) covers Mexico-Brazil energy cooperation (Pemex-Petrobras MoU, Global South diversification [17]).

## GPF Analysis Across the Bundle

Beyond the frameworks above, GPF analysis appears across multiple concept types in the bundle: the structural rupture in [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html)'s military command [1] and its Maoist ideological turn [8]; pre-war analysis of [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s factional dynamics and IRGC erosion [2]; [Horn of Africa]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/africa.html) competition including Israel's Somaliland recognition and the [Tigray]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/tigray-conflict.html) powder keg [3][9]; [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html)'s Chechnya problem [4] and declining capacity in Africa where Turkey sees an opening [5]; [Central Asia]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/central-asia.html) investment patterns [6]; Middle East alliance evolution — middle powers supplementing Cold War-era alliances with region-specific arrangements [10], the CSTO as failed alliance model [11], the US transition from Israel-centric to multilateral security with [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) as dominant regional player [12], and the return of the nation-state [13]; and the southern flank erosion from the Black Sea through the South Caucasus to Central Asia [14].
