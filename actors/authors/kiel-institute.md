# Background

The Kiel Institute for the World Economy (Kiel Institut) is a research institute focusing on global economic policy, international trade, and macroeconomic analysis. Its June 2026 report "Endgame: The State of the Russian Economy" provides a comprehensive assessment of [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html)'s wartime economic trajectory, produced by a distinguished group of economists. The report's quantitative rigor and institutional credibility make it a key reference for analysts tracking the sustainability of Russia's war effort [1].

# Key Frameworks

## Russia's Unsustainable Macro Stance

The Kiel report concludes that Russia's current macro stance is "not sustainable." High interest rates are stifling the economy while loose fiscal policy and quasi-fiscal operations prop up the defense sector. The Q1 2026 budget deficit exceeded the full-year target in just three months. The choice is between fiscal consolidation or monetary accommodation resulting in even higher inflation. Export revenues from the sale of raw materials remain the decisive variable: with fiscal buffers spent, Russia's war capacity is more than ever directly coupled to hydrocarbon export income [1].

## Fiscal Buffers Exhausted

[Putin]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/putin.html) has thrown away the entirety of Russia's once large sovereign wealth fund. The liquid portion has shrunk from 6.5% of GDP at the start of the war to 1.8% in April 2026. Russia is essentially living hand-to-mouth, with all buffers spent and the war effort funded entirely through energy sales. This creates a structural vulnerability: any disruption to hydrocarbon export revenue — whether from [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html)'s long-range strike campaign or from sanctions enforcement — directly translates into reduced war capacity [1][2].

## European Opportunity

The report argues that Russia's economic fragility creates an opportunity for Ukraine's European partners. Because Russia's war capacity is now directly coupled to hydrocarbon income, strict enforcement of sanctions on Russia's shadow fleet and secondary sanctions on facilitating banks in [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), Hong Kong, and other countries could push the Russian economy toward exhaustion. The Kiel report frames this as a time-constrained window: the moment is ripe for a pressure campaign [1][2].
