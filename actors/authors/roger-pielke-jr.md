# Background

Roger Pielke Jr. is a political scientist and climate policy analyst whose work focuses on the integrity of climate science and its use in policy. He has long been a critic of the misuse of extreme emissions scenarios (particularly RCP8.5) and of climate economics research that produces inflated damage estimates. His analysis of the retraction of a major Nature paper claiming $38 trillion per year in climate damages provides a case study in how methodological failures propagate through the policy apparatus.

# Key Frameworks

## Climate Economics Methodology Critique

Pielke argues that climate economics research is built on a flawed foundation: the conflation of worst-case emissions scenarios (RCP8.5) with business-as-usual projections, the use of temperature-impact functions calibrated to implausible assumptions, and the absence of adaptation in damage models. The retraction of the Nature paper — which was cited in major policy documents and central bank guidance — exposes a systemic problem: institutions are building regulations on unsound science [1].

## Institutional Propagation of Error

The retraction cascade illustrates how methodological errors propagate: a paper published in a top journal gets cited in IPCC reports, central bank guidance (NGFS), and regulatory impact assessments. By the time the error is discovered, the policy infrastructure has already been built on the flawed foundation. The implication is that the peer review system is not sufficient to catch errors that confirm institutional preferences [1].
