# Background

The Stiftung Wissenschaft und Politik (SWP) is Germany's foremost foreign and security policy research institute, headquartered in Berlin. It advises the Bundestag and the federal government on international affairs, producing peer-reviewed policy research across regional and thematic divisions: Africa and Middle East, Asia, Eastern Europe, EU, and global issues including climate and energy policy. SWP publications are subject to internal peer review, fact-checking, and copy-editing [1][2][3][4][5].

# Key Frameworks

## Climate and Energy Diplomacy

Jule Könneke (Global Issues Research Division) analyses Colombia's supply-side climate policy under Petro as a test case for fossil fuel-dependent emerging economies. The framework examines whether decarbonisation can be achieved alongside economic stability and social cohesion in states with limited fiscal space — and whether international support (German, EU) is available on sufficient scale and affordable terms. The framework identifies implementation gaps between international climate leadership and national institutional capacity as a structural vulnerability [1].

## Conflict Prevention and Regional Security

Gerrit Kurtz (Africa and Middle East Research Division) analyses the Tigray conflict's "gray zone" between war and peace — neither the Pretoria Agreement's full implementation nor a return to open war, but a strategic game of incremental escalation. The framework identifies three fault lines of Pretoria (disputed territories, IDP return, DDR stagnation) and traces how internal TPLF dynamics, Abiy Ahmed's divide-and-rule strategy, Eritrea's tactical realignment, and regional spillover risks (Sudan, Egypt, UAE) create escalation potential. The analysis prescribes preventive diplomacy through balanced mediation and IMF/World Bank leverage [2].

## European Foreign Policy and Soft Power

Isabelle Werenfels (Africa and Middle East Research Division) identifies Europe's "visibility dilemma" in Tunisia — a structural mismatch between substantial European engagement (85% of FDI, budget support, grants) and near-zero public attribution, contrasted with China's high-visibility engagement. The framework examines how authoritarian constraints, migration diplomacy, and the Gaza war have eroded European soft power while Chinese influence grows through infrastructure and cultural projects [3].

## Indo-Pacific Strategy

Pia Dannhauer (Asia Research Division) analyses Southeast Asia's strategic significance for German foreign policy beyond the de-risking frame. The framework argues that Germany's Indo-Pacific policy has over-focused on Northeast Asia and India, neglecting Southeast Asia's role as an arena of great-power rivalry, hub for global supply chains, and increasingly vocal multilateral presence. Country-specific strategies are prescribed over one-size-fits-all approaches, recognizing ASEAN heterogeneity in political systems, geopolitical orientations, and economic structures [4].

## Turkey's Autocratisation

Yaşar Aydın (Centre for Applied Turkey Studies / CATS) documents Turkey's trajectory from competitive authoritarianism to full autocracy, using the 2016 failed coup as the inflection point. The framework traces how emergency decrees (130,000 dismissed, 332,000 arrested), the 2017 constitutional referendum (contested, narrow victory), and the concentration of executive power produced institutional erosion. The economic dimension — declining GDP growth, falling FDI, central bank independence loss, lira volatility — is documented as a direct consequence of autocratisation [5].

## Xenophobic Mobilization as Governance Substitute

Melanie Müller (Africa and Middle East Research Division) analyses the 2026 South African anti-migration movement as a case study in how anti-migration sentiment is organized into a substitute for governance. The framework traces Operation Dudula and March and March as networked, social-media-amplified mobilizations that exploit structural unemployment (32% official, 60%+ youth), service-delivery failure, and the ANC's unfulfilled transformation promise. It identifies the orchestration dimension — funding links to the Zuma family circle, MK party backing, algorithmic online amplification — and the policy response pattern: the GNU's "Comprehensive Approach for Migration Management" prioritizing enforcement over migrant rights despite the November 2025 High Court judgment. The framework prescribes collaborative German-South African and EU-South African engagement on rights-respecting migration management, including support for neighboring states absorbing returnees. See [South Africa]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/south-africa.html) and [Migration Policy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/migration-policy.html) [6].

## Healthcare Cybersecurity as National Security

Bayerlein, Bendiek, Hemmelskamp, and von der Forst analyse the cybersecurity of the healthcare sector as a systemic threat to public health and national security, drawing on the European Repository of Cyber Incidents (EuRepoC) database of 351 significant cyberattacks on health facilities between 2022 and 2025. The framework classifies attacks into six clusters by intensity, pattern, attribution, and operational consequence — from data theft (C2/C5, most frequent) to disruptive long-term outages (C0/C1/C4, most consequential). It identifies the structural drivers: digitalization expanding attack surfaces, supply-chain cascades (the Stryker and Octapharma cases), and the gap between national critical-infrastructure protection and the international dimension of the threat. The Stryker case — a "wiper" attack claimed by the Iranian Handala group in retaliation for a February 2026 Israeli missile strike that killed 175 at an Iranian school — illustrates the direct linkage between kinetic conflict and cyber escalation against healthcare infrastructure. The framework proposes a coordinating role for the German National Security Council across 18 critical infrastructure sectors, and an extension of the WHO's Surveillance System for Attacks on Health Care (SSA) to cover cyberattacks, modelled on IAEA confidential incident-reporting. See [Cognitive Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/cognitive-warfare.html) and [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html) [7].

## Route-Based Migration: Protection and Control

Nadine Knapp and Anne Koch (Global Issues Research Division) map the rise of route-based approaches to migration policy — treating migration corridors as connected political and humanitarian spaces rather than isolated country contexts. The framework identifies the concept's inherent ambiguity: the same "route-based" framing serves humanitarian organizations (IFRC, UNHCR, IOM) seeking better protection along corridors and European governments seeking to externalize migration control through cooperation with origin and transit states. The EU's "Whole-of-Route Approach," its Team-Europe Initiatives along the central Mediterranean and Atlantic routes, and its migration deals with Tunisia and Mauritania illustrate the control orientation. The framework prescribes binding the route-based approach with verifiable protection indicators, independent monitoring, local-actor participation, and inclusion of South-South mobility patterns rather than treating all routes as European-bound. See [Migration Policy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/migration-policy.html) and [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) [8].
