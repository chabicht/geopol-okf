# Background

Michael Beckley is a political scientist whose work on great power competition challenges the declinist narrative by emphasizing the structural durability of US hegemony. His core argument is that the United States possesses the only true sphere of influence in the Western Hemisphere — a geographic, economic, and military asymmetry that no rival can match. This does not mean the US is invincible, but it means the competition is not symmetric: the US has deep defensive depth, while China and Russia are surrounded by hostile or wary neighbors.

# Key Frameworks

## Asymmetric Spheres of Influence

Beckley argues that the US enjoys a monopoly on true sphere-of-influence control in the Western Hemisphere, rooted in the Monroe Doctrine and sustained by geographic isolation and overwhelming military dominance. China and Russia, by contrast, cannot consolidate spheres of influence in their own neighborhoods — they are surrounded by states that resist their dominance. The asymmetry is dangerous but also creates an opening: as allies rearm and the US leans into its hemispheric advantage, a tougher, more resilient free world is possible [1].

## Military and Economic Centrality

The US remains the military and economic center of gravity for the Western coalition. The dollar zone, military preponderance, and geographic depth combine to create a structural advantage that persists even as individual capabilities erode. The implication is that declinist narratives that extrapolate from specific failures (e.g., the [Iran war](/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.md)) to systemic decline miss the structural resilience of the US position [1].
