# Background

Aurelien is a pseudonymous writer whose essays on the Substack publication "Aurelien" combine historical analysis, institutional critique, and conflict theory. His work is distinguished by a skepticism toward formal models of international relations — particularly game theory and escalation theory — which he argues fail to capture the actual dynamics of contemporary conflicts. His analysis of the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html), the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html), and the broader [global system rupture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) provides a counterpoint to the framework-driven approaches of [Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) and [O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html).

# Key Frameworks

## Critique of Escalation Theory

Aurelien argues that Western conflict models — Game Theory, Escalation Theory, and Herman Kahn's Escalation Ladder — fail to explain current conflicts because they originate in mathematical economics with rational-actor assumptions that do not apply to real international politics. The theories assume unitary rational actors, symmetric information, and calculable utility functions — none of which hold in the actual conflicts driving the current rupture [1].

## Western Objectives as Unreconcilable

Aurelien's analysis of the Iran war frames the US and Israeli positions as fundamentally unreconcilable with Iran's: the US wants to damage and destroy Iran as a functioning state; Israel wants to eliminate the only country standing between it and regional domination; Iran wants an end to sanctions, recognition as the dominant regional power, and the eviction of the US from the region. These positions have zero overlap, meaning there is nothing to negotiate — diplomacy is theater, and the nuclear enrichment issue is an excuse [2].

## Elite Homogenization

Aurelien's concept of elite homogenization — the convergence of Western ruling elites toward a single worldview regardless of nominal political affiliation — is cited by [The Honest Sorcerer]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-honest-sorcerer.html) as a structural feature of the "Zero State": a system unable to generate growth, where elites choose war as their "last sniff" and govern through bureaucracy and corruption rather than innovation [3].

## Strategic Surprise Taxonomy

Aurelien develops a typology of strategic surprise — the persistent phenomenon of governments being caught off guard by events that were in principle foreseeable. Five types [4][5]:

1. **Surprise-at-failure**: The most common type. The outbreak of conflict was predicted, the methods were expected, but the *outcome* was a surprise because the overall strategic situation was read wrongly. The [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) is a classic case: the US assumed Iran's state was fragile, that bombing would degrade it, and that the Iranian people would rise up — all necessary assumptions that *had* to be true for the objective to succeed, and therefore were assumed true. Operation Barbarossa (1941) and the Iraq invasion (2003) fit the same pattern of backwards logic from desired outcome to necessary false assumptions [4].

2. **Surprise-by-indifference**: A lack of interest in finding out what the situation actually is. The Wehrmacht did not bother studying the Red Army before Barbarossa. The Argentine junta gave no thought to possible British reaction before invading the Falklands. Western planners gave little thought to the Sunni-Shia divide before dismantling the Iraqi state [4].

3. **Surprise-by-complacency**: Believing the current situation is understood and stable, so further research is unnecessary. The failure to anticipate the 1979 Islamic Revolution in Iran: US embassies had few Farsi speakers, interacted only with westernized elites, and conceptual blindness prevented imagining a religious political movement — Khomeini was compared to Martin Luther King and Gandhi [4].

4. **Surprise-by-ignorance**: Genuine impossibility of knowing what you need to know. Pearl Harbor: the US Navy did not believe the Japanese had torpedoes capable of operating in shallow harbor waters — and they were right, until a few months before the attack when the Japanese developed the necessary fuse [4].

5. **Surprise-as-state-change**: A sudden discontinuity revealing that reality was always different from what was supposed. The collapse of Communist regimes in Eastern Europe in 1989; the collapse of the Afghan National Army in 2021; the fall of the Assad regime in 2024. In each case, the existing system's capabilities were massively overestimated — the surprise reflects not the strength of the challenger but the weakness of the incumbent [4].

Aurelien identifies **conceptual blindness** as the most important root cause: in order to anticipate something, you must believe it is possible. The "plodding, materialist, reductive conception of politics" dominant in Western capitals has proven "basically useless at anticipating real events in the world" — from the barbarities of the Islamic State to the rise of nationalism in Eastern Europe to how China, Russia, and Iran actually think [4].

## Policy Management vs Substance

Aurelien argues that policy-making has been progressively replaced by "Policy Management" — virtually all time and attention goes to procedure, and almost nothing is left for substance. The head of a Foreign Ministry department spends their day on meetings, briefings, media preparation, interagency coordination, and crisis response — with no time for structured analysis of the actual situation. Procedural issues take priority because they are immediate and everyone can understand them, while substantive analysis is deferred. The result is a system that reacts to surprises rather than anticipating them [4].

## Western Influence Decline

Aurelien traces the erosion of Western influence from its historical roots. The missionary and colonial era was characterized by seriousness, long-term commitment, and moral purpose — administrators and educators spent lifetimes in-country, built schools and hospitals, and took their vocation seriously. The post-independence aid industry, by contrast, is characterized by short rotations (three years), performative projects, mind-numbing bureaucracy, and a self-sustaining consultancy class that extracts rents from the aid pipeline [6].

The structural problems are compounded by the contemporary West's loss of credibility: "We continue to preach governance although our political systems are collapsing. We continue to try to influence foreign militaries when our own have practically ceased to exist. We offer assistance on combating drug smuggling when parts of Europe have themselves become narco-states." The West is no longer listened to out of respect, but out of nostalgia and habit — and because it still has the money to fund the projects. The deeper problem is the West's "soulless and unappealing liberal-technocratic managerial ideology" competing against states whose elites project "not just competence, but a genuine civilisational ideology" [6].
