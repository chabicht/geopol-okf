# Background

War on the Rocks is a defense and foreign policy analysis publication featuring commentary from practitioners, scholars, and military officers. Its positioning — at the intersection of serving and former officers, area specialists, and policy analysts — gives it a distinctive voice: technically granular, operationally literate, and oriented toward the mechanics of warfare rather than the theoretical framing of it. The bundle draws on six WOTR contributions that cluster around three analytical lenses: the architecture of Middle East coercion and diplomacy, the Russia-Ukraine war's lessons on mass, drones, and wartime governance, and the shape of the Indo-Pacific coalition and its logistical underpinning. The publication also produces region-focused digests such as *The Ukraine Compass*, which curates Ukrainian-language commentary to widen Western coverage beyond the battlefield [1].

# Key Frameworks

## Middle East: Doctrine and Diplomacy

Two WOTR contributions frame the Middle East settlement from opposite ends — the coercive doctrine that creates leverage, and the diplomatic pattern that determines how leverage is converted (or not) into sovereignty-restoring agreements.

Pnina Shuker and Andrew Milburn document how [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s success in the [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) was not improvised but the product of a twenty-year campaign plan. The mosaic defense doctrine — formalized in 2005 by the IRGC's Center for Strategy under Mohammad Ali Jafari and ideologue Hassan Abbasi — created a decentralized command structure designed to survive decapitation strikes. Iran's theory of victory was narrow but robust: survive long enough to impose costs through maritime coercion (Hormuz), economic disruption, information dominance, and political will. The deal sequence favors Iran: before it has to resolve a single hard issue — the nuclear stockpile, the missile program, or its regional proxies — the United States lifts the naval blockade, withdraws forces, issues sanctions waivers, and makes frozen assets available. "Pay first, verify later is not a strategy. It is leverage leaving the room." The full analysis is dissolved into [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html), [Iran Ceasefire MoU]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html), and [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) [2].

Ahmed Saber Abbas — a contributing analyst at New Lines Institute — supplies the complementary diplomatic lens: peace treaties do more than end a phase of fighting, they redistribute leverage, sovereignty, and risk across the fronts left outside the agreement. The pattern is "front separation": closing — or attempting to close — the front that imposes the greatest immediate cost while detaching the unresolved fronts from the wider political and bargaining context that had linked them. The [Camp David Accords]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/camp-david-accords.html) addressed the Egyptian front first because [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/egypt.html) possessed the greatest capacity to impose direct military and political costs on [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html); the Palestinian and Syrian fronts were left outside a comprehensive settlement. The same pattern is reasserting itself in the negotiations around the [Iran Ceasefire MoU]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html): [Lebanon]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/lebanon.html) is being detached from the Iran track and recast as a security problem, with Israeli withdrawal made conditional on Hizballah's disarmament rather than governed by a defined timetable. The danger is that peace becomes fragile when it closes the front that imposes the greatest immediate cost while leaving sovereignty unresolved on the others — the agreement preserves the grievances, political vacuum, and unequal security conditions through which future resistance can grow. The full analysis is dissolved into [Lebanon]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/lebanon.html), [Camp David Accords]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/camp-david-accords.html), [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html), [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/egypt.html), and [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) [6].

Read together, the two frameworks describe a single structural problem: Iran's mosaic defense manufactures the coercion that makes front separation attractive to the stronger party, and front separation in turn preserves the unresolved sovereignty through which Iran's leverage renews itself.

## The Russia-Ukraine War: Mass, Drones, and Wartime Governance

Two WOTR contributions address the Russia-Ukraine war's central paradox from opposite sides: why Russia's mass answer failed, and how Ukraine's techno-reformist answer is being politically undone.

Greg Whisler and Michael Kofman provide the most detailed account of why [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html)'s wartime force expansion failed to deliver battlefield success despite sustaining a manpower advantage through most of the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html). The most significant Russian military expansion in decades grew the deployed force to over 700,000 personnel, yet as Russia fielded more forces, the value of that mass steadily diminished: cycles of technological innovation — particularly Ukrainian investment in [drones]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) — led to greater dispersal, negating advantages from manpower superiority, while the Russian military chose a cost-ineffective approach to offensives. The first large-scale extended war Russia has fought using primarily contracted volunteers challenges prior assumptions about mobilization. The full analysis is dissolved into [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html), [Drone Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html), and [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html) [3].

The Ukraine Compass special edition documents the other side of the paradox: the dismissal of Ukrainian Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov in July 2026 and the protests it sparked in Kyiv. The analysis identifies three elements central to Ukrainian political life since 2022 — trust (which partners, officials, and media can be trusted), technology (Fedorov's doctrine of rapid tech iteration over mass mobilization, making drone warfare Ukraine's most lethal capability), and turnover (the fifth Defense Minister since the full-scale invasion, in a system where appointment and dismissal run on loyalty rather than parliamentary process). The divide between Kyiv's streets and the frontline is real: a democracy at war cannot survive without public trust and a hopeful way forward, and an army at war cannot survive without soldiers who have learned not to wait for a savior. The full analysis is dissolved into [Fedorov Dismissal (July 2026)]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/fedorov-dismissal-2026.html), [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html), [Zelensky]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/zelensky.html), [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html), [Drone Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html), and [NATO Ankara Summit 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html) [7].

Whisler/Kofman explain why Russia's mass failed against Ukraine's drone-innovation answer; the Ukraine Compass explains how that same techno-reformist answer is being rolled back by a trust and turnover dynamic that rewards loyalty over capability. The war's military logic favors Ukraine's approach; its political logic may undo the very reforms that produced the advantage.

## Indo-Pacific: Alignment and Sustainment

Two WOTR contributions address the Indo-Pacific coalition from its strategic premise and its operational geography.

War on the Rocks argues that [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html) will stick with the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) despite Trump-era friction because [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) is the overriding strategic concern driving the alignment. The analysis is dissolved into the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html) region concept [4].

Dan Justice — a U.S. Navy foreign area officer serving as a federal executive fellow at the RAND Corporation — maps the logistical geography that this alignment presupposes. As the Navy shifts from a decade of developing unmanned systems to fielding them in the operational fleet, it lacks a framework for fixing these platforms overseas. Medium and large unmanned surface and undersea vehicles are subject to the same unforgiving maintenance realities as manned warships (oil filters, O-rings, corrosion control, sensor calibration) but carry no crew to perform on-station maintenance or casualty control. The paradox of access: shallower drafts and smaller footprints allow unmanned vehicles to use port locations physically inaccessible to large warships (HMAS Cairns, Lombrum), and uncrewed diesel-electric submarines can bypass restrictions limiting manned nuclear submarines. But uncrewed hulls lack a worldwide settled legal status as warships, requiring individual host-nation agreements for transiting territorial waters — archipelagic states in the Pacific may deny transit, and compulsory pilotage may be enforced. The Navy should build a distributed network of smaller, austere ports (Australia, PNG, Philippines, Vietnam, Indonesia) rather than concentrating repair capability at large, static, vulnerable bases like Guam or Yokosuka. The organizational choice is between organic expeditionary repair squadrons and a contractor-led "lead sustainment integrator" model — a blended approach may prove optimal. The full analysis is dissolved into [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html), [Drone Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html), [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html), and the relevant country concepts [5].

Both arguments take the same coalition as given and identify China as the organizing pressure: the strategic argument explains why the alignment holds, the sustainment argument explains where the alignment's hardware actually lives and how it would be repaired in conflict.
