# Background

Professor Robert Pape runs the "Escalation Trap" newsletter, focused on staying ahead of risks that matter. His analytical approach centers on leverage dynamics — how the distribution of coercive advantage shifts during a war and how those shifts determine whether a conflict moves toward termination or new escalation. Pape's work on the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) provides a leverage-centered counterpoint to O'Brien's leader-centered framework and Tchakarova's system-centered framework.

# Key Frameworks

## Leverage and War Termination

Pape's central framework: states gaining leverage rarely hurry to end wars. The logic is structural — if your position is improving, the cost of continued fighting is decreasing relative to the expected gains from a better settlement. This framework inverts the common assumption that wars end when both sides are exhausted; Pape argues wars end when the leverage gradient stabilizes or reverses.

## Iran's Leverage Shift

Pape argues that [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) shifted from negotiating to stop losing to negotiating to win more. This is a phase transition, not a marginal change: the basis of Iran's negotiating posture flipped from damage limitation to advantage extraction. The implication is that Iran's growing leverage may weaken the [US]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) military position in the Gulf and set the stage for Iranian nuclear weapons — the leverage is not merely military but strategic, creating space for nuclear escalation.

## Hormuz as Leverage, Not Theater

Hormuz remains open because Iran permits it. Pape's framework reframes the chokepoint: the [Strait of Hormuz]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) is not a battlefield where shipping happens to pass through but a lever that Iran controls. Control of the chokepoint is the real leverage — not the ability to close it, but the ability to decide whether it stays open. This makes every shipping passage through Hormuz a concession Iran grants, not a right that exists independently.

## Commercial Shipping as Escalation Pathway

Pape predicted commercial shipping as the next escalation pathway — a forecast confirmed by subsequent attacks. This prediction flowed from the leverage framework: if Iran's leverage is chokepoint control, then the natural escalation vector is the traffic that depends on the chokepoint. The confirmation of this prediction validates the predictive power of the leverage-centered approach.

## Three Indicators Framework

Pape uses three indicators for assessing whether a war is ending or entering a new phase. This framework operationalizes the leverage concept into trackable signals — moving from abstract leverage gradients to observable indicators that can be monitored in real time.

## Controlled Instability

Pape introduces "controlled instability" as Iran's post-war strategic doctrine: persistent low-level disruptions — drone incidents, insurance volatility, shipping harassment — that sustain a geopolitical risk premium on oil without requiring total Hormuz closure. If oil stays above $90 even after Hormuz reopens, Iran wins through a continuous revenue stream from the risk premium. This transforms Iran's chokepoint leverage from a binary weapon (open or closed) into a continuous revenue stream [4].

## The August Leverage Window

Pape forecasts that August 2026 could become the decisive month of the Iran War. The US Strategic Petroleum Reserve is approaching its operational floor of ~243 million barrels by mid-to-late August, precisely as hurricane season threatens Gulf Coast production. The [Supreme Leader's funeral]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/supreme-leader-funeral-2026.html) has stiffened Iranian resolve through massive nationalist mobilization, making harder demands more politically feasible. By August, Iran is likely to possess three forms of leverage simultaneously: growing nationalist confidence, continuing Hormuz influence, and an opponent with diminishing economic room. The greatest danger is that both sides conclude they cannot afford to let the other enter September with greater leverage [5].

## New War Aim: Ending US Gulf Presence

Pape identifies a strategic shift enabled by the funeral: using political momentum to weaken and ultimately end the permanent American military presence in the Gulf. Iran is appealing directly to Gulf monarchies — offering lower transit fees for states that distance themselves from Washington, higher costs for those hosting US bases. The central demand is becoming the gradual removal of American military power from the Gulf itself [5].

## The Escalation Trap Three-Stage Framework

Pape's foundational framework, published February 25, 2026 before the first bombs fell, models the war as proceeding through three stages based on over 20 years of modeling the bombing of Iran:

- **Stage 1**: Initial bombing is tactically successful — bombs hit targets, kill leaders, destroy launchers — but the target can lash back. This is exactly what happened.
- **Stage 2**: Horizontal escalation — Iran counter-escalated and took Hormuz, as Pape predicted before the first bombs fell. The US shifted to a punishment campaign within a week, then to an eight-week blockade, then to the MOU inducement strategy (the Biden approach). All failed.
- **Stage 3**: Ground war — the stage the US is now approaching. Pape argues there is no third choice between surrender and escalation: either Trump capitulates and accepts Iran as the rising regional hegemon (and likely the fourth center of world power within a year or two), or he escalates. "Everybody wants there to be a third choice. That doesn't make a third choice." [7][8]

The Escalation Trap occurs when the attacker is tactically successful but becomes strategically trapped: each escalation produces a counter-escalation that raises the political costs of exit, creating a self-reinforcing spiral. The framework has been the most accurate predictor of the war's trajectory over its four and a half months [8].

## Two Engines of Escalation

Pape identifies two distinct, mutually reinforcing pathways now driving the war:

1. **Strategic escalation**: Iran's Revolutionary Guard insists only Tehran will control Hormuz; US Central Command continues preparing to guarantee freedom of navigation. These objectives cannot both be achieved. [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) has moved from punishment to reversing the strategic defeat at Hormuz.

2. **Revenge escalation**: Millions filled Iranian streets beneath red flags symbolizing vengeance. The new Supreme Leader's call to action — vowing to avenge "the pure blood... and all the martyrs" — has transformed revenge from rhetoric into an independent political objective. The Intelligence Ministry released an official decree stating vengeance is "a national demand." [8]

The critical insight is that these engines interact: even if one slows, the other can continue driving escalation. "It takes two to taco" — Iran is not simply forgiving the killing of its leaders and thousands of civilians. This makes the conflict far more dangerous than most commentary recognizes [7][8].

## Trump's Escalation Logic

Pape explains why Trump is escalating despite the midterms — a question puzzling many analysts. Two factors drive the logic:

**Factor 1 — The Hoover Risk**: Trump signed the MOU to avoid an economic crisis, explicitly saying he did not want to be "Herbert Hoover." But the MOU failed to stop the oil inventory drawdown. The world gained only about 14 days of supply from a couple hundred million barrels released. The inventory cliff is still coming in August or late August. The SPR salt caverns face structural risk: oil is pumped out by injecting water, and at low levels the water eats away the cavern walls causing collapse. Nobody knows where that threshold is. "We are on the Titanic and the economy is heading toward a cliff" [8].

**Factor 2 — MAGA Turnout**: According to the Reagan Institute's nationwide Summer Survey (May 26–June 3), 51 percent of self-identified MAGA Republicans favor replacing Iran's government, while only 25 percent prefer a negotiated settlement. If Trump fails to open Hormuz and allows Iran to become the fourth center of world power, a significant portion of MAGA will not show up at the polls. "You only need to lose 5 or 10 percent of your voting base before you lose a lot of seats." The Tucker Carlson isolationist wing has not taken over MAGA — that's why Carlson wants to start a third party [8].

Trump is escalating to reopen Hormuz — not because he wants a ground war, and not because the military option is certain to succeed, but because it is certain that if he doesn't open Hormuz, he will fail politically. "He's trying to find a way to not have it be just about him" [8].

## Iran's Three-Phase Evolution

Pape tracks Iran's political objectives through three expanding phases:

1. **Survival** (Feb–Mar 2026): After decapitation of senior leadership, Tehran's overriding goal was preserving the regime and preventing military collapse.
2. **Ambition** (Apr–Jun 2026): Once Iran demonstrated it could contest Hormuz, it began using that leverage to pursue a new regional security order centered on Iranian influence stretching from Lebanon through Iraq and the Gulf to Yemen and the Red Sea.
3. **Revenge** (Jul 2026–): The Supreme Leader's call to action adds a third objective that does not replace the first two. Revenge is now an independent political objective that leaders must satisfy alongside survival and ambition, even when doing so increases escalation risk [8].

## Suicide Terrorism Strategic Logic

Pape, who compiled the first complete database of all suicide attacks after 9/11, applies 25 years of research to the current situation. His central finding: 95% of suicide attacks are carried out for a strategic purpose — to coerce democracies to withdraw military forces from territory the terrorists prize. This was the stunning finding that led to his unique prediction (no other terrorism expert made it) that invading Iraq would touch off the largest suicide terrorist campaign in modern times — a prediction that came true [8].

The data show that suicide terrorism exploded after the US occupied Iraq and Afghanistan, and declined when the forever wars ended. "We are doing the reverse" in Iran — escalating in a way that historically generates suicide terrorism. The greatest danger is not centrally directed operations but **inspired attacks** by individuals or small networks responding to a broader political appeal [8].

**Rushdie fatwa parallel**: The closest historical parallel to the Supreme Leader's call to action is Ayatollah Khomeini's 1989 fatwa calling for the execution of Salman Rushdie — which led to attacks and deaths for decades around the world. The main difference is that the strategic implications of the current call to action are significantly greater. Pape expects "one possibly two attacks" — mostly inspired, not command-directed — to fuel public support for Stage 3 [8].

## Stage 3 Military Preparation

Pape argues the US military is already pre-positioning and preparing the battlefield for Stage 3 ground operations, based on CENTCOM data and satellite imagery:

- **Carrier repositioning**: Two carrier strike groups moved from the Indian Ocean (1,000 miles away, out of drone/missile range) into the Gulf of Oman — a costly signal placing 5,000–6,000 personnel within Iranian missile and drone range. States do not normally accept these risks unless they believe the strategic gains justify them [8].
- **Coastal suppression bombing**: The overwhelming majority of recent strikes target coastal surveillance radars, command-and-control networks, anti-ship missile batteries, drone launch sites, naval facilities, and ammunition depots along Iran's southeastern coast — the classic preparatory targets for establishing local air and maritime superiority before amphibious or littoral operations [8].
- **Marine Expeditionary Unit**: Repositioned closer to the Gulf of Oman, giving commanders a rapidly deployable force for limited ground operations [8].

**Plan A**: Move carriers close enough to provide air cover for the "Southern Passage" — the route near Oman — protecting ships from Iranian drones and anti-ship missiles. This aligns with CENTCOM rhetoric [8].

**Plan B**: If air cover is insufficient, take territory on the coast — specifically Chabahar, far from Iran's major population centers and IRGC concentrations. Marines and special forces would establish beachheads in two stages: initial entry and sustained control. The coastline is a "moonscape" — horrible for landings, creating wide open terrain that exposes forces to maximum risk. "This would be the worst losses we've seen since Vietnam" [8].

Pape warns that a symbolic victory — taking a piece of territory or an island with little initial resistance — does not mean success. Resistance typically emerges in weeks, not days. In other campaigns, resistance took 4–6 months to emerge. "This is probably in for a dime, in for a dollar" — any ground war will likely last at least six months [8].

## SPR Salt Cavern Structural Risk

Pape identifies a little-discussed physical constraint on the Strategic Petroleum Reserve: oil is stored in salt caverns and extracted by injecting water. As oil levels get low, the water eats away the cavern walls, causing collapse. The exact threshold is unknown. The DOE's 2016 strategic review flagged this risk. As the SPR approaches its operational floor in August, "we don't really know what's going to happen to those caverns" — entering new territory with potentially cascading failures [8].

## Three Updated Indicators

Pape updates his three-indicator framework for assessing whether the war is entering Stage 3:

1. **Iran's revenge campaign amplification**: Watch whether Tehran continues amplifying the Supreme Leader's call through state media, religious leaders, proxy organizations, and social media. If so, revenge is becoming an enduring political objective, and the risk of inspired attacks becomes a growing operational risk.
2. **Expansion of US bombing beyond coastal defenses**: If strikes expand to missile depots, drone production, logistics networks, transportation infrastructure, and command facilities deep inside Iran, Washington is systematically reducing Iran's ability to sustain prolonged resistance — preparation for ground operations. Taking down electricity and bridges would be about stopping the movement of forces.
3. **Preparations for littoral operations**: Additional amphibious shipping, Marine units, engineering forces, mine-clearing assets, and reports of increased reconnaissance by US special operations forces along Iran's southeastern coast. Most will remain classified, but changes in force posture, satellite imagery, and operational leaks often reveal when planners are making ground operations a realistic option [8].

## War Duration Assessment

Pape assesses the war will continue "at least through January" 2027. The idea that it would be over by July 1 was "wishful thinking." The war has opened too many problems, and the balance of power is still shifting — parties don't agree on the new balance right away. Americans may stop the war when gasoline reaches $6–$8 per gallon, but that has not yet happened. The comparison to Vietnam is instructive: public opposition grew over years, and the war ended only when Congress cut off the money [8].

## Nuclear Weapons Impracticality

Pape addresses suggestions (including from Lindsey Graham) of using nuclear weapons against Iran: radiation clouds cannot be controlled and travel for hundreds of miles. The radioactive soot would kill large numbers of American troops — not immediately but through radiation sickness and cancers over years. Using nuclear weapons in the Gulf would kill 20,000–30,000 American troops over time. "I just can't even imagine" [8].

## Markets and Packed Journalism

Pape observes that oil traders are terrified to buck Trump because if they get it wrong (going long on oil and prices fall), they are fired — "not politically fired, but that guy's gone." This produces groupthink: packed journalism, packed trading, packed everything. Career risk determines market behavior. "I'm tenured. My kids are through college. This is a different world for someone like me" [8].
