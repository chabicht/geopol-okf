# Background

Professor Robert Pape runs the "Escalation Trap" newsletter, focused on staying ahead of risks that matter. His analytical approach centers on leverage dynamics — how the distribution of coercive advantage shifts during a war and how those shifts determine whether a conflict moves toward termination or new escalation. Pape's work on the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) provides a leverage-centered counterpoint to O'Brien's leader-centered framework and Tchakarova's system-centered framework.

# Key Frameworks

## Leverage and War Termination

Pape's central framework: states gaining leverage rarely hurry to end wars. The logic is structural — if your position is improving, the cost of continued fighting is decreasing relative to the expected gains from a better settlement. This framework inverts the common assumption that wars end when both sides are exhausted; Pape argues wars end when the leverage gradient stabilizes or reverses.

## The Escalation Trap (Three-Stage)

Pape's foundational framework, published February 25, 2026 before the first bombs fell, models the war as proceeding through three stages based on over 20 years of modeling the bombing of Iran:

- **Stage 1**: Initial bombing is tactically successful — bombs hit targets, kill leaders, destroy launchers — but the target can lash back. This is exactly what happened.
- **Stage 2**: Horizontal escalation — Iran counter-escalated and took Hormuz, as Pape predicted before the first bombs fell. The US shifted to a punishment campaign within a week, then to an eight-week blockade, then to the MOU inducement strategy. All failed.
- **Stage 3**: Ground war — the stage the US is now approaching. Pape argues there is no third choice between surrender and escalation: either [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) capitulates and accepts Iran as the rising regional hegemon (and likely the fourth center of world power within a year or two), or he escalates. "Everybody wants there to be a third choice. That doesn't make a third choice." [7][8]

The Escalation Trap occurs when the attacker is tactically successful but becomes strategically trapped: each escalation produces a counter-escalation that raises the political costs of exit, creating a self-reinforcing spiral. The framework has been the most accurate predictor of the war's trajectory over its four and a half months [8].

To operationalize the leverage concept into trackable signals, Pape uses a three-indicator framework that he updates as the war evolves: (1) Iran's revenge campaign amplification through state media, religious leaders, and proxy organizations; (2) expansion of US bombing beyond coastal defenses to missile depots, drone production, logistics networks, transportation infrastructure, and command facilities deep inside Iran; (3) preparations for littoral operations — amphibious shipping, Marine units, engineering forces, mine-clearance assets, and increased reconnaissance along Iran's southeastern coast [8]. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) for the operational tracking.

## Hormuz as Leverage

Pape's framework reframes the [Strait of Hormuz]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) from a battlefield where shipping happens to pass through into a lever that [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) controls. The real leverage is not the ability to close the chokepoint but the ability to decide whether it stays open — making every shipping passage a concession Iran grants rather than a right that exists independently. This reframe generated a confirmed forecast: commercial shipping would be the next escalation pathway, a prediction validated by subsequent attacks that flowed directly from the leverage framework — if Iran's leverage is chokepoint control, the natural escalation vector is the traffic that depends on the chokepoint.

The framework also tracks phase transitions in Iran's negotiating posture. Pape argues Iran shifted from negotiating to stop losing to negotiating to win more — a phase transition rather than a marginal change, flipping the basis of its posture from damage limitation to advantage extraction. The implication is that Iran's growing leverage may weaken the [US]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) military position in the Gulf and set the stage for Iranian nuclear weapons: the leverage is not merely military but strategic, creating space for nuclear escalation.

## Controlled Instability

Pape introduces "controlled instability" as Iran's post-war strategic doctrine: persistent low-level disruptions — drone incidents, insurance volatility, shipping harassment — that sustain a geopolitical risk premium on oil without requiring total Hormuz closure. If oil stays above $90 even after Hormuz reopens, Iran wins through a continuous revenue stream from the risk premium. This transforms Iran's chokepoint leverage from a binary weapon (open or closed) into a continuous revenue stream [4].

## Iran's Political Evolution (Three Phases)

Pape tracks Iran's political objectives through three expanding phases — a framework for understanding how war aims mutate as leverage shifts, not a live tracker of day-to-day dynamics:

1. **Survival** (Feb–Mar 2026): After decapitation of senior leadership, Tehran's overriding goal was preserving the regime and preventing military collapse.
2. **Ambition** (Apr–Jun 2026): Once Iran demonstrated it could contest Hormuz, it began using that leverage to pursue a new regional security order centered on Iranian influence stretching from Lebanon through Iraq and the Gulf to Yemen and the Red Sea.
3. **Revenge** (Jul 2026–): The Supreme Leader's call to action adds a third objective that does not replace the first two. Revenge becomes an independent political objective that leaders must satisfy alongside survival and ambition, even when doing so increases escalation risk [8].

## Suicide Terrorism Strategic Logic

Pape, who compiled the first complete database of all suicide attacks after 9/11, applies 25 years of research to the current situation. His central finding: 95% of suicide attacks are carried out for a strategic purpose — to coerce democracies to withdraw military forces from territory the terrorists prize. This was the stunning finding that led to his unique prediction (no other terrorism expert made it) that invading Iraq would touch off the largest suicide terrorist campaign in modern times — a prediction that came true [8].

The data show that suicide terrorism exploded after the US occupied Iraq and Afghanistan, and declined when the forever wars ended. "We are doing the reverse" in Iran — escalating in a way that historically generates suicide terrorism. The greatest danger is not centrally directed operations but **inspired attacks** by individuals or small networks responding to a broader political appeal. The closest historical parallel to the Supreme Leader's call to action is Ayatollah Khomeini's 1989 fatwa calling for the execution of Salman Rushdie — which led to attacks and deaths for decades around the world. Pape expects "one possibly two attacks" — mostly inspired, not command-directed — to fuel public support for Stage 3 [8].

## Posture on Trump's Escalation Logic and Nuclear Taboos

Pape explains why Trump is escalating despite the midterms — a question puzzling many analysts — through two factors. **The Hoover Risk**: Trump signed the MOU to avoid an economic crisis, explicitly saying he did not want to be "Herbert Hoover," but the MOU failed to stop the oil inventory drawdown — the world gained only about 14 days of supply from a couple hundred million barrels released, and the inventory cliff is still coming in August. **MAGA Turnout**: According to the Reagan Institute's nationwide Summer Survey (May 26–June 3), 51 percent of self-identified MAGA Republicans favor replacing Iran's government, while only 25 percent prefer a negotiated settlement; if Trump fails to open Hormuz, a significant portion of MAGA will not show up at the polls. Trump is escalating to reopen Hormuz — not because he wants a ground war, but because it is certain that if he doesn't open Hormuz, he will fail politically. "He's trying to find a way to not have it be just about him" [8].

On nuclear weapons, Pape rejects suggestions (including from [Lindsey Graham]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/lindsey-graham.html)) of using them against Iran: radiation clouds cannot be controlled and travel for hundreds of miles, and the radioactive soot would kill 20,000–30,000 American troops over time through radiation sickness and cancers. "I just can't even imagine" [8]. On markets, Pape observes that oil traders are terrified to buck Trump because if they get it wrong (going long on oil and prices fall), they are fired — "not politically fired, but that guy's gone." This produces groupthink: "packed journalism, packed trading, packed everything," where career risk determines market behavior. "I'm tenured. My kids are through college. This is a different world for someone like me" [8].

## Posture Toward Stage 3 and War Duration

Pape assesses the US military is already pre-positioning and preparing the battlefield for Stage 3 ground operations: two carrier strike groups moved from the Indian Ocean into the Gulf of Oman — placing 5,000–6,000 personnel within Iranian missile and drone range; coastal suppression bombing targets surveillance radars, command-and-control networks, anti-ship missile batteries, drone launch sites, and naval facilities along Iran's southeastern coast; and a Marine Expeditionary Unit has repositioned closer to the Gulf of Oman [8]. He forecasts August 2026 as the decisive month of the war: the [SPR approaches its operational floor]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/spr-depletion-crisis.html) of ~243 million barrels by mid-to-late August as hurricane season threatens Gulf Coast production, while the [Supreme Leader's funeral]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/supreme-leader-funeral-2026.html) has stiffened Iranian resolve through massive nationalist mobilization [5]. By August, Iran is likely to possess three forms of leverage simultaneously — growing nationalist confidence, continuing Hormuz influence, and an opponent with diminishing economic room [5].

The war aim is itself expanding: using political momentum to weaken and ultimately end the permanent American military presence in the Gulf, with Iran offering lower transit fees to states that distance themselves from Washington and higher costs for those hosting US bases [5]. Two mutually reinforcing engines now drive the war: **strategic escalation** (the IRGC insists only Tehran will control Hormuz; CENTCOM continues preparing to guarantee freedom of navigation — objectives that cannot both be achieved) and **revenge escalation** (the Supreme Leader's call to action has transformed vengeance into an independent political objective, with the Intelligence Ministry releasing an official decree stating it is "a national demand"). The critical insight is that these engines interact — even if one slows, the other can continue driving escalation. "It takes two to taco" [7][8].

Pape assesses the war will continue "at least through January" 2027 — the idea that it would be over by July 1 was "wishful thinking," and the balance of power is still shifting. Americans may stop the war when gasoline reaches $6–$8 per gallon, but that has not yet happened [8]. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) for the operational timeline and [SPR Depletion Crisis]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/spr-depletion-crisis.html) for the salt-cavern structural risk.

## The Escalation Trap Mechanics: Vertical, Horizontal, and Civilian-Infrastructure Thresholds

Pape's Breaking Analysis series (July 14–18, 2026) tracks the discrete thresholds by which the Escalation Trap deepens — each one a categorical escalation rather than a marginal increase.

**The casualty threshold (July 15).** Iran's use of heavy anti-ship missiles with up to 1,000-pound warheads against tankers in the Strait of Hormuz marks the shift from controlled brinkmanship to high-risk, uncontrolled escalation. The weapons are capable of catastrophic, ship-sinking destruction — far eclipsing minor drone strikes. With US Navy ships repositioned close to Iran's coast within anti-ship cruise missile range, even 30–40 US casualties would instantly shatter diplomatic options, forcing an uncontrollable, public-fueled demand for payback that could lock both sides into a costly spiral and make Stage Three politically difficult to avoid. Neither Washington nor Tehran fully controls what happens once those casualties occur [11].

**Horizontal escalation to a second front (July 16).** The Houthi barrage targeting Saudi Arabia's Abha International Airport — in direct retaliation for airstrikes on Sanaa — opens a second maritime front at the [Bab el-Mandeb]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html) gateway. Until then the war had centered on the Strait of Hormuz; opening a second front transforms the crisis from a regional confrontation into a broader contest over the security of global energy flows. The move expands the political stakes for every major actor while shrinking their room to compromise: leaving two strategic chokepoints under threat would appear politically unacceptable. Iran gains additional leverage without directly committing its own forces [12].

**Shaping operations for ground assault (July 17).** US Central Command's multiple waves of airstrikes against key bridges, railway junctures, and power lines in Hormozgan province and around Bandar Abbas are classic "shaping" operations in preparation for a ground assault, consistent with the air strikes over all six days in the previous week. Once the United States begins shaping the battlefield for possible ground operations, stopping becomes politically harder than continuing — every additional strike raises the political price of restraint while making a larger military commitment appear increasingly necessary to justify what has already been done. Iran's trap deepens in parallel: standing down after these strikes would risk signaling weakness both domestically and across the region [13].

**Civilian-infrastructure targeting (July 18).** Iran's direct targeting of a power and water desalination plant in [Kuwait]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/kuwait.html) — part of the system producing roughly 90% of the country's drinking water — crosses another escalation threshold: civilian survival infrastructure is becoming a direct battlefield target. Drawing on his *Bombing to Win* research, Pape's central finding is that when civilians suddenly lose access to life's basic necessities, survival rapidly overwhelms every other concern — "anger comes after survival." But the Escalation Trap operates in the opposite direction from civilian morale: leaders confronted with collapsing civilian conditions often seek to shift public anger outward, while neighboring states face growing pressure to respond to humanitarian and security spillovers. The result could be a wider regional war whose consequences become increasingly difficult for any side to control. The strike proves the concept for a vulnerability pattern that extends across the Gulf: Qatar and Bahrain each rely on only three to five desalination plants, while Saudi Arabia and the UAE rely on 30 and 70 respectively [14]. See [Geoeconomics of Water]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/geoeconomics-of-water.html).
