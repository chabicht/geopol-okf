# Background

Derek Thompson is a journalist and writer whose work covers economic and social trends. His interview with economist Jesus Fernandez-Villaverde on the global fertility decline brought attention to what may be the most undercovered structural trend of the 21st century: 2023 was the first year global fertility fell below the replacement rate, with cascading implications for welfare states, economic growth, and geopolitical power.

# Key Frameworks

## Global Depopulation as Structural Force

Thompson's analysis frames depopulation not as a Western phenomenon but as a global one. Fertility decline is occurring in countries at all income levels, from South Korea's catastrophic low to historically high-fertility societies now approaching replacement. The drivers are complex — smartphones, social media, declining socialization, housing costs, educational intensity — but the implication is clear: the global population will begin contracting within decades, with consequences for every assumption underlying modern economic and political systems [1].

The welfare state is particularly exposed: pay-as-you-go systems assume a growing base of contributors relative to beneficiaries, an assumption that depopulation invalidates. Immigration can delay but not reverse the trend, as fertility decline is global, not regional [1].
