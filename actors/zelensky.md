# Background

Volodymyr Zelensky has led [Ukraine](/actors/ukraine.md) through the full-scale Russian invasion since February 2022. His wartime leadership has evolved from a defensive, survival-oriented posture to an increasingly offensive strategy that extends beyond Ukraine's borders into Russian territory and the politics of neighboring states.

# Current Situation

## Pressure Campaign Against Belarus

Zelensky has run an active coercive campaign against [Belarus](/actors/belarus.md) and its dictator [Aleksandr Lukashenka](/actors/lukashenka.md). He publicly listed in detail every plant in Belarus working for the Russian war effort, effectively warning Lukashenka that Ukraine knows the full supply chain and can hold it at risk. [1]

The campaign achieved a concrete result: Lukashenka halted radio guidance equipment that had been helping Russia direct strikes into Ukrainian territory. O'Brien treats this as a notable success of Ukrainian coercion over a regime that is nominally Russia's closest ally. [1]

## Long-Range Strike Campaign

Zelensky is leading Ukraine's deep-strike campaign against Russian war production. He personally released a tweet about the Titan-Barrikady strike in Volgograd — a plant producing mobile air-defense systems — signaling both the target's significance and Ukraine's intent to degrade Russia's capacity to produce the systems it needs to defend against the very drones and missiles Ukraine employs. [2]

The campaign targets refineries, munitions plants, and defense-industrial facilities across [Russia](/actors/russia.md), stretching Russian air defense thin and compounding the fuel and air-defense shortages documented by O'Brien. [2]

# Analysis

### Coercion Beyond the Battlefield

Zelensky's Belarus pressure campaign represents a sophisticated form of gray-zone coercion: rather than attacking Belarus directly, Ukraine used the credible threat of strikes on Belarusian industrial infrastructure to change Lukashenka's behavior. The success — turning off radio guidance — is a rare case of a smaller power coercing a larger power's ally into de-escalation without firing a shot on that front.

### Industrial Attrition as Strategy

The long-range strike campaign reflects a strategic logic of industrial attrition: Russia's war effort depends on a relatively narrow set of production facilities. By targeting them, Ukraine aims to create cascading shortages — fewer air-defense systems mean more successful Ukrainian strikes, which mean more damage, in a compounding cycle.
