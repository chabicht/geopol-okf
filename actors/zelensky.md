# Background

Volodymyr Zelensky has led [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) through the full-scale Russian invasion since February 2022. His wartime leadership has evolved from a defensive, survival-oriented posture to an increasingly offensive strategy that extends beyond Ukraine's borders into Russian territory and the politics of neighboring states.

# Current Situation

## Pressure Campaign Against Belarus

Zelensky has run an active coercive campaign against [Belarus]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/belarus.html) and its dictator [Aleksandr Lukashenka]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/lukashenka.html). He publicly listed in detail every plant in Belarus working for the Russian war effort, effectively warning Lukashenka that Ukraine knows the full supply chain and can hold it at risk. [1]

The campaign achieved a concrete result: Lukashenka halted radio guidance equipment that had been helping Russia direct strikes into Ukrainian territory. O'Brien treats this as a notable success of Ukrainian coercion over a regime that is nominally Russia's closest ally. [1]

## Long-Range Strike Campaign

Zelensky is leading Ukraine's deep-strike campaign against Russian war production. He personally released a tweet about the Titan-Barrikady strike in Volgograd — a plant producing mobile air-defense systems — signaling both the target's significance and Ukraine's intent to degrade Russia's capacity to produce the systems it needs to defend against the very drones and missiles Ukraine employs. [2]

The campaign targets refineries, munitions plants, and defense-industrial facilities across [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), stretching Russian air defense thin and compounding the fuel and air-defense shortages documented by O'Brien. [2]

# Analysis

### Coercion Beyond the Battlefield

Zelensky's Belarus pressure campaign represents a sophisticated form of gray-zone coercion: rather than attacking Belarus directly, Ukraine used the credible threat of strikes on Belarusian industrial infrastructure to change Lukashenka's behavior. The success — turning off radio guidance — is a rare case of a smaller power coercing a larger power's ally into de-escalation without firing a shot on that front.

### Industrial Attrition as Strategy

The long-range strike campaign reflects a strategic logic of industrial attrition: Russia's war effort depends on a relatively narrow set of production facilities. By targeting them, Ukraine aims to create cascading shortages — fewer air-defense systems mean more successful Ukrainian strikes, which mean more damage, in a compounding cycle.

### The Cabinet Reshuffle Pattern and the Trust-Turnover Dynamic

The July 16, 2026 dismissal of Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov — documented by [War on the Rocks]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/war-on-the-rocks.html)' Ukraine Compass — illustrates a structural feature of Zelensky's wartime governance that has now become a strategic liability. Fedorov was Ukraine's youngest-ever Defense Minister and is credited with making [drone warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) Ukraine's most lethal capability through rapid innovation. He refused the advisory role Zelensky offered him, citing a conflict with Commander-in-Chief Syrskyi and a system in which ministers are appointed and dismissed by loyalty rather than parliamentary process. He is the fifth Defense Minister since the full-scale invasion [3].

The dismissal turned a cabinet reshuffle into a full political crisis. MP Mykyta Poturaev resigned in protest; the EU's Commissioner Kubilius voiced concern; protests broke out in Kyiv. Vitaliy Portnikov argued that Fedorov's complaints reveal political inexperience — he failed to understand that structural change is impossible without deeper reform of an arbitrary system. Boryslav Bereza noted a pattern under Zelensky: once someone falls out of favor with the inner circle, criminal cases against their associates mysteriously surface, and he predicted investigations into Fedorov's associates would emerge. The timing — right after the [NATO Ankara summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html) pledged €70 billion in 2026 aid — suggests a "corporate war" over controlling those funds [3]. See [Fedorov Dismissal (July 2026)]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/fedorov-dismissal-2026.html).

### Gulf Defense Agreements and Strategic Repositioning

Ryvkin (GPF) reports that Zelensky played a central role in securing 10-year defense agreements with [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html), [Qatar]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/qatar.html), and the [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html) — covering counter-drone technology, air defense, cybersecurity, joint production, and training. The agreements include Gulf investment in Ukraine's defense industry, creating a reciprocal economic relationship that extends beyond military aid. Zelensky's strategic logic is clear: by positioning Ukraine as a provider of battlefield-tested defense technology rather than solely an aid recipient, he repositions the country from perpetual victim to strategic partner contributing to global security architecture. This repositioning is increasingly urgent as war fatigue in Europe makes aid packages harder to sell [4].

### The Drapatyi Appointment

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) reports that Zelensky appointed Mykhailo Drapatyi as Commander in Chief of Ukrainian forces, replacing Oleksandr Syrskyi. Drapatyi was promoted from Joint Forces Commander. The appointment was widely hailed — analyst Bielieskov called it the "best choice of Zelensky." The command change follows the [Fedorov dismissal]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/fedorov-dismissal-2026.html) governance crisis and intersects with [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html)'s ongoing force transformation [5].

### The Fedorov Dismissal: Reform vs Command

[RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) (Jim McLean) adds substantive depth to the Fedorov dismissal, framing it as an unresolved debate about the future of Ukraine's way of war and civilian control of the military. Fedorov's reform agenda — consolidating poorly performing brigades, collocating corps constituent brigades, and empowering tech-leveraging commanders — was systematically obstructed by General Syrskyi, who established new brigades instead of consolidating, split best units across opposite ends of the front, and prioritised personnel for 425 Assault Regiment's human-centric attacks while stripping equipment from innovative formations. Zelensky removed Fedorov because the Ministry–General Staff rift was unsustainable, but he did not side with Syrskyi on substance — directing that the reforms continue under the new minister. The conscription reform question also remains: Fedorov identified that the problem was not recruitment volume but training and corruption, requiring changes the General Staff refused to implement. The decision calculus reveals Zelensky prioritising institutional stability over reform momentum — removing the reformer while preserving the reform mandate, a compromise that leaves the civilian-control question unresolved [6].
