# Background

Volodymyr Zelensky has led [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) through the full-scale Russian invasion since February 2022. His wartime leadership has evolved from a defensive, survival-oriented posture to an increasingly offensive strategy that extends beyond Ukraine's borders into Russian territory and the politics of neighboring states.

# Current Situation

## Pressure Campaign Against Belarus

Zelensky has run an active coercive campaign against [Belarus]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/belarus.html) and its dictator [Aleksandr Lukashenka]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/lukashenka.html). He publicly listed in detail every plant in Belarus working for the Russian war effort, effectively warning Lukashenka that Ukraine knows the full supply chain and can hold it at risk. [1]

The campaign achieved a concrete result: Lukashenka halted radio guidance equipment that had been helping Russia direct strikes into Ukrainian territory. O'Brien treats this as a notable success of Ukrainian coercion over a regime that is nominally Russia's closest ally. [1]

## Long-Range Strike Campaign

Zelensky is leading Ukraine's deep-strike campaign against Russian war production. He personally released a tweet about the Titan-Barrikady strike in Volgograd — a plant producing mobile air-defense systems — signaling both the target's significance and Ukraine's intent to degrade Russia's capacity to produce the systems it needs to defend against the very drones and missiles Ukraine employs. [2]

The campaign targets refineries, munitions plants, and defense-industrial facilities across [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), stretching Russian air defense thin and compounding the fuel and air-defense shortages documented by O'Brien. [2]

# Analysis

### Coercion Beyond the Battlefield

Zelensky's Belarus pressure campaign represents a sophisticated form of gray-zone coercion: rather than attacking Belarus directly, Ukraine used the credible threat of strikes on Belarusian industrial infrastructure to change Lukashenka's behavior. The success — turning off radio guidance — is a rare case of a smaller power coercing a larger power's ally into de-escalation without firing a shot on that front.

### Industrial Attrition as Strategy

The long-range strike campaign reflects a strategic logic of industrial attrition: Russia's war effort depends on a relatively narrow set of production facilities. By targeting them, Ukraine aims to create cascading shortages — fewer air-defense systems mean more successful Ukrainian strikes, which mean more damage, in a compounding cycle.

### The Cabinet Reshuffle Pattern and the Trust-Turnover Dynamic

The July 16, 2026 dismissal of Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov — documented by [War on the Rocks]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/war-on-the-rocks.html)' Ukraine Compass — illustrates a structural feature of Zelensky's wartime governance that has now become a strategic liability. Fedorov was Ukraine's youngest-ever Defense Minister and is credited with making [drone warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) Ukraine's most lethal capability through rapid innovation. He refused the advisory role Zelensky offered him, citing a conflict with Commander-in-Chief Syrskyi and a system in which ministers are appointed and dismissed by loyalty rather than parliamentary process. He is the fifth Defense Minister since the full-scale invasion [3].

The dismissal turned a cabinet reshuffle into a full political crisis. MP Mykyta Poturaev resigned in protest; the EU's Commissioner Kubilius voiced concern; protests broke out in Kyiv. Vitaliy Portnikov argued that Fedorov's complaints reveal political inexperience — he failed to understand that structural change is impossible without deeper reform of an arbitrary system. Boryslav Bereza noted a pattern under Zelensky: once someone falls out of favor with the inner circle, criminal cases against their associates mysteriously surface, and he predicted investigations into Fedorov's associates would emerge. The timing — right after the [NATO Ankara summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html) pledged €70 billion in 2026 aid — suggests a "corporate war" over controlling those funds [3].
