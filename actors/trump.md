# Background

Donald Trump is the 45th and 47th President of the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html), returned to office in 2025. His second term has been defined by a discretionary strike on Iran, a continued personal soft spot for [Vladimir Putin]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/putin.html), and an aggressive unilateral trade and diplomatic posture that has disrupted long-standing US alliances.

# Current Situation

## Leader Psychology and Decision-Making

O'Brien's treatment of Trump is the clearest empirical case for the proposition that major wars are products of individual leader psychology and preference, not structural state logic. Trump's decision to attack [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) in 2026 was, per O'Brien, not driven by national interest or political pressure: there was no constituency demanding strikes, no rally-round-the-flag effect, and no obvious strategic gain that justified the costs. The episode is proof that wars are choices of *individual leaders*, not the inevitable output of states acting in national interest [1]. The US public response broke the standard pattern — Trump's base supported whatever he wanted to do, the opposition opposed the war from the start, and there was no measurable patriotic surge behind the commander-in-chief [1]. Every prior administration, faced with the prospect of attacking Iran alongside [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html), concluded "that is not what we are going to do."

[Adam Tooze]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/adam-tooze.html) frames Trump as a case study in "hyper-agency" — the question of why men with destructive fantasies are now in a position to act on them. The chain of contingent events leading to Trump acting on his impulses can be traced; the situation is overdetermined but not necessary. The dismantling of institutional restraints (the courts via [Slaughter]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/slaughter-scotus-ruling.html), the civil service via oligarchic capture, the alliance system via transactional diplomacy) has left the personal psychology of the leader as the remaining constraint — the most volatile possible [24].

O'Brien asserts that Trump is still helping Putin despite occasional critical words, and that observers should not listen to the words — they are meant to deceive [3]. Trump has also been rewriting history about the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html), constructing narratives that serve his political needs rather than reflecting the record [2]. This pattern — rhetoric diverging from substance while personal psychology drives the actual decisions — recurs across every domain of Trump's second term. O'Brien identifies Trump's claims about "tremendous love" and caring as a reliable tell that he is lying: Trump does not believe in such things, and when he invokes them, it signals the opposite [29].

## Transactional Alliance Posture

Trump's second term has accelerated a pattern visible in his first: alliances are treated as bilateral deals, not as institutional commitments. By launching the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) without informing European allies, Japan, or South Korea, Trump "threw them under the bus." The failure to consult partners who host US forces and depend on American extended deterrence corroded trust in US alliance guarantees [1]. The systemic effect is a slow unwinding of the post-1945 alliance scaffolding.

The same transactional logic governs the [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html). Despite occasionally saying non-critical things about Ukraine, Trump has "done things to really help Putin" according to O'Brien [3]. He continues to "desperately want Putin to do well," rewriting history to preserve the relationship [2]. His posture has consistently tilted toward Moscow in substance even when rhetoric diverged.

In South Asia, Trump imposed tariffs on [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html) while simultaneously claiming credit for "stopping" an India-Pakistan clash, then invited Pakistan's military chief to the White House — a signal that his regional engagement is transactional and unpredictable. India, per War on the Rocks analysis, will nevertheless stick with America because the strategic alternatives are worse [4]. The May 2026 [Trump-Xi summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/trump-xi-summit-2026.html) introduced deep uncertainty into US-[Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html) relations: Xi framed Taiwan as the "most important issue in China-US relations," and the summit produced little clarity on continued US support for Taiwan, underlining Taipei's predicament of relying on questionable American promises [18].

Trump has declared his intention to "take" Greenland, imposing 10% punitive tariffs on eight NW European states (Denmark, Norway, Sweden, Finland, the UK, [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html), [France]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/france.html), and the Netherlands) to force European acquiescence. The move has fractured the European Right, alienated NATO allies, and signaled the end of "the West" as a cohesive concept. Europe's inability to respond — divided between sanctioned and unsanctioned member states, lacking military and economic cohesion — demonstrates the primacy of geography and hard power over institutional frameworks. See [Trump's Greenland Crisis]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/trump-greenland-crisis.html) [8][9][10][11].

Trump's approach to U.S. troops in Europe is not genuine retrenchment but "illiberal hegemony" — using troop threats to extort defense spending pledges that are strategically worthless to the U.S. The threats are not accompanied by a coherent plan for European security autonomy; they serve as leverage for extracting spending commitments without genuine strategic recalibration. A deliberate, phased withdrawal of most U.S. conventional forces from Europe over the next decade — coupled with real European defense capacity building — would be more effective than the current extortion approach [14]. At the [NATO Ankara Summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html), O'Brien offered a sharply dissenting assessment, calling it the worst in NATO history — a posture of threat-making without commitment that crystallized the transactional logic [29].

# Analysis

Trump's second term is best understood as the convergence of three logics — leader hyper-agency, transactional alliance management, and corruption as governing logic — each reinforcing the others as institutional restraints are dismantled.

The leader-as-decision-maker thesis is the clearest empirical thread. The Iran strike had no bureaucratic momentum behind it, no public demand, and no alliance consensus — it was Trump's call [1]. This contrasts with realist models that treat states as unitary rational actors. Tooze extends the argument: the dismantling of institutional restraints (courts via Slaughter, civil service via oligarchic capture, alliance system via transactional diplomacy) has left the personal psychology of the leader as the remaining constraint — the most volatile possible [24]. Trump's deception on Russia — words meant to mislead while actions help Putin [2][3] — and his reliable "tremendous love" tell [29] show a leader whose rhetoric is decoupled from his decisions, with psychology doing the work institutions once did.

The transactional alliance posture is the external face of the same logic. Alliances are treated as bilateral deals, not institutional commitments — skipping allies on Iran, tariffing India while courting Pakistan's military, indulging Putin, threatening Denmark over Greenland, extorting European defense spending without offering genuine retrenchment [1][4][8][14]. The systemic effect is a slow unwinding of the post-1945 alliance scaffolding, visible from the Greenland crisis through the NATO Ankara summit.

Corruption is the domestic engine. Krugman argues corruption is not a side-effect but the central dynamic, judicially enabled by Slaughter and operationally documented by Applebaum's Kleptocracy Tracker across June and July 2026 [6][13][20]. The oligarchy spiral — policy tilt toward the hyper-wealthy reinforcing their political power — is the structural backdrop [23]. The authoritarian-ideological project (blood and soil nationalism, institutional defacement, ICE as spectacle, the intelligence master list, the anticommunism and election-interference pivots) supplies the legitimating narrative that keeps the base in line while the monetization proceeds [12][13][16][19][22].

On Iran, the three logics converge catastrophically. The war was a personal choice [1]; the inability to admit failure prevents a coherent exit [22]; the escalation toward reopening Hormuz is driven by the MAGA turnout risk and the Hoover economic risk rather than any viable military plan [25][26]. The posture is erratic — wildly illegal toll proposals alternating with de facto pullout — because it is generated by psychology under political pressure, not by strategy.

## The $1.5 Trillion Defense Budget: Budget-Driven, Not Strategy-Driven

The Trump administration's FY 2027 defense request — $1.15 trillion plus $350 billion in reconciliation and a $67 billion supplemental, totaling roughly $1.5 trillion — was driven by an arbitrary 5% of GDP target declared on Truth Social rather than by any coherent strategy. Todd Harrison (AEI) told a Brookings panel that the Pentagon was told at the last minute to produce a request reaching the $1.5 trillion number, meaning no hard tradeoffs were required: fourth-generation F-15s, fifth-generation F-35s, and sixth-generation fighters were all funded simultaneously. The budget makes easy choices, not strategic ones [34].

Mara Karlin (SAIS) identified the central paradox. The Trump national strategy prioritizes the Western Hemisphere and alleged narco-terrorists as the primary threat, mentions [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) only in a circumscribed way limited to the first island chain, downgrades involvement in European affairs, and signals no appetite for broader Middle East conflict. Yet the budget is at World War levels. If the strategy were taken at face value — Western Hemisphere focus and a limited China posture — the administration could have proposed a quite small defense budget. The $1.5 trillion number is not merited by the strategy as written, producing what Karlin called "a bit of perplexity" [34]. The disconnect between a narco-terrorist-focused strategy and World War-level spending encapsulates the budget-driven logic: the number came first, the strategy was retrofitted.

David Wessel (Brookings) added the fiscal dimension: the budget is proposed at a time of unsustainable fiscal trajectory, with the administration and Congress avoiding the tradeoffs that budgeting requires — neither cutting elsewhere nor raising taxes. Wessel also warned that Secretary Hegseth's politicization of military personnel decisions is eroding public trust in the military as a nonpartisan institution, compounding the strategic incoherence [34].

### The Canada Tariffs: Temper Tantrum as Trade Policy

[Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html) analyzes Trump's 50% tariffs on Canadian goods — imposed via the obscure, never-before-used Section 338 of the Smoot-Hawley Trade Act — as a USMCA-violating temper tantrum with no strategic logic. The tariffs explicitly override USMCA origin rules, confirming that a deal with Trump lasts only until he feels like breaking it — a conclusion already reached by the European Commission and Iran's IRGC. The pretext oscillated between Canadian provincial alcohol bans (themselves retaliation for Trump's earlier tariffs and his 51st-state demands) and Canadian wildfire smoke, which Krugman calls "unutterably idiotic" given that Canada's boreal forest spans roughly 2 million square miles of largely inaccessible, climate-driven terrain. The tariffs will hurt American consumers and extract no concessions from Canadians who are only growing more outraged. Canada was America's closest ally; Canadians now view the US less favorably than [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html). The immediate economic impact is limited — the auto industry is exempt — but the action puts all cross-border commerce on notice. Krugman situates the move within the historical arc from Smoot-Hawley to the 1934 Reciprocal Trade Agreements Act, arguing that Trump has surpassed Smoot-Hawley as the worst trade policy in US history, with Section 338's invocation adding a new dimension. See [USMCA Renegotiation 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/usmca-renegotiation-2026.html). [37]

## Domestic Austerity Amid War Spending

[Heather Cox Richardson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/heather-cox-richardson.html) documents the simultaneous gutting of domestic programs and escalation of war spending under the [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) administration. Under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, 440,000 Arizonans lost SNAP benefits — the deepest food-assistance cuts in history, achieved through expanded work rules and shifted costs to states, with Arizona laying off a third of caseworkers. Agriculture Secretary Rollins defended the cuts by claiming fraud, but the error rates in question reflect classification mistakes, not fraud. Separately, [RFK Jr.]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) announced the withholding of over $1 billion in Medicaid funding from California and Minnesota on alleged fraud grounds — California had already had $1.3 billion withheld since May, now facing $867 million more; Minnesota had $243 million withheld since February, now facing $200 million more. The National Park Service is being systematically dismantled under Interior Secretary Burgum — staff slashed, funds starved, and political narrative imposed on historical interpretation, with recovery expected to take years or decades. TSA began reverting to pre-9/11 private security screening at Des Moines and Tampa airports, abandoning the post-9/11 federal model created precisely because private screening had proven vulnerable. Meanwhile, the Pentagon seeks a $67 billion supplemental for the [Iran war]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html). The pattern reveals an ideological preference for military spending over domestic welfare, enforced through executive action [36].

## War as Faux-Testimonial: The "Honor" Framing

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) identifies a recurring rhetorical pattern in which [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) frames military violence as a tribute to fallen soldiers. Trump declared that the US strikes on [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) were carried out "in honor of" three servicemen killed in action — a formulation that substitutes sentimentality for strategy, wrapping the use of force in a movie-script rhetoric of commemoration. The pattern is characteristic: rather than articulating a coherent military objective, Trump invokes the sacrifice of the dead as a justifying narrative, converting operational decisions into performative acts of remembrance. O'Brien reads this as continuous with the broader romanticization of military sacrifice that defines the administration's posture — a culture that celebrates bravery and honor while neglecting the logistics, technology, and education that actually determine battlefield outcomes [38]. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html).

## No Achievable Objective: The Self-Weakening War

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documents that as of July 22, 2026, Trump has ordered eleven consecutive nights of strikes against [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) with no achievable military objective. Trump is hoping Iran will offer him peace terms he can sell — a nuclear concession plus the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz without tolls — but no military operation short of full-scale invasion and regime change can produce this outcome. General McCaffrey estimates a full invasion would require 600,000 troops and one year; it is not happening. Taking Kharg Island achieves nothing political. The result is a campaign that operates as daily self-harm: every day of strikes depletes US munitions, demonstrates the hollowness of US security guarantees to Gulf allies, and degrades readiness for the primary great-power competitor — all without moving toward any achievable political goal [39]. See [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) and [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html).

## Section 338 Tariffs on Canada: Coercion as Trade Policy

Trump's July 20, 2026 invocation of Section 338 of the Tariff Act of 1930 — a provision dormant since the 1940s — imposed 50% tariffs on approximately $20 billion in Canadian imports across three separate proclamations targeting alcoholic beverages, motor vehicles, and dairy. Gundermann, Grant-Chapman, and Marroquín Bitar note this is the first presidential use of Section 338; no court has interpreted the statute, and its legal foundations are uncertain, with questions about the ITC's monitoring role, possible implicit repeal by later legislation, and likely WTO violations. The tariffs apply regardless of USMCA compliance status and are not layered on goods already subject to Section 232 duties. The structural design resembles Section 301 but with a less-clear investigative requirement and a shorter timeline. The dairy proclamation is notably flawed — US dairy exports to Canada remain below quota thresholds — and the auto proclamation targets non-auto goods except motorcycles. The $20.2 billion in targeted goods represents 4.9% of total US imports from Canada (2024). Beyond punishment for Canada's retaliatory measures, the tariffs serve multiple motivations: demonstrating trade-tool viability after the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs on February 20, 2026, replacing Section 122 tariffs set to expire July 24, and leveraging Canada back to the USMCA negotiating table — US-Mexico negotiations advanced speedily while Canada remained reluctant. Sustained threats had the opposite effect on Canada compared to Mexico's conciliatory posture, which kept negotiations moving. The action raises broader uncertainty about US willingness to abide by trade-agreement terms [41].

Luck documents the costs of this coercion. For every dollar in tariff revenue (~$9.9 billion total), US border workers absorbed 5–10 cents in lost wages. The Canadian visitor decline cost 10,000–30,000 leisure and hospitality jobs and $0.5–1 billion per year in lost wages in border communities. The US Travel Association estimated the Canadian decline cost the US economy $5.7 billion in travel spending in 2025. An independent study by Kurmann et al. found 14,000–42,000 jobs lost at small retail and leisure establishments, concentrated in fewer than 3% of US ZIP codes, with lower-income and Democratic-leaning areas hardest hit. Brown-Forman saw a 62% drop in Canadian sales; Kentucky and Tennessee spirits exports to Canada fell approximately 50%. The consumer boycott persisted after tariff adjustments — land crossings remained roughly 20% below 2024 levels in March–April 2026. Luck's central insight is that consumer boycotts are not state action: state-to-state economic statecraft cannot reliably dictate citizen behavior. "The coercer pays too" — through game theory and through independent citizen choices. Credibility and consumer trust do not recover on the schedule of trade negotiations. See [USMCA Renegotiation 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/usmca-renegotiation-2026.html), [Insurance and Trade Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/insurance-and-trade-warfare.html), and [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) [35][41].

## Pickaxe Mountain as Potential Target

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) reports (July 21, 2026) that [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) has repeatedly identified the Pickaxe Mountain facility near Natanz as a potential military target — yet it remains untouched. [Israeli]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) intelligence assesses that [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) has moved thousands of uranium-enrichment centrifuges into tunnels buried 300–450 feet beneath solid rock, beyond the reach of existing conventional bunker-busting munitions. The gap between Trump's rhetorical identification of the target and the absence of action against it reveals the operational constraint: the facility is too deep for the weapons the US possesses [40].

The political clock linkage intensifies the pressure for escalation. Pape argues that if the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) cannot reopen [Hormuz]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) before the November midterms, pressure for broader escalation — including attacks on deeply buried nuclear facilities like Pickaxe Mountain — will grow. Trump's escalating rhetoric about the facility is best understood as stage-setting: creating the political conditions under which a more dangerous escalation becomes domestically justifiable. The fear-driven escalation logic means that the mere belief that Iran's nuclear program has become invulnerable — regardless of what inspectors might discover — generates its own momentum toward military action. The 2003 Iraq WMD precedent illustrates how intelligence uncertainty, rather than resolution, drives escalation [40]. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html).
