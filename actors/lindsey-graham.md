# Background

Lindsey Graham was a Republican senator from South Carolina, a JAG Corps lawyer, and a fervent American patriot who believed that America should stand at the center of a broad democratic alliance. Like his close friend John McCain, Graham championed pragmatic governance, bipartisanship, and American internationalism. He attended European-American gatherings regularly and described himself as a politician who eschewed populist slogans: "I know Washington is broken, but what's broken about it is everybody yelling and nobody trying to fix it" [1].

# Current Situation

## The Trump Transformation

When [Donald Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) first appeared in U.S. politics, Graham recognized him immediately for what he was: the spokesman for an alien ideology radically different from the idealistic patriotism Graham had practiced since childhood. In 2015, Graham described Trump as a "race-baiting, xenophobic, religious bigot" who should "go to hell" and as a "nutjob" [1].

When Trump won, Graham understood he would have to make important choices. After a period of silence, he — like many other Republicans, and like many people who have lived under political occupation or experienced radical regime change — made the decision to abandon his previous ideals, bury the patriotism that was once so important to him, and become a loud, opportunistic collaborator [1]. [Anne Applebaum]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/anne-applebaum.html) frames Graham's trajectory through the lens of collaboration, comparing him to historical figures like Markus Wolf, who stuck with the East German regime and became its spy boss, while others like Wolfgang Leonhard escaped. Graham's transformation was 180 degrees: from a fervent patriot and reserve officer to one of the most important defenders of a president who believes soldiers are "suckers and losers" and shows no respect for law of any kind [1]. As his politics changed, his language grew coarser and his behavior worsened. He urged [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) to "level the place" in Gaza and insulted the Danish prime minister in Munich, prompting a colleague, Senator Elissa Slotkin, to issue an apology [1].

# Analysis

### The Psychology of Collaboration

Applebaum observes that it is very difficult to look into anyone's heart and understand their motivations from the outside. But Graham appeared to have a deep need to be relevant, to be in the game, to be a player, at any cost. That need — rather than any particular achievement — is what he will be remembered for [1]. Graham's trajectory illustrates the broader pattern of [ideology and democracy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ideology-and-democracy.html) erosion under Trump: institutional figures who once defended founding ideals choosing collaboration over resistance, not through dramatic conversion but through incremental accommodation that eventually became indistinguishable from allegiance. The comparison to Romney, who voted to impeach Trump despite having seemed less deeply attached to patriotic ideals, underscores that the clichés about who will resist and who will comply are often wrong [1].

### Ukraine Policy Record

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) compiled Graham's own quotes — without editorializing — to demonstrate that Graham was never a genuine friend of [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) [2]. The record reveals a consistent pattern of subordinating Ukraine policy to Trump's agenda across three dimensions: aid reversal, institutional complicity, and public deception.

On aid, Graham previously supported military assistance for Ukraine and criticized President Biden for not giving more. When [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) demanded aid be restructured as loans — putting Ukraine into debt — Graham changed with him, calling it a "blow to Putin" (February 2024). When Trump ended all US military aid to Ukraine in 2025, O'Brien could find no criticism by Graham of this decision [2].

On institutional complicity, the Graham-Blumenthal Russia sanctions bill — co-authored with approximately 80 Senate supporters — became the emblematic case. Trump blocked the vote. Graham repeatedly claimed Trump would allow it — on June 29, July 8, July 9, July 13, mid-November 2025, and January 7, 2026 — saying Trump had "greenlit" or "blessed" the bill. No vote ever happened. Graham never criticized Trump for preventing it. The pattern extended to the Oval Office: on February 28, 2025, when Trump and Vance ambushed [Zelensky]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/zelensky.html), Graham said he had "never been more proud of the president," praised Vance for "standing up for our country," blamed Zelensky for being "disrespectful," and suggested Zelensky should "resign and send somebody over that we can do business with" [2].

On public deception, Graham repeatedly told the public throughout 2024–2025 that Trump was about to approve arms aid or pressure Putin — claims with no validity. In July 2025, Graham predicted "weapons flowing at a record level" and "a turning point." Shortly after, Trump invited Putin to the Anchorage Summit and coordinated policy with him. The deception extended beyond Ukraine: in February 2026, Graham called Danish Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen a "little lady" and said "who gives a shit who owns Greenland?" while pressuring [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html) to hand the territory to Trump [2]. O'Brien's conclusion: "If you believe these are the actions of a 'friend' of Ukraine, so be it" [2].
