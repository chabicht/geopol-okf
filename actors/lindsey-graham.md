# Background

Lindsey Graham was a Republican senator from South Carolina, a JAG Corps lawyer, and a fervent American patriot who believed that America should stand at the center of a broad democratic alliance. Like his close friend John McCain, Graham championed pragmatic governance, bipartisanship, and American internationalism. He attended European-American gatherings regularly and described himself as a politician who eschewed populist slogans: "I know Washington is broken, but what's broken about it is everybody yelling and nobody trying to fix it" [1].

# The Trump Transformation

When [Donald Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) first appeared in U.S. politics, Graham recognized him immediately for what he was: the spokesman for an alien ideology radically different from the idealistic patriotism Graham had practiced since childhood. In 2015, Graham described Trump as a "race-baiting, xenophobic, religious bigot" who should "go to hell" and as a "nutjob" [1].

When Trump won, Graham understood he would have to make important choices. After a period of silence, he — like many other Republicans, and like many people who have lived under political occupation or experienced radical regime change — made the decision to abandon his previous ideals, bury the patriotism that was once so important to him, and become a loud, opportunistic collaborator [1].

[Anne Applebaum]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/anne-applebaum.html) frames Graham's trajectory through the lens of collaboration, comparing him to historical figures like Markus Wolf, who stuck with the East German regime and became its spy boss, while others like Wolfgang Leonhard escaped. Graham's transformation was 180 degrees: from a fervent patriot and reserve officer to one of the most important defenders of a president who believes soldiers are "suckers and losers" and shows no respect for law of any kind [1].

As his politics changed, his language grew coarser and his behavior worsened. He urged [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) to "level the place" in Gaza and insulted the Danish prime minister in Munich, prompting a colleague, Senator Elissa Slotkin, to issue an apology [1].

# Analysis

## The Psychology of Collaboration

Applebaum observes that it is very difficult to look into anyone's heart and understand their motivations from the outside. But Graham appeared to have a deep need to be relevant, to be in the game, to be a player, at any cost. That need — rather than any particular achievement — is what he will be remembered for [1].

Graham's trajectory illustrates the broader pattern of [ideology and democracy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ideology-and-democracy.html) erosion under Trump: institutional figures who once defended founding ideals choosing collaboration over resistance, not through dramatic conversion but through incremental accommodation that eventually became indistinguishable from allegiance. The comparison to Romney, who voted to impeach Trump despite having seemed less deeply attached to patriotic ideals, underscores that the clichés about who will resist and who will comply are often wrong [1].
