# Background

Mexico is Latin America's second-largest economy and a major oil producer through its state-owned company Petroleos Mexicanos (Pemex). Deeply integrated into US manufacturing supply chains, Mexico's strategic posture is defined by the tension between economic dependence on the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) and the pursuit of strategic autonomy from its northern neighbor. Mexico's pursuit of strategic autonomy is aimed at preventing domination by northern powers — namely the United States at this moment in history [1].

# Current Situation

## Pemex and Energy Strategy

Pemex has experienced persistent production declines and faces structural and operational constraints that have limited growth. The company's strategic plan for 2025-2035 sets a production target of 1.8 million barrels of oil per day from the current 1.6-1.7 million bpd. Pemex's deep-water extraction capacity is limited — its only deep-water project is operated in partnership with Woodside Energy Group, and ExxonMobil's evaluation of a potential acquisition of Woodside makes diversifying Pemex's technological partnerships urgent [1].

## Pemex-Petrobras MoU

Mexico and [Brazil]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/brazil.html) signed a memorandum of understanding establishing strategic and technical cooperation in the hydrocarbons sector. The MoU covers technology sharing in exploration and production, industrial processes, and regulatory frameworks, as well as refining, petrochemicals, fertilizers, gas processing, energy efficiency, carbon capture, and environmental protection. Petrobras' deep- and ultra-deep-water technology is expected to help Pemex expand opportunities in the Gulf of Mexico and revitalize existing fields. The MoU also includes projects exploring biofuels from maguey and fertilizer production — Brazil is the world's fourth-largest fertilizer importer [1].

See [Pemex-Petrobras MoU]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/pemex-petrobras-mou.html).

## USMCA Renegotiation

The July 1 USMCA negotiations ended before they truly began, with Washington making clear that it does not want to renew the agreement on current terms and prefers bilateral deals with Canada and Mexico. This reflects US concerns over trade deficits, rules of origin, and possible tariff circumvention, as well as the Trump administration's push for better terms in key sectors such as the automotive industry. Mexico and the US are set to negotiate on July 20, with Mexico signaling openness to Washington's demands. With a $2 trillion intra-regional market at stake and all three economies deeply interdependent, some agreement is likely, but its terms could significantly reshape North American trade [1].

See [USMCA Renegotiation 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/usmca-renegotiation-2026.html).

## Iran War Economic Repercussions

Mexico remains vulnerable to tighter global financial conditions due to its deep integration into US manufacturing supply chains and reliance on external demand and capital. During the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html), Mexico deepened fuel price controls and expanded subsidies to manage energy cost inflation [2].

# Key Dynamics

- **Strategic autonomy vs economic dependence**: Mexico's pursuit of autonomy through diversification is structurally constrained by its geographic and economic integration with the US.
- **Energy cooperation as autonomy tool**: The Pemex-Petrobras MoU is emblematic of a larger trend — the diversification of strategic resource exploration, exploitation, and refinement aimed at reducing vulnerabilities, alongside a stronger push for cooperation among countries of the Global South [1].
- **USMCA as leverage point**: The US preference for bilateral deals over trilateral frameworks creates both risk and opportunity for Mexico to negotiate terms favorable to its strategic interests.
- **Pemex structural constraints**: Production declines, operational limitations, and dependence on foreign deep-water expertise constrain Mexico's energy strategy.
