# Background

South Africa is the continent's most industrialized economy and a founding member of the BRICS grouping. The end of apartheid in 1994 produced a settlement in which the African National Congress (ANC) governed with an absolute majority for thirty years, presiding over a constitutional order whose promise of economic transformation was only partially fulfilled. The country's structural vulnerabilities — extreme unemployment, service-delivery failure, energy insecurity, and one of the world's highest inequality rates — have produced a volatile domestic environment in which migration has become the most combustible political issue.

A Government of National Unity (GNU) emerged from the 2024 elections after the ANC lost its absolute majority for the first time, forcing a ten-party coalition. The coalition includes the Democratic Alliance (DA), which holds the Interior Ministry, and the right-wing Patriotic Alliance, which presses internally for harsher migration enforcement. Jacob Zuma's Umkhonto weSizwe (MK) party, formed after his expulsion from the ANC, operates outside the GNU and is implicated in the orchestration of anti-migration unrest [1].

# Current Situation

## The 2026 Xenophobic Mobilization

Since 2022, anti-migration mobilization has shifted from episodic violence (the 2008, 2015, and 2019 xenophobic outbreaks) into a sustained, increasingly national campaign. Two organizations anchor the movement: **Operation Dudula** ("push out" in isiZulu), founded during the Covid-19 pandemic and registered as a political party in 2023, and **March and March**, founded in 2024 by radio presenter Jacinta Ngobese-Zuma, whose 168,000-follower Instagram platform projects a "concerned citizen" persona. Both groups conduct identity checks, evict migrants from housing, block access to hospitals and schools, and have issued an ultimatum: undocumented migrants were to leave by 30 June 2026 [1].

The 30 June deadline produced 120 protest actions nationwide. Although the feared mass escalation did not materialize — a coalition of over 160 civil-society organizations mobilized in defense of migrants, employers told foreign-born staff to stay home, and police arrested over 900 people — the threats had their effect: many migrants, including those with valid permits, have left the country under pressure. The campaigns have announced weekly Thursday protests continuing through the 4 November 2026 municipal and local elections, with the slogan "Until they're gone" [1].

## The Mobilization Base

The mobilization exploits genuine precarity. Official unemployment stands at 32%, rising above 60% for those aged 15–24. Service-delivery failures — housing, water, electricity, healthcare — produce chronic local protest against governance failure. In this context, migration becomes a scapegoat: despite official migrant numbers of approximately 3 million (5.1% of the population), and despite studies showing positive economic effects of migration and no robust link between migration and crime, the narrative that migrants "take jobs" has gained traction. Research from the Human Sciences Research Council documents a steady rise in anti-immigrant sentiment [1].

## Orchestration and Political Capture

Müller ([SWP]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/swp.html)) flags evidence that the movement is not purely grassroots. A Murmur Intelligence analysis traced a six-year-old coordinated online network using algorithms to spread anti-migrant narratives; the ISS characterized the online amplification as a "lucrative" influence operation. Investigations by amaBhungane traced March and March's funding through a private account linked to the Zuma family circle. Media speculation about foreign — including Russian — financing draws on Zuma's documented ties to [Putin]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/putin.html). MK politicians have publicly backed March and March events, particularly in KwaZulu-Natal (KZN), the movement's home province and Zuma's base. The pattern echoes the 2021 unrest that followed Zuma's arrest, when social-media-mobilized dissatisfaction produced widespread violence in KZN and Gauteng [1].

## The Government's Policy Response

Despite a November 2025 High Court judgment affirming that human rights apply regardless of citizenship or residency status, ordering Operation Dudula to cease hate speech and harassment, and directing the government to implement the 2019 National Action Plan to Combat Racism, Racial Discrimination, Xenophobia and Related Intolerance, the GNU has responded primarily with enforcement. The cabinet-approved "Comprehensive Approach for Migration Management" provides for deportations, enhanced border controls, voluntary return programs, and measures against employment of undocumented persons — but contains no measures to push back xenophobic attacks or strengthen migrant rights. By mid-July the government claimed 53,000 deportations or returns, many to Zimbabwe, Mozambique, and Malawi, where socioeconomic prospects are far worse [1].

# Key Dynamics

- **Scapegoating as governance substitute**: The ANC, having failed to deliver the economic transformation promised after apartheid, faces a political vacuum that organized xenophobia fills. Opposition to migration becomes a substitute for governance — directing popular anger away from service-delivery failure and toward a visible minority.
- **Coalition strain**: The GNU's internal dynamics push policy toward enforcement. The DA, holding the Interior Ministry, wants to prove it can govern; the Patriotic Alliance presses for harder measures; the ANC fears further electoral losses. Each incentive points in the same direction.
- **Regional spillover**: Deportations to neighboring states risk destabilizing the region. Zimbabwe, Mozambique, and Malawi lack the economic capacity to absorb large returnee flows, creating potential for cross-border deterioration.
- **Orchestration risk**: The documented links between March and March, the Zuma circle, and MK raise the prospect that xenophobic mobilization is being instrumentalized for political ends — including the kind of social-media-driven unrest that produced the 2021 KZN-Gauteng violence.

# Analysis

## Migration as the Achilles Heel of the Post-Apartheid Settlement

Müller ([SWP]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/swp.html)) frames the South African case as a test of whether a post-conflict settlement can absorb migration pressure without authoritarian capture. The structural ingredients are familiar across the post-colonial world: a liberation movement (ANC) whose legitimacy rests on a transformation promise it cannot deliver, a large youth population with no economic prospects, service-delivery failure, and a visible migrant minority that can be scapegoated. What distinguishes South Africa is the institutional inheritance — a constitutional court, an independent press, a vibrant civil society — that has so far contained the worst scenarios. The November 2025 High Court judgment and the 160-organization counter-coalition illustrate the resilience of that inheritance [1].

The deeper structural question is whether that resilience can hold as economic conditions worsen and political entrepreneurs learn to weaponize xenophobia. The 2024 GNU was itself a signal: the ANC's absolute majority is gone, and the political marketplace now rewards those who can mobilize the precariat. March and March's combination of social-media reach, "concerned mother" framing, and plausible-deniability distance from MK is a template for the kind of networked populist mobilization that can scale faster than institutions can respond [1].

## The German and European Parallel

Müller notes that Germany and South Africa face structurally similar migration-management challenges: both must implement policy without losing sight of migrant protection, both face organized anti-migration movements, and both operate under constitutional constraints that the political right seeks to erode. She recommends a collaborative exchange within the existing German-South African strategic dialogue, and stronger EU-South Africa cooperation on migration management — including support for neighboring states absorbing returnees, given that South Africa's deportation policy directly affects regional stability [1].
