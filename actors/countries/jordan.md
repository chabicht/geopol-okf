# Background

Jordan is a Hashemite kingdom bordering [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html), [Iraq]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iraq.html), [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html), and Syria, with a narrow coastline at Aqaba on the [Red Sea]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html)'s northern tip. Aqaba is Jordan's only coastal city and port — simultaneously a luxury tourist destination, a deep-water commercial harbor, and a gateway to Jordan's desert interior. The country's dependence on this single maritime access point makes it acutely vulnerable to any escalation that targets Red Sea infrastructure.

# Current Situation

## The Aqaba Threat

In mid-July 2026, the US Embassy in Amman issued an emergency security alert advising Americans to avoid Aqaba's international airport and seaport due to a specific and credible threat. The advisory followed [Iranian]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) missile and drone attacks targeting US forces in Jordan that resulted in American service member deaths, prompting retaliatory US strikes on IRGC coastal facilities. Air raid sirens subsequently sounded in Jordan, warning of incoming Iranian missiles against Aqaba [1].

## The Basra-Aqaba Pipeline

The threat to Aqaba emerged shortly after a meeting between Iraq's Prime Minister and Jordan's foreign minister in Washington to accelerate the long-delayed Basra-Aqaba pipeline — a 1,600 km conduit from southern [Iraq]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iraq.html) to Jordan's Red Sea coast, designed to carry 2.25 million barrels per day at a cost of $18 billion. The [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) cut southern Iraqi exports by roughly 70%, exposing Baghdad's dependence on the Strait of Hormuz. Jordan would gain transit fees and 150,000 barrels per day for its Zarqa refinery. For Iraq, the pipeline is about security rather than profit: Aqaba costs approximately $6 per barrel versus 60 cents through the Gulf [1].

# Analysis

## Energy Bypass Infrastructure as Target

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) frames the Aqaba threat within his Escalation Trap framework: Iran has powerful incentives to widen the conflict beyond Hormuz itself and strike any infrastructure that could permanently reduce its coercive leverage. A functioning Basra-Aqaba pipeline would undercut Iran's vision of a "security resistance belt" stretching from the Mediterranean through the Red Sea to the Gulf — a network intended to deny rivals permanent energy bypasses and preserve Tehran's long-term leverage over global oil flows. The pipeline would not merely bypass one waterway; it would weaken Iran's ability to threaten global energy flows, making projects such as Aqaba increasingly attractive military targets — not because of their immediate economic value, but because they challenge Iran's broader strategy of preserving leverage through chokepoint control [1].

See [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) for regional context and [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) for the operational timeline.

## US Base Attacks and Regional Coalition Pressure

[Heather Cox Richardson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/heather-cox-richardson.html) reports that Iranian attacks on US bases in Jordan killed two soldiers with one missing — the fourth such attack in five days — wounding dozens and damaging helicopters. Retired Air Force Lt. Gen. David Deptula, who helped plan the 1991 Gulf War air campaign, framed the strikes as targeting the US regional coalition: making the political cost of hosting American forces exceed the security benefit. The repeated strikes demonstrate that bases in [Jordan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/jordan.html) give the US broader reach across [Syria]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html), [Iraq]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iraq.html), and beyond — which is precisely why Iran is targeting them. The pattern of four attacks in five days, each more damaging than the last, signals an Iranian effort to drive a wedge between the US and its regional hosts by making basing arrangements politically unsustainable [2].

## Muwaffaq Salti Air Base and Aqaba Under Fire

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documents that Iranian forces struck Jordan's Muwaffaq Salti Air Base in Azraq on July 17–18, 2026, killing two US service members — a continuation of the pattern of strikes on US-hosting bases. On July 19, Iran fired ballistic missiles at Aqaba, Jordan's only port city, near the Israeli city of Eilat. The Aqaba strike extends the threat beyond US military infrastructure to civilian and commercial maritime infrastructure, compounding the risk to the long-delayed Basra-Aqaba pipeline project [3].
