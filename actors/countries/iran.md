# Background

Iran has long been a central actor in Middle Eastern geopolitics, leveraging its geographic position, asymmetric military capabilities, and network of regional proxies to project power far beyond its conventional means. The [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) represented the most direct military confrontation between Iran and a US-led coalition, culminating in a ceasefire that left the underlying balance of power fundamentally unresolved.

## Pre-War Internal Dynamics

GPF assessed Iran's regime was headed for a "hard landing" before the war — elite factionalism, IRGC erosion, and rising pragmatists. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Pre-War Context" [1].

# Current Situation

## Maximum Leverage

Iran entered the post-war period in a position of maximum leverage, articulating three conditions: $12B in frozen assets, end to Israeli operations in Lebanon, and reduced US military presence. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Escalation Pathways" [3].

## Strait of Hormuz

Iran retained effective leverage over Hormuz throughout the conflict — the strait remains open because Iran permits it, with de facto control over transit, 45% of pre-war production levels, and shipping costs 5.5x pre-war. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Price Recovery vs Supply Recovery" and "Scenario Modeling" [8].

## Hormuz Toll System

Iran proposed a permanent [toll system]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/hormuz-toll-system.html) (~$2M/transit in rials, negotiated through [Oman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/oman.html)). See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Regional Realignment" [2].

## Ceasefire as Axis Course Correction

The ceasefire was a DragonBear axis course correction — Russia and China green-lit the MoU. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Diplomatic Dimensions" [7].

## War Without Resolution

The war ended without resolving the underlying balance of power — Iran survived, the US accepted an agreement, and Israel remains dissatisfied. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) [1].

## External Operations in Europe

Iran's IRGC has escalated external operations in Europe — assassination plots via gaming platforms, "gig economy" model, oil-backed shadow banking. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Iran's External Operations Escalation" [9].

## US-Israel Friction

Washington's pursuit of diplomatic accommodation with Iran created friction with [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) — ceasefire negotiations through Pakistani mediation without Israeli participation. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "US-Israel Strategic Divergence" [10].

## The Funeral and Hardened Resolve

The [funeral of the Supreme Leader]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/supreme-leader-funeral-2026.html) produced massive nationalist mobilization, enabling a more ambitious war aim: ending the permanent American military presence in the Gulf. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "The Funeral and the Coming August Shock" [11].

## Turkish-Iranian Competition

As Iran declines, [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) is advancing into the vacuum. For more than a millennium, the northern rim of the Middle East has been an arena for geopolitical competition between Turkic and Persian powers. Iran's contiguous arc of influence had obstructed Turkey's ambitions to revive its influence along its southern flank — until the post-October 7 wars severely weakened Iran and Hezbollah, creating an unprecedented strategic opening for Ankara [12].

Washington's strategy rests on fostering a new geopolitical order anchored by Turkey, Arab states, and [Pakistan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/pakistan.html) to prevent Tehran from reclaiming the dominant position it has enjoyed in [Syria]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html), [Lebanon]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html), and [Iraq]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iraq.html). The collapse of the Syrian government in December 2024 transformed Syria from a central pillar of Iranian influence into a springboard for Turkish regional ambitions [12].

Iran is unlikely to relinquish its regional position without a fight, particularly because [Iraq]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iraq.html) continues to serve as a strategic buffer at a moment of pronounced vulnerability. Iran maintains considerable influence in Iraq — underscored by the decision to route Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei's funeral procession through Najaf and Karbala before his final burial in Iran. Iraq is poised to reemerge as the principal arena of geopolitical competition between Turkey and Iran [12].

## Endless Warfare Networks

Iran's network of proxies and surrogates constitutes a core instrument of its [Endless Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/endless-warfare.html) strategy. The IRGC is an enabler of Iran's distributed networks with global reach — projecting power, coercing neighbors, and getting inside the decision space of adversaries at relatively low cost. Iraqi militias have proven the most consistent direct threat to U.S. forces, responsible for more than 180 attacks on U.S. bases in the 18 months before the war began. Deeply embedded in [Iraq]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iraq.html)'s official security architecture, they emerge from the war with greater public standing and strategic importance, their relative weight within Iran's proxy network having grown as Hizballah and Hamas absorbed losses [14].

## Russia-Iran Cyber Collaboration

Iran's cyber ecosystem (IRGC, MOIS, hacktivist groups like Handala Hack and CyberAv3ngers, private sector front companies) is complex and decentralized. Collaboration with Russia has been overstated — hacktivist coordination ≠ state-to-state collaboration, and historical distrust (Turla false-flag) limits capability sharing. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Russia-Iran Cyber Dimension" [16].

## From Survival to Revenge

Iran's political objectives expanded through three phases — survival, ambition, revenge — with the new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei's nationally broadcast call to action creating a self-reinforcing escalation spiral. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Iran's Three-Phase Evolution" [15].

# Analysis

Iran's post-war position demonstrates the limits of conventional military power against a geographically entrenched adversary with asymmetric leverage. The [energy dimension]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/energy-and-resources.html) is central: Iran's control over Hormuz gives it influence over global oil and LNG flows that no amount of US military superiority has been able to neutralize. The [ceasefire MoU]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html) institutionalized an outcome that favors Iran's long-term strategic position.

## Mosaic Defense and Theory of Victory

Iran's success was the product of a twenty-year campaign plan — the mosaic defense doctrine (decentralized command surviving decapitation strikes), a sanctions-proof economy (tanker fleet 70→550 vessels, 90% of oil sales to Chinese refineries), and a narrow theory of victory: survive and impose costs. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) — "Iran's Theory of Victory" [13].
