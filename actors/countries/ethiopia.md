# Background

Ethiopia is the most populous landlocked country in the world (approximately 135 million people), and its lack of sea access is a defining strategic constraint. Since [Eritrea]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/eritrea.html)'s independence in 1993, Ethiopia has been a landlocked state — a condition that Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed Ali has increasingly framed as intolerable, calling it a "geographic prison" and declaring that Ethiopia "will not remain a landlocked state." The pursuit of port access drives Ethiopian foreign policy and military posture, and intersects with multiple regional proxy competitions [1][2].

Historically, despite earlier Abyssinian influence, the Eritrean coast was controlled for centuries by Ottoman-dependent rulers, then came under Egyptian-British influence, and finally Italian colonial rule (Italy purchased the port of Assab in 1869). Ethiopia only gained direct [Red Sea]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html) access in the second half of the 20th century and built an imperial navy. After Eritrea's independence, Ethiopia used the ports of Massawa and Assab at favourable rates until the devastating border war of 1998–2000 [2].

# Current Situation

## Strategic Posture: Pursuit of Sovereign Sea Access

Abiy speaks of Ethiopia becoming a "superpower in Africa," which he argues requires sovereign sea access. The rationales are both economic and military: a militarised port with land connection would allow Ethiopia to receive arms shipments by sea without third-party knowledge. Ethiopia is building its own navy to project power in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden [2]. Currently, 95% of Ethiopian imports and exports transit through [Djibouti]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/africa.html), costing approximately USD 1.5 billion annually in port and berthing fees paid in scarce foreign currency — plus significant logistical inefficiencies. This dependence on a single small state, which hosts both US and Chinese military bases, constitutes a critical vulnerability [2].

Ethiopia's military leadership has continued to signal its determination to secure maritime access, with Navy Commander Kindu Gezu calling it "inevitable" that Ethiopia would regain what he described as its "unfairly lost" access to the Red Sea, while Army Chief Birhanu Jula told senior officers that "elaborate preparations" were needed to achieve that objective [3]. Under Abiy, sea access has increasingly been framed as a national security priority. The government has sought arrangements with coastal actors, including Somaliland and Puntland, to diversify its maritime options. The fallout from the Tigray War has complicated the matter; relations with Eritrea remain tense, and Addis Ababa wants to prevent Asmara from curbing its regional ambitions while managing internal political and ethnic challenges. Ethiopia's efforts to reach the ocean reflect a broader push to restore strategic autonomy, expand regional influence, and reduce vulnerability to neighboring powers [3].

## Relationships & Regional Alignments

Ethiopia's alignment with the [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) in Sudan's civil war places it on one side of a regional proxy conflict, with the UAE serving as one of Abiy's closest financial and military partners. Expanding Egyptian cooperation with Eritrea and Somalia causes unease in Addis Abeba — particularly the June 2026 agreement between Eritrea and [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/egypt.html) to cooperate in maritime economy and develop the ports of Assab and Massawa, the same ports Abiy has signaled interest in [1][2]. Multiple middle powers — [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html), the UAE, [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html), and [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) — pursue maritime interests in the Red Sea region, complicating Ethiopia's calculus [1][2]. Saudi Arabia remains skeptical of further militarization of the Red Sea; prior mediation attempts between Ethiopia and Eritrea failed over the exclusion of military use of Assab [2].

## Posture Constraints & Compromise Path

A direct conventional war with Eritrea carries high risk for Ethiopia. While Ethiopia possesses modern drones and a large army, Eritrea is a heavily militarised society. Even Eritrean opposition figures would likely fight on the side of the authoritarian Isaias regime rather than accept an Ethiopian occupation. Eritrea could exert pressure through cooperation with various Ethiopian opposition movements. A further regionalisation of conflicts in Sudan and Somalia would be probable [2].

The US and other governments have told Addis Abeba in clear terms that an open invasion of Eritrea would be unacceptable and would jeopardise the IMF's billion-dollar programme with Ethiopia. Both Washington and the [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) are currently pursuing rapprochement with Ethiopia [2]. A compromise is conceivable that combines regional integration and cross-border transport infrastructure with confidence-building security measures and eventually an Ethiopian naval base in Somaliland or Djibouti — but without an extraterritorial corridor to the Ethiopian heartland. Such a solution requires trust among all parties, which is currently scarce in the Horn of Africa [2].

## Domestic Dynamics

Ethiopia's external posture is constrained by deep internal fragmentation. The Tigray conflict's aftermath has produced a parallel governance crisis: the TPLF, its legal status revoked by the National Election Board in May 2025, has reinstated the 2020 regional parliament and elected Debretsion Gebremichael as regional president — a government that does not recognize Addis Abeba and is not recognized by it. The TPLF has twice replaced transitional administrations by force (March 2025, May 2026), generating legitimacy problems within Tigray itself, compounded by crackdowns on media and the departure of tens of thousands of young people seeking work abroad [2].

The Tigray region faces acute material deprivation: cash, fuel, and medicine shortages, with Addis Abeba behind on budget transfers to the regional administration since October 2025 and public-sector employees including teachers going unpaid. A June 2026 mobilization law established a Command Post under Debretsion's leadership, and the TDF has reportedly engaged in forced recruitment. War weariness is widespread. Simultaneously, Abiy faces the Fano insurgency in Amhara — Amhara-nationalist rebels who turned against the Ethiopian state in 2023 and whom Eritrea has supported. The combination of an unresolved Tigray crisis, an active Amhara insurgency, and the ethnic-federal constitutional structure means that Ethiopia's pursuit of sea access is pursued by a government whose domestic authority is contested across multiple regions [1][2].

## Tigray: Posture Summary

Ethiopia's internal [Tigray conflict]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/tigray-conflict.html) persists in a gray zone between war and peace, with the TPLF establishing a parallel regional administration in Mekelle while Addis Abeba contests its legitimacy and backs the rival Tigray Peace Force splinter. The conflict constrains Ethiopia's external posture by consuming political bandwidth, complicating relations with Eritrea, and drawing Ethiopia into Sudan's civil war as a transit route for UAE supplies to the RSF.
