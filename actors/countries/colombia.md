# Background

Colombia is a net energy exporter whose fossil fuel sector — concentrated in coal and crude oil — accounts for approximately 10% of government revenue, 35% of exports, and 4% of total economic value added. It is the world's sixth-largest coal exporter and Latin America's fourth-largest oil exporter. The country faces a structural challenge: declining reserves (7.2 years of oil, 5.9 years of gas), heavy domestic reliance on fossil fuels for transport and industry, and constrained fiscal space due to high debt and rising interest rates [1].

The 2016 peace agreement with the FARC-EP did not end armed conflict; rural areas remain fragile due to the presence of armed groups, and disputes over land and territorial control persist. Extreme concentration of land ownership and insecure land titles fuel conflict and hinder forest conservation, renewable energy projects, and alternative economic development [1].

# Current Situation

## Petro's Reform Agenda

President Gustavo Petro (Pacto Histórico, took office 2022) embarked on a far-reaching socio-ecological reform agenda combining decarbonisation, energy sovereignty, biodiversity protection, and social inclusion. Petro's [land reform](https://www.minjusticia.gov.co/Sala-de-prensa/Documents/ABC_JURISDICCI%C3%93N.pdf) is essential to his environmental, climate, and energy policies — insecure land rights and the presence of armed groups in rural areas block progress on all fronts [1].

### Energy Policy

The Petro government set out a "Roadmap for a Just Energy Transition" through a nationwide dialogue process. Key measures:

- No new exploration contracts for coal, oil, and gas — a unique supply-side approach among Global South fossil fuel exporters. Existing contracts remain in force.
- Solar capacity reached 1.9 GW by end of 2024 (~9% of installed electricity generation capacity). Non-conventional renewable energy's share has risen sharply since 2022.
- Large-scale wind energy in La Guajira is progressing slowly due to grid bottlenecks, regulatory uncertainty, conflicts with Wayúu communities over territory and benefit distribution.
- Electric vehicle registrations increased 252% (Jan–May 2025 vs. same period 2024). Bogotá has one of Latin America's largest electric bus fleets.
- Initial steps toward phasing out fossil fuel subsidies through adjustments to the Fuel Price Stabilisation Fund (FEPC) [1].

### Climate Policy

Colombia committed to carbon neutrality by 2050. Its updated NDC (September 2025) sets an emissions corridor of 155–161 million tonnes CO₂e/year by 2035, supplemented by the 2030 target of 51% reduction vs. business-as-usual. The NDC integrates mitigation, adaptation, and biodiversity targets under a socio-ecological transformation principle, with a roadmap for gradual fossil fuel phase-out and subsidy removal by 2029 [1].

### Environmental Policy

Around 60% of Colombia's greenhouse gas emissions come from agriculture, forestry, and other land use (AFOLU). Deforestation fell by 54% between 2021 and 2023 (lowest since records began) but rose again in 2024, highlighting the fragility of progress. The Escazú Agreement on environmental participation was ratified [1].

## International Climate Leadership

Colombia assumed a leadership role under Petro:

- First Latin American country and largest fossil fuel producer to support the [Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty](https://www.fossilfueltreaty.org/).
- Joined the [Powering Past Coal Alliance](https://poweringpastcoal.org/) (PPCA) and the [Beyond Oil and Gas Alliance](https://beyondoilandgasalliance.org/) (BOGA).
- Drove the transition away from fossil fuels (TAFF) agenda at COP28 (Dubai 2023) and COP30 (Belém 2026), where ~80 countries called for a global roadmap with milestones and financing.
- Initiated the Santa Marta Conference (April 2026, co-organised with the Netherlands) to pursue TAFF implementation outside formal COP negotiations.
- Announced withdrawal from the Investor-State Dispute Settlement (ISDS) system (March 2026) to limit financial and legal risks of energy policy [1].

Diplomatic tensions with the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) have grown: the Trump administration pursues energy dominance through aggressive fossil fuel-centric foreign policy while attacking international climate protection efforts [1].

## Implementation Shortfalls

Petro's international leadership contrasted with national-level weaknesses:

- Frequent staff changes in ministries, limited bureaucratic continuity, and an overburdened state apparatus made implementation difficult.
- Vested interests and interdependencies between central government, fossil fuel companies, royalty-dependent departments, and local employment schemes created entrenched resistance.
- Petro's personalised and populist communication raised the profile of his agenda but linked the energy transition too closely to his person, intensifying resistance and complicating political continuation [1].

## 2026 Election and Political Reversal

The March 2026 parliamentary elections produced a polarised, fragmented landscape. In the June 21 presidential run-off, right-wing "law and order" candidate Abelardo de la Espriella narrowly defeated Iván Cepeda (Pacto Histórico). De la Espriella takes office August 8 [1].

A complete departure from climate and energy policy is unlikely — geoeconomic and energy-security constraints have become too strong. Renewable energy expansion, grid buildout, and electric mobility will likely continue. But the pace will slow and the focus will shift: the energy transition will be treated more as energy security and economic modernisation than socio-ecological transformation. New oil and gas concessions and renewed fracking debate are expected. Colombia is unlikely to continue driving the international TAFF agenda [1].

## Financing Gap

Colombia requires investment equivalent to 7–10% of GDP annually until 2030 for the energy transition. The government's [country platform](https://www.colombiaemb.org/post/colombia-launches-the-country-platform-in-washington-d-c) seeks to mobilise US$40 billion from development banks, private investors, and international partners. However, the platform has not yet secured the level of external financing and risk mitigation needed. Petro's decision to stop new exploration contracts was viewed negatively by rating agencies and investors, raising the cost of capital precisely when substantial funds are needed [1].

# Analysis

Colombia's trajectory is a test case for whether fossil fuel-dependent emerging economies can transition without jeopardising economic stability, security of supply, and social cohesion. The outcome is being closely watched by other Global South fossil fuel exporters. Germany and the [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html)'s engagement — through a 2023 bilateral climate partnership (€200M German pledge) and the EU's Global Gateway strategy (€106M for 2021–2027) — makes them stakeholders in the outcome. The EU is perceived as fragmented and hesitant, with financing materialising only once projects are well advanced and little presence during high-risk early phases [1].

The political reversal under de la Espriella illustrates the structural fragility of supply-side climate policy in emerging economies: without deep institutional embedding and broad coalitional support, ambitious reforms remain vulnerable to electoral backlash and vested-interest resistance [1].
