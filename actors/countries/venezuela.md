# Background

Venezuela holds the world's largest proven oil reserves, concentrated in the Orinoco Belt. Years of political crisis under Maduro, US sanctions, and institutional decay left the oil industry in ruins. In early 2026, Maduro was ousted and replaced by an administration under President Delcy Rodriguez, under heavy US pressure. The reopening is a managed transition shaped by Washington's influence rather than a genuine domestic reform process [1].

# Current Situation

## Oil Sector Reopening

Recent deals signed by Caracas and Chevron, SBP, Shell, Eni, Maurel & Prom, SLB, and Repsol show international companies reengaging with Venezuela. Many deals focus on the Orinoco Belt and Monagas state, positioning oil and natural gas at the center of the reopening strategy. The primary US objective appears to be the rapid restoration of an orderly oil industry capable of attracting investment and stabilizing production [1].

## Structural Challenges

Venezuela's recovery strategy is designed less around rebuilding domestic purchasing power and more around creating conditions to lure foreign investment. The deeper structural problem is that the governing apparatus — after years of politicization — is fragmented and ineffective. Many officials and state managers are Maduro-era appointees who benefited from the previous system and may be ill-suited to operate within a more internationally integrated, rules-based economic structure. Investors face a dilemma: moving too early exposes them to major political and legal uncertainty, while waiting too long risks missing the initial market opportunity [1].

## US Influence

Washington reportedly maintains close and constant engagement with the Rodriguez administration. "Rapid" and "orderly" remain fundamentally difficult to reconcile in a country where the rule of law, financial governance, and institutional predictability remain weak. The critical issue is no longer simply sanctions relief or oil production growth but whether Venezuela can rebuild a functioning state capable of enforcing contracts, regulating investment, and operating under a more predictable legal framework — a process likely to take years [1].

# Key Dynamics

- **US pressure as dominant force**: The reopening is shaped by Washington's influence, not organic domestic reform. This makes Venezuela's trajectory dependent on US political continuity.
- **Institutional rebuilding precedes economic recovery**: Rebuilding the state — selective retention of experienced personnel, institutional restructuring, continued external pressure — is the prerequisite for sustainable oil sector growth.
- **High-risk, high-reward market**: Opportunities exist in energy, infrastructure, logistics, and services, but political, legal, and sanctions-related risks remain substantial.
