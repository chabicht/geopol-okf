# Background

Eritrea is a coastal state on the Horn of Africa whose independence from [Ethiopia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ethiopia.html) in 1993 left Ethiopia landlocked. The resulting geographic asymmetry — Eritrea controls the coastline, Ethiopia needs port access — is the structural driver of recurring conflict between the two states. Eritrea's coastline on the Red Sea, including the ports of Massawa and Assab, was controlled for centuries by Ottoman-dependent rulers, then came under Egyptian-British influence, and finally Italian colonial rule (Italy purchased Assab in 1869) [2].

After the devastating border war of 1998–2000, an independent border commission ruled in Eritrea's favour, but Ethiopia did not implement the decision and was not sanctioned for non-compliance — a source of deep grievance in Asmara. The UN Security Council imposed a weapons embargo and sanctions on Eritrea in 2009 for supporting Al-Shabaab; these were lifted only in 2018 [2].

# Current Situation

## Regime Character

Eritrea is governed by President Isaias Afewerki under a highly authoritarian system that has persisted for decades. The regime warns incessantly of foreign interference and has very low trust in international actors. Eritrea feels repeatedly treated unjustly — particularly by Ethiopia's refusal to implement the border commission ruling without consequences, contrasted with the UN sanctions imposed on Eritrea [2].

## Tigray and the Ethiopian Relationship

After Abiy Ahmed's inauguration in 2018, the two countries approached each other, including personal meetings between Abiy and Isaias. However, this cooperation served primarily to fight the TPLF together — Isaias held the TPLF (which had dominated the Ethiopian government during the 1990s war) responsible for the conflict and Eritrea's subsequent international isolation. Eritrean troops participated in the Tigray war (2020–2022) in large numbers and reportedly committed massive war crimes against civilians [2].

Eritrea was not party to the Pretoria Agreement that ended the Tigray war. As Abiy later acknowledged, Eritrea wanted the TPLF completely destroyed rather than returned to political life. The relationship subsequently cooled: Eritrea supported the Fano, Amhara-nationalist rebels who turned against the Ethiopian state in 2023 [2].

## Tactical Alliance with TPLF

In a remarkable shift, both Asmara and the TPLF set aside their mutual enmity and entered a tactical cooperation aimed at weakening the government in Addis Abeba. This followed the stalling of the Pretoria Agreement's implementation and the takeover of the Tigray transitional government by TPLF hardliners hostile to Addis Abeba [2].

## Regional Alignments

Eritrea is aligned with [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/egypt.html) and Somalia against the [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html)-[Ethiopia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ethiopia.html)-RSF bloc in the broader Red Sea competition. Expanding Egyptian cooperation with Eritrea and Somalia causes unease in Addis Abeba [1][2].

# Analysis

## Deterrence Through Militarisation

Eritrea is a heavily militarised society that has been preparing for confrontation for decades. Even Eritrean opposition figures would likely fight on the side of the Isaias regime rather than accept an Ethiopian occupation. Eritrea can exert pressure through cooperation with various Ethiopian opposition movements, including the Fano and the TPLF. The country's ability to deny Ethiopia the sea access it seeks makes the standoff structurally durable [2].

## Ethiopian Escalation Rhetoric

Ethiopia's October 2025 letter to the UN accusing Eritrea of preparing war with the TPLF, and Foreign Minister Gedion's November 2025 statement that a "casus belli" already exists, mark a rhetorical escalation. However, mediation efforts by the US, Saudi Arabia, and European actors have so far been unsuccessful. A compromise combining regional integration with confidence-building measures and an Ethiopian naval base in Somaliland or Djibouti (without an extraterritorial corridor) is conceivable but requires trust that is currently absent [2].
