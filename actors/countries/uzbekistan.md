# Background

Uzbekistan is the most populous country in [Central Asia](/regions/central-asia.md) and has undergone significant economic liberalization since 2016. Its investment climate has transformed dramatically, making it one of the fastest-growing FDI destinations in the region alongside [Kazakhstan](/actors/countries/kazakhstan.md).

# Current Situation

## Investment Boom

Uzbekistan hosted the Tashkent International Investment Forum, attracting over 10,000 participants from more than 100 countries. FDI has increased more than 45 times since 2016 — a staggering transformation. Average annual growth in capital investments reached 21.7% in 2023-25, outpacing Kazakhstan significantly.

Domestic investment is driving this growth: more than 55% of capital investment comes from businesses and households, indicating that the boom is not merely foreign-driven but reflects genuine internal economic dynamism.

## Chinese Engagement

Uzbekistan is part of the concentration of Chinese FDI in Central Asia, with [China](/actors/countries/china.md) directing investment toward manufacturing relocation and mineral extraction. However, the relationship is not without friction: Chinese investors have been accused of pressuring Uzbek farmers to hand over land — a pattern that echoes the protests against Chinese expansion seen in [Kazakhstan](/actors/countries/kazakhstan.md) under Nazarbayev.

# Analysis

Uzbekistan's investment trajectory is the most dynamic in Central Asia, driven by a combination of domestic reform and foreign capital. The tension between economic opportunity and sovereignty concerns — exemplified by the land pressure allegations — will define the trajectory of Uzbek-Chinese relations. The scale of the Tashkent forum signals that Uzbekistan is positioning itself as a central node in Central Asian economic architecture.
