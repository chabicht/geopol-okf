# Background

The Philippines is an archipelagic state of more than 7,600 islands whose geographic position astride the South China Sea makes it a central actor in the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html) strategic competition. It is one of [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html)'s most outspoken maritime challengers and one of the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html)' oldest treaty allies under the 1951 Mutual Defense Treaty. The two countries' competing claims — Manila's exclusive economic zone off the West Philippine Sea and Beijing's nine-dash line — produced the 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration ruling that rejected China's historic claims as legally baseless. Ten years on, the award is binding but unenforced: Mischief Reef is a functioning Chinese airbase inside the Philippine EEZ, and pressure has migrated to Second Thomas Shoal, Scarborough, Sandy Cay, and Reed Bank. See [South China Sea Arbitration Anniversary]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/south-china-sea-arbitration-anniversary-2026.html) [1].

The Philippines held the [ASEAN]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/asean.html) chair in 2026, giving Manila a regional platform just as the South China Sea dispute intensified and the US began recalibrating its regional posture.

# Current Situation

## Proactive Security Actor

Long regarded as one of Washington's closest treaty allies and one of China's most outspoken maritime challengers, Manila has become a proactive security actor in its own right rather than a passive dependent of the US alliance. Over the first half of 2026, the Philippines significantly expanded its defense relationships beyond the US, pursuing parallel security arrangements with [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html), [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html), Canada, and New Zealand, while simultaneously strengthening cooperation with fellow [ASEAN]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/asean.html) members. These initiatives no longer revolve solely around the US-Philippines alliance but place Manila at the center of an expanding regional security network connecting Northeast Asia, Oceania, and Southeast Asia [2].

The instruments of this expansion are concrete and operational: negotiations over Visiting Forces Agreements with Canada and New Zealand; discussions regarding the transfer of Japanese Abukuma-class destroyer escorts and Type 88 anti-ship missiles; Australia's expanding military exercises with Philippine forces; and deeper intelligence cooperation with Tokyo [2].

## Enhanced Strategic Partnership with Vietnam

The Philippines' growing strategic partnership with [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html) is the most instructive example of the emerging regional architecture. Manila and Hanoi agreed to establish maritime hotlines, expand coast guard coordination, and reaffirm their commitment to international law in the South China Sea. The timing was particularly significant, coming shortly before the 10th anniversary of the Permanent Court of Arbitration ruling — and pairing the two ASEAN states with the strongest South China Sea claims. The partnership institutionalizes practical mechanisms meant to reduce risk while boosting collective capacity to manage Chinese maritime pressure. Although Vietnam continues to balance cooperation with strategic autonomy (its "four noes" forbid formal military alliances), the steady expansion of practical security cooperation with the Philippines reflects a shared view that maritime stability is better preserved through closer coordination than through isolated bilateral engagement with Beijing [2].

## Energy-Import Vulnerability

The Philippines declared a national emergency during the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) due to its weak domestic energy base and low reserves. Its dependence on Gulf energy imports created acute economic pressure at the height of the Middle East energy crisis — a vulnerability that briefly opened an aperture for Chinese economic diplomacy. At the height of the crisis, Manila appeared willing to reopen discussions on joint energy development and broader cooperation with Beijing, provided it remained consistent with international law and progress toward a South China Sea Code of Conduct [1][2].

## ASEAN Chair Role

The Philippines holds the ASEAN chair in 2026, and the November ASEAN Summit in Manila is the real deadline for a Code of Conduct. Marcos is pinning the COC to that summit. The risk to watch is any COC provision requiring joint development with China to the exclusion of external parties, which would render the 2016 arbitration award moot by contract rather than by defiance [1].

## Forward Unmanned-System Sustainment: Subic Bay

Dan Justice ([War on the Rocks]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/war-on-the-rocks.html)) identifies the growing U.S. Navy storage facility at Subic Bay as one proximate solution to the "tyranny of distance" problem for sustaining the unmanned fleet in the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html). The Navy aims to field 39 unmanned surface vehicles in 2027 and at least 30 in the Indo-Pacific alone by 2030 under the CNO's "hedge strategy" of tailored force packages — but for all the attention paid to the technology and its employment, the service still lacks a framework for fixing these platforms overseas. The Theodore Roosevelt Carrier Strike Group deployed in 2026 with a medium-sized unmanned surface vehicle. Subic Bay offers geographical proximity to potential flashpoints that the major hubs at Guam and Yokosuka cannot match, but the full distributed network should also include more austere and secure locations such as HMAS Cairns in Northern [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html) and Lombrum Naval Base in Papua New Guinea — both beyond the primary range of Chinese cruise missiles and medium-range ballistic missiles [3].

# Analysis

## Manila at the Center of an Emerging Regional Security Network

[Victoria Herczegh]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) identifies the Philippines as the most striking example of an evolving regional security architecture. The crucial shift is that Manila's defense diplomacy no longer revolves around the US-Philippines alliance but places the Philippines at the center of an expanding regional security network connecting Northeast Asia, Oceania, and Southeast Asia. Instead of waiting for Washington to coordinate collective responses, regional actors are intensifying coordination among themselves [2].

The pattern reflects a structural change in how middle powers respond to US retrenchment. As the Trump administration's "flexible, practical realism" seeks increased transactional burden-sharing and the reversion to the "Pacific Command" designation signals a narrower regional posture, regional allies and partners are building a more interconnected security network of their own — not accommodating or ceding power to [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), but diversifying their security relationships to hedge against uncertainty in the American commitment [2].

## The Closing Chinese Window

Herczegh argues that the Philippines' trajectory illustrates a narrowing Chinese opportunity. Earlier in 2026, Beijing appeared to possess a rare opening to improve relations with several ASEAN members — particularly Manila, whose Gulf energy dependence created acute economic pressure. But as Japan simultaneously offered assistance in expanding regional energy resilience, and as ASEAN members accelerated their own cooperation, Beijing's comparative diplomatic advantage decreased. More importantly, the rapid acceleration of security cooperation among regional actors fundamentally changed the strategic environment. Instead of moving closer to China in response to perceived US retrenchment, prominent ASEAN states diversified their security relationships with Japan, Australia, and one another. China's initiatives through the Comprehensive Strategic Partnership, its "3+3" dialogue with Vietnam, and its brief engagement with the Philippines have remained comparatively limited in practical strategic effect — overtaken by the more dynamic expansion of the emerging regional security network [2].

China's economic influence remains substantial: trade, investment, supply chains, and infrastructure links give Beijing considerable regional influence that no external actor can easily replace. But security dynamics increasingly follow a different trajectory. As Japan and Australia assume more proactive leadership roles and key ASEAN states deepen cooperation among themselves, Beijing has less room to maneuver — a divergence China itself appears to recognize, judging by its recent military posture: reinforcing ties with [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) and North Korea, increasingly criticizing US regional allies (especially Japan), and rare test-firing of a ballistic missile from a nuclear submarine into the Pacific Ocean as a warning to neighboring countries [2].
