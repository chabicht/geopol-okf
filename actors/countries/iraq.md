# Background

Iraq is a major Middle Eastern state bordering [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html), Saudi Arabia, Jordan, Syria, and Kuwait, with a short coastline on the Persian Gulf. The country's Shiite majority and its position between Turkish and Persian spheres of influence make it a perennial arena of geopolitical competition. Decades of war, sanctions, and internal conflict have left Iraq with weak institutions and a political landscape deeply influenced by external powers.

# Current Situation

## Arena of Turkish-Iranian Competition

As Iran declines, Iraq is poised to reemerge as the principal arena of geopolitical competition between Turkey and Iran, echoing the centuries-long struggle for dominance over the region [1]. [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) is leveraging its direct border with Iraq and its close relationship with the Kurdistan Democratic Party (KDP), led by the Barzani clan, which controls the Kurdish-majority areas in northern Iraq along the Turkish border [1]. Turkey is also leveraging Iraq's oil export crisis, in the wake of the Iranian disruption of the Strait of Hormuz, to press Baghdad into a deeper energy partnership [1].

## Shiite Politics and Iranian Influence

Demographics are the challenge: Shiites represent a majority in Iraq, and the political principals of the Shiite community are tightly linked to the Iranian government [1]. Iran maintains considerable influence in Iraq despite its weakened position, underscored by the decision to route Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei's funeral procession through Najaf and Karbala, the two holiest cities for Shiite Islam in southern Iraq, before his final burial in Iran on July 9 [1].

Baghdad's new Shiite-led government has shown willingness to work closely with Washington to rein in Iraq's powerful Shiite militias, an issue that figured prominently in the July 14 meeting between Trump and Prime Minister Ali al-Zaidi [1]. Iran's weakening position is likely compelling Iraq's Shiite political forces to reassess Tehran's influence. Even if change comes, it will be slow and complicated, given the intense factionalism and varying degrees of dependence on Iran among competing actors [1].

## Turkish Strategic Calculus

From Ankara's perspective, it is not necessary to displace Iranian influence entirely; the emergence of even a handful of Shiite factions seeking to balance between Iran and Turkey would constitute a meaningful strategic gain [1]. A Sunni-dominated government in Syria could aid the political revival of the Sunni minority concentrated in western Iraq, extending through the north and center of the country [1].

Turkey faces a different kind of competition in Iraq than in Lebanon: unlike Lebanon, Iraq has the benefit of a direct Turkish border, enabling more direct leverage [1].

# Analysis

Iraq is the emerging pivot point of the post-Iran-war Middle East, the principal arena where Turkish and Iranian interests collide as Tehran's regional power wanes. The Shiite demographic reality creates a structural pull toward Iran that Turkey cannot fully overcome, but Ankara's geographic proximity, Kurdish relationships, and economic leverage provide countervailing tools. The Khamenei funeral procession through Najaf and Karbala demonstrated that Iran's cultural and religious influence in Iraq remains profound even as its political and military power declines.

Iraq's trajectory is therefore central to the evolving balance of power in the [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html).
