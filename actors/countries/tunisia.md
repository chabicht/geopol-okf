# Background

Tunisia is a North African Mediterranean state whose 2011 revolution made it the Arab world's democratic beacon. The [EU]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/eu.html) and its member states — particularly [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html) and France — built substantial soft power through hundreds of projects and development partnerships. Europe remains by far Tunisia's most important economic partner: it accounts for 85% of foreign direct investment, provides budget support, and finances numerous projects through grants. Chinese engagement, by contrast, is largely credit-based and adds to Tunisia's already strained public finances [1].

# Current Situation

## Saied's Authoritarian Consolidation

President Kais Saied has systematically dismantled democratic institutions since 2021. Dozens of opposition figures have been accused of conspiring against the state with foreign support and sentenced to lengthy prison terms. Saied frequently alludes to hidden networks seeking to destabilize Tunisia and draws on anti-Western, sovereignty-centred sentiment to justify his grip on power [1].

The president's authoritarian control extends to the bureaucratic apparatus: ministries are constrained by fear of making a wrong move, and European diplomats struggle even to visit or inaugurate projects they are financing, as such visits require complicated administrative procedures and usually exclude the press. Tunisian officials and media appear less hesitant to publicly associate with Chinese projects [1].

## The Visibility Dilemma

A striking mismatch exists between European engagement and visibility:

- The "Chinese Bridge" under construction in Bizerte is financed by the European Investment Bank but is universally known in Tunisia as a Chinese project — built by a Chinese company, presented by the Chinese ambassador, with no European attribution visible to the public [1].
- Two large water reservoirs expanded thanks to German-funded dam extensions are virtually unknown. Wastewater treatment plants built with German grants go unrecognised. France's donation of 165 buses in 2025 went largely unnoticed. By contrast, the arrival of 461 Chinese buses generated extensive media coverage and official fanfare. Tunisia's second Confucius Institute opened in May 2026 to considerable attention [1].

The visibility deficit stems from several factors:

- **Fear of meddling accusations**: European policy-makers have been reluctant to publicise engagement, fearing backlash from Saied's sovereignty-centred rhetoric. The EU Delegation can no longer freely communicate on joint projects or hold town hall meetings as it did before 2021.
- **Migration diplomacy**: The 2023 EU-Tunisia MoU (up to €1 billion, primarily for migration cooperation) was poorly received by civil society. Saied returned €60 million, calling it insufficient and declaring sovereignty was not for sale. Significant portions of EU promised aid remain delayed or undisbursed [1].
- **Gaza war strain**: European politicians' uncritical backing of Israeli military action boosted Saied's populist rhetoric. Criticism of "European double standards" on Ukraine vs. Gaza resonated strongly. Germany faced calls for its ambassador's expulsion. Europe's popularity reached a low point in 2024 [1].
- **Authoritarian constraints**: Saied summoned the EU Ambassador in late 2025 for meeting with the UGTT trade union. European officials retreat to safer, diffuse initiatives like ELMED (undersea power cable to Italy) and the Pact for the Mediterranean, where visibility is less politically sensitive [1].

## China's Soft Power Advance

[China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) has gained in visibility and popularity in Tunisia while Europe has struggled with both. China has no reason to downplay its engagement, whereas EU member states have become increasingly cautious. The result is that European-funded infrastructure bankrolls major contracts awarded to Chinese companies in Europe's neighbourhood — the Bizerte bridge being the emblematic case [1].

# Analysis

Europe's dilemma is structural: it wants its cooperation to generate visibility and soft power, but political conditions in Tunisia make publicising engagement risky. The consequence is that China benefits from European financing without Europe gaining the diplomatic credit [1].

Isabelle Werenfels (SWP) argues that Europe should not hesitate to showcase infrastructure cooperation that brings tangible benefits. The path forward involves: highlighting concrete results rather than lofty statements; creative communication strategies (diaspora actors, humour, social media); honesty about European interests (especially migration); and acknowledging past errors. Failure to address the visibility gap inadvertently fuels right-wing populist narratives in Europe questioning the value of foreign aid [1].
