# Background

Indonesia is the largest economy in Southeast Asia and the most populous Muslim-majority country in the world, with approximately 280 million people. Its archipelagic geography controls critical maritime straits — the Strait of Malacca, Sunda Strait, Lombok Strait, and Makassar Strait — through which a significant portion of global trade and energy shipments transit. Indonesia is the de facto leader of [ASEAN]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/asean.html) and a pivotal actor in the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html) strategic competition between the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) and [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html).

# Current Situation

## Emerging Asian Alliance Network

- In July 2026, Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi visited Indonesia as part of a broader Asian tour that also included Australia and New Zealand, reaching agreements on economic and defense cooperation [1].
- [George Friedman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) argues that this emerging coalition — [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html), [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html), [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html), Indonesia, New Zealand, and [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html) — is far more significant than the Quadrilateral Security Dialogue (Quad) because it has an economic dimension and includes Indonesia, which could further weaken and potentially limit China's maritime access to Europe and the [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) [1].
- The coalition would make China dependent on a trans-Pacific route through the Panama Canal or around South America for its most critical trade routes [1].

## ASEAN Energy Leadership

- Indonesia has proposed an ASEAN-wide oil storage hub to reduce the region's structural vulnerability to Middle East energy shocks [2].
- The initiative frames energy as a geoeconomic security issue tied to trade resilience and industrial continuity, integrated into the ASEAN 2045 framework alongside digital infrastructure and manufacturing diversification [2].
- The ASEAN Power Grid and cross-border electricity trade, with Laos as a regional electricity exporter, illustrate a broader push toward integrated energy systems [2].

## Strategic Position

- Indonesia's geographic position — controlling key straits between the Indian Ocean and the Pacific — makes it indispensable to any maritime containment strategy against China.
- The country's participation in Modi's emerging coalition adds a naval dimension that the Quad alone lacks, potentially enabling a maritime barrier from Japan through Indonesia to Australia [1].
- Indonesia is projected to become part of the fourth-largest economy by 2030, as part of ASEAN's 684 million people, giving it growing economic weight in [great power competition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/great-power-competition.html) [3].

## Deepening Regional Security Cooperation

[Victoria Herczegh]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) documents Indonesia's accelerating defense cooperation across the emerging regional security architecture. Indonesia has strengthened defense ties with [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) through a new defense cooperation agreement, while simultaneously deepening its security partnership with [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html) under the Jakarta Treaty. With [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html), Indonesia established a comprehensive strategic partnership and participates in joint maritime law enforcement training involving Indonesia, Vietnam, the [Philippines]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/philippines.html), and the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html). Expanding coast guard cooperation with Vietnam and Indonesia completes a gradual but unmistakable movement toward greater operational coordination among key ASEAN states [4].

Individually, these agreements may appear incremental, but collectively they reveal an unprecedented frequency of defense agreements, exercises, strategic affirmations, and institutional mechanisms emerging since early spring 2026. Indonesia's role as de facto ASEAN leader — combined with its geographic control of the maritime straits — places it at the southern anchor of an emerging regional security network that rests on US influence but no longer depends on direct US leadership [4].

## Forward Unmanned-System Repair and the Archipelagic Passage Problem

Indonesia's commercial and state shipyards — Surabaya and Daya Radar Utama — are identified by Dan Justice ([War on the Rocks]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/war-on-the-rocks.html)) as candidate nodes in a distributed network for sustaining the U.S. Navy's unmanned fleet. They offer the dry docks and crane lift capacity necessary for maintaining medium and large unmanned vehicles. As with [Vietnam]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/vietnam.html)'s Ba Son and Ha Long, the model would route through commercial yards rather than U.S.-owned infrastructure, fitting regional political constraints while providing resilience against the vulnerability of large, static bases [5].

The deeper Indonesian dimension is legal. The rights of archipelagic passage are critical given the number of archipelagic states in the Pacific, and the nuanced classification distinctions of unmanned systems may prevent their transit through Indonesian waters. The United States asserts its unmanned systems have the same sovereign immunity as manned warships, but other nations disagree. If states do not recognize unmanned systems as warships, they may attempt to enforce regulations such as compulsory pilotage. Lacking an agreed legal regime, the Navy risks unmanned systems traversing long, wasteful routes — and alienating existing and potential partner nations. Indonesia's control of the Strait of Malacca, Sunda Strait, Lombok Strait, and Makassar Strait makes its interpretation of these rules a strategic variable for any distributed unmanned-sustainment architecture [5].

# Analysis

Indonesia's strategic value lies in its geography: control of the maritime straits that connect the Indian Ocean to the Pacific makes it the southern anchor of any Asian containment architecture.

Modi's Asia tour elevated Indonesia from an ASEAN leader to a participant in a broader Asian security framework — a shift that Friedman frames as potentially "the beginning of a fundamental shift in the global system" [1].

The economic dimension of the emerging coalition — combining India's labor pool, Japan's technology, Australia's resources, and Indonesia's market size and geographic position — creates a bloc capable of both containing China militarily and competing with it economically.
