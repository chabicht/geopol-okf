# Background

The United States remains the world's dominant superpower, yet its position rests on a foundation of extraordinary energy throughput. With roughly 4% of the global population, the US consumes approximately 16% of world energy — a structural reality that underpins both its military reach and its economic primacy. This energy advantage, however, is increasingly contested as rival powers close the gap and as the global system itself fractures.

The US-led international order is under simultaneous pressure from multiple theaters: an unresolved [Cold War 2]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html) with the DragonBear axis of [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), a [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) that exposed deep military and diplomatic weaknesses, and an accelerating [AI military race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html) where technological advantage has not yet translated into operational integration.

# Current Situation

## Strategic Posture: Military Decline & Overstretch

The [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) revealed serious deficiencies in American military planning and execution. The US failed to protect key facilities, could not achieve its strategic objectives, and demonstrated poor operational planning throughout the campaign. Critically, there was no rally-round-the-flag effect — American society remained locked in a partisan death match, denying the wartime president the domestic political consolidation that typically accompanies foreign military operations. [Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documented that a CSIS report confirmed munitions depletion, and that the Trump administration's corruption has "percolated to the military" [2][8]. [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) made a personal decision to attack Iran that was not grounded in national interest; there was no meaningful political pressure compelling the strike. Despite occasional critical rhetoric, Trump has continued policies that effectively assist [Putin]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/putin.html), raising questions about the alignment of executive action with declared strategic priorities. By May 2026, O'Brien reported Trump was "desperate to exit" the war but unable to, with no good military options [9]. The operational specifics of the degradation — aircraft losses, equipment damage, base destruction — are examined in the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) conflict file.

The war accelerated an existing trend of diplomatic decline. The US alienated European allies, [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html), and [South Korea]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/south-korea.html). Gulf states increasingly view Washington as an unreliable security partner. The inability to maintain alliance cohesion during a major military campaign signals a structural weakening of the US-led coalition system. In Latin America, the US is attempting to reconstruct a hemispheric bloc through the Shield of the Americas framework, seeking to consolidate influence in its immediate neighborhood even as its global reach contracts [6].

## Strategic Posture: Retrenchment and Regional Recalibration

The Trump administration is publicly choosing to reduce American military exposure abroad — not ceding ground under compulsion, but ceding power by choice as the necessary implementation of a strategy that turns responsibility for regional defense over to regional powers. The approach is visible in both Trump's statements and policy papers such as the National Security Strategy, though the [Iran war]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) sits in tension with this stated posture. The US remains engaged economically and in terms of defense building and spending, even as military presence contracts [25].

In the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html), [Victoria Herczegh]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) documents the doctrinal label: Secretary of Defense Hegseth's 2026 Shangri-La Dialogue speech articulated a strategy of "flexible, practical realism" that seeks increased transactional burden-sharing from regional allies — including those that meet new defense spending standards and accelerate military transformation to take on more active roles in maintaining security. This stands in stark contrast to the traditional model in which the US acted as the sole security guarantor for the region. The Trump administration's reversion to the military designation "Pacific Command" (from "Indo-Pacific Command") is a performative narrowing of the regional posture. The result is an emerging regional security architecture in which principal allies — particularly Japan and Australia — assume more proactive leadership roles while the resulting network continues to rest on US influence but no longer depends on direct US leadership [25].

In the Middle East, [Andrew Davidson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) frames the US as facing a sustained-campaign cost problem in the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html). The military balance strongly favors the United States, but achieving Washington's objectives — suppressing Iranian threats, protecting commercial traffic, defending regional bases, denying Iran de facto authority over an international waterway — would require a sustained campaign whose costs rise with time. The US can absorb those costs far longer than [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), but a prolonged operation places growing demands on forces and munitions already committed across several regions. Further Iranian escalation would require the US to defend not only personnel and bases but also ports, shipping routes, and energy infrastructure throughout the Gulf — consuming air and missile defense interceptors, surveillance capacity, and naval assets at significant cost. Because the campaign also depends on continued Gulf access and cooperation, prolonged resource expenditure without restored passage or Iranian concessions becomes increasingly difficult to justify. The approaching US midterm elections sharpen this pressure, but the larger constraint is the cost of sustaining regional protection without a clear political result [26].

## Doctrine: The AI-Military Integration Gap

The United States holds a 3-6 month lead in AI model development, but trails [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) by years in military integration. The policy environment is characterized by a vacuum: Congress and the executive branch operate with friction, and no coherent national strategy has emerged for closing the integration gap. Beijing's coordinated, national-level approach contrasts sharply with Washington's fragmented response [3]. The Director of National Intelligence faces a set of strategic decisions: refocusing the ODNI, resourcing the intelligence community for AI and space domains, and adapting Cold War-era institutions to contemporary great-power competition [4].

[Adam Tooze]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/adam-tooze.html) frames the US AI boom as a world-transforming technology analogous to the atom bomb — potentially disrupting work, culture, and warfare on a scale that cannot be calculated. The key contrast is political economy: the mid-century transformative technologies (nuclear, rockets) were under state control (Oppenheimer, von Braun), whereas AI is driven by privately developed technology, private money, and the "unfettered egos of tech entrepreneurs" — the broligarchs of Gotham City. The BIS Annual Economic Report identifies the AI boom as endogenously generating a China-scale investment and credit boom, with the labor share of income potentially collapsing to 20% over the next forty years. Trillions of dollars are wagered on a vision of total disruption of familiar ways of living and working [18]. The [Cipher Brief]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-cipher-brief.html) underscores that the AI race will not be won by the best model but by the fastest military — a domain where China's integrated national approach currently holds the advantage [3].

## Doctrine: Military Mobility and Infrastructure Security

Retired Gen. Mike Minihan and Rear Adm. Mark Montgomery ([The Cipher Brief]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-cipher-brief.html)) argue that American military power is meaningless if it cannot move. Every war plan — from deterring aggression in [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html) to prevailing in the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html) — depends on the ability to maneuver people, equipment, fuel, information, and combat power faster than any adversary can react. Yet every deployment begins on commercial railroads, moves through civilian ports and airports, depends on privately owned communications networks, and increasingly relies on digital logistics systems that the Department of Defense does not own. This is America's strategic advantage and one of its greatest vulnerabilities [27].

[China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) does not need to destroy American combat power to gain an advantage — it simply needs to delay it. Every day equipment sits in a rail yard, every hour a port is offline, every aircraft waylaid by corrupted logistics data creates the friction an adversary seeks during the opening days of a conflict. For more than two years, US intelligence officials have warned that Chinese cyber operators are compromising hundreds of American transportation, communications, energy, and logistics systems, gaining persistent access so that Beijing can disrupt and destroy systems at the time of its choosing. [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html)'s cyberattacks against [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html)'s transportation, energy, and communications infrastructure reveal what this looks like in practice: modern wars are fought as much against the systems that sustain military power as against the military itself [27].

The policy prescriptions: harmonize cybersecurity standards across transportation sectors so operators spend more time improving security than satisfying overlapping regulations; provide cybersecurity grant programs for under-resourced transportation infrastructure operators (smaller ports, airports, and rail operators cannot reasonably defend themselves against nation-state adversaries without federal partnership); and begin exercising the way the US expects to fight — national, regional, and local exercises should assume degraded communications, cyberattacks against transportation systems, corrupted logistics data, and contested movement inside the United States. "Amateurs study tactics, professionals study logistics. The next war demands we take one step further. Victors study maneuver." If America cannot connect, it cannot maneuver; if it cannot move, it cannot fight [27]. This vulnerability connects directly to the broader [defense industrial resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html) agenda — the binding constraint is no longer kinetic reach but the energy and digital substrate that sustains it.

## Domestic Dynamics: OBBBA, SNAP Cuts, and the Reconciliation Drive

The OBBBA increased the child tax credit, but by less than an inflation adjustment would have; the benefits went mostly to the richest fifth of Americans, dropping to zero by the poorest fifth. Between the OBBBA's passage in July 2025 and March 2026, more than 4 million people lost access to the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program — at least a quarter of them children. Those losses will mount in 2027, after the midterm elections, when states must assume a much larger share of SNAP costs [28].

The Republican response to the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html)'s unpopularity has been a $95 billion budget framework — released July 15, 2026 — to provide another $73 billion in military funding for the war, a $12 billion bailout for farmers hurt by [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html)'s tariff wars, and $10 billion to enact aspects of the SAVE America voter-suppression measure that Trump has been unable to convince Congress to pass. ## Domestic Dynamics: The Powermaxxing Spiral and Billionaire Political Capture

## Domestic Dynamics: Antitrust Backsliding and AI Policy Capture

Krugman's interview with Lina Khan — who chaired the FTC under Biden and played an important role on the Mamdani transition team — extends the powermaxxing analysis into AI policy and antitrust. Khan frames the AI supply chain as a stack in which each layer (chips, hyperscalers, cloud infrastructure, models, apps and services) has economic properties that can easily tip toward monopoly through network effects. Allowing monopolization at one layer without rules such as common carriage or equal access can result in other layers becoming monopolized or distorted. The FTC had been investigating bottlenecks at the chip layer (Nvidia as gatekeeper) and the cloud layer (hyperscalers privileging their own AI products). The "interlocking directorates" pattern — Microsoft and OpenAI, Google and Amazon — raises questions about whether existing monopolists are gaining outsized control over competitively strategic decisions through opaque financial relationships [33].

Khan's February 2025 *Times* op-ed "Stop Worshiping the American Tech Giants" framed the choice starkly: paradigm-breaking innovations historically come from competitive markets, not from markets where gatekeepers decide who enters. The catalyst was DeepSeek — a Chinese AI firm whose model rivalled American sophistication with much greater efficiency. The conflict of interest is structural: the major cloud providers have their own cloud computing businesses, so they have incentives to favor AI models that are inefficient (consuming more compute) rather than efficient. The historical cautionary tale is Boeing — the late-1990s merger approval allowing Boeing to buy up its last US rival, McDonnell Douglas, led down a path where Boeing's planes started falling apart and people died. The Trump administration's AI policy embodies the national-champions risk: the CEOs of the major technology companies have made currying favor with the White House a top business priority, and there is a very serious risk of regulatory capture — rules favoring the biggest incumbents at the expense of startups and newer firms that may have better ideas. The empirical evidence on the Schumpeter-Arrow debate shows that monopolies can be good at incremental improvements but the breakthrough, paradigm-shifting innovations have come from outsiders and competitive markets. The fundamental breakthrough that made modern AI possible came from Google — which did nothing with it until people left and brought it to other places [33]. See [Tech Stack Diplomacy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/tech-stack-diplomacy.html) and [AI Military Race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html).

## Domestic Dynamics: The AI Backlash & Demographic Crunch

A dramatic shift in American public opinion on AI data centers has emerged as a new political dynamic. According to Heatmap polling, Americans went from being evenly split on local data center construction (43% support / 42% oppose in August 2025) to overwhelmingly opposed (21% support / 71% oppose) by spring 2026 — a 50-point swing that may be the fastest opinion shift in modern American politics [24]. The backlash has a strong NIMBY core: the top concerns are water use and electricity prices, which unite Americans across party and age lines. When data center projects are actually canceled, the reason is typically noise. Among younger opponents, suspicion of AI itself drives opposition. The movement draws on deeper anxieties: a few tech executives perceived as laying claim to the country's future, communities feeling powerless against forces they cannot control, and the sense that decades of computing innovation have left the world shallower rather than richer [24]. The political implications are significant. The backlash is underwater among all parties — Republicans, independents, and Democrats. Democrats are already running against AI data centers in midterms, benefiting from voter instinct to reach for Democrats when an industry appears out of control. However, the coalition fractures when moving beyond local concerns: Democrats care about environmental impact; Republicans do not. If the data center were powered by renewable energy, Democrats would support it and Republicans would oppose it. Bernie Sanders has proposed a national moratorium, though his rationale (existential AI risks) differs from the NIMBY concerns driving most opposition [24].

The demographic foundation compounds these tensions. The US has been below the replacement fertility rate of 2.1 children per woman for nearly two decades (1.6 in 2024). [Mexico]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/latin-america.html), the top provider of immigrant workers, has been below replacement for about a decade (1.9 in 2024). [Africa]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/africa.html)'s working-age population is projected to rise from ~850 million today to over 1.5 billion by 2050, accounting for 85% of the global workforce increase — positioning Africa as a sought-after source of workers for shrinking developed economies [20].

## Structural Foundations: Energy & the Dollar

[The Honest Sorcerer]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-honest-sorcerer.html) debunks the "energy independence" narrative: the US is a net crude oil importer. NGLs are not oil and cannot make diesel or jet fuel. US refineries need heavy crude from [Canada]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/canada.html), [Venezuela]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/latin-america.html), and [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html). During the Iran war, an armada of 121 empty tankers headed to the US to export refined fuels, draining the [SPR]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/spr-depletion-crisis.html) to approximately 349 million barrels — approaching 1983 lows. Post-peak shale is evident from declining rig counts [10]. Tooze identifies the US AI boom as a "K-shaped expansion" — one of four disrupters of the 2026 world economy. The boom is sucking in imports at a furious rate, but from [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html) and Mexico, not from China. The US-China bilateral trade truce is holding; the bilateral deficit has narrowed dramatically. The US has effectively excluded the leading edge of China's export drive with 100% tariffs on Chinese EVs, making China Shock 2.0 largely a Europe problem. Trump is dismantling the IRA's energy transition subsidies even as the AI boom generates unprecedented asset price inflation [12].

Tooze argues that the US dollar system has undergone five distinct metamorphoses, each with its own political economy. The current regime — Dollar #5, the "profit dollar" — is sustained not by official reserve accumulation by foreign central banks (as in Bretton Woods 2.0, ~2000-2015) but by private capital inflows attracted by the dominance of US financial markets. America's unequal, K-shaped economy is the "honeypot of global capitalism." Since 2015, Beijing has imposed tight capital controls trapping $50-60 trillion; America's deficits are financed by inflows from Europe, South Korea, Taiwan, and Japan — not China. The dollar is no longer a "reserve currency" in the traditional sense but a promise of liquidity and a vehicle for unfettered capital accumulation. See [Monetary System Transition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/monetary-system-transition.html) [23]. The implication for US power: the current dollar regime depends on the attractiveness of US private assets and the institutional stability that underwrites them. If Trump's institutional degradation undermines that attractiveness, the "profit dollar" could metamorphose again — but not into a post-dollar regime, rather another mutation of the dollar system. The dedollarization trend visible in reserve data captures the shift from Dollar #4 to #5 (official reserves declining as private inflows take over) rather than a flight from the dollar per se [23].

## Industrial Base: Rare Earths, EV, Defense Mobilization

[George Friedman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) reports that the US has launched a program for funding the exploration and processing of heavy rare earth minerals within its own territory. Deposits exist in California and Texas. The government will provide funding to private companies, but they are not state-run and will need additional capital. The Defense Department will likely be the first customer. The driver is China's near-monopoly over heavy rare earth processing, which effectively constitutes an embargo — heavy rare earths are required for military applications including magnets and lasers that function in extreme heat. The US is late to the party but, it seems, not too late. See [Critical Minerals]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/critical-minerals.html) [21].

The US response to China's [EV dominance]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ev-competition.html) is more focused on security risks than market-driven policies. With the exception of [Tesla]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/tesla.html), US companies have been reluctant to ramp up EV production rapidly, focusing on profitable trucks and SUVs. Policy volatility compounds the problem — battery plants, mines, and refineries require long investment horizons, while tax credits and consumer-side support have been inconsistent. Washington has stronger bipartisan agreement to reduce Chinese imports than to curb EV adoption itself, but lacks the scale and innovation to effectively compete with China's whole-process ecosystem. Mining companies, refiners, battery recyclers, and logistics providers stand to benefit from the policy push [21].

The US is part of a structural defense spending boom — global military expenditures rose from $2.1 trillion in 2021 to nearly $2.9 trillion in 2025. The wars in [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) and the Middle East exposed the limits of lean inventories and slow weapons production. [NATO]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/organizations/nato.html)'s 5% spending target (3.5% core defense + 1.5% security-related investment by 2035) gives industry a longer demand signal. The most strategically valuable firms are those controlling capacity governments cannot quickly recreate: critical minerals, semiconductors, specialty metals, nuclear-grade components, and certified production capacity. See [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html) [21].

# Analysis

The United States is experiencing a compounding crisis: military overstretch, diplomatic isolation, domestic political dysfunction, and a technology race where its lead is narrow and poorly leveraged. The [global system rupture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) is not merely happening to the US — it is being accelerated by American decisions that diverge from strategic logic. The [energy and resource]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/energy-and-resources.html) foundation of US power remains robust in absolute terms but is diminishing relative to rivals, particularly China, which uses nearly twice as much energy and houses four times the population.

Tooze identifies two of his four disrupters as American: Trump's trade policy rampage and US fiscal incontinence. The AI boom — the third disrupter — is centered in the US but its benefits are K-shaped, enriching a tiny elite while the institutional fabric degrades. The fourth disrupter, China's economic gear-shift, interacts with the other three to produce a world economy in which "two powerful forces are at work in the same world, at the same time" but are "to a degree separated and disconnected" [12][13].

## The Consequence-Free World: Colby's Middle-Powers Panic

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documents two extraordinary outbursts from the US administration in mid-July 2026 that exemplify the strategic cost of the "consequence-free world" the US has chosen to inhabit. Trump's July 11 tweet threatening to "completely decimate and destroy all areas of Iran" — "PRAISE BE TO ALLAH" — should Iran attempt to assassinate him is juxtaposed against Trump's own celebration of the assassination of Khamenei at the start of the war. O'Brien reads the thread as panic: the administration is realizing that its own Europe-withdrawal policies have produced the strategic autonomy it now pleads with Europeans not to pursue. The thread insults Europeans as too weak to act independently while simultaneously threatening and pleading with them not to do so — a self-destroying case. It is finally dawning on the administration that actions have consequences. ## Oligarchic Media Consolidation

[Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html) documents the systematic takeover of US media institutions by the wealthiest Americans — a process he frames as the fundamental story of the current era. The Ellison family acquired CBS (hiring Bari Weiss to reshape its orientation) and is attempting a Warner/CNN takeover; [Musk]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) seized Twitter/X and tilted its algorithm toward right-wing content; Zuckerberg is tilting Facebook; and Bezos shifted the *Washington Post*'s editorial policy hard right while eviscerating its news division. ## The AI Bubble and US Tech-Stock Risk

Ed Zitron (*Where's Your Ed At*) argues that the US stock market and, to a significant extent, the global economy rest on the continued solvency of OpenAI — a company with no viable business model that accounts for $748 billion of the remaining performance obligations of Microsoft, Amazon, and Oracle. The "AI bubble" is not the product of return on investment but of a cult-like psychosis that infected some of the most powerful and wealthy individuals and institutions, producing the greatest capital misallocation in history. Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) sank more than a trillion dollars into AI capex without ever showing a single dollar of profit, justifying literally anyone investing in AI data centers under the logic that "the largest companies in the world couldn't be wrong" — even though the reason they were doing so was to expand capacity for OpenAI and Anthropic, whom the hyperscalers themselves incubated. Zitron's thesis: if OpenAI collapses — which he considers an inevitability given its loss-laden economics, doomed subscriptions, pathetic advertising revenue, and the absence of measurable AI ROI — it will be the "Lehman Brothers of the AI bubble," seizing up AI data center debt, pulling back compute demand, and calling into question the rationality of investing in any AI startup. The Bank of England governor Andrew Bailey warned that the bursting of the AI bubble would have effects significant enough to change British monetary policy [35].

The downstream consumer effect Zitron documents is a "memory crisis": Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron (the DRAM triopoly controlling 90%+ of the world's memory) diverted manufacturing supply from consumer RAM to high-bandwidth memory for AI GPUs, jacking up the price of laptops, smartphones, consoles, and consumer electronics. Micron posted 84.9% gross margins in Q3 2026. SK Hynix's chairman warned the shortage would last until 2030; a Bank of America report said SK Hynix may add only a sixth of its planned capacity by 2028. The price of consumer electronics will be inflated for the foreseeable future, even if the AI bubble bursts. See [AI Military Race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html) and [Tech Stack Diplomacy]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/tech-stack-diplomacy.html) [35].

## The Hormuz Political Clock: Midterms and the Suppression Problem

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) argues (July 20, 2026) that America is unlikely to reopen the [Strait of Hormuz]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) before the November midterm elections. He draws a critical distinction between tactical and strategic victory: tactical success measures military performance — launchers destroyed, attacks declined — while strategic success requires the political objective of restoring normal commercial shipping. The judge of strategic victory is not the Pentagon but the market: shipping companies, insurers, and commodity traders must conclude that transiting Hormuz is routine again. Only 50–100 drones or missiles are needed to swarm ships, and [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) possesses tens of thousands; a single successful strike can reverse months of restored confidence [36].

For the United States, the suppression problem is a force-allocation trap: guaranteeing passage requires persistent coverage of a long waterway against dispersed, cheap launch systems, so even high attrition rates do not free scarce air-defense, intelligence, and strike capacity for other theaters [36]. The detailed geography, inventories, and operational requirements are kept in [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html).

## The $1.5 Trillion Defense Budget: Budget-Driven, Not Strategy-Driven

A Brookings Institution panel convened in July 2026 to assess the [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) administration's FY 2027 defense request — roughly $1.15 trillion in baseline spending plus $350 billion in reconciliation and a $67 billion supplemental, combining toward a $1.5 trillion headline — produced a devastating consensus: the budget was constructed backward. Todd Harrison (AEI) argued that the number was set first — an arbitrary 5%-of-GDP target declared on Truth Social — and the Pentagon was then told to fill it at the last minute. The tell is the absence of hard tradeoffs: the request buys fourth-generation F-15s, fifth-generation F-35s, and sixth-generation fighters simultaneously, a sure sign that no prioritization occurred. Harrison flagged a $54 billion line item in the Defense Autonomous Warfare Group (DAWG) — 243 times the prior year's appropriation, exceeding the entire Marine Corps budget — with almost no program detail: no quantities, no specific drone types, no articulated concept of use. The battleship program drew equal scorn: a clean-sheet design starting in FY 2027, lead-ship procurement in FY 2028, and full-rate production at one per year by FY 2031 — a timeline Harrison said does not pass the laugh test, likely setting up the Navy to spend billions chasing a presidential whim before canceling it. He likened the need for congressional budget process reform to the early 1970s, when the system last required fundamental restructuring [38].

Mara Karlin (SAIS) highlighted the strategic incoherence. The Trump National Defense Strategy breaks with decades of bipartisan threat consensus by prioritizing the Western Hemisphere and alleged narco-terrorists, circumscribing China to the first island chain, downgrading Europe, and barely mentioning the Middle East — even as the administration launched the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html). The paradox is stark: if the strategy genuinely centers on the Western Hemisphere and a narrow China focus, it could justify a quite small defense budget — not $1.5 trillion at World War levels. The number is not merited by the strategy, and the strategy is not merited by the number [38].

David Wessel (Brookings) framed the fiscal dimension: the administration is proposing record defense spending against an unsustainable fiscal trajectory while avoiding the tradeoffs — spending cuts elsewhere or tax increases — that budgeting demands. Equally troubling, Wessel argued, is the erosion of public trust in the military. The nonpartisan character of the armed forces — the foundation of democratic civilian control — is being degraded by Secretary Hegseth's personnel decisions, which are politicizing the officer corps in ways that compound the institutional damage documented throughout this concept [38]. See [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html) and [Donald Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html).

## Agentic AI and the DoD Software Acquisition Paradigm

Jacob Green argues that the "Make-or-Buy line" in DoD software acquisition has shifted fundamentally with the arrival of agentic AI. The secretary of defense's November 2025 commercial-first acquisition reform directive assumed a world where building custom software was slow and expensive, making commercial purchase the default. Green's empirical demonstration overturns that premise: using an accredited agentic AI development environment, he built a 60,000-page legal-regulatory knowledge base wrapped in a Model Context Protocol (MCP) server — 9,500 lines of code — in under 20 hours of focused work at roughly $1,700 in model inference costs. A traditional COCOMO estimate for the same deliverable ran 6,000–7,000 engineering hours (12–13 months with a four-person team) at $840,000–$1.2 million through a comparable Army small business prototype award. Agentic AI is turning elite coding into a ubiquitous commodity delivered at previously unfathomable speed for pennies on the dollar [39].

The implication is not that DoD should abandon commercial procurement but that it should buy the *infrastructure layer* and *build the application layer* in-house. Green's prescription: procure cloud infrastructure, agentic harnesses, and model access commercially; then empower existing DoD development organizations — Army Software Factory, Science and Technology Centers, program offices — to build mission-specific applications with full government data ownership and no recurring licensing. The financial logic is stark: $178 billion in venture funding flowed to top-tier AI model companies in Q1 2026 alone — over three times the $58.5 billion DoD requested for the entire FY 2027 AI and CJADC2 portfolio. The department cannot match industry's model development cadence and should not try; it should buy access to the best models and redirect its resources toward building applications on top of them [39].

Green identifies three architectural enablers. First, AI gateway services provide a single model-agnostic endpoint, allowing users to select the best model for each task while consolidating monitoring, compliance, and pricing — a deliberate hedge against the volatile cadence of model capability growth. Second, the Model Context Protocol — an open-source standard adopted industry-wide, including by major data platform vendors — enables plug-and-play data access: DoD can build MCP servers around its authoritative data sources, allowing any compliant agentic application to retrieve data in real time without costly system-to-system integrations. This realizes the long-sought Data Mesh vision, where data lives in its functional domain system and is accessible as a product through agent-to-agent interactions — divesting the costly and duplicative back ends of monolithic platforms (the $2.3 billion earmarked for Maven Smart System and Joint Fires Network in FY 2027 alone illustrates the recurring licensing burden). Third, pre-accredited development and production environments with streamlined onboarding would let internal development teams deliver value on day one rather than after a 6-to-18-month authorization wait, compressing timelines more than any vendor-side reform [39].

Green also proposes introducing internal market forces: reimbursable funding models and a central system where program offices submit work requests that DoD development organizations bid on, incentivizing efficient value creation through cross-service competition without mandated personnel reductions. Open-source tools — Google's Gemma, Hermes Agent, Open Code — offer additional capability with no licensing costs, subject to the same code-scanning and red-teaming scrutiny applied to any software. The broader strategic context connects directly to the [AI Military Race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html): the US lead in AI model development is real but narrow, and Green's framework suggests that the integration gap with [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) could be closed not by building better models but by building better applications faster and cheaper than the current acquisition system permits. See [AI Military Race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html) and [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html) [39].

## The Bravery Myth and Wrong Military Priorities

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) argues that the US military is culturally preparing for failure by celebrating the qualities that do not win wars while neglecting those that do. The historical record is unambiguous: the bravest soldiers of World War II — the Japanese — lost. Italian soldiers were brave but underequipped. US and British soldiers, who had the highest survival rates, prevailed through superior logistics, technology, and adaptability, not superior valor. Wars are won by the smartest, most adaptable, best-equipped soldiers who believe they are not being sacrificed for nothing. Yet the Department of War under Secretary Hegseth is stressing all the wrong things — romantic sacrifice narratives instead of education, technological sophistication, and thoughtfulness [43].

The most emblematic manifestation is Hegseth's announcement of testosterone screening for soldiers — what O'Brien calls the "High-T Department of War." The policy equates warfighting capacity with testosterone levels, despite the medical reality that testosterone boosting is linked to infertility. Hegseth has long emphasized hyper-masculine bravery as a military virtue, citing E.B. Sledge's Pacific campaign accounts on the 2024 Kelly podcast and in speeches to generals. The institutional prioritization of testosterone and sacrifice rhetoric over the logistics, technology, and education that actually win wars is not merely a cultural eccentricity — it is a component of the broader strategic incoherence that has produced the [Iran war]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) debacle, the $1.5 trillion budget without a strategy, and the AI-military integration gap [43]. See [Revolution in Military Affairs]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/revolution-in-military-affairs.html) and [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html).

## Munitions Depletion, Institutional Hollowing, and the China Window

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documents a compounding crisis of munitions depletion and institutional breakdown as the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) enters its fifth month of renewed operations. THAAD interceptor stocks, already low before the latest round of strikes, were 53–80% expended by the April ceasefire. Patriot PAC-3/MSE stocks were 45–61% expended over the same period. An anonymous US official told O'Brien: "We do not have enough to safely sustain operations, and I don't think the White House is aware of that." The Pentagon is panicking, seeking billions from Congress — but Congress remains inert. The War Powers Act has not been invoked 144 days into the war, no press briefings are being held, and CNN reported on the Pentagon actively suppressing press coverage [44].

The China implications are severe. Combined Patriot and THAAD inventories stand at approximately 1,500 interceptors. With the standard two-interceptors-per-target doctrine, that yields perhaps 1,000 successful intercepts — against a [Chinese]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) missile arsenal estimated at roughly 3,500. O'Brien now assesses that the US might not win even the opening battles of a China war after the Iran-era depletion. US bases in Guam and the Marianas could be devastated at the start of a Pacific conflict. A "short war scenario" — in which the US runs out of systems and suffers such high losses it cannot continue — has become plausible. The war of choice against Iran is degrading readiness for the primary great-power competitor in real time [44]. See [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), [Taiwan Standoff]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html), and [Great Power Competition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/great-power-competition.html).

## The Ground Power Dilemma: Pickaxe Mountain and Diminishing Returns

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) identifies a new strategic problem facing the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) in his Breaking Analysis #7 (July 21, 2026): [Israeli]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) intelligence reports that [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) has moved thousands of uranium-enrichment centrifuges into tunnels beneath Pickaxe Mountain near Natanz, buried 300–450 feet under solid rock — beyond the reach of existing conventional bunker-busting munitions. The US and Israeli bombing campaign achieved tactical successes throughout the war, but Iran responded by moving enrichment deeper underground. Airpower did not solve the nuclear problem; it transformed it [45].

The facility's depth creates what Pape calls the Ground Power Dilemma. If Washington concludes that Pickaxe Mountain has become Iran's protected nuclear sanctuary, the choice narrows to two unpalatable options: tolerate a hardened nuclear program, or accept far greater risks of operations beyond an air campaign — including ground operations or nuclear options. As Iran's nuclear assets become harder to destroy, airstrikes yield diminishing strategic returns while pressure for more dangerous military options grows. This is the classic Escalation Trap mechanics: tactical success that deepens strategic entrapment [45].

Pape draws on the 2003 Iraq WMD precedent to identify a fear-driven escalation logic. Leaders escalate based on what they believe adversaries are capable of, not what later proves true. If Washington believes Pickaxe Mountain has made Iran's nuclear program invulnerable, that belief alone creates pressure for broader military action regardless of what inspectors might discover. Uncertainty favors escalation — the absence of verifiable information generates its own coercive momentum. The political clock linkage intensifies the trap: if the US cannot reopen [Hormuz]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) before the November midterms, pressure for broader escalation including attacks on deeply buried nuclear facilities will grow [45]. See [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) and [Escalation Theory Critique]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/escalation-theory-critique.html).

## The US-Japan Alliance and Indo-Pacific Force Posture

The US-Japan alliance is adapting to a new form of sustained short-of-war pressure from [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) that falls below the threshold of armed conflict but steadily erodes Japan's security environment. Aizawa identifies the December 2025 joint strategic bomber flight as a turning point: timed to China's political interests, with a coercive route extending toward Tokyo, and involving nuclear-capable platforms. The alliance has responded with bilateral exercises — two B-52s with six JASDF fighters in December 2025, and four B-52s with eleven JASDF fighters in February 2026 — but Aizawa argues that these represent deterrence responses to what is increasingly a compellence problem. The boundary between deterrence and compellence has blurred: China can now apply military pressure with Russian support without crossing the armed conflict threshold, and the alliance's current framework — built around Flexible Deterrent Options — does not adequately address this gray zone. Aizawa calls for shared threat perceptions, reaffirmed common interests, and examination of the full range of response approaches including compellence [46].

Ueki documents how the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) has directly undermined US force posture in the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html), creating tensions within the alliance. The 31st Marine Expeditionary Unit and amphibious warship USS Tripoli were deployed from US Forces Japan to the Middle East, and Marine Commandant General Eric Smith testified that the gap in the Marine Corps' ability to deter adversaries in the Indo-Pacific remains unfilled — prompting public and legislative concern in [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html). US munitions depletion from the Iran war — approximately half of key munitions used in 39 days — has cascading effects on allied readiness. Tomahawk deliveries to Japan have been delayed by up to four years, impeding Japan's plans to rapidly strengthen its counterstrike capability. The structural problem is an offense-defense cost imbalance: 1,900 to 3,000 interceptors consumed in less than two weeks of the Iran war against a production capacity of 600 Patriot PAC-3 per year, while [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) dominates drone production through companies like DJI (over 70 percent market share). The Hegseth-Koizumi Operation Supercharge agreement in May 2026 — accelerating codevelopment and coproduction of SM-3 Block IIA and AMRAAM missiles — represents a structural shift toward allied industrial-base cooperation, but the production timelines mean near-term gaps will persist [47]. See [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html), [Taiwan Standoff]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html), and [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html).
