# Background

Ukraine has been at war with [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) since 2022, developing into one of the most technologically consequential conflicts of the modern era. Under [Zelensky]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/zelensky.html), Ukraine has evolved from a post-Soviet state with limited military capability into a formidable drone warfare innovator and long-range strike practitioner. The [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) remains the central strategic commitment defining Ukrainian identity, doctrine, and external relationships.

# Current Situation

## Doctrine: Deep-Strike & Drone Innovation as Strategic Culture

Ukraine's strategic approach demonstrates how a numerically inferior force can impose disproportionate costs through technological innovation, deep strike capability, and astute exploitation of adversary vulnerabilities. Drones are changing the character of warfare, and Ukrainians are adjusting better than Russians — fighting with fewer losses through novel tactics and technological adaptation. The lessons from the Ukrainian theater are directly relevant to understanding the broader [drone warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) revolution.

Dr. Jack Watling (RUSI) describes Ukraine's transformation as driven by civil society and a burgeoning private defense industry that rapidly iterates equipment based on frontline feedback. The critical lesson Watling draws is that engaging civil society honestly — not shielding the public from hard trade-offs — creates the political freedom to solve defense problems. The vulnerability of globalized defense supply chains (Chinese magnets, Russian titanium, Indian cotton) highlights the need for Western governments to define "sufficient" rather than perfect equipment standards [4].

[CSIS]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/csis.html) documents Ukraine's fielding of the Hornet — a one-way autonomous attack drone costing ~$6,000 with a range of up to 150 km. The Hornet uses AI to analyze live video feeds, identify legitimate targets, detect Russian decoys based on geometry, surface texture, and thermal signature, and strike targets during the terminal phase using onboard AI without requiring a satellite connection — making it resistant to Russian electronic warfare. Over 90% of Russian casualties are now from drone attacks, a statistic that underscores how deeply unmanned systems have permeated Ukrainian operational doctrine [5]. The Crimea campaign and the Belarus pressure campaign are complementary efforts to compress Russia's operational depth, applying this doctrine across geographic domains.

## Force Posture & Transformation

Ukraine has undertaken one of the most rapid force transformations in modern military history. Watling describes the evolution from an underfunded Soviet-legacy force to a 960,000-person defense force integrating UAVs as a universal fires platform — a scale of expansion achieved through civil society mobilization rather than top-down conscription alone [4]. The private defense industry that emerged around this expansion iterates equipment at speeds impossible under traditional procurement cycles, with frontline feedback loops directly shaping production priorities.

Operationally, Ukraine has matured its planning architecture to match its technological innovation. The corps structure and the Delta battlefield management system represent a deliberate institutional modernization, enabling the intermediate-range strike campaign that uses AI-enabled Hornet drones to degrade Russian ground lines of communication at 100-160km depth, alongside SEAD/DEAD operations and tactical drone supremacy. The combination has driven Russian advance rates to approximately 3 sq km/day — a tempo among the slowest in any war over the last century [3].

## Relationships: Belarus Leverage

Ukraine has successfully pressured [Belarus]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/belarus.html) to halt radio guidance equipment that was helping Russia direct long-range strikes. [Lukashenka]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/lukashenka.html) bent to Ukrainian threats — an outcome that reveals the credibility of Ukrainian leverage. Ukrainian intelligence has listed every plant in Belarus working for Russia: components for armored vehicles, missile systems, and fuel. Gasoline supplies from Belarus to Russia increased 13x and diesel 3x in January-May versus the same period last year, making Belarus a critical supply node that Ukraine is now pressuring. Minsk's proximity to Kyiv — much closer than Kyiv is to Crimea — means Belarus-based assets are exposed to Ukrainian retaliation in ways that Russian rear areas are not [2].

## Relationships: US Aid Cutoff & Western Dependency

Ukraine's war effort depends structurally on Western military assistance, and the rupture in that relationship has become a defining strategic constraint. [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) ended all US military aid to Ukraine in 2025, and his vague Patriot "license" pledge will take years to materialize — gifting Putin 1-3 winters of unrestricted bombardment. The dependency on American interceptors and munitions, combined with the political unreliability of the supplier, has forced Ukraine to seek alternative sourcing paths and accelerated domestic production. Japan, after years of licensing, produces only 30 PAC-3 interceptors annually — a trickle insufficient to replace expenditure rates [6]. The air defense crisis produced by this dependency is examined in the [Ukraine Air Defense Crisis 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/ukraine-air-defense-crisis-2026.html) event file.

## Ongoing War: Summary Reference

The [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) continues to define Ukraine's existence as a state. Ukraine is executing parallel campaigns to isolate [Crimea]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/crimea-isolation-campaign.html), deny Russia use of the [Sea of Azov]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/sea-of-azov-campaign.html), and conduct deep long-range strikes against Russian refining and military-industrial infrastructure — reaching as far as Omsk, 2,500 km from the border. The operational narratives, escalation dynamics, and timelines of these campaigns belong in the conflict file. Ukraine's posture — its doctrine, force structure, and relationships — is what this actor concept owns.
