# Background

Sudan is a large country in northeastern Africa with a strategically significant coastline along the Red Sea. The country has been devastated by civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), a conflict that has become an arena for regional competition. [1]

# Current Situation

## Civil War

The conflict between the SAF and RSF has created a major source of instability in the Red Sea basin. Outside actors back rival factions in pursuit of influence over Sudan's political future, security architecture, and access to maritime infrastructure. A prolonged war or consolidation of power by one faction could create opportunities for external powers to establish military, economic, or logistical footholds along Sudan's coast. [1]

## Regional Proxy Arena

Sudan's civil war intersects with broader Red Sea competition involving [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html), which supports the SAF, and the [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html), which backs the RSF, alongside other regional actors. [Ethiopia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ethiopia.html) is aligned with the UAE and RSF, placing it on one side of the proxy conflict. [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/egypt.html) has sought to influence the trajectory of Sudan's civil war, reflecting Cairo's effort to reassert its regional role. The conflict's outcome will shape the strategic balance along the Red Sea coastline. [1]

## Strategic Coastline

Sudan possesses a strategically significant coastline along the Red Sea. Control of coastal access makes Sudan a prize for external powers seeking maritime infrastructure footholds. The instability creates both risk and opportunity for the broader [Red Sea]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html) strategic landscape. [1]

# Analysis

Sudan's civil war transforms it from a peripheral state into a contested arena in the broader Red Sea strategic competition. The conflict illustrates how local wars become enmeshed in interstate rivalries when strategic coastline and maritime access are at stake. Sudan's position connects the security dynamics of [Africa]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/africa.html) and the [Middle East]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html), making the war's trajectory consequential beyond Sudan itself.
