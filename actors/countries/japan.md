# Background

Japan is a key node in the US-led alliance architecture in the [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html), with a constitutionally constrained but increasingly capable military. Its geographic position — astride the first island chain — makes it a critical actor in any contingency involving [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) and [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html).

# Current Situation

## Tensions with Beijing

Tensions with Beijing have intensified since PM Takaichi suggested that Tokyo's military could become involved if China moves against Taiwan. This statement represents a significant departure from Japan's traditionally more circumspect public posture on cross-strait contingencies, signaling a growing willingness to acknowledge the defense implications of a Taiwan conflict for Japanese security.

## Maritime Architecture

Japan is planning to delimit overlapping EEZs with the Philippines, part of a broader effort to contest China's claims over the entire maritime architecture of the region. This aligns with [US]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) efforts to build a coalition of maritime powers in the first island chain.

## Alliance Role

Japan remains an integral part of the US-led alliance architecture in the Indo-Pacific, though the reliability of American security guarantees has come under question as the US confronts its own strategic overstretch.

## Proactive Regional Security Provider

As the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) shifts toward "flexible, practical realism" — seeking increased transactional burden-sharing and reverting to the "Pacific Command" designation — [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) has transformed from a traditional US ally into an influential regional security provider in its own right. [Victoria Herczegh]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) documents how Japan has steadily expanded its defense diplomacy, defense industrial cooperation, and operational presence throughout Southeast Asia. On the sidelines of the 2026 [NATO Ankara summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html), Japan, [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html), and New Zealand reaffirmed their commitment to closer trilateral defense cooperation. Discussions on defense technology cooperation, Pacific Island engagement, and New Zealand's potential acquisition of Japan's upgraded Mogami-class frigates illustrate these relationships becoming increasingly institutionalized [3].

Japan has also become a critical security partner for the [Philippines]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/philippines.html): discussions on the transfer of Abukuma-class destroyer escorts and Type 88 anti-ship missiles, deeper intelligence cooperation with Manila, and a new defense cooperation agreement with [Indonesia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/indonesia.html) all place Tokyo at the center of an emerging regional security network that rests on US influence but no longer depends on direct US leadership. Instead of waiting for Washington to coordinate collective responses, principal allies are intensifying coordination among themselves [3].

## IP4 and NATO Partnership

Japan is a member of NATO's IP4 (Indo-Pacific 4), alongside Australia, New Zealand, and South Korea. At the 2026 [Ankara summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html), Japan sent both defence and foreign ministers (PM Takaichi having committed to domestic legislative duties). [RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) notes that Japan's acquisition of Tomahawk cruise missiles is discussed in Moscow less as a Japanese capability than as an extension of American power — increasing the strategic value of European long-range strike. PM Takaichi is seeking to leverage the G7 and NATO to manage supply chain pressure from [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), while the US has no interest in the use of these structures to complicate US diplomacy with Beijing [2].

The Ukraine war has created real material links between Japan and the Euro-Atlantic security environment: lessons from modern warfare disseminated eastward from Ukraine are now flowing to Japan and beyond to [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html). Japan's defence manufacturers are restocking European armouries stripped bare to supply Ukraine — creating industrial cooperation links that sustain NATO-IP4 mutual attraction [2].

# Analysis

## The Abe–Kishida Divide and Takaichi's Inaction

[RUSI]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/rusi.html) (James Brown) identifies a structural split within the Liberal Democratic Party that explains [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html)'s ambivalent [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) policy. Two competing approaches coexist within the ruling party. The first, associated with former Prime Minister Abe Shinzō, sought to drive a wedge between Russia and [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) by prioritizing closer relations with Moscow — Abe met Putin 27 times, secured Japanese investment in Arctic LNG-2, and avoided sanctions after the 2014 Crimea annexation. This vision survives through figures like Suzuki Muneo (Abe's unofficial advisor on Russia), Imai Takaya (former METI official and advisor to PM Takaichi), and Abe Akie (the former PM's widow), who have collectively driven a reengagement trend: LDP politicians attending Russian embassy events, a METI-led business delegation to Moscow in May 2026, and the resumption of high-level diplomatic contacts [4].

The second approach, personified by former Prime Minister Kishida Fumio, treats [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html)'s aggression in [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) as directly threatening Japanese security — encapsulated in Kishida's phrase "Ukraine today may be East Asia tomorrow." This calculation produced multiple rounds of sanctions against Russia, a 10-year support accord with Ukraine, and Japan's joining the NATO Prioritised Ukraine Requirements List (PURL) and dispatching Self-Defence Force personnel to the NSATU headquarters in Germany [4].

Prime Minister Takaichi Sanae, a protégée of Abe, has been largely inactive on the Russia-Ukraine issue rather than straightforwardly reviving Abe's policy. She has not visited Kyiv, skipped bilateral meetings with Zelenskyy at the G7 and NATO summits, and dropped Kishida's "Ukraine today" slogan. While she has not reversed the inherited sanctions and support mechanisms, her silence has given a green light to advocates of Abe's reengagement agenda. Japan's policy now sits between the Abe and Kishida poles, creating some distance from Ukraine's most enthusiastic supporters such as the UK — though RUSI assesses that structural factors (Russia's deepening ties with China and North Korea, Moscow's overplayed diplomatic demands, and intensifying Japan-Ukraine defense cooperation) will likely keep Japan's overall policy from diverging far from the European mainstream [4].

## Defense Cooperation with Ukraine

Cooperation between [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) and [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ukraine.html) is intensifying despite Takaichi's ambivalence. Ukraine's world-class expertise in military drones — especially interceptors — is directly relevant to Japan's SHIELD coastal defense system, which proposes more than 10 types of drone for multilayered defense of Japan's coastline. On 9 July 2026, President Zelenskyy raised the prospect of cooperation with Mitsubishi Heavy Industries in the production of [Patriot]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) missiles within Ukraine, following [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html)'s announcement granting Ukraine a production license. MHI is an attractive partner because it already manufactures Patriots under licence within Japan [4].

## China-Russia Military Coordination and Short-of-War Pressure

China-Russia military coordination has evolved from a future scenario into an operational challenge for [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html). Aizawa identifies the December 2025 joint strategic bomber flight as a qualitative turning point: its timing aligned with [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html)'s political interests — applying pressure on Japan following PM Takaichi's November 2025 statement in the National Diet that a Taiwan contingency would constitute a "survival-threatening situation" — and its route extended over 600 miles past the Southwest Islands toward Tokyo, marking a more coercive pattern than previous iterations. The participation of nuclear-capable H-6N bombers alongside Russian Tu-95s elevated the mission from a patrol to a signal of combined nuclear-capable reach. Japan's 2025 Defense White Paper mentioned "China-Russia" approximately 47 times, far exceeding references to Russia-North Korea or China-North Korea cooperation, reflecting Tokyo's assessment that this coordination is now the primary structural pressure vector [5].

Aizawa characterizes the pressure as intensifying across three dimensions: horizontally (from China alone to China-Russia joint operations), vertically (from conventional to nuclear-capable platforms), and qualitatively (from standard patrol routes to coercive flight patterns directed at the capital). This sustained short-of-war pressure blurs the boundary between deterrence and compellence — the [US-Japan alliance]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) is calibrated to deter overt aggression, but China can now apply military pressure on Japan with Russian support at times serving Beijing's interests without crossing the threshold of armed conflict. The alliance has responded with bilateral exercises: two B-52s and six JASDF fighters on December 10, 2025, and four B-52s with eleven JASDF fighters in February 2026. Aizawa argues that the alliance must examine the full range of response approaches, including compellence options, and develop shared threat perceptions and common interests beyond deterrence [5].

Ueki provides complementary analysis of Japan's threat hierarchy and strategic response. Japan's threat perception ranks China as the primary threat, followed by North Korea, [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html), and then [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) — a ranking reinforced by the 2025 Defense White Paper's designation of China as an "unprecedented strategic challenge." Japanese security elites assess that China is willing to pay the cost to hurt Japan, and that Tokyo's leverage over Beijing has diminished since China surpassed Japan economically. The China-Russia coordination challenge prompted Japan to accelerate its review of strategic documents from 2027 to 2026. Public concern about a [Taiwan contingency]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) is acute: 89 percent of respondents in a Kyodo News survey expressed concern. However, Ueki cautions that few Japanese security elites see CRINK as a single group; commentary on the concept is largely driven by US discourse rather than indigenous Japanese analysis [6].

Ueki also documents how the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) has directly undermined Japan's defense modernization timeline. Japan purchased 400 Tomahawk missiles as the main component of its counterstrike capability, with deliveries now delayed by up to four years due to US munitions consumption in the Iran war. The delay impedes Japan's plans to rapidly strengthen deterrence. In response, Defense Minister Koizumi proposed Operation Supercharge — a Japan-US missile coproduction initiative agreed with Secretary Hegseth in May 2026 to accelerate codevelopment and coproduction of SM-3 Block IIA and AMRAAM missiles, addressing the structural risk of dependency on foreign supply [6]. See [Great Power Competition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/great-power-competition.html) and [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html).
