# Background

Latin America has historically been within the US sphere of influence, shaped by Cold War competition and the Monroe Doctrine. The region cycles between left and right populist waves, often driven by commodity prices, crime, and institutional weakness rather than pure ideological conviction. The current period reflects a convergence of security concerns, migration politics, and US pressure under the Trump administration. See [United States](/actors/united-states.md) and [Trump](/actors/trump.md).

# Current Situation

- A rightward wave has swept the region: Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, Colombia, Costa Rica, Ecuador, and Honduras have shifted right.
- [Colombia](/actors/) elected Trump-backed outsider de la Espriella; Peru's Fujimori won a razor-thin runoff.
- Maduro was ousted in Venezuela in January; a tightening oil embargo is being applied on Cuba.
- The Shield of the Americas grouping has been established for hemispheric security cooperation.
- A militarized turn is underway: El Salvador's Bukele-style mass-incarceration model is spreading; drones sourced from Ukraine are being used for anti-drug operations.
- Mexico and Brazil remain as surviving leftist strongholds.
- Peru has had 9 presidents in a decade; fraud allegations and institutional weakness persist.
- South America is becoming a secondary theater of U.S.-China competition. [Brazil](/actors/) is building domestic defense capacity (Gripen fighters, SISFRON border system, Tamandare frigates) while [Argentina](/actors/) rebuilds relevance through U.S.-linked F-16s and security cooperation. The contest plays out through ports, infrastructure, satellites, and defense supply chains rather than formal alliances. States that control their peripheries gain autonomy; those that cannot find external powers shaping their resource flows [2].

# Key Dynamics

- **Not purely Trump-driven** — Attitudes on crime and migration moved rightward independently of US pressure. The ideological shift reflects genuine domestic dynamics, not merely external imposition.
- **Instability within the wave** — Disputed elections, institutional weakness, and razor-thin mandates mean the rightward shift carries its own fragility. See [Ideology and Democracy](/themes/ideology-and-democracy.md).
- **Hemispheric security bloc** — Washington is converting ideological alignment into a security architecture, the Shield of the Americas, that integrates the region into a collective defense framework.
- **DragonBear flank closure** — For the axis (see [Cold War 2](/conflicts/cold-war-2.md)), consolidation of Latin America under US-aligned regimes closes a flank that was previously exploitable.
- **Backlash risk** — If militarized interventions overreach, they could generate backlash that reopens the region to axis influence. See [Great Power Competition](/themes/great-power-competition.md).
