# Background

The Red Sea is a strategic maritime basin connecting the Mediterranean, via the Suez Canal, to the Indian Ocean, via the Bab el-Mandeb Strait. It is one of the world's most critical trade corridors. The basin borders [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html), [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/egypt.html), [Sudan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/sudan.html), Eritrea, [Ethiopia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ethiopia.html) (landlocked but pursuing access), [Somalia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/somalia.html)/Somaliland, Djibouti, and [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) (via the Gulf of Aqaba).

The basin's strategic landscape has historically been dominated by the Houthi threat to commercial shipping, but is increasingly shaped by broader interstate rivalries.

# Current Situation

## From Non-State Threats to Interstate Rivalries

- The Red Sea is evolving from a theater dominated by non-state maritime threats, chiefly the Houthi rebels, into one defined by overlapping interstate rivalries with potentially broader implications for regional stability [1].
- The Houthis themselves avoided direct involvement in the Iran war and remain focused primarily on consolidating control over northwestern Yemen, including the capital, Sanaa, and much of the country's Red Sea coastline, while contending with the Saudi-backed internationally recognized government based in Aden and the UAE-backed Southern Transitional Council [1].
- The more consequential changes are taking place around Yemen, where states are pursuing competing military, economic, and political objectives [1].

## Saudi Pipeline Expansion

- [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html) is weighing a multibillion-dollar expansion of its East-West oil pipeline to the Red Sea, a project that could add up to 2 million barrels per day in capacity, as it seeks to reduce reliance on the Strait of Hormuz for energy exports [1].

## Shipping Route Recovery

- Container shipping giants Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd announced they would reroute a key Asia-Mediterranean service back through the Red Sea and Suez Canal rather than around Africa's Cape of Good Hope [1].

## Ethiopia's Drive for Maritime Access

- [Ethiopia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/ethiopia.html)'s military leadership has continued to signal its determination to secure maritime access, with Navy Commander Kindu Gezu calling it "inevitable" that Ethiopia would regain its "unfairly lost" access to the Red Sea, while Army Chief Birhanu Jula told senior officers that "elaborate preparations" were needed [1].
- Ethiopia has sought arrangements with coastal actors, including Somaliland and Puntland, to diversify its maritime options [1].

## Egypt's Military Reassertion

- [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/egypt.html)'s July 3 inauguration of the Octagon, a sprawling military headquarters designed to centralize command and control, improve crisis management, and integrate intelligence capabilities, reflected Cairo's ambitions to enhance its regional military posture [1].
- Egypt's approach includes efforts to influence Sudan's civil war, continued pressure on Ethiopia over the GERD dispute, and closer alignment with Saudi Arabia, including the 2017 transfer of sovereignty over the Red Sea islands of Tiran and Sanafir to Riyadh [1].

## Israel-Somaliland and Turkish Expansion

- [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html)'s [recognition of Somaliland]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/israel-somaliland-recognition.html) in 2025 reflects its effort to extend its security perimeter into the southern Red Sea, near the Bab el-Mandeb, monitoring and countering Houthi activity while safeguarding maritime routes [1].
- [Turkey]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/turkey.html) has expanded its influence through its military partnership with [Somalia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/somalia.html), its support for the Sudanese Armed Forces, and its presence in Libya, seeing engagement across the Red Sea and toward the Indian Ocean as essential to securing its regional position [1].
- The evolving Egypt-Turkey relationship adds another dimension concerning for Israel, as Ankara builds political, economic, and security partnerships in the Horn of Africa [1].

## Sudan's Civil War

- [Sudan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/sudan.html)'s civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces is a major source of instability, particularly because Sudan possesses a strategically significant coastline along the Red Sea [1].
- The conflict has become an arena for regional competition as outside actors back rival factions in pursuit of influence over Sudan's political future and access to maritime infrastructure [1].

## Iran Asks the Houthis to Close Bab el-Mandeb (July 2026)

[Heather Cox Richardson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/heather-cox-richardson.html) reports that as [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) announced plans to attack [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s bridges and power plants in mid-July 2026, Iranian leaders asked the Houthis they back in Yemen to close the Bab el-Mandeb strait that commands the opening between the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean. The Strait of Hormuz and the Bab el-Mandeb are the two main routes for oil exports from the Middle East; closure of the second strait would exacerbate energy shortages even as the U.S. [SPR]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/spr-depletion-crisis.html) dropped to its lowest level since 1983. The move operationalizes Iran's capacity to widen the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) geographically by activating its Red Sea proxy — a deliberate escalation of the cost imposed on the global economy without requiring Iran itself to close Hormuz [2].

## The Second Front: Houthis Strike Abha (July 16)

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) documents that within 48 hours of July 16, 2026, the Houthis launched a coordinated barrage of ballistic missiles and drones targeting [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html)'s Abha International Airport in direct retaliation for airstrikes on Sanaa. The assault heavily risks *horizontal escalation* that would widen the Iran war by opening a second maritime front at the Bab el-Mandeb gateway. If realized, the second front would trap critical Saudi exports and inflict far more damage on world energy markets at a time they are already highly vulnerable, with oil inventories running down. Until now the Iran war had centered on the Strait of Hormuz; opening a second front at Bab el-Mandeb transforms the crisis from a regional confrontation into a broader contest over the security of global energy flows, increasing pressure on Washington, Riyadh, and their allies to respond militarily rather than diplomatically. Iran gains additional leverage without directly committing its own forces [3].

# Key Dynamics

- **Chokepoint geography** — The Bab el-Mandeb Strait and the Suez Canal together make the Red Sea a global trade artery. Disruptions cascade through energy markets, shipping costs, and supply chains worldwide.
- **Interstate rivalries replacing non-state threats** — The strategic landscape is shifting from Houthi maritime disruption to competing state-level ambitions for coastline, ports, and maritime infrastructure.
- **Hormuz bypass dynamic** — Saudi Arabia's pipeline expansion reflects a broader effort to reduce dependence on the Strait of Hormuz, making the Red Sea an alternative energy export corridor.
- **Horn of Africa-Middle East linkage** — The Red Sea basin connects [African]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/africa.html) and [Middle Eastern]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) security dynamics, with Turkey, Israel, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE all projecting influence across the waterway.
- **Port infrastructure as geopolitical currency** — Port concessions (Berbera, Djibouti, Mogadishu, Tadjourah) translate commercial investment into military and diplomatic leverage.

## Houthi Red Sea Port Blockade Announcement (July 20)

Colibasanu reports that on July 20, the Houthis announced a blockade of [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html)'s Red Sea ports, a move that could potentially halt Saudi oil exports to Asia — approximately 7% of global oil supply. Whether the Houthis can enforce a total blockade is, Colibasanu argues, irrelevant: the demonstrated will and capability to disrupt traffic is sufficient to deter shipping, as precedent from 2018 and 2024 confirms. The announcement compounds the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html)'s Hormuz disruption by opening a second maritime front — effectively threatening both major Middle East oil export corridors simultaneously. This escalation extends the pattern documented by Pape (see above) in which Iran activates its Red Sea proxy to widen the conflict's geographic scope and increase costs on the global economy without direct Iranian military commitment [4].

# Analysis

## Houthi Naval Blockade of Saudi Arabia

[Heather Cox Richardson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/heather-cox-richardson.html) reports that the Houthis announced a naval blockade on [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html), threatening passage through the Bab el-Mandeb strait. If the strait is closed, the global oil supply would take another 7% hit on top of existing Hormuz disruptions. The announcement compounds the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html)'s chokepoint pressure by threatening both major Middle East oil export corridors simultaneously — a scenario Colibasanu had already flagged as escalating the cost cascade across insurance, freight, and energy markets [5].

## Houthis Targeting Saudi Tankers

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) reports that the Houthis announced targeting tankers carrying [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html)'s oil — a direct threat to Saudi export revenue that extends the Red Sea maritime threat from general commercial shipping to Saudi-specific vessels. The announcement compounds the existing Houthi blockade of Saudi Red Sea ports and the Bab el-Mandeb threat, creating a layered maritime coercion campaign against Saudi energy exports [6]. See [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html) and [US-Iran War of 2026]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html).

## S1→S5 Generative Linkage and Houthi Blockade

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html)'s Day 144 diagnostic identifies a generative cross-domain pairing between S1 (US-Iran War and Hormuz) and S5 (Global Supply Chain Fracture): strikes on [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) power infrastructure produced the Red Sea blockade through a third-party actor. Unlike transmissive pairings — where stress in one domain worsens conditions in another — a generative pairing creates entirely new disruption vectors. The US strikes on Iranian infrastructure did not merely worsen existing Red Sea tensions; they produced the Houthi blockade of [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html) as a qualitatively new interdiction vector, upgrading S5 from CRITICAL to RUPTURE [7].

The Houthi blockade of Saudi Arabia in the Red Sea is identified in the diagnostic as one of three simultaneous maritime interdictions that define the S5 RUPTURE status. The Red Sea thus functions not merely as a secondary theater but as a generative output of the S1 domain — the US-Iran War's kinetic dynamics produce Red Sea disruptions through proxy activation, making the [Red Sea]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html) a downstream consequence of Hormuz rather than an independent crisis vector [7].
