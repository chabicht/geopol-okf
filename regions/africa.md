# Background

Africa is a continent of growing strategic importance, characterized by vast territory, fractured state authority, and porous borders. The Sahel region has become a persistent insurgency zone, while the Horn of Africa controls maritime chokepoints connecting the Indian Ocean to the Red Sea. Multiple external actors — [Russia](/actors/russia.md), [Turkey](/actors/turkey.md), the United States, France, and [China](/actors/china.md) — compete for influence across security, minerals, and trade.

# Current Situation

- Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger: military regimes have expelled Western partners and turned to Russia for security support.
- The Alliance of Sahel States operates on sovereignty rhetoric and anti-French sentiment.
- An April 2025 jihadist offensive exposed the limits of Russian security support, revealing that regime change in patrons did not translate into battlefield success. See [Sahel Insurgency](/conflicts/sahel-insurgency.md).
- [Turkey](/actors/turkey.md) is exploiting the gap left by departing Western actors: providing drones, training, and private security services.
- The Wagner→Africa Corps transition produced a more bureaucratic structure that is vulnerable to strategic constraints. See [Wagner/Africa Corps](/actors/wagner-africa-corps.md).
- Russia's reputation as a regime guarantor has been damaged by its failures in Syria and Venezuela.
- Covert discussions between the Mali junta and US intelligence suggest hedging behavior.

# Key Dynamics

- **Multi-actor competition** — Russia, Turkey, the US, France, and China all pursue competing interests. No single external power dominates; regimes exploit the competition to extract concessions. See [Drone Warfare](/themes/drone-warfare.md).
- **Fractured terrain** — Landlocked, vast territory with weak state authority creates operational environments where non-state actors and external powers operate with minimal oversight.
- **Drones as modernization signaling** — Regimes use drone acquisitions to project an image of modernization and military capability, even when battlefield impact is limited.
- **Illicit economies** — Porous borders and jihadist governance of rural areas sustain conflict economies that external security partnerships cannot dismantle.
