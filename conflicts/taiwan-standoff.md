# Background

[China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) claims [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/taiwan.html) as its territory and has conducted an ongoing gray-zone warfare campaign designed to erode Taiwan's will and capacity to resist unification. Taiwan is the central front of the new Cold War — the point at which the competition between the US-led order and the Chinese system is most acute and most potentially escalatory. The standoff encompasses military, economic, informational, and legal dimensions, with China employing a whole-of-state approach that falls below the threshold of armed conflict but steadily shifts the facts on the water and in the air.

The [Indo-Pacific]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/indo-pacific.html) theater is increasingly interconnected with other theaters of great power competition, as described in the [Cold War 2.0]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html) framework. The bandwidth-exploitation logic by which the DragonBear axis stretches Western attention across simultaneous theaters — contesting Taiwan's maritime approaches while Moscow ties down the West in Ukraine — is analyzed there.

# Current Situation

China is opening a new front by shifting operations to the waters east of Taiwan, with coast guard and survey presence being normalized in zones previously free of Chinese activity [1]. Key developments include:

- The research vessel *Xiang Yang Hong 22* deployed east of Taiwan with China Coast Guard (CCG) escort, using environmental framing for what is functionally military survey activity [1].
- The Fujian carrier sailed through the Taiwan Strait on the same day Taiwan began a military exercise — a deliberate signal of timeline awareness and coercive intent [1].
- CCG vessels are harassing commercial vessels and demanding route reports, constituting an incremental assertion of administrative authority over Taiwan's maritime approaches [1].
- A PLA Navy carrier strike group has been operating east of the Philippines since May 19, with a temporary structure erected at Scarborough Shoal [1].
- The opposition KMT and TPP parties have been delaying the 2026 budget for over 9 months and are threatening defense cuts, creating a domestic political crisis that mirrors Chinese pressure [1].
- Taiwan's defense budget stands at approximately $17.8 billion (a 20% increase), with the goal of reaching 3.3% of GDP from the current 2.4%. The United States is pressing for 3.5% [1].
- Chinese espionage operations continue: a former Air Force Academy colonel was sentenced to 11 years for espionage [1].
- Tensions with [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) are rising: PM Takaichi suggested that Tokyo's military could become involved in a Taiwan contingency [1].

# Analysis

## China's Gray-Zone Coercion Campaign

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) describes China's comprehensive campaign as a combination of military encirclement, grey-zone harassment, economic inducement, espionage, and united-front messaging — all working in concert to erode Taiwan's defensive will [1]. Environmental conservation is used as a pretext for territorial claims, with China converting one-time survey deployments into recurring presence, thereby establishing facts on the water that normalize Chinese authority [1]. The deployment of the *Xiang Yang Hong 22* with CCG escort is emblematic: what is framed as oceanographic research is functionally a military survey establishing baseline data for future operations, while the CCG escort asserts a governance claim over waters Taiwan considers its own [1].

Victoria Herczegh (GPF) identifies a dual-track approach that combines increased pressure with selective engagement at a politically sensitive moment — a transition from static geopolitical rivalry to a more fluid and multilayered contest [6]. The coercive track was exemplified by the cancellation of President Lai Ching-te's planned visit to Eswatini (formerly Swaziland), the last African country that formally recognizes Taiwan, after several African nations — including Seychelles, Mauritius, and Madagascar — abruptly denied overflight permissions for the president's plane. Taipei cited Chinese economic coercion, including threats tied to debt relief, as the reason for the cancellations. The episode points to a more assertive and targeted tactic: physically constraining Taiwan's ability to maintain diplomatic relations [6]. Simultaneously, Beijing signaled willingness to resume certain forms of cooperation, including direct flights and trade in specific sectors such as aquaculture, following a visit by an opposition leader from Taiwan's Kuomintang (KMT) party. Discussions of a long-term cooperation mechanism between the KMT and the Chinese Communist Party suggest Beijing is trying to influence Taiwan's internal political dynamics more directly, while infrastructure proposals such as a bridge linking mainland China to the offshore islands of Kinmen and Matsu underscore this combination of symbolic and practical outreach [6]. The two tracks operate against a backdrop of structural decoupling: Taiwanese businesses have dramatically reduced their exposure to China, with investment flows dropping from over 80 percent in 2010 to under 4 percent in 2025. Production has increasingly relocated to Southeast Asia, to Mexico, or back to Taiwan, while trade ties with the US and the West have deepened, particularly in high-value sectors like semiconductors and artificial intelligence. The timing intersects with ongoing efforts to stabilize US-China relations — any progress in bilateral negotiations could create space for a partial recalibration of cross-strait dynamics, though any change would likely remain limited and conditional [6].

China's declaratory posture on the South China Sea reinforces the coercive track. On the 10th anniversary of the 2016 arbitration ruling, Beijing's Foreign Ministry stated that China "does not accept any means of third-party dispute settlement or any solution imposed on China" — not a claim about the 2016 tribunal specifically but about a category of institution, the same claim [Moscow]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) makes about the International Criminal Court [7]. This institutional rejection, combined with maritime coercion and sanctions against officials of neighboring states, represents a comprehensive campaign that sets precedents applicable to a Taiwan contingency [7].

## Blockade-Rehearsal Operational Arc

The December 2025 [Justice Mission 2025]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/justice-mission-2025.html) exercise was the most operationally detailed blockade rehearsal to date, simulating interdiction of Taiwanese energy imports, counter-intervention against U.S. forces, and potential decapitation strikes across multiple zones surrounding Taiwan [3]. The absence of aircraft carriers suggests the exercise practiced only the inner layer of a larger multi-domain blockade. Each iteration of these exercises improves the PLA's ability to execute a real blockade, while serving coercive-psychological-warfare purposes aimed at intimidating Taiwan's DPP government and deterring [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) from supporting Taiwan [3]. The vessel, sortie, and zone detail is tracked in the [event file]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/justice-mission-2025.html).

## PLA Capability & Adaptation

ISW analysis suggests the PLA's pre-existing investments in AI-enabled swarm technology and sophisticated reconnaissance drones are skewing the lessons it draws from [Ukraine]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html) and Middle East conflicts, where low-cost attritable FPV drones dominate [4]. Xi's purges and institutional rigidity discourage adaptation, leading the PLA to downplay the central role of cheap, disposable systems while continuing to develop expensive, survivable platforms — creating a potential asymmetric advantage for Taiwan if it can learn more effectively from contemporary warfare [4].

The February 2026 [CMC purge]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/cmc-purge-2026.html) compounds these adaptation limits. Xi's removal of Zhang Youxia and Liu Zhenli — the only CMC members with combat experience — damages command cohesion: Zhang anticipated full joint operational capability closer to 2035, while Xi insists on 2027 [2]. The purge reduces the likelihood of a near-term invasion, since complex joint operations require experienced commanders who trust one another, but new commanders eager to demonstrate loyalty may favor aggressive tactical behavior, increasing the risk of miscalculation [2]. The intersection of institutional rigidity and command disruption means the PLA may simultaneously be less capable of executing a clean blockade and more prone to incidents that trigger escalation. China's broader use of Taiwan as a bargaining chip in great-power negotiations is tracked in [Cold War 2.0]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/cold-war-2.html).

## Diplomatic & Coalition Dimension

Tchakarova identifies a consequential development: three major NATO members — [Britain]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-kingdom.html), [France]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/france.html), and [Germany]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/germany.html) — issued a coordinated statement on the Pacific maritime dispute, signaling that European powers now treat Taiwan as integral to the global order and are extending their deterrence posture into the Pacific beyond NATO's traditional Euro-Atlantic focus [1]. The defense of Taiwan is no longer framed solely as a US commitment but as a broader Western interest.

The May 2026 [Trump-Xi summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/trump-xi-summit-2026.html) highlighted a shift in Washington's posture that introduces deep uncertainty into US-Taiwan relations. Xi framed Taiwan as "the most important issue in China-US relations," warning that mishandling it would jeopardise the Sino-American relationship; while the meeting was not a turning point, it underlined Taipei's predicament of relying on now-questionable American promises [5]. Xi may have been testing Trump's willingness to soften US language on Taiwanese sovereignty, reduce diplomatic support, or cut arms sales [5]. If Washington does reduce its security guarantees, Taiwan could strengthen its strategic alignment with [Europe]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/europe.html): the EU's expanding network of Security and Defence Partnerships — including with [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html), [South Korea]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/south-korea.html), [Australia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/australia.html), and [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html) — creates institutional frameworks for deeper Taiwan-European cooperation, with risks for Europe but also opportunities in drone technology, defence industry cooperation, and supply chain diversification [5]. The volatility of US commitment and the European response are thus two sides of the same coalition question: who defends Taiwan, and on what terms.

### All-Domain Megacity Incapacitation Warfare

Wang & Beauchamp-Mustafaga present a concept — "all-domain megacity incapacitation warfare" — published in the March 2025 edition of *Military Art*, China's leading internal military theory journal, which is overseen and approved by the Central Military Commission. While not yet official doctrine, the concept carries credibility: its authors include Lt. Col. Li Hengrui and Senior Col. Cheng Jinming, researchers at the Academy of Military Science, and Lt. Col. Wang Haiyuan, an instructor at the People's Armed Police Command College. The PAP co-authorship is notable given the population-management role the PAP plays within China — a role it could potentially serve in occupied Taiwan [8].

The concept proposes three lines of effort to incapacitate Taipei. A military blockade would sever supplies and information through siege tactics and strikes on aid en route, combining kinetic strikes on command centers and communication hubs with jamming, cyberattacks, DDoS attacks, and AI deepfakes. Precision strikes would conduct decapitation attacks on commanders and key personnel while special forces seize infrastructure — power grids, maintenance depots — that can support Chinese forces after landing, deliberately avoiding hard-to-rebuild targets like power plants and reservoirs to facilitate post-conflict occupation. Cognitive attacks would create "information bubbles and echo chambers" targeting military and civilian populations separately through algorithmic content delivery, deepfakes, and "information munitions" embedded in Meta and TikTok content, with the objective of engineering cognitive submission — breaking Taipei's will to fight — rather than achieving physical destruction [8].

The concept marks an evolution in [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html)'s systems confrontation thinking: from information-paralysis (paralyzing information nodes so the enemy military cannot operate) to cognitive-engineering (ensuring the adversary accepts defeat). Traditional systems confrontation avoids striking civilian targets to minimize collateral damage, but this concept designates civilian communications channels and facilities connecting Taipei to the outside world as legitimate targets from the outset of conflict, because complete information control is necessary to suppress disruption to the intended cognitive effects. Non-military capabilities are placed on co-equal footing with military firepower: the "integrated national strategic system and capabilities" would coordinate between military and civilian forces to reshape ideas in favor of China's narratives [8].

China's 15th Five-Year Plan, issued in early 2026, makes developing the necessary strategic capabilities a priority — providing a benchmark for evaluating whether the broader military adopts this concept through incorporation into future Taiwan-focused exercises and continued investments in cognitive warfare capabilities. U.S. wargames and analyses overwhelmingly focus on kinetic exchanges during blockades or invasions and rely on survey data about will to fight, but rarely examine wartime cognitive attacks directly targeting Taiwan's will to resist. Wang & Beauchamp-Mustafaga argue that U.S. analysts should incorporate cognitive effects into wargames, refuse to treat will-to-fight as a static input, and adjudicate cognitive effects on military, civilian, and political leadership throughout simulations, while including Taiwanese defense mobilization institutions such as the All-Out Defense Mobilization Agency and NTU's societal resilience indicators as standard wargame elements [8]. See [Cognitive Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/cognitive-warfare.html).

## The US Might Not Win the Opening Battles

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) revises his earlier assessment of the Taiwan balance: the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html) has degraded US military readiness so severely that the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) could be overwhelmed at the start of a [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) conflict. Combined Patriot and THAAD inventories stand at approximately 1,500 interceptors; with a two-per-target doctrine, that yields perhaps 1,000 intercepts against China's roughly 3,500 missiles. US bases in Guam and the Marianas could be devastated at the opening. The "short war scenario" — in which the US runs out of systems and suffers such high losses it cannot continue — has become plausible. The implication for Taiwan is stark: the US security guarantee, already questioned after the [Trump-Xi summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/trump-xi-summit-2026.html), may now be militarily unexecutable at the munitions level regardless of political will [9]. See [Great Power Competition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/great-power-competition.html) and [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html).

## CCG Patrols East of Taiwan

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html)'s Day 144 diagnostic identifies Chinese Coast Guard patrols east of Taiwan as an S3 (US-China and DragonBear) domain indicator, confirming the continuation of the gray-zone encirclement campaign documented above. The diagnostic also notes Russian military training for [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) as a DragonBear linkage — the Kremlin-approved programme transferring Ukraine-war combat lessons to the PLA continues to operate alongside the CCG maritime presence. The combination of CCG patrols east of Taiwan and Russian military training for China represents the DragonBear axis operating at full simultaneity across the Indo-Pacific theater, with Russia supplying combat experience and China projecting maritime coercion [10].

## Taiwan Contingency and China-Russia Military Coordination

The [Taiwan standoff]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) has become increasingly entangled with China-Russia military coordination and Japan's evolving security posture. Aizawa traces the escalation chain to PM Takaichi's November 2025 statement in the National Diet that a Taiwan contingency would constitute a "survival-threatening situation" for [Japan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/japan.html) — a significant departure from Tokyo's traditionally circumspect posture. [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) responded with a multi-layered military escalation: the Liaoning carrier was deployed, a PLA naval vessel conducted a radar lock-on on a Japanese P-3 patrol aircraft, and the December 2025 joint China-Russia strategic bomber flight followed a coercive route extending over 600 miles past the Southwest Islands toward Tokyo. The timing of the bomber flight — aligned with China's political objective of punishing Japan for Takaichi's Taiwan remarks — demonstrated that China can now apply combined military pressure on Japan with Russian support at moments that serve Beijing's cross-strait agenda [11].

Ueki documents the depth of Japanese public concern: 89 percent of respondents in a Kyodo News survey expressed worry about a Taiwan contingency. Japanese security elites identify multiple conflict scenarios that could trigger or intersect with a Taiwan crisis: an extraregional conflict (such as the [US-Iran War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/us-iran-war-2026.html)) could open a window of opportunity for China if US forces appear tied down; a Korean Peninsula conflict could lead to a Taiwan contingency; or conversely, a Taiwan contingency could trigger Korean Peninsula instability. The 2026 US National Defense Strategy explicitly notes the "possibility that one or more potential opponents might act together in a coordinated or opportunistic fashion across multiple theaters." Miscalculation risk is acute: if China assesses that US forces are occupied elsewhere and its chances of victory have increased, the temptation for opportunistic action rises — a dynamic with historical precedent in the Korean War, when Mao's planned Taiwan invasion was disrupted by the US deployment of the Seventh Fleet [12].

Ueki identifies a structural constraint on CRINK involvement in a Taiwan contingency: China sees Taiwan as a domestic problem and may prefer not to involve other countries, as their participation would delegitimize China's claim that the issue is internal. This suggests that while Russia and North Korea may not be invited to participate directly in a Taiwan operation, they could act opportunistically in adjacent theaters. The coordination challenge for the US-Japan alliance is thus not a unified CRINK assault but a set of concurrent, potentially uncoordinated pressures that exploit the same bandwidth constraints [12].
