# Background

The US-Iran War began on February 28, 2026, when the United States initiated military action against Iran. Despite the scale of the intervention, Iran retained control of the Strait of Hormuz throughout the conflict — a chokepoint through which roughly 20 million barrels per day (mbpd) of petroleum liquids flow, representing approximately 20% of global supply. The war resulted in the loss of approximately 10 mbpd of effective daily supply against the Hormuz baseline. Pipeline bypass options proved structurally thin: Saudi and UAE limited capacity was an order of magnitude short of what would be needed to compensate for the disrupted Hormuz throughput.

The conflict drew in multiple actors and exposed structural vulnerabilities in the global energy system, US military posture, and the Western alliance framework. The [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) initiated hostilities as a personal decision by [Donald Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html), while [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) leveraged its geographic control of Hormuz to maintain coercive leverage even under bombardment. [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) operated as a parallel actor whose interests overlapped with but did not align perfectly with US objectives.

## Pre-War Context

In the weeks before the war, [Geopolitical Futures]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) assessed that Iran's regime was "headed for a hard landing." The 47th anniversary of the 1979 revolution exposed elite factionalism, the erosion of the IRGC, and the rise of pragmatists seeking a deal with the Trump administration. The internal crisis created both an opportunity for diplomatic resolution and a risk that military action would be seen as a way to resolve it externally. The war launched before the internal dynamics could play out [1].

# Current Situation

The kinetic phase of the war was formally concluded by the June 17 Versailles Memorandum (MoU), an arrangement that produced an immediate market response: Brent crude fell from approximately $126 to below $73 per barrel. The [FACE Intelligence]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/energy-and-resources.html) Simultaneity reading dropped from S5-S6 to S3-S4, reflecting a reduction in systemic crisis intensity.

Critically, Iran retained effective leverage over who controls, inspects, escorts, and charges for Hormuz passage. The MoU did not resolve the underlying balance of power — it paused the kinetic exchange while leaving Iran's coercive infrastructure intact. Secondary cascades — fertilizer/food, emerging-market debt, and EU gas storage deficits — continue propagating on 6–12 month lags; see [Global System Rupture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) for the cascade framework. Post-MoU escalation events (IRGC drone attacks on commercial vessels, US reprisal strikes, attacks on Bahrain facilities) confirmed the fragility from the outset. As [Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) observed, "The Iran War ended without resolving the underlying balance of power." See [Iran Ceasefire MoU]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html) for details on the diplomatic instrument.

# Analysis

## Strategic Assessment: Why the US Lost

The central analytical question of the war is how a superpower with overwhelming conventional superiority failed to compel outcomes from a geographically entrenched adversary with a fraction of its military budget. Multiple analysts converge on a shared diagnosis: the United States fought the wrong war — bombing the wrong targets, measuring success against the wrong metrics, and pursuing political objectives disconnected from military reality.

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) offers the most scathing assessment. He argues the United States bombed girls' schools, failed to protect its own facilities, and achieved no meaningful strategic objectives. He dismisses the sinking of the Iranian Navy as a "derisory achievement" given that those vessels were not the source of Iran's coercive power — control of Hormuz was exercised through geography, anti-ship missile threat, and drone swarms, not surface combatants [2]. O'Brien catalogued US aircraft losses at 17 advanced aircraft (fighters, AWACS, special forces helicopters), billions in equipment damage, bases hit extensively, and munitions burned through decades of production in six weeks. A CSIS report confirmed munitions depletion. The Trump administration's corruption has "percolated to the military," degrading institutional competence [3]. An earlier Atlantic article by O'Brien identified the first signs of US military "rot": Iranian drones penetrating US defenses (6 soldiers killed in Kuwait), F-15E friendly-fire shootdowns, the destruction of a Fifth Fleet radar in Bahrain by a $30K drone, and the erosion of alliances under Trump — dynamics he compared to Roman imperial decline [4].

[Aurelien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/aurelien.html) classifies the war as a classic case of "surprise-at-failure" — the most common type of strategic surprise. The outbreak was not surprising: US leaders had threatened Iran for a decade, the methods were predictable, and Israel's participation was expected. The surprise came because the *overall strategic situation* was read wrongly [21]. The dynamic Aurelien identifies is "backwards logic from desired outcome to necessary false assumptions": since a ground invasion was infeasible, all faith had to be placed in an air campaign — but the campaign could only succeed *if* the Iranian state was very fragile, *and* if main state targets could be reliably hit, *and* if the Iranian people would rise up once the state was weakened. These assumptions *had* to be true for the objective to be viable, and therefore the assumptions were treated as true. The US appreciation of Iran's conventional weakness was broadly correct, but Iran's *capability* (asymmetric, geographic, drone-based) was not properly understood, and the *objectives* of the Iranian state (survival and retaliation) were wrongly evaluated. Unlike the Americans, the Iranians were not surprised — they had expected the attack and developed capabilities to pursue their objective [21]. Aurelien places this pattern alongside Operation Barbarossa (1941), the Iraq invasion (2003), and the Falklands (1982) as cases where the wished-for political outcome generated a chain of necessary false assumptions about military effectiveness [21].

O'Brien argues that Trump's personal decision to attack Iran was not in the national interest and was made without political pressure to do so — a finding that directly challenges the assumptions of realism. Wars are choices of individual leaders, not state actors pursuing national interest [2]. By May 2026, O'Brien reports Trump was "desperate to exit" the war but unable to, with no good military options. The emerging Iran deal was worse than the JCPOA. Trump called on Arab states to join the Abraham Accords to appease Netanyahu and GOP hardliners, while [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) was backing Iran and treating [Taiwan]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/taiwan-standoff.html) as a "chip" in the broader negotiation [19]. Pape reinforces this by noting that states gaining leverage rarely hurry to end wars. Once Iran recognized it held the dominant position, it had no incentive to concede quickly [5].

On the home front, O'Brien observes that there was no rally-round-the-flag effect during the war. US society remains locked in a partisan death match: one side does whatever Trump wants, while the other opposed the war from the start. The old patriotic ideas that would have generated national cohesion in a wartime setting are gone [2]. The gap between stated objectives and observable outcomes has introduced uncertainty about the credibility and predictability of US policy — shaping how both partners and adversaries will assess future American commitments. The fracturing of Gulf cohesion and the economic volatility generated by the strait's disruption are consequences that will define the strategic environment long after any agreement is reached [28].

The war can be understood as a manifestation of [Endless Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/endless-warfare.html) — the persistent, below-threshold conflict that adversaries wage against the United States. Iran's network of proxies and surrogates, built over decades, allows Tehran to project power and coerce neighbors at relatively low cost. The war demonstrated that when adversaries practice Endless Warfare, conventional military superiority alone cannot compel outcomes — the adversary's theory of victory may be narrower (survival rather than conquest) and therefore more resilient [29].

## Iran's Theory of Victory

The outcome of the war aligns with Iran's strategy all along — not a failure of execution by the US and Israel but a successful implementation of a twenty-year campaign plan. Pnina Shuker and Andrew Milburn (War on the Rocks) document how Iran fought a different war than the one it was offered: rather than contesting air superiority symmetrically, it moved the contest onto terrain its adversaries were not equipped to contest — maritime coercion, economic disruption, information dominance, and political will [28].

The doctrine behind this has a name: mosaic defense, formalized in 2005 by the IRGC's Center for Strategy under Mohammad Ali Jafari and the ideologue Hassan Abbasi — a decentralized command structure designed to survive exactly the kind of decapitation strikes that opened this war. Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi stated: "We've had two decades to study defeats of the U.S. military to our immediate east and west. We've incorporated lessons accordingly." Iran's theory of victory was simple: survive long enough to impose costs by striking the Gulf States and playing the Hormuz card [28]. Iran had studied the force structure it faced and designed its counter-campaign accordingly — targeting not strike aircraft but the tankers, radar networks, communications nodes, and command platforms that sustained them. The cost exchange told its own story: Iran's drones and missiles cost thousands of dollars apiece; the interceptors fired to stop them ran into millions [28].

Economically, Tehran spent five years building a sanctions-proof economy — its tanker fleet grew from roughly 70 vessels in 2020 to nearly 550 by 2025, sustained by Chinese refineries that alone accounted for close to 90 percent of Iran's foreign oil sales. Iran's kinetic targeting of Gulf states served a wedge strategy: demonstrating that supporting the US campaign has a price. When Iran struck the UAE's oil terminal, Kuwait and Saudi Arabia closed their airspace to US military aircraft. [Project Freedom]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/project-freedom-collapse.html) — Trump's tanker escort plan — collapsed when Saudi Arabia refused basing, citing lack of confidence in US strategy coherence [28].

Pape frames the dynamic as a shift in Iran's negotiating posture: Iran moved from negotiating to stop losing to negotiating to win more — a transition enabled by the realization that the US had no viable path to compelling Iranian compliance on Hormuz [5]. He introduces the concept of "controlled instability" as Iran's post-war strategic doctrine: persistent low-level disruptions — drone incidents, insurance volatility, shipping harassment — that sustain a geopolitical risk premium on oil without requiring total Hormuz closure. This allows Iran to monetize its chokepoint leverage continuously rather than wielding it as a binary weapon. If oil stays above $90, Iran's fiscal position strengthens while Western economies absorb the inflationary cost [16]. Two theories of victory clashed: the US and Israel wanted surrender and Iran's collapse; Iran wanted survival. Tehran's narrower objective gave it the capacity to keep imposing costs and a negotiating position strong enough to turn resilience into political leverage. The deal's own sequence settles the question: before Iran has to resolve a single hard issue — the nuclear stockpile, the missile program, or its regional proxies — the United States lifts the naval blockade, withdraws forces, issues sanctions waivers, and makes frozen assets available. "Pay first, verify later is not a strategy. It is leverage leaving the room" [28].

## The Energy War: Operations and Market Dynamics

The energy dimension was the war's primary battlefield. Iran never needed to achieve sea control in the Strait of Hormuz; it needed only to make transit uninsurable and unprofitable. Within days, crude oil exports fell 95% and LNG 99% — the largest supply disruption in the history of the oil market — achieved with technologically unsophisticated means: sea mines, short-range missiles, swarming fast boats, and enough demonstrated resolve to convince insurers, shipowners, and energy markets that transit was not worth the risk [38].

[The Honest Sorcerer]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/the-honest-sorcerer.html) provides the most detailed week-by-week tracking of the cascade:

- **Week 1 (early March)**: The Strait shutdown reduced tanker traffic from 40/day to 1. The diesel/jet fuel crisis began. LNG disruption took [Qatar]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/qatar.html)'s 1/5 of global capacity offline. [Egypt]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html)'s existential crisis began as Suez transit collapsed. European gas storage dropped below seasonal norms [7].
- **Week 2 (mid-March)**: The 16M barrel/day shortfall (crude + refined) became visible. Pipeline diversion to Yanbu was insufficient. Iran continued exports to China. The Bab-el-Mandeb chokepoint was affected by Houthi activity. Operation Prosperity Guardian's failure was confirmed. Insurance withdrawal from Gulf shipping [8].
- **Week 4-5 (early April)**: 500m barrels lost cumulatively. Iran's 80% missile hit rate against US bases. The US running out of munitions. Iran proposed a $2M toll per tanker transit (paid in rials). Trump threatened to escalate to wholesale destruction [9].
- **Week 6 (mid-April)**: The US imposed a [naval blockade]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/us-naval-blockade-iran.html) on Iranian ports — a counter-blockade. Secondary sanctions on Chinese banks. Iran threatened to block the Red Sea. The US struck the South Pars gas field (shared with Qatar). The Pentagon sought help from automakers for military production [10].
- **Week 8 (late April)**: The "polycrisis" framing crystallized. The damage to supply chains of energy, fertilizer, food, and industrial inputs was "already done." A super El Nino threatened concurrent crop failures [11].
- **Week 10 (mid-May)**: Cumulative 1B+ barrels lost. 14.4 mb/d shut in. OECD inventories hitting operational minimums. SPR releases unsustainable. Iran retained 70% of its missile stockpile [12].

[Endtropy]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/endtropy.html) draws a structural parallel between the US-Iran counter-blockade dynamics and Napoleonic warfare. Napoleon's Continental System — the attempt to blockade British trade — generated adaptive responses (smuggling, redirecting trade through neutral ports, financial workarounds) that ultimately undermined the blockade's effectiveness. The US counter-blockade of Iranian ports produced analogous responses: Iran redirected oil exports through non-dollar channels, Chinese banks developed alternative settlement systems, and the [monetary system transition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/monetary-system-transition.html) accelerated [13]. The counter-blockade revealed the war's true objective: controlling oil flow to [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html), not uranium enrichment. By blockading Iranian ports while imposing secondary sanctions on Chinese banks, the US attempted to sever the energy pipeline sustaining the DragonBear axis [10][13].

Why didn't oil hit $150 despite three-plus months of Hormuz disruption? [Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html) identifies three buffers absorbing the shock: the US (record exports + 66M SPR drawdown, stocks at 349M approaching 1983 lows), China (crude imports collapsed to 8-year low of 7.8 mb/d, 29% YoY decline — demand destruction), and Russia (forced to cut exports as Ukrainian strikes cripple refineries). The calm is "engineered, temporary, and depleting" — each buffer is a one-time measure, not a structural fix [14][15]. See [SPR Depletion Crisis]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/spr-depletion-crisis.html).

Tchakarova's follow-up analysis dismantles the market narrative that the oil price collapse signals a Hormuz recovery. Brent fell from a wartime peak above $180 to around $72, with the forward curve moving into contango. But the physical data tell a different story: Persian Gulf production remains roughly 45% below its pre-war level, regional exports sit around 25% below February levels, and shipping companies remain reluctant to send vessels back through Hormuz given unresolved insurance costs and residual mine risk [22]. The price decline was driven by four distinct offsets working simultaneously: physical rerouting (Saudi Arabia through its East-West pipeline, UAE through its bypass route, Iran itself exporting more oil via shadow fleets), coordinated reserve releases (more than 300 million barrels — the largest emergency drawdown in history), China demand destruction (crude imports fell roughly 40%, close to five million barrels a day), and broader demand erosion (global oil demand contracted an estimated 5.5 million barrels a day in Q2 2026) [22]. Full restoration of export capacity is expected to take many months, with Qatar's damaged LNG facilities potentially requiring years. Shipping costs in waters south of Iran have climbed to roughly 5.5 times pre-war levels, and tanker charter rates have surged to nearly nine times pre-war norms [22].

Scenario modeling built around this evidence assigns the following probability weights [22]: fragile equilibrium (50%, oil $70–85 while the memorandum holds despite recurring friction); renewed rupture (30%, a serious incident sends prices back toward $100+); and durable stabilization (20%, a lasting settlement allows prices to settle near pre-war levels). The price collapse is best read as evidence that global demand management and reserve coordination can now absorb shocks that would have been unmanageable a decade ago — but Iran's retained leverage over Hormuz means the underlying rupture has not closed, only been priced as if it has.

After the ceasefire collapse in July, [Paul Krugman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/paul-krugman.html) argued the binding constraint shifted from crude supply to refining capacity. Roughly 10% of world refining capacity is offline, primarily from Ukraine's drone campaign against Russian refineries in the parallel [Russia-Ukraine War]({{ site.baseurl }}/conflicts/russia-ukraine-war.html). The effective consumer price of oil (weighted gasoline + diesel) rose approximately $75/barrel during the war, while crude rose only $45 — high consumer prices kept demand depressed even when crude prices nearly normalized. A Hormuz reopening would add crude but not usable fuel [30]. The Honest Sorcerer extends this with physical supply chain data: nearly 3 million bpd of Middle East refining capacity shut down; refineries globally cut back by 9 million bpd (more than 10% of global capacity). China's refinery runs crashed to pandemic lows. Ultra-low sulfur diesel reached $168 per barrel against ~$80 crude. Russian diesel exports collapsed from 800,000 bpd to zero under the Ukrainian drone campaign. The [SPR]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/spr-depletion-crisis.html) drawdown rate fell from ~1.3 million bpd in mid-June to 0.43 million by July 10, trending toward zero by early August. Basing decisions on crude prices alone — in a world stripped of refining capacity — has become a civilizational risk [42].

## Diplomatic and Regional Realignment

The war produced lasting diplomatic fractures on multiple fronts. O'Brien emphasizes that the United States threw its European allies, Japan, and South Korea under the bus by starting the war without informing them — a decision that inflicted lasting damage on alliance trust and cohesion [2]. Tchakarova reframes the ceasefire not as an American coercive success but as an axis course correction: [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) and [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) green-lit the MoU through the [DragonBear]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) coordination mechanism because uncontrolled escalation risked a Global System Rupture that would damage their own foundations [6]. Aurelien provides the sharpest framing of the diplomatic impasse: the US and Israeli positions are fundamentally unreconcilable with Iran's. These positions have zero overlap — there is nothing to negotiate. Diplomacy is theater, and the nuclear enrichment issue is an excuse, not a real subject of bargaining [11][17].

The war produced a lasting strategic divergence between the United States and Israel. Washington seeks to use military gains to create a diplomatic framework that constrains Iran while stabilizing the region, whereas Israel remains skeptical that an ideologically driven regime can be durably moderated through negotiation. VP Vance publicly rebuked Israeli cabinet members for criticizing U.S. diplomacy, stating that Israel "might not be attacking the only powerful ally that I have anywhere left in the entire world." Washington conducted ceasefire negotiations through Pakistani mediation without Israeli participation, despite Israel bearing much of the war's cost and risk. Israel has shifted operational focus to Lebanon, where Hezbollah represents a more immediate threat, and fears that any Iranian financial relief will rebuild Hezbollah [23]. Joe Kent reveals that Israel's long-term goal for Iran is complete regime change toward a secular, business-friendly government. In the short term, Israel views a chaotic Iran — potentially fracturing into ethnic states — as beneficial, despite the disaster it would cause for the US. The nuclear issue was a pretext used by Israel to draw the US into a war to neutralize Iran, a country Israel couldn't defeat alone [26].

For Israel, the reckoning is stark. Israel was not a party to the talks and, by its own officials' account, had not even seen the text of the agreement it would have to live with. Hizballah gains new protection from Israeli strikes under the deal's terms, and the missile program Israel considers existential goes unaddressed. The Gulf states Israel hoped would drift toward Israel have done the opposite — maintaining and in some cases deepening ties with Tehran. When Netanyahu pressed for strikes in Beirut that risked the deal, Trump reportedly told him he "would be in prison" without American protection. One Israeli diplomat summed up the result as "a glorious failure" [28].

The war accelerated regional diplomatic realignment. [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html) conducted secret negotiations with Iran through [Pakistan]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/pakistan.html), with Russian and Chinese support, aiming at a non-aggression pact. [Oman]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/oman.html) co-negotiated a [permanent toll system]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/hormuz-toll-system.html) for Hormuz. These initiatives signal the end of the US monopoly on Middle East security architecture — regional powers are negotiating their own stability with non-Western guarantors [18].

Kent asserts that China is exponentially benefiting from the conflict, positioning itself as a more appealing partner to Middle Eastern states by offering deals instead of regime change. The war erodes US security guarantees and the petrodollar system, demonstrating US limitations to the world and bolstering China's position in [great power competition]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/great-power-competition.html), including regarding Taiwan [26]. George Friedman (GPF) argues the most important long-term aspect is how the war will affect US-China bilateral relations. China's abstention from the conflict is twofold: China needs to rehabilitate economic ties to the US — still its largest customer — and is heavily dependent on oil and LNG imports through Hormuz. Beijing criticized the war and called on Washington to end it but otherwise stayed out of it, assigning its ambassador in Pakistan to attend mediation talks in Islamabad. Friedman notes a historical parallel: after World War II, the US helped Germany and Japan evolve economically out of self-interest. A shared interest in reaching an understanding on trade and the muted reaction to the Iran war are important indicators that a US-China agreement on trade is emerging [27].

## Escalation Dynamics: The Trap Deepens

The Versailles Memorandum did not hold. Around July 13, 2026, the ceasefire collapsed — [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) reinstated the blockade; Iran resumed drone and missile strikes on shipping. Iran, emboldened by its success in defying America, demanded sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz. Trump declared he would collect 20% tolls on all shipping traffic, then shifted to demanding that countries invest in the United States as a condition for passage — both proposals wildly illegal and impossible to enforce. The full tactical timeline and market repricing of the collapse are tracked in [Hormuz Ceasefire Collapse]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/hormuz-ceasefire-collapse-2026.html) [30][31][36].

Pape argues that the ceasefire collapse is not simply another round of strikes but evidence that the conflict has entered a fundamentally new phase — the Escalation Trap is becoming self-reinforcing. Each side is no longer trying merely to impose military costs; each is attempting to reverse what it sees as an unacceptable political defeat. "Both governments are becoming politically invested in objectives that cannot both be achieved at the same time" [32][33].

The war is now driven by two separate, mutually reinforcing engines. **Strategic escalation**: Iran's Revolutionary Guard insists only Tehran will control Hormuz; US Central Command continues preparing to guarantee freedom of navigation. Trump has moved from punishment to reversing the strategic defeat — his goal is no longer to coerce Iran but to undo the perception that the US lost [33]. **Revenge escalation**: The new Supreme Leader's nationally broadcast statement — "We pledge to avenge your pure blood and the blood of all the martyrs... Soon individuals among the freedom-seekers across the world will each carry out part of this divine mission" — has transformed revenge into an independent political objective. The Iranian Intelligence Ministry released an official decree stating vengeance is "a national demand." This is not temporary rhetoric but a structural feature of the conflict [33]. The critical insight: even if one engine slows, the other can continue driving escalation. Most analysts are treating these as separate problems; they are not — they are beginning to interact in ways that fundamentally change the logic of the war [33].

Trump's decision to declare the ceasefire over, reimpose a blockade, and announce American control over Hormuz fundamentally changes the political objectives. He declared the United States the "Guardian of the Strait of Hormuz," said America will "probably run it," and announced plans to charge a 20% fee on cargo — a political commitment that makes backing down significantly harder [34]. Pape identifies two factors driving Trump's escalation despite midterm risks. **The Hoover risk**: Trump signed the MoU to avoid an economic crisis, explicitly saying he did not want to be "Herbert Hoover." But the MoU failed to stop the oil inventory drawdown — the world gained only about 14 days of supply from a couple hundred million barrels released. The SPR salt caverns face a structural risk: at low levels, water eats away the cavern walls causing collapse. "We're getting into new territory" [33]. **MAGA turnout risk**: According to the Reagan Institute's Summer Survey, 51% of self-identified MAGA Republicans favor replacing Iran's government; only 25% prefer a negotiated settlement. If Trump fails to open Hormuz, a significant portion of MAGA will not show up at the polls — losing 5–10% of the voting base means losing many seats and becoming "the lamest of lame ducks" [33].

Iran's political objectives have expanded through three phases: **Survival** (Feb–Mar 2026, preserving the regime after decapitation of senior leadership), **Ambition** (Apr–Jun 2026, using Hormuz leverage to pursue a new regional security order from Lebanon through the Gulf to Yemen), and **Revenge** (Jul 2026–, the Supreme Leader's call to action adding vengeance as a third objective). Pape draws on 25 years of suicide terrorism research: 95% of suicide attacks coerce democratic withdrawal from territory. The Rushdie fatwa (1989) is the closest historical parallel — it produced attacks and deaths for decades. The current call to action has significantly greater strategic implications [33]. The self-reinforcing spiral works as follows: every US bombing campaign kills more Iranians who are then portrayed as martyrs, strengthening the legitimacy of the revenge call. If even one limited revenge attack succeeds — against an American official, business executives, a commercial airliner — public pressure for decisive retaliation would intensify, giving Trump even stronger incentives to authorize larger operations including ground options [33].

The [funeral of Iran's Supreme Leader]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/supreme-leader-funeral-2026.html) produced perhaps the largest public mobilization in the history of the Islamic Republic, with millions filling the streets chanting "Kill Trump" and "Revenge." Pape argues this is not political spectacle but strategic shift: massive nationalist mobilization changes Tehran's bargaining position by increasing the political costs of moderation. The funeral has enabled a new, more ambitious Iranian war aim: weakening and ultimately ending the permanent American military presence in the Gulf. Iran is appealing directly to Gulf monarchies — offering lower transit fees for states that distance themselves from Washington. Pape forecasts that August could become the decisive month: the SPR falling toward its operational floor as hurricane season threatens Gulf Coast production, and Iran possessing growing nationalist confidence, continuing Hormuz influence, and an opponent with diminishing economic room [25].

The US military is already pre-positioning for Stage 3 ground operations. Two carrier strike groups moved from the Indian Ocean into the Gulf of Oman — placing 5,000–6,000 personnel within Iranian missile and drone range. Coastal suppression bombing concentrates on surveillance radars, command-and-control networks, anti-ship missile batteries, and drone launch sites along Iran's southeastern coast — the classic preparatory targets for littoral operations. A Marine Expeditionary Unit has been repositioned closer to the Gulf of Oman. Pape identifies two operational plans: **Plan A** — carriers provide air cover for the "Southern Passage" near Oman, protecting ships from Iranian drones and missiles; **Plan B** — if air cover is insufficient, take territory on the coast, specifically Chabahar. "This would be the worst losses we've seen since Vietnam." Any ground war will likely last at least six months [33].

Pape's framework is stark: there is no third choice between surrender and escalation. "Everybody wants there to be a third choice. That doesn't make a third choice." Either Trump capitulates and accepts Iran as the rising regional hegemon, or he escalates toward Stage 3 ground operations. The war is likely to continue "at least through January" 2027 [33]. He also addresses suggestions of using nuclear weapons: radiation clouds cannot be controlled and would kill large numbers of American troops over time. Lindsey Graham's call for nuclear strikes is "clickbait" — a nuclear detonation in Gaza or the West Bank would kill tens of thousands of Jews, echoing the bombing of Auschwitz dilemma [33].

Lebanon represents a critical pressure point for horizontal escalation. A parallel US-Israel deal in Lebanon undermines the MoU with Iran — the two documents became contradictory. Kent calls US intervention in Lebanon "absurd" and warns that Houthi attacks in the Red Sea targeting Saudi oil would be a major game-changer. The apparent division between VP Vance pushing for the MoU and Secretary Rubio undermining it is a genuine internal conflict, not a "good cop, bad cop" strategy. Kent advises Trump to militarily restrain Israel by cutting significant chunks of aid — providing maximum space for the MoU to work [26]. Pape identifies commercial shipping as the key escalation pathway: Hormuz remains open not because the US forced it open, but because Iran permits it — and Iran can withdraw that permission at any time. Iran's three conditions for sustained passage are: release of $12 billion in frozen assets, an end to Israeli operations in Lebanon, and a reduction of US military presence in the region [5][20]. The Houthi situation in the Red Sea is concurrently escalating — Saudi Arabia has been trying to move 4 million barrels/day through the Red Sea, and the Houthis have shut that down before [33].

Tchakarova warns of a "very bumpy second half of 2026": US-Israel-Iran escalations will continue with no end in sight; Yemen-Saudi Arabia tensions are coming; Türkiye and Syria might also get involved. Russia will face a strong recession scenario and boost its summer offensive in Ukraine. Two needed ceasefires in Ukraine and Iran are the mutually strengthening signal toward preventing the [Global System Rupture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/global-system-rupture.html) scenario [37].

## The Infrastructure-Strike Escalation and the Bab el-Mandeb Card

[Heather Cox Richardson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/heather-cox-richardson.html) documents the next phase of the escalation arc in mid-July 2026. On July 15, Trump reversed his reimposed blockade on Iran within 24 hours — claiming that Gulf allies told him they would rather invest directly in the U.S. than pay tolls — only to convene a Situation Room meeting with his top national security team to discuss "new plans for devastating strikes" against Iran. Those in the room included Vice President Vance, Secretary of State Rubio, Defense Secretary Hegseth, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs General Caine, CIA Director Ratcliffe, and Middle East envoy Witkoff. Before the meeting, Trump told Fox News that after the "hard" strikes that week, "next week, it gets really bad for them because next week comes the power plants. Next week comes the bridges. We're gonna knock out all their power plants. We're gonna knock out all their bridges unless they get to the table and negotiate." Attacks on civilian infrastructure are usually illegal under international law. A former member of Trump's negotiating team told Laura Rozen of *Diplomatic* that the escalation was probably a ploy to kick-start further negotiations — "a very risky and low probability gamble, but nothing else makes sense" [44].

The next day, Reuters reported that Iranian leaders had asked the Houthis they back in Yemen to close the [Bab el-Mandeb]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html) strait — the second of the two main routes for Middle East oil exports, alongside Hormuz — in response to the threatened strikes on Iran's power network. Closure of the second strait would exacerbate energy shortages even as the U.S. [SPR]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/spr-depletion-crisis.html) dropped to its lowest level since 1983. Iran thus activated its geographic second front: rather than bearing the entire escalation cost through Hormuz, Tehran widened the pressure by operationalizing its Red Sea proxy [45].

## Mutual Exhaustion: Both Sides on the Clock

[Andrew Davidson]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/geopolitical-futures.html) (GPF) frames the renewed fighting as an unequal but ultimately inconclusive contest. Destroying Iranian capabilities is not the same as compelling Tehran to accept US terms. Washington can weaken [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s ability to threaten shipping and prevent it from exercising control over the Strait of Hormuz. It cannot, however, cheaply guarantee normal commercial passage or force Iran to surrender its remaining leverage without accepting a prolonged campaign. If Iran cannot withstand the campaign indefinitely, it can preserve enough uncertainty to deny the US a clean political victory [43].

The two sides are pursuing different forms of exhaustion. Iran is trying to outlast Washington politically even as military losses, economic pressure, and leadership uncertainty weaken its own position. The US is trying to force Tehran to pause before the costs of regional defense exceed the political value of the campaign. The military balance strongly favors the United States — American forces can identify and destroy Iranian maritime and coastal systems faster than Tehran can replace or employ them — yet Iran's remaining systems are dispersed across a large territory, and commercial confidence depends not on how many Iranian assets are destroyed but on whether another vessel might still be hit. Washington must provide security continuously; Iran needs only intermittent success. A single attack can restore uncertainty after numerous safe transits and keep commercial traffic below normal levels [43].

Iran's margin for victory is shrinking. US strikes are degrading the military systems needed to sustain disruption, while the [reinstated blockade]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/us-naval-blockade-iran.html) is constraining seaborne oil exports that provide Iran with critical foreign currency and state revenue. Overland routes through the North-South corridor can only partially offset these losses. As the margin narrows, limited attacks become less effective because they impose lower costs on Washington than Tehran is absorbing in return. Iran's clearest means of changing the calculation would be to attack Gulf energy infrastructure or expand maritime disruption to the Bab el-Mandeb strait — but either option could unite its adversaries: ending the Gulf states' role as mediators, deepening their military cooperation with Washington, bringing [Israel]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) back into the campaign, and justifying broader strikes on Iranian territory. Iran's strongest remaining escalation option is also the one most likely to unite its adversaries [43].

The pressure is not confined to the waterways and coastlines. The US strike on the Aq Taqeh Khan railway bridge in Aqqala County — on a corridor linking Iran to [Central Asia]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/central-asia.html) and onward to [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) and [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) — demonstrated that pressure would not remain confined to the waterways. These losses are accumulating amid uncertainty over Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, whose prolonged absence from public view has weakened the regime's ability to project clear authority [43].

Yet time is not entirely on Washington's side either. The US can absorb the costs of the campaign far longer than Iran, but a prolonged operation places growing demands on forces and munitions already committed across several regions. Further Iranian escalation would require the US to defend not only personnel and bases but also ports, shipping routes, and energy infrastructure throughout the Gulf — consuming air and missile defense interceptors, surveillance capacity, and naval assets. If commercial traffic remains disrupted, the economic costs compound the military burden. Because the campaign depends on continued Gulf access and cooperation, prolonged resource expenditure without restored passage or Iranian concessions becomes increasingly difficult to justify. The approaching US midterm elections sharpen this pressure, but the larger constraint is the cost of sustaining regional protection without a clear political result [43].

The likely outcome is a truce rather than a settlement. If neither side escalates, Iran is more likely to seek a ceasefire before the US abandons its core objectives — even if Tehran briefly intensifies attacks to improve its bargaining position. Iran's release of a US citizen amid the renewed exchanges suggests Tehran already has de-escalation in mind. Washington has greater room to adjust the practical terms — easing blockade enforcement, allowing limited Iranian exports, or modifying transit and escort procedures — without abandoning its core demand that Iran stop threatening commercial passage. A pause would likely involve Iran scaling back attacks enough for commercial traffic to recover while preserving its formal claims over Hormuz and presenting the outcome as proof that resistance forced negotiations. Washington would likewise portray any reduction in attacks as evidence that military pressure worked. Neither side would have resolved the underlying dispute, and both would retain incentives to test the balance sometime in the future. Washington can keep winning the strikes, and Iran can keep denying Washington a clean victory, but neither can sustain that contradiction indefinitely [43].

## The Casualty Threshold and Uncontrolled Escalation

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) identifies a discrete escalation threshold crossed on July 15, 2026: Iran's use of heavy anti-ship missiles with up to 1,000-pound warheads against tankers in the Strait of Hormuz. The weapons are capable of catastrophic, ship-sinking destruction — far eclipsing minor drone strikes. Numerous US Navy ships had been dangerously repositioned close to Iran's coast, well within Iran's anti-ship cruise missile range. Pape's assessment is that even 30–40 US casualties would instantly shatter diplomatic options, forcing an uncontrollable, public-fueled demand for payback that could lock both sides into a costly spiral and make Stage Three politically difficult to avoid. From that moment forward, the political logic of the war changes: the imperative becomes responding to American deaths, not just protecting freedom of navigation. Every death — and every successful retaliation — creates new domestic pressure to escalate further, including for ground operations as payback rather than merely to "reopen" Hormuz. Neither Washington nor Tehran fully controls what happens once those casualties occur. The shift is from controlled brinkmanship to high-risk, uncontrolled escalation [46].

## The Second Front: Horizontal Escalation to Bab el-Mandeb

Within 48 hours of July 16, Yemen's Iran-backed Houthis launched a coordinated barrage of ballistic missiles and drones targeting [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html)'s Abha International Airport in direct retaliation for airstrikes on Sanaa. Pape frames the assault as a classic case of *horizontal escalation* that widens the war by opening a second maritime front at the [Bab el-Mandeb]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/red-sea.html) gateway. Until then, the war had centered on the Strait of Hormuz; opening a second front at Bab el-Mandeb transforms the crisis from a regional confrontation into a broader contest over the security of global energy flows. The move expands the political stakes for every major actor while shrinking their room to compromise: leaving two strategic chokepoints under threat would appear politically unacceptable, forcing Washington, Riyadh, and their allies toward military rather than diplomatic responses. [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) gains additional leverage without directly committing its own forces — operationalizing its Red Sea proxy to impose costs Tehran itself need not bear. As energy markets tighten and oil inventories continue falling, every new disruption raises both the economic costs of restraint and the political incentives for further escalation. Every Houthi attack on Saudi Arabia — and every Saudi retaliation — creates new pressure to escalate further, including for ground operations to "reopen" Hormuz, not just to respond to the Houthis [47].

## Shaping Operations for Ground Assault

On the evening of July 17, US Central Command conducted multiple waves of airstrikes against [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html), targeting key bridges, railway junctures, and power lines connecting key cities, particularly in Hormozgan province and around the major port city of Bandar Abbas. Pape assesses these as classic "shaping" operations in preparation for a ground assault, consistent with the air strikes over all six days in the previous week. The latest strikes disrupt the logistics and mobility of Iranian military forces in the south, while the prior strikes degrade coastal missile storage and launch sites, air defense systems, and surveillance facilities from Chabahar to Bushehr — along hundreds of miles of coastline that complicates the defender's problem in stopping any landing or seizure of specific islands [48].

The shaping operations deepen both sides' Escalation Traps. Once the United States begins shaping the battlefield for possible ground operations, stopping becomes politically harder than continuing: having already accepted the risks of sustained escalation, Washington faces growing pressure to demonstrate that those costs will produce tangible strategic gains. Every additional strike raises the political price of restraint while making a larger military commitment appear increasingly necessary to justify what has already been done. Iran's trap deepens in parallel: having survived six days of US attacks while still contesting Hormuz, Tehran faces mounting pressure to demonstrate that American escalation cannot succeed. Standing down after these strikes would risk signaling weakness both domestically and across the region, so the political incentives for Iranian leaders increasingly favor further escalation even as the military and economic costs mount. Pape expects multi-faceted escalation in the near future [48].

## Civilian Infrastructure as Battlefield Target

On July 18, Iran crossed another escalation threshold: instead of concentrating on military bases, Iranian missile and drone strikes hit a vital power and water desalination plant in [Kuwait]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/kuwait.html) — part of the system that produces roughly 90% of the country's drinking water. Unlike past fragments from Iranian strikes that had inadvertently hit desalination plants, this plant was directly targeted. Pape identifies the strike as a categorical escalation: civilian survival infrastructure is becoming a direct battlefield target [49].

The strategic logic runs counter to the conventional expectation that targeting civilians breaks their will. Drawing on his *Bombing to Win* research, Pape's central finding is that when civilians suddenly lose access to life's basic necessities, survival rapidly overwhelms every other concern in the affected areas. Instead of rallying around the flag, people become consumed by the immediate struggle to keep themselves and their families alive — "anger comes after survival." But the Escalation Trap operates in the opposite direction from civilian morale: leaders confronted with collapsing civilian conditions often seek to shift public anger outward, while neighboring states face growing pressure to respond to humanitarian and security spillovers. The result could be a wider regional war whose consequences become increasingly difficult for any side to control. The strike proves the concept for a vulnerability pattern that extends across the Gulf: [Qatar]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/qatar.html) and Bahrain each rely on only three to five desalination plants, while [Saudi Arabia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/saudi-arabia.html) and the [UAE]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/uae.html) rely on 30 and 70 respectively. See [Geoeconomics of Water]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/geoeconomics-of-water.html) [49].

## Technology, Cost, and Innovation

O'Brien identifies a structural asymmetry in the conflict: cheap mass has greater advantage than expensive small-batch systems. The United States relied on pricey Patriot and THAAD interceptor systems to defend against cheap Iranian drones — an exchange ratio that is mathematically unsustainable over any prolonged engagement. Furthermore, the US did not possess a cheap mass attack weapon, and its munitions stocks were greatly run down by the time the MoU was signed. Rebuilding these inventories will take years. The broader conclusion O'Brien draws is that the US war machine is built on wrong principles — expensive, technologically advanced systems in small numbers, optimized for a paradigm of warfare that [drone warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/drone-warfare.html) has rendered obsolete [2].

RUSI (Carneiro Júnior) documents the operational role of Palantir's Maven Smart System during Operation Epic Fury. The platform — descended from Project Maven, which provoked internal revolt at Google before Palantir took it over — fused satellite reconnaissance, persistent drone surveillance, signals intelligence, and population pattern-of-life analysis to identify, prioritise, and recommend strike packages. By April 9, US Central Command reported more than 13,000 strikes against Iranian targets, roughly 1,000 on the opening day alone. Palantir's CTO told Bloomberg Television the conflict would be remembered as the first major war in which AI played a central role. The juxtaposition is analytically significant: a war fought substantially over the survival of Iran's nuclear programme is difficult evidence for Palantir's manifesto claim that "the atomic age is ending, and a new era of deterrence built on AI is set to begin." See [AI Military Race]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/ai-military-race.html) [41].

Urbanski (RUSI) argues that the Iran war and the Ukraine war carry opposite signs — in Iran, an authoritarian regime struck by a superpower; in Ukraine, a young democracy attacked by an authoritarian superpower — yet for NATO's budget planners they yield one coherent set of lessons. Neither Iran nor Ukraine found a way to defeat a superpower decisively on the battlefield. Both found ways to make a superpower's victory expensive — and potentially unaffordable — by shifting the contest into domains where the Western alliance has under-invested: the economics of munitions, the protection of infrastructure far behind the front line, commercial risk, and the public's confidence in everyday security. The US expended roughly half its Patriot inventory, more than 850 Tomahawks, and over 1,000 air-launched stand-off weapons. Rebuilding those stockpiles will take four to five years [38].

## Second-Order Cascades and Systemic Effects

Post-war Iran has escalated external operations in Europe through the IRGC's "gig economy" model — recruiting disposable agents via gaming platforms for assassination plots and surveillance. These operations are funded through an oil-backed shadow banking system that circumvents Western financial architecture. The EU's designation of the IRGC has not materially degraded this network. A post-war Iran governed through an IRGC lens will likely escalate operations abroad, complicating any diplomatic stabilization framework. See [Organised Crime and Statecraft]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/organised-crime-and-statecraft.html) [24].

The war's most consequential structural impact may be the transformation of insurance markets. War-risk premiums in the Strait of Hormuz surged — in some cases by more than 1,000% — forcing a shift from annual policies to voyage-by-voyage underwriting. Risk, once pooled broadly, is now assessed in granular, transactional terms. As private insurers reduced exposure, governments stepped in with sovereign-backed mechanisms: the US International Development Finance Corporation supported maritime insurance capacity, [India]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/india.html) developed sovereign-backed insurance pools, and major insurers formed new consortia to maintain coverage in high-risk corridors. The emergence of regionalized insurance systems means that the ability to insure trade is becoming a strategic tool. See [Insurance and Trade Warfare]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/insurance-and-trade-warfare.html) [27].

The war exposed structural vulnerabilities in the defense industry that extend beyond munitions depletion. In US Army deployments, more than 70 percent of logistics tonnage is fuel, and some 55 percent is consumed by the logistics systems themselves. The war demonstrated that fuel supply chains can be disrupted more quickly and effectively than front-line forces can be destroyed. NATO's Locked Shields 2026 exercise simulated large-scale attacks on power grids, telecom networks, and government systems, revealing the increased vulnerability of digital infrastructure. The defense industry is shifting toward energy resilience (microreactors, microgrids, storage) and cyber-physical integration. See [Defense Industrial Resilience]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/defense-industrial-resilience.html) [27].

Colibasanu (GPF) identifies the energy-to-food cascade as a defining second-order effect. The Gulf was the world's largest regional exporter of urea and ammonia and the second-largest exporter of diammonium phosphate (DAP) and monoammonium phosphate (MAP). Around one-third of global seaborne fertilizer trade passes through Hormuz. Food shocks will be strongest in regions that rely heavily on imported fertilizer, subsidized agricultural systems, and nitrogen-intensive crops. See [Food and Agriculture]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/food-and-agriculture.html) [27]. Colibasanu frames the broader lesson as the replacement of efficiency with resilience as the organizing principle of the global economy. The key pattern across all regions is not collapse but persistent cost inflation and volatility — resilience is replacing efficiency, and the cost of resilience is permanent [27].

The US focus on Iran had a direct impact on sanctions against Russia. RUSI documents how the US issued general licences allowing Russian oil sales during the Hormuz crisis, kneecapping G7 price cap efforts just as they were biting (Urals fell below $50/barrel by December 2025). Nearly 30% of Russian crude in April 2026 was transported on G7 tankers. The price cap is exhausted — RUSI recommends replacing it with a full maritime services ban [39].

CSIS (Cancian and Park) analyze the Trump administration's $87.6 billion supplemental request to Congress (June 24, 2026). Approximately one-third (~$32.7 billion) is driven by Iran war costs. Key DOD line items include $21 billion for munitions (only ~$7.6B estimated for war-use replacement), $17.3 billion for operational costs, and $12.1 billion for classified programs. A major omission is base repair — 228 destroyed structures identified by satellite imagery. Estimates reaching hundreds of billions did not account for the decline in daily costs after the first days [40].

CSIS (Shah and Sherman) also assess that Russia-Iran cyber collaboration during the conflict has been overstated. An unverified Ukrainian intelligence assessment suggested interactions between Russian hacktivist groups and Iranian Handala Hack via Telegram, but the evidence base is thin. Historical distrust (Russian Turla's commandeering of Iranian cyber infrastructure for false-flag operations) limits capability sharing. Drone technology and satellite imagery sharing — where Russia provided Iran with targeting intelligence for US assets — have demonstrated far greater strategic impact [40].

## The Ceasefire Reignition: July 11–16

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documents the day-by-day military operations that reignited the war in mid-July, framing them as the inevitable product of a "consequence-free world" the US chose to inhabit. The [Versailles Memorandum]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html) was a sham: Iran gave up nothing, Trump lacked any foundation to declare victory, and when the truth dawned he faced the binary choice between admitting the greatest strategic defeat in US history or restarting the bombing. He chose the latter. Trump declared the ceasefire formally over on July 8 at the [Ankara Summit]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/nato-ankara-summit-2026.html); what that meant became clear over five consecutive days of US strikes [50]:

- **July 11**: CENTCOM struck approximately 140 targets and set terms to end hostilities (open the strait, halt shipping attacks, surrender enriched uranium). The IRGC struck the container ship *GFS Galaxy* in Hormuz and declared the strait closed; a claimed strike on Jordan's Prince Hassan Air Base was disputed.
- **July 12**: US strikes on Qeshm Island, Bandar Abbas, and Hajiabad; first use of one-way attack drones this campaign. Iran fired its widest missile/drone volley yet at UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman, and Jordan.
- **July 13**: Five-hour US strike wave on Bushehr, Chah Bahar, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa, Bandar Abbas. IRGC missiles struck two UAE-flagged tankers (*al-Bahiya*, *Mombasa*) in Omani waters — 1 Indian crew killed, several injured; oil price spiked almost 10%.
- **July 14**: Third consecutive night of coastal strikes; Trump reinstated the naval blockade and threatened infrastructure (bridges, power plants) next.
- **July 15**: Seven-hour fourth-night strike wave (command centers, air defense, Bandar Abbas) plus a separate round on Greater Tunb Island; an empty tanker was disabled with Hellfire missiles for ignoring the blockade. Kuwait intercepted Iranian missiles/drones.
- **July 16**: Fifth consecutive day of strikes (Bandar Abbas, Ahvaz, Chabahar); Trump reportedly weighing ground operations and bombing an underground site linked to Iran's nuclear program.

O'Brien's likelihood table crystallizes the strategic assessment: 99% that Iran has made no meaningful concession; 98.5% that [Russia]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/russia.html) and [China]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/china.html) are benefitting significantly (the US blowing through depleted munitions means no new Patriots for Ukraine and a weaker Taiwan deterrent; rising oil prices boost Russian revenue); 90% that oil markets still believe Trump will "TACO" (Trump Always Chickens Out); 80% that ground-troop threats are hot air; 66% that Trump still needs a deal by the midterms; and 99.9% that the US is haemorrhaging allies and partners. The campaign is an extraordinary gamble: restarting the bombing in a weaker strategic position than when operations ended in April, with the midterm elections approaching and 60–66% of Americans opposing the war [50].

The "consequence-free world" framing extends to the assassination-policy hypocrisy. Trump's July 11 tweet threatening to "completely decimate and destroy all areas of Iran" should Iran attempt to assassinate him is juxtaposed by O'Brien against Trump's own celebration of the assassination of Khamenei at the start of the war. The US pursued and legitimized a policy of leader assassination; the same policy now makes Trump a legitimate target for Iran. Undersecretary of State Colby's parallel tweet thread — insisting that a "collective middle powers strategy" is a distraction and that "access to the American DIB is a privilege, not a right" — is read as panic: the administration is realizing that its own Europe-withdrawal policies have produced the strategic autonomy it now pleads with Europeans not to pursue. See [Donald Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html), [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html), and [Hormuz Ceasefire Collapse]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/hormuz-ceasefire-collapse-2026.html) [50].

## The Aqaba Threat and the Basra-Aqaba Pipeline

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) identifies a new escalation vector in his Breaking Analysis #6 (July 19, 2026): [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html)'s threat to Aqaba, Jordan's only coastal city on the northern tip of the Red Sea. The US Embassy in Amman issued an emergency alert advising Americans to avoid Aqaba's airport and seaport due to a specific credible threat, and air raid sirens went off in Jordan warning of incoming Iranian missiles. Aqaba functions as both a tourist destination and a deep-water commercial port — a gateway to desert attractions and, crucially, a potential terminus for the long-delayed Basra-Aqaba pipeline [51].

The threat follows a Washington meeting between Iraq's prime minister and Jordan's foreign minister to accelerate the Basra-Aqaba pipeline — a 1,600-kilometer, $18 billion project designed to carry 2.25 million barrels per day from southern [Iraq]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iraq.html) to the Red Sea. The Iran war has cut southern Iraqi exports by approximately 70%, exposing Baghdad's dependence on the Strait of Hormuz and making the bypass pipeline a strategic imperative. Jordan gains transit fees and 150,000 barrels per day for its Zarqa refinery. For Iraq, the calculus is about security rather than profit: Aqaba costs roughly $6 per barrel versus 60 cents through the Gulf, but the premium buys insulation from Iranian coercion [51].

Under the Escalation Trap framework, Pape argues that Iran has powerful incentives to widen the conflict beyond Hormuz and strike any infrastructure that could permanently reduce its coercive leverage. A functioning Basra-Aqaba pipeline would undercut Iran's "security resistance belt" — a chain of control stretching from the Mediterranean through the Red Sea to the Gulf — by creating a permanent alternative outlet for Iraqi oil. The pipeline would weaken Iran's long-term ability to threaten global oil flows. Projects like Aqaba are increasingly attractive military targets — not for their immediate economic value but because they challenge Iran's strategy of preserving leverage through control of regional energy chokepoints. Striking bypass infrastructure before it becomes operational is cheaper than competing with it after it exists [51].

## Can America Open Hormuz? The Political Clock

Pape's analysis (July 20, 2026) argues that America is unlikely to reopen the Strait of Hormuz before the November midterm elections. He draws a critical distinction between tactical and strategic victory: tactical success measures military performance — launchers destroyed, attacks declined — while strategic success requires the political objective of restoring normal commercial shipping. The judge of strategic victory is the market itself: shipping companies, insurers, and commodity traders must conclude that transiting Hormuz is routine again. Only 50–100 drones or missiles are needed to swarm ships, and Iran possesses tens of thousands; a single successful strike can reverse months of restored confidence [52].

The suppression problem is structural. Iran spans 1.65 million square kilometers — three times the size of Iraq, four times California — with a population of 92 million and roughly one million personnel. Mobile launchers, anti-ship missiles ranging 80–600+ miles, and Shahed-136 drones with 600–1,500 mile range are dispersed deep into Iran's interior, requiring suppression across roughly 500 miles of waterway. Destroying 80–90% of these assets is insufficient — all must be suppressed continuously. A Shahed-136 drone is approximately the size of an F-150 truck; Iran has an estimated 2.5–3 million comparable vehicles, making visual identification of launch platforms effectively impossible [52].

Pape demolishes the prospect of a limited landing. Iran's coastline rises into rugged mountains with concealed firing positions — there is no accessible beachhead. A Marine Expeditionary Unit of roughly 2,200–2,500 Marines controls only a one-by-two-mile area; once a first landing succeeds, the campaign revolves around protecting the growing force ashore rather than reopening Hormuz. Iran does not need to defeat a landing — it needs to make remaining there dangerous enough that the mission expands beyond its original scope [52].

The political clock is Iran's strategic advantage. Iran fights for regime survival; the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) fights for a narrow objective with a domestic deadline. Iran needs only to prevent the reopening of Hormuz before November. If Hormuz remains contested through the election, Congress may become the decisive constraint: by 1974, Congress refused further military assistance to South Vietnam. If Republicans retain Congress, [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) has greater latitude; if Democrats capture either chamber, investigations and funding battles narrow his options. Iran does not need to win militarily — it needs to keep the contest going until America's political clock overtakes its military clock [52].

War-risk premiums for vessels transiting the strait have risen from approximately 2% to 3–10% of hull value — a 40-fold increase over prewar rates. ## Western Strategic Incompetence and the Escort Fantasy

[Aurelien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/aurelien.html) argues the West cannot understand the Iran conflict because its strategic concepts are outdated and its ruling class lacks military literacy. The post-1945 legal framework created a grey area where wars are no longer "declared," yet politicians and pundits continue using century-old vocabulary — "acts of war," "belligerents," *casus belli* — that no longer applies. This conceptual confusion compounds the practical inability to assess what is happening on the ground [54].

The "escort" paradigm for [Hormuz]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/hormuz-toll-system.html) epitomizes doctrinal obsolescence. The concept derives from World War-era anti-submarine warfare, when grouping merchant vessels with escorting warships reduced losses from U-boats. Today's threat is not individual submarines but swarms of drones and missiles that cannot be defended against at scale. Escorts provide more targets, not protection. The purpose of attack is not necessarily to sink ships but to discourage sailing — a political and economic effect, not a kinetic one [54].

Aurelien's assessment is stark: [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) holds the initiative and the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) cannot win. Western failure resulted from arrogance, inherited military folklore, and a simple disinclination to study Iranian military capability. The prospect of a renewed conflict with Iran, as some US pundits already map out, is not possible. Future evolution of the crisis depends primarily on Iran. The most likely outcome is prolonged low-level tension that suits Iran and strains Western cohesion — and it will not be the West that decides when it stops [54]. See [Revolution in Military Affairs]({{ site.baseurl }}/themes/revolution-in-military-affairs.html) for the missile-dominance dimension.

The exchange crystallizes the central gap between the administration's declared victory narrative and the operational reality of a war that continues to impose costs on American forces and taxpayers alike. ## The "Honor" Framing: Sentimentality as Strategy

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) identifies a rhetorical dimension of the July 20 strikes that illustrates the broader cultural dysfunction in the US war effort. [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) declared that the strikes were conducted "in honor of" three servicemen killed in action — a formulation that substitutes faux-testimonial sentiment for strategic logic. The rhetorical move is characteristic of an administration that wraps military violence in movie-script commemoration rather than articulating operational objectives. O'Brien frames this as continuous with Secretary Hegseth's broader romanticization of sacrifice — testosterone screening, hyper-masculine bravery rhetoric — while the actual determinants of battlefield success (logistics, technology, education, adaptability) are neglected. The "honor" framing is not merely a speech act; it is symptomatic of an institutional culture that celebrates the qualities that do not win wars [57]. ## Eleven Nights and No Objective: The Self-Weakening Dynamic

[Phillips O'Brien]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/phillips-obrien.html) documents the continuation of US strikes through eleven consecutive nights as of July 22, 2026 — a campaign with no achievable military objective. [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) is hoping [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) will offer peace terms he can sell: a nuclear concession plus the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz without tolls. No military operation short of full-scale invasion and regime change can produce this outcome. General McCaffrey estimates a full invasion would require 600,000 troops and one year — a commitment that is not happening. Taking Kharg Island, often discussed as a limited objective, would achieve nothing political. Every day of strikes thus depletes US munitions while strengthening Iran's political position, with no theory of how military action produces the desired political result [58].

Iran's "sideways escalation" strategy compounds the dynamic: rather than attacking only US forces, Iran is systematically striking the infrastructure of US-allied Gulf states, making the war politically costly for host governments and demonstrating that the US cannot protect its allies. The timeline from July 12–21 documents this widening pattern: July 12 strikes on Qatar, UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan, and Oman (including a Patriot battery and ammo depot in Kuwait and Duqm port in Oman); July 13–14 cluster munition warhead against Bahrain; July 14–15 strikes on Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan; July 16–17 strikes on Kuwaiti desalination infrastructure; July 17–18 strike on Jordan's Muwaffaq Salti Air Base (2 US KIA) and a ballistic missile on a US base in Saudi Arabia (first in 3+ months); July 19 strikes on Jordan, Kuwait, Bahrain, a second strike on Kuwaiti desalination, and ballistic missiles on Aqaba near Eilat; July 20 strikes on two oil tankers in Hormuz, US assets in Jordan/Kuwait/Bahrain, and an IRGC-claimed hit on an Amazon data center in Bahrain; July 21 claimed destruction of US radar and air defense in Muharraq and Riffa, Bahrain [58]. ## S1 Domain: Dual Blockade and Routing Regime Enforcement

[Velina Tchakarova]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/velina-tchakarova.html)'s Day 144 diagnostic places the US-Iran War domain (S1) at RUPTURE — the most severe status level in her seven-domain framework. The ninth consecutive strike night is underway, with a dual blockade active (US naval blockade on Iranian ports and Iranian chokepoint control over Hormuz). The campaign has struck 95 locations across 12 cities, representing a scale of bombardment that continues to escalate. The diagnostic identifies kinetic enforcement of a routing regime — the US attempting to dictate which vessels may transit and on what terms — as the defining operational pattern. Crucially, the diagnostic also notes the first credible de-escalation channel since the [MoU collapse]({{ site.baseurl }}/events/iran-ceasefire-mou.html), suggesting that even at RUPTURE the system generates its own counter-dynamics. ## Pickaxe Mountain: The Underground Nuclear Sanctuary

[Robert Pape]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/authors/robert-pape.html) identifies a new strategic dimension of the conflict in his Breaking Analysis #7 (July 21, 2026): [Israeli]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/israel.html) intelligence reports that [Iran]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/iran.html) has moved thousands of uranium-enrichment centrifuges into deeply fortified tunnels beneath Pickaxe Mountain near Natanz, buried 300–450 feet under solid rock — beyond the reach of existing conventional bunker-busting munitions. Satellite imagery shows continued construction, reinforced tunnel entrances, and heavy vehicle activity. Iran has denied IAEA access for verification. [Trump]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/trump.html) has identified the facility as a potential military target, but it remains untouched [59].

The core analytical insight is that airpower can destroy targets but rarely compels determined regimes to abandon core political objectives. US and Israeli bombing achieved tactical successes throughout the war, but Iran responded by moving enrichment capability deeper underground. Airpower did not solve the nuclear problem — it transformed it. A facility 300–450 feet beneath solid rock cannot be reliably eliminated by conventional bunker-busting alone. Pape frames this as "the Ground Power Dilemma": if Washington concludes Pickaxe Mountain has become Iran's protected nuclear sanctuary, the choice narrows to tolerating a hardened nuclear program or accepting far greater risks of operations beyond an air campaign [59].

The fear-driven escalation logic compounds the trap. Drawing on the 2003 Iraq WMD precedent, Pape argues that leaders escalate based on what they believe adversaries are capable of, not what later proves true. If Washington believes Pickaxe Mountain has made Iran's nuclear program invulnerable, that belief alone creates pressure for broader military action regardless of what inspectors might discover. Uncertainty favors escalation. The political clock linkage intensifies the pressure: if the [United States]({{ site.baseurl }}/actors/countries/united-states.html) cannot reopen [Hormuz]({{ site.baseurl }}/regions/middle-east.html) before the November midterms, pressure for broader escalation — including attacks on deeply buried nuclear facilities — will grow [59].
